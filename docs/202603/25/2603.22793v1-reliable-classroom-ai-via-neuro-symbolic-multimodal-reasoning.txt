## Abstract 

Classroom AI is rapidly expanding from low-level perception toward higher-level judgments about engagement, confusion, collaboration, and instructional quality. Yet classrooms are among the hardest real-world settings for multimodal vision: they are multi-party, noisy, privacy-sensitive, pedagogically diverse, and often multilingual. In this paper, we argue that classroom AI should be treated as a critical domain , where raw predictive accuracy is insufficient unless predictions are accompanied by verifiable evidence, calibrated uncertainty, and explicit deployment guardrails. We introduce NSCR ,a neuro-symbolic framework that decomposes classroom analytics into four layers: perceptual grounding, symbolic abstraction, executable reasoning, and governance. NSCR 

adapts recent ideas from symbolic fact extraction and ver-ifiable code generation to multimodal educational settings, enabling classroom observations from video, audio, ASR, and contextual metadata to be converted into typed facts and then composed by executable rules, programs, and pol-icy constraints. Beyond the system design, we contribute a benchmark and evaluation protocol organized around five tasks: classroom state inference, discourse-grounded event linking, temporal early warning, collaboration analysis, and multilingual classroom reasoning. We further specify reli-ability metrics centered on abstention, calibration, robust-ness, construct alignment, and human usefulness. The paper does not report new empirical results; its contribution is a concrete framework and evaluation agenda intended to sup-port more interpretable, privacy-aware, and pedagogically grounded multimodal AI for classrooms. 

## 1. Introduction 

Computer vision and multimodal foundation models are moving into educational settings with the promise of help-ing teachers notice participation patterns, summarize class discussions, identify confusion, and reflect on instructional practice. The appeal is obvious: classrooms are rich multi-modal environments in which video, audio, language, ges-ture, gaze, and temporal interaction all convey pedagogi-cally meaningful information. This is precisely why class-room AI has become a growing topic within multimodal learning analytics (MMLA) and classroom observation re-search [1, 6, 10, 14, 22, 29, 31, 34]. At the same time, classrooms are one of the least forgiv-ing environments in which to deploy black-box multimodal systems. They contain many people, substantial occlusion, shifting seating layouts, overlapping speech, varied activ-ity structures, and strong distribution shift across schools, cultures, age groups, and subjects. More importantly, educa-tional targets such as engagement, confusion, self-regulation, participation opportunity, or collaboration quality are not directly visible in the way that object categories are visible. They are theory-laden constructs inferred from partial evi-dence, shaped by pedagogy and context, and often contested across stakeholders [ 8, 9, 28 ]. A student looking away from the board may be disengaged, reading a worksheet, checking peer work, waiting for a speaking turn, or simply processing a question. A long pause after a teacher prompt may indicate confusion, reflection, lack of opportunity, translation delay, or ASR failure. The dominant modeling pattern in the area still tends to couple low-level detection with direct label prediction: esti-mate gaze, posture, speech, or facial activity and map those signals to a downstream classroom judgment. This has pro-duced important advances in gaze-following and classroom observation [ 2 ], affect- and engagement-related modeling, and multimodal dashboards. But the path from signals to 

claims remains under-specified. When the system concludes that a learner is confused or that a group is collaborating well, it often cannot say which evidence mattered, which as-sumptions were invoked, how uncertainty propagated across modalities, or when it should abstain and defer to a teacher. A parallel line of work in language reasoning suggests a promising alternative. Recent neuro-symbolic meth-ods combine structured fact extraction with executable code or logical reasoning, improving interpretability and compositional reliability over purely prose-based genera-tion [ 11 , 12 , 19 , 23 ]. In multilingual long-context settings, 1

In formal reasoning, verifiable code generation with execution-time feedback can shift failures from opaque logical errors to inspectable programmatic ones [ 4]. The central lesson is simple: if the model must externalize evidence as structured facts and then compute through a verifiable substrate, the resulting system becomes easier to debug, constrain, and trust. Alongside the framework, we define a benchmark protocol centered on educationally meaningful tasks and reliability-oriented evaluation. Our contributions are fourfold: • We formalize classroom multimodal inference as a neuro-symbolic problem in which perceptual outputs become explicit intermediate facts rather than hidden latent states. • We propose NSCR, a concrete systems blueprint for grounding, reasoning, abstention, and governance in class-room AI. • We define an evaluation agenda spanning state inference, language–vision alignment, temporal intervention logic, collaboration analysis, and multilingual classroom rea-soning. • We articulate why classrooms should be treated as a criti-cal domain for multimodal AI, requiring construct align-ment, privacy-aware data minimization, and explicit hu-man oversight. 

## 2. Related Work 

Multimodal learning analytics and classroom AI. MMLA was introduced to move beyond online logs and capture learning processes through richer embodied and social sig-nals [ 6 , 22 , 34 ]. Subsequent work developed conceptual models for turning raw signals into higher-level educational knowledge [ 10 ], surveys of multimodal fusion in educational settings [ 14 , 21 ], and discussions of the promises and chal-lenges of MMLA for authentic educational environments [ 9]. On the perception side, classroom video has been used for gaze-following and observation support [ 2 , 29 ], while de-ployed and semi-deployed classroom systems have explored practical sensing, engagement analysis, and discourse-based teacher feedback [ 1, 20 , 31 , 33 ]. Our paper builds on this tradition but shifts attention from multimodal fusion alone to multimodal reasoning over explicit evidence. 

Human-centred and privacy-aware learning analytics. 

Human-Centred Learning Analytics (HCLA) emphasizes stakeholder participation, interpretability, and the sociotech-nical consequences of learning systems [ 8 , 28 ]. The field has also long recognized privacy, consent, and data-minimization concerns in educational analytics [ 24 ]. These concerns are amplified for classroom vision systems, where raw audio-video streams can reveal sensitive information about minors, teacher practice, and peer dynamics; recent classroom work has even studied automated anonymisation as a prerequi-site for responsible reuse of observational data [ 30 ]. Our framework aligns with this literature by making uncertainty, abstention, and retention policy first-class design elements rather than afterthoughts. 

Multimodal representation learning. Beyond educa-tion, multimodal machine learning has developed general tax-onomies and fusion architectures for combining language, vi-sion, and audio, including early tensor-fusion approaches and transformer-based cross-modal models [ 5, 32 , 35 ]. These works are not classroom-specific, but they inform how per-ceptual features can be aligned, fused, and temporally com-posed before symbolic abstraction. 

Neuro-symbolic reasoning and verifiable code genera-tion. Neuro-symbolic approaches aim to combine the flex-ibility of neural models with the structure and verifiability of symbolic reasoning [ 11 , 23 ]. In LLM-based reasoning, tool use and program generation can improve mathematical and logical tasks by moving computation outside natural-language rationales [ 12 ]. Recent work has extended this idea to symbolic fact extraction for multilingual reasoning [ 3] and to verifiable code generation with self-debugging loops [ 4 ]. We adapt these ideas to classrooms, where the challenge is not only computation but the alignment between ambiguous multimodal evidence and pedagogically meaningful con-structs.

4.2. Symbolic Abstraction 

Grounded observations are mapped into a limited but ex-pressive vocabulary of classroom facts. We recommend a schema organized around five predicate families: • OBS(entity, attribute, value, time, conf) for low-level observable properties; • EVENT(actor, action, target, interval, conf) for discrete events or actions; • REL(entity 1, relation, entity 2, interval, conf) for interaction or spatial re-lations; • CONTEXT(key, value, interval) for pedagog-ical phase, activity structure, or lesson metadata; and • POLICY(id, condition, consequence) 

for deployment rules, abstention logic, or retention constraints. Table 1 gives representative examples. Two design choices matter. First, symbolic facts should remain close enough to detector outputs to be grounded, but far enough from raw signals to be pedagogically mean-ingful. Second, the vocabulary should be construct aware :“help request” and “speaking opportunity” are better building blocks for classroom reasoning than unconstrained latent embeddings because they can be tied to educational theory, annotation protocols, and teacher interpretation. 

4.3. Executable Reasoning over Classroom Facts 

Once facts are created, higher-level classroom inference is delegated to an executable reasoning layer. We envision three complementary mechanisms. 

Rule-based composition. Some classroom constructs can be expressed as compositional patterns. For example, a 

confusion candidate may be supported by a recent teacher question, a failed attempt, a help request, and sustained attention on the task artifact. A participation opportunity 

may require that a student was oriented to the interaction, the floor was open, and the student was not already speaking. A collaboration episode may combine mutual orientation, balanced turns, and references to a shared artifact. These patterns need not be universally fixed; they can be encoded as templates to be refined with domain experts. 

Programmatic aggregation. Other queries are easier to express in executable code: counting how many distinct students asked for help after a specific demonstration, iden-tifying who contributed during a discussion phase, or rank-ing groups by balance of turns. This follows the spirit of program-aided reasoning [ 12 ] and symbolic fact extrac-tion [3]. 

LLM-assisted program synthesis. For more complex or open-ended queries, an LLM may propose a reasoning pro-gram from the symbolic facts and the user query. Here, the key contribution of S YM CODE -style design is that the gener-ated program becomes the reasoning artifact itself and can be executed, checked, and possibly debugged using inter-preter feedback [ 4 ]. In classroom settings, program synthesis should be constrained by a schema, a whitelist of operators, and policy checks. A generic support function for a hypothesis h ∈ H can be written as

4.4. Uncertainty, Abstention, and Governance 

Classroom systems should not equate low confidence with low stakes. We therefore make governance explicit. Policies can encode deployment rules such as: never issue a student-level alert unless two modalities agree ; when ASR confidence is below threshold, downgrade all discourse-derived claims ;

retain only symbolic traces for routine analytics ; or require teacher confirmation before any student-specific recommen-dation is shown . These rules belong in the executable layer so that they are inspectable and machine-checkable. In practice, a governance module uses confidence esti-mates, contradiction checks, and task-specific risk thresholds to decide whether to emit a label, return a structured expla-nation, request additional evidence, or abstain. This makes NSCR particularly suitable for selective prediction settings, where coverage and risk must be traded off deliberately. 44.5. Privacy-Aware Data Minimization 

A further advantage of symbolic reasoning is that it enables stronger data minimization. Many classroom uses do not require long-term storage of raw video once grounded events have been extracted. A deployment can therefore separate three retention layers: (1) short-lived raw buffers used for on-device or near-device perception, (2) symbolic traces with timestamps, uncertainty, and provenance, and (3) ag-gregate reports for teachers or researchers. This structure is aligned with established privacy principles in learning ana-lytics [ 24 ] and gives system designers a clearer handle on consent, retention, and audit than end-to-end embeddings alone. 

## 5. Task Suite and Benchmark Design 

To make NSCR actionable, we propose a benchmark suite that emphasizes educational reasoning rather than only per-ception. Table 2 summarizes five task families. 

T1: Classroom state inference. This task concerns hy-potheses about individual, group, or whole-class states such as confusion candidates, participation opportunity, or off-task behavior. We intentionally use the term candidate for many targets because educational constructs are rarely di-rectly observable. Ground-truth annotation should therefore include both target labels and coder rationales or supporting evidence spans where possible. 

T2: Discourse-grounded event linking. Here the system answers structured questions such as: Which students re-quested help after the teacher introduced the denominator example? ; Which group responded to a prompt with a peer explanation rather than a teacher-directed reply? This task directly tests the ability to link language, time, video events, and instructional context. 

T3: Temporal early warning. Educationally meaningful patterns often unfold over minutes rather than frames. A use-ful benchmark should therefore evaluate early-warning logic for confusion, disengagement, or collaborative breakdown, while also measuring whether the system knows when not to raise an alert. 

T4: Collaboration analysis. Small-group classrooms re-quire reasoning over turn balance, mutual orientation, shared artifact references, and role shifts. Because collaboration quality is context-sensitive, the benchmark should include both structured indicators and human ratings with clear rubrics. 

T5: Multilingual classroom reasoning. Many real class-rooms are multilingual or code-switched. This task extends NSAR-style symbolic reasoning to settings where transcript evidence, retrieval, translation, and classroom events must be integrated across languages. It is especially relevant for educational equity because ASR and discourse models often fail disproportionately in such settings. 

Benchmark dimensions. Across all tasks, benchmark splits should vary systematically by classroom layout, grade band, subject, activity type, number of cameras, audio qual-ity, missing modalities, and language configuration. This is essential for measuring generalization beyond the lab. 

## 6. Evaluation Protocols Beyond Accuracy 

A central thesis of this paper is that classroom AI should be evaluated at the level of reasoning and governance, not only low-level detection. We recommend a five-level protocol. 

Level 1: Perception quality. Standard metrics such as mAP, event F1, WER, DER, or temporal localization remain necessary, but they should be treated as upstream diagnostics rather than end goals. 

Level 2: Grounding fidelity. The symbolic abstraction should be evaluated directly: did the system extract the right facts, time spans, and relations from the perceptual layer? Metrics can include fact-level precision/recall, argument accuracy, and provenance correctness. 

Level 3: Reasoning correctness and faithfulness. Exe-cutable outputs should be assessed with exact match, exe-cution success, contradiction rate, and evidence sufficiency. Importantly, a system should be penalized if it returns the right answer for the wrong reasons or if the explanation omits decisive evidence.

## 7. Illustrative Use Cases 

We include three concise examples to show how the frame-work changes classroom inference. 

7.1. Confusion Candidate after a Teacher Prompt 

Assume a teacher asks an open-ended question during a geometry lesson. The system grounds the following facts: 

EVENT(teacher, open_question, class, [120,123], 0.97) EVENT(student_4, failed_attempt, proof_step, [124,127], 0.76) UTTER(student_4, help_request, step_clarification ,[128,130], 0.88) OBS(student_4, gaze_target, worksheet, 129, 0.81) CONTEXT(activity, guided_proof, [100,180]) 

A reasoning program combines these signals into a confusion candidate because the help request follows a failed attempt under an active teacher prompt. If the help request is un-supported by ASR confidence or if the same evidence could indicate translation delay rather than confusion, the system may lower support or abstain. The key difference from di-rect prediction is that the decision is now accompanied by inspectable evidence and conditions. 

7.2. Participation Opportunity vs. Non-Participation 

A classroom dashboard that simply counts speaking turns can misclassify quiet students as disengaged even when they never had a speaking opportunity. In NSCR, participation opportunity is a separate reasoning target: the system checks whether the interaction floor was open, whether the student was oriented to the discussion, whether overlapping speech blocked entry, and whether the activity phase expected in-dividual speaking. This prevents a common fairness failure 6in which absence of speech is conflated with absence of opportunity. 

7.3. Multilingual Classroom Query Answering 

Consider a bilingual classroom in which the query is posed in English but relevant discourse is partly in Spanish and partly in English. A teacher might ask: Who requested help after the fraction example? An NSAR-style pipeline can extract symbolic facts from ASR transcripts, align them with visual entities and time spans, and compute the answer through executable code. This use case links the multilingual reasoning strengths of neuro-symbolic fact extraction with the classroom setting’s need for grounded event linkage. 

## 8. Discussion, Limitations, and Ethics 

The main strength of NSCR is not that it eliminates ambi-guity, but that it localizes ambiguity in inspectable places: detector outputs, symbolic abstractions, reasoning rules, and governance thresholds. This is valuable for debugging and for participatory design with teachers and learning scientists. Still, several limitations remain. First, a symbolic schema can be transparent and yet pedagogically invalid. If the chosen predicates do not correspond to meaningful educa-tional constructs, the system will produce neat but mislead-ing explanations. Second, rule-based components can be brittle, whereas LLM-generated code can still be wrong or socially inappropriate. Third, annotation cost is substantial: construct-aligned datasets require richer labels than simple detection benchmarks. Fourth, neuro-symbolic pipelines do not remove surveillance risk. Symbolic traces may be safer than persistent raw video, but they still encode potentially sensitive information about minors and classroom practice. These limitations reinforce the need for ethics-aware de-ployment. Educational datasets involving human subjects may require IRB review, consent procedures, retention lim-its, and careful subgroup analysis. Reviewers should be able to inspect not only model performance but also the policies encoded in the system, including anonymisation and reten-tion choices [ 30 ]. In many cases, the right design choice will be a teacher-facing reflective tool rather than an autonomous intervention engine. 

## 9. Conclusion 

We presented NSCR, a neuro-symbolic framework for class-room AI that separates multimodal perception from pedagog-ically meaningful reasoning. By grounding classroom events into typed symbolic facts and composing them through exe-cutable rules, programs, and policies, NSCR offers a path toward more interpretable, uncertainty-aware, and privacy-conscious learning analytics. We also proposed a benchmark and evaluation agenda centered on reasoning quality, absten-tion, construct alignment, and human usefulness. We hope this paper helps shift classroom AI from black-box label prediction toward verifiable multimodal inference suitable for real educational settings.
