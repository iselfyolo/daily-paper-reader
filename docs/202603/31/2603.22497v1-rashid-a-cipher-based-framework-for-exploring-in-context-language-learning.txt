# Rashid: A Cipher-Based Framework for Exploring In-Context Language Learning

Abstract 

While there is growing interest in in-context language learning (ICLL) for unseen languages with large language models, such languages usu-ally suffer from the lack of NLP tools, data re-sources, and researcher expertise. This means that progress is difficult to assess, the field does not allow for easy large-scale experimentation, and findings on ICLL are often limited to very few languages and tasks. In light of such lim-itations, we introduce a framework ( Rashid )for studying ICLL wherein we reversibly cipher high-resource languages (HRLs) to construct truly unseen languages with access to a wide range of resources available for HRLs, unlock-ing previously impossible exploration of ICLL phenomena. 1 We use our framework to assess current ICLL methods for machine translation with SOTA evaluation tools and manual analy-sis, explore new directions leveraging otherwise expensive resources for improving ICLL, and test ICLL strategies for rich downstream tasks beyond machine translation. These lines of ex-ploration showcase the possibilities enabled by our framework, as well as providing actionable insights regarding current performance and fu-ture directions in ICLL. 2

1 Introduction 

The task of processing unseen or extremely low-resource languages with large language models (LLMs), first introduced by Tanzer et al. (2023), holds the potential to make important strides in ex-tending the benefits of LLMs to the long tail of languages. This is typically done by augmenting the prompt context with information about the lan-guage, using materials such as lexicons, grammar

However, progress in the field of in-context lan-guage learning (ICLL), by its nature, is hindered by resource scarcity. The unseen or truly low-resource languages of relevance to ICLL are the very languages for which we lack a) quality evalua-tion strategies including access to human expertise, b) high-quality resources for cheaply exploring a range of new strategies without significant invest-ment, and c) task data from frontier benchmarks, for testing the performance of these strategies in var-ious application scenarios. Further, insights from these setups regarding language learning are often confounded by the model’s differing existing capa-bilities in various low-resource languages. In this work, we introduce Rashid , a general-purpose framework for systematically assessing and exploring the phenomenon of ICLL. We construct truly unseen languages by reversibly ciphering high-resource languages (HRLs), preserving underlying linguistic characteristics, and providing us access to a wide array of available resources in these lan-guages for experimentation. See Figure 1 for an illustration of the framework. Crucially, this al-lows us to bypass the above problems of resource scarcity and factors confounding insights, enabling sophisticated evaluation as well as the assessment of techniques otherwise requiring potentially ex-pensive resource development for true LRLs. We make the following contributions: 

Framework We construct a sandbox for explor-ing in-context language learning for languages guar-anteed to be unseen, with access to the range of NLP tools and resources available for HRLs. 

Assessing status quo For the first time, we use SOTA evaluation techniques and manual analysis to assess current methods for ICLL for machine trans-lation (MT) in both directions. Exploring new directions We explore two re-search directions for improving ICLL. We find that near-perfect lexicon coverage would be helpful, and that cascading through a related high-resource lan-guage for generation makes considerable progress on the problems of syntactic coherence and trans-lation of idiomatic language. We find that added materials may not be directly beneficial for downstream tasks, but that a cascaded approach (MT with ICLL → task-solving) improves performance across the board. Instead, we showcase the power of our framework in providing a supplementary sandbox for systematic and broad experimentation in cases where this is hindered by resource scarcity, and for guiding expensive re-source creation. 

2 Previous Work 

In-context Language Learning Tanzer et al. (2023) first showed MT performance on an “un-seen”, language, Kalamang, by including word meaning, bitext examples, and a grammar book in the context of the prompt. This was followed by work that explores this phenomenon as aided by sev-eral such tools and materials. This includes using lexicons, morphological tools, such as morphologi-cal analysers and interlinear glossed text to augment the prompt with relevant information (Elsner and Needle, 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b; Lu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025; Ramos et al., 2025), as well as syntactic information, such as curated syntax rules and grammar documentations (Zhang et al., 2024a; Hus and Anastasopoulos, 2024; Hus et al., 2025). Pei et al. (2025) and Aycock et al. (2024) compare the usefulness of different materials for Manchu and Kalamang respectively. Li et al. (2025) com-pares few-shot ICL with provided word meanings with fine-tuning for low-resource languages. These studies largely focus on machine translation into English evaluated with string-based metrics, often involve expensive resource curation sometimes lim-iting experiments to a single language, and do not provide manual analyses or usability assessments of outputs, presumably due to hard-to-find resources and expertise in studied languages. 

Ciphering in NLP has been used for varied rea-sons besides decipherment studies (Knight et al., 2006; Berg-Kirkpatrick and Klein, 2013; Kamb-hatla et al., 2018; Shen and Smith, 2025). K et al. (2020) used a ciphered version of natural language data to study structural linguistic transfer as op-posed to surface-similarity-based transfer in BERT-like models (Devlin et al., 2019). In the context of LLM safety and jailbreaking, Yuan et al. (2023) ex-plore LLM capability to comprehend and generate text in conventional ciphers (such as shift-based) given instructions to do so, and Guo et al. (2025) show that LLMs struggle to provide reasoning in well-known ciphers, given instructions. Substitu-tion ciphers have also been used to remove LLM reliance on pretraining data while testing ICL abili-ties (Fang et al., 2025; Pei et al., 2025). As far as we know, we are the first to use substitution ciphers as a strategy for overcoming the challenges of low-resource multilingual settings to enable systematic experimentation in these settings. 

3 Constructing unseen languages: Ciphers 

Our goal is to construct truly unseen languages that have a wide range of tools and resources available for them. We obtain these languages by cipher-ing high-resource languages at the character-level in a reversible manner. Each ciphered language is referred to by an invented name and presented to the model as a “new” lan-guage. See more details about the ciphers and ex-amples of ciphered text in Appendix A. Our cipher has the following key characteristics. 

Reversibility Ciphered text should be losslessly revertible to the original language, allowing deci-pherment of model outputs for evaluation. 

Obscuring underlying language The model should process Lχ as an independent unseen lan-guage, and should not be capable of deciphering it under task constraints. Our character substitution cipher yields ≈ | C|! maps for a character inven-tory C, and would require cryptanalytic expertise, a considerable amount of ciphertext data, and crypt-analytic intent, to decipher (Shannon, 1949; Knight et al., 2006). We validate that LLMs are unable to undo the cipher even with explicit step-by-step in-structions via a decipher-then-translate test (§ A.2); thus, they are forced to rely on language learning to perform the task. We further provide a sanity check baseline in § 5), showing that models perform as expected for unseen languages. 

Maintaining underlying natural language prop-erties Our character-level cipher maintains all structural linguistic properties of the base language and its language family apart from surface forms, in-cluding usage patterns of (ciphered) words and mor-phemes, and morphosyntactic and semantic proper-ties. 4 Evaluating on ciphered languages thus lends insights on hypothetical truly unseen languages with similar properties. We acknowledge caveats with this claim regarding the disruption of certain ortho-phonological properties like vowel harmony in § 9. 

> 4Named entities should generally be recognizable to the model even in unseen languages. We deal with this by always providing a glossary of ciphered named entities to the LLM, using named entity recognition over the original language text. We manually confirm that the LLM is able to trivially copy these into outputs and exhibit expected behaviour as over true unseen languages with respect to named entities.

4 Experimental setup 

Languages We work with ten mid-to-high resource languages covering seven language (sub)families and three scripts in order to assess the effectiveness of ICLL strategies across ty-pologically diverse scenarios: German, Turkish, French, Spanish, Telugu, Hindi, Marathi, Viet-namese, Czech, and Polish. These include lan-guages with various linguistic profiles in terms of morphology (e.g. Vietnamese is isolating, Polish is fusional, and Turkish is agglutinative) and syntax (e.g. Hindi is SOV while Spanish is SVO, Turkish and Marathi are freer word order unlike French). 

Models Our primary analysis is conducted with 

GPT-5.1 (closed-source), and we additionally provide results with Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct 

(Grattafiori et al., 2024) and Qwen-2.5-7B-Instruct (Team, 2024) (open-source). We use default sampling hyperparameters for GPT-5.1 and greedy decoding for the other models. 

Tasks We primarily work with MT and evaluate on the WMT24++ dataset, which contains paragraph-level text across four domains (Deutsch et al., 2025). We describe our experiments on three more task datasets, MMLU-ProX (Xuan et al., 2025), XNLI 

(Conneau et al., 2018), and XStoryCloze (Lin et al., 2022), in § 8. 

Evaluation metrics While we are often forced to use string-based metrics for MT evaluation in low-resource settings, these have been shown to be much worse than semantics-based metrics in terms of correlation with human-perceived quality (Freitag et al., 2021, 2022). We accordingly report 

xCOMET (Guerreiro et al., 2024) and GEMBA-MQM 

(Kocmi and Federmann, 2023) in addition to chrF 

(Popović, 2015). The former is a trained neural metric. The latter represents increasingly popular LLM-based metrics where an LLM is prompted to list errors in a translation with associated category and severity. This list is used to derive a weighted score ranging from 25 to 0, lower being better. 5

5 Current approaches 

Previous work mentioned in § 2 uses four basic types of resources to augment the prompt: lexi-cons with word-level alignment, in-context task

exemplars , morphological information or glosses per word including part-of-speech, and grammar books with the syntactic profile of the language. We assess the following approaches for both MT directions ( →eng and eng →), including two base-lines ( only-input , L-str ), and five methods of constructing the prompt for the LLM that use incre-mentally added materials. The materials are appro-priately formatted by direction, e.g. word meanings provided as mapping from input language to the output language. • topline : We compute the topline perfor-mance for the (unciphered) language, with a basic task prompt and no extra information. • only-input : Zero-resource LLM baseline, consisting of simply the task prompt with ci-phered language input. This produces unre-lated text showing trivial performance, e.g. from copying the input, punctuation, or named entities ( < 5 Bleu for all languages), and serves as a sanity check random baseline. • L-str (non-LLM): Word-for-word translation using a lexicon, constructed by string concate-nation of top lexical equivalents for every word in the input. We use fuzzy lexicon search for the input word with normalized edit distance as per previous work (Zhang et al., 2024b). • L: Word meanings from a lexicon provided in the LLM context for every word in the input. We use the top k = 2 source matches per word with fuzzy matching, listing up to two equivalents per source match in the prompt if the lexicon lists multiple. • LE : (Additionally) E = 3 input-output exem-plar pairs, selected by BM25 similarity to the input from a held-out exemplar pool, in line with previous work (Zhang et al., 2024a). • LELem : (Additionally) Lexicon matches for the lemmas of input words, obtained with Stanza lemmatizers (Qi et al., 2020). This mimics a scenario where we can build a ba-sic morphological lemmatizer but not more complex tools for a new language. • LELemM : (Additionally) Richer morphologi-cal glosses including part-of-speech tags and word features from Stanza. This typically in-cludes plurality, person, case, tense-aspect-mood, and other such information. • LELemMS : (Additionally) High-level syntactic profile , including 8 primary word order fea-tures and comments about its morphosyntactic typology based on WALS features (Dryer and Haspelmath, 2013). While previous works have used grammar books for this purpose, Aycock et al. (2024) shows that gains from this are largely due to bitext examples or lexi-cal leakage in the grammar books. We instead use a manually constructed grammatical de-scription as a controlled manner of providing syntactic information to the model. We use ChiKhaPo lexicons from Chang and Bafna (2025), consisting of Gatitos (Jones et al., 2023) and PanLex (Kamholz et al., 2014). 6 See more details about all strategies and prompts used in the paper in Appendix C. 

6 Assessment of current strategies 

In this section, we discuss the MT performance of the current approaches described above, shown for both directions and three metrics in Figure 2. 7 In general, we see that →eng performance improves over baselines with incrementally added materials, and LELemMS performs the best of current strate-gies. This is consistent with previous literature. However, for both directions and all metrics, we see that LELemMS performance for each language is considerably below topline performance, which indicates a wide scope for improvement. 

Comprehension vs. generation We see that while current approaches are helpful for →eng ,they do not help much with eng → performance, which does not show much improvement from the non-LLM word-for-word baseline L-str to 

LELemMS as per semantics-based metrics. This is not surprising for the following reasons. 7.1 Impact of better lexicon coverage 

RQ: What is impact of near-perfect lexicon cov-erage, including coverage over all morphological variants of nearly all input words? 

We use Google Translate over individual words as a proxy for a lexicon with near-perfect coverage of input words in running text ( Lcov ELemMS ). This means that each input word is covered by this hypo-thetical lexicon, including morphological variants and inflections, and that the obtained translation is often aligned morphologically to some extent (e.g. plurals would map to plurals). 11 However, note that problems like word-sense and phrase-level issues shown in Table 1 still remain. We test this approach for all languages and both directions. We see that performance generally im-proves, especially for →eng , e.g. jumping by more than 10 xCOMET points for deu . This highlights the importance of lexicon coverage as the main support-ing material aiding the model. For generation, the lack of morphosyntactic capabilities of the model

still results in low-quality or nonsensical outputs. Note that in practice, a lexicon such as this, cov-ering all word forms in running text may involve building assistant technologies such as morphologi-cal parsers and morpheme-to-meaning dictionaries, depending on the morphological profile of the lan-guage. Further, performance even for →eng still lags considerably behind topline , given that is-sues such as idiomatic language translation and word sense still remain. Our experiments indicate that a hypothetical lexicon with near-perfect cov-erage, outputs of the current paradigm would be improved for →eng , with the important caveat that other language learning challenges still need to be addressed for both directions.

8 Beyond MT: Other tasks 

We evaluate current strategies on the MMLU-ProX 

(Xuan et al., 2025), XNLI (Conneau et al., 2018), 

XStoryCloze (Lin et al., 2022) tasks, containing multiple-choice questions for reasoning, entailment, and natural story continuation respectively. See accuracy results for MMLU-ProX in Figure 4. We find that providing exemplars (LE ) is useful, suggesting that task label alignment in the unseen language may play an important role. We see that providing further information such as word features and syntax overview (as in LELemMS )does not benefit performance, indicating that model may fail to leverage this information in a task setup rather than for translation. In general, performance lags far behind topline performance. 

Cascade We also test a cascaded approach, that is often more performant for low-resource lan-guages (Liu et al., 2025). That is, we first trans-late the ciphered language input into English using 

LELemMS , and then performing task inference in English ( LELemMS-casc ). Note that the informa-tion available to the model remains the same as in 

LELemMS . We find that LELemMS-casc performs the best for our tasks. This leading to several task-dependent questions regarding the role and trade-offs of using translation cascades as part of LLM pipelines for extremely low-resource languages. 

These initial findings motivate a task-oriented exploration of ICLL beyond the current focus on machine translation. 

9 Conclusion 

Our work addresses the problem of resource scarcity that hinders the systematic and low-cost exploration of in-context language learning for un-seen languages. We present Rashid , a framework which applies reversible character-level ciphers to high-resource languages to construct artificial truly unseen languages, unlocking access to the range of NLP tools and resources available for underly-ing high-resource languages. We demonstrate how this framework can be used for state-of-the-art eval-uation of current approaches, exploration of new otherwise expensive strategies, and task-oriented investigation and insights. We aim to provide a grounded assessment of current techniques in in-context language learning, as well as enable further progress despite the many challenges of the field. 

Limitations 

Unexplored directions Our framework can be used to explore several research questions in lan-guage learning that we do not investigate in this work. It remains to explore the effectiveness of parametric methods (continued pre-training, task fine-tuning) at different data scales for adaptation to unseen languages, as well alternative inference techniques (chain-of-thought, various prompting styles). It remains to explore the effect of factors such as linguistic typology, relatedness to other high-resource languages, and script on language learning approaches, and to draw targeted conclu-sions regarding these, which we do not discuss in our work. Finally, there is naturally a vast range of different approaches to explore with regard to what type of materials can be leveraged for language learning to address the various challenges that we discuss in our work, and how they are presented to be used by LLMs. The goal of this work is to introduce our framework and demonstrate its flexi-bility in enabling cheap experimentation in all these directions. We leave it to future work interested in specific aspects to explore them in detail. 

Ciphering While we claim that our ciphering technique maintains the linguistic properties of the underlying language, this has important caveats. Firstly, real unseen languages may have words in common or dialectal relationships with related lan-guages, which our cipher does not currently em-ulate. This is intentional, allowing us to isolate “from-scratch” language learning from the effects of cross-lingual transfer, or of traces of language data in pretraining data. Our framework allows for controlled studies of these confounding effects, e.g. via partial ciphering for controlled token over-lap, or other design choices modeling relatedness. We leave this to future work. Secondly, there may be particular ortho-phonological characteristics we may lose by random shuffling of the character inven-tory, such as vowel harmony, and the effect of tone. We leave it to future work to explore ciphers that can maintain these properties of interest for targeted studies of these phenomena. Finally, our current ciphering method would require adaptation to work for logographic scripts, such as Mandarin, where single graphemes are associated with meaning. We note that our framework only requires that a cipher be perfectly reversible, or bijective. There are various ways to construct such ciphers other than monoalphabetic substitution ciphers as em-ployed by us. For example, a cipher may have different and arbitrary input and output character inventories (i.e. mapping the Latin script to De-vanagari instead of itself). Future studies modeling various scenarios may therefore design and employ arbitrary suitable ciphers as per research questions of interest as long as they satisfy this constraint. 

Modeling language-specific insights for true un-seen languages Our framework assumes that we can gain insights on true unseen languages by us-ing artificial unseen languages, constructed using high-resource languages. This only holds if the tar-get unseen language has similar properties to the considered HRLs, e.g. if they belong to the same language family, or share typological characteristics in terms of morphosyntax. However, if the target unseen has unusual properties that are not repre-sented in the world’s HRLs, our framework will not be capable of emulating the effect of those prop-erties on language learning. More generally, our framework is not capable of providing language-specific insights for particular unseen languages and their niche typology and circumstances. 

Ethics Statement 

Our framework uses ciphered high-resource lan-guages as proxies for extremely low-resource or unseen languages, leveraging resources in the for-mer that are unavailable for the latter. This risks the erasure of language-specific phenomena in such studies for languages that are already marginalized and under-represented if insights from this frame-work are not validated for real targeted languages. We emphasize in § 1 as well in this statement that we intend our framework to supplement rather than replace targeted studies for truly low-resource lan-guages, in cases where experimentation on the latter is difficult or expensive due to resource scarcity. We do not work with any truly low-resource languages or language communities in this work, and acknowl-edge that the various language communities may have different goals than those of mainstream NLP (Bird, 2020).

We describe our ciphering method here, and talk about its undecipherability given the context of ex-perimental setup. 

A.1 Building cipher maps 

Our studied languages include 7 languages using the Latin script, 2 languages using Devanagari script, and Telugu, which uses the Telugu script. In prac-tice, it is common for extremely low-resource lan-guages to develop a Latin-alphabet-based orthogra-phy if none exists (Jones and Mooney, 2017). We also include the Devanagari script, which is used by more than a hundred high- and low-resource lan-guages and dialects (Eberhard et al., 2025), and Tel-ugu script, which can be considered a low-resource script. 

Script-specific design Seven out of ten of our languages use the Latin alphabet, although often with extra characters depending on the language. For each language, we construct disjoint consonant and vowel inventories. Each inventory is shuffled separately, so that a character maintains its class after the map. This is to avoid (for example) unreal-istically long consonant sequences in the ciphered language. The remaining three languages use the Devana-gari and Telugu scripts. These scripts uses alternate graphemes (diacritics) for a vowel, with determinis-tic occurrence depending on the previous character (if any) in a word. We therefore use three classes: consonants, vowels, and vowel diacritics, and shuf-fle them separately. This allows us to maintain the orthographic conventions of the scripts. 

General design choices We sample the cipher map once, and apply the same map over all input materials (i.e. input, lexicon entries, exemplars, etc.). This maintains any underlying correspon-dence between them. Note that in our experiments, we choose to main-tain the script of the source language as a design choice, in order to i) maintain a connection of the ci-phered language with its language family ii) process various scripts. This is not necessary: it is possible to construct a bijective random map between equal-sized subsets of any two character inventories. This can be used to study script effects in a controlled manner in the future.

A.2 Discussing indecipherability of ciphers by LLMs 

Several past work has discussed that substitution ci-phers such as ours have an exponential keyspace and are only decipherable using statistical frequency analysis given considerable amounts of ciphertext data (Shannon, 1949; Knight et al., 2006; Nuhn and Ney, 2013). Work specifically in the context of LLM ability to work with ciphers shows that even when cipher rules are provided to LLMs for much simpler types of shift-based ciphers, they are not consistently able to engage in reasoning in cipher-text (Guo et al., 2025). We provide an explicit decipher-then-translate test, and find that ciphers built in the above manner are secure in the context of our prompting setup. 

A.2.1 Decipher-then-translate test Experiment setup We maintain our prompting setup with the relevant ciphertext material, i.e. the exemplars, input text, and word meanings. We ex-plicitly ask GPT-5.1 to first decipher the input text and then translate it to English. We also provide it with the necessary steps in order to provide maxi-mum aid. That is, we ask it to first guess the under-lying language, then the method of ciphering, then decipher the input text, and finally, translate it to English. We do this over a sample of 150 inputs, taken uniformly over 5 languages covering families and scripts (Hindi, French, Turkish, Telugu, Polish). We tried a few prompting settings, such wrapping the

C Details of all language learning strategies 

C.1 Additional approaches 

We tried three other approaches with negative re-sults, described here. 

Providing word sense We explore annotating tar-get language words with word senses during X →En-glish Machine Translation, to provide more con-text beyond lexical information. In practice, we retrieve multilingual WordNet definitions for each word in the input using the wn package (Goodman and Bond, 2021). However, the definition coverage of words proved to be too sparse for this approach to be effective. 

Syntax guidance via related language example 

In addition to CL cov ELemMS , which uses a related high-resource language as a pivot, we also try a minimalistic approach, i.e. simply providing the re-lated language translation as part of the context and asking the LLM to be guided by it for word order. This is potentially cheaper than CL cov ELemMS since it does not require lexicons between the RHRL and the target language (alternatively, in the case that the latter are easier to obtain than lexicons with En-glish, CL cov ELemMS might be easier.) This approach is tested against Lcov ELemMS . This approach offers markedly less reduced benefits than CL cov ELemMS .The LLM often fails to follow related language syn-tax closely and defaults to producing an English-influenced word ordering. 

Cheap reinflection We explore a cheap strategy for targeting verbal inflection for generation by addi-tionally providing inflection paradigms to the model (LELemMSI ). This information is much simpler to obtain than building morphological reinflection tools for a new language; and it is therefore worth testing how far it takes us. We test this for Hindi, German, Turkish, French and Spanish. See the in-flection information provided in Table 4. Manual comparison of outputs with LELemMS shows sev-eral cases of better verb inflection with approach, although inconsistently and with several remaining problems, further discussed with examples in Ap-pendix H. However, this effect is not strong enough for quantitative metrics to show gains in overall quality, and outputs continue to show several re-maining problems in this regard due to inconsistent application of rules, as well as incomplete specifi-cation of potentially complicated verbal paradigms. We highlight the significant scope for improvement in this regard. 

C.2 Materials 

We describe the materials used for our approaches in more detail here. 

Lexicons We use ChiKhaPo lexicons 15 as de-scribed in § 5. For the Google Translate “lexicon” used in approach Lcov ELemMS and CL cov ELemMS ,we pass single words (without any context) into Google Translate API 16 and use the returned string as its lexicon equivalent. This simulates a lexicon with near-perfect coverage. 

Exemplars Since WMT24++ does not provide splits, we create our own split of of 300 samples, disjoint from the test split, fixed across languages and across approaches, as a pool for selecting exem-plars at inference time. We note that this is the best case scenario, where the exemplar pool comes from the same distribution as the test set. In practice, such a resource may be difficult to construct. 

Named-entity recognition, lemmatizer and word features We use Stanza (Qi et al., 2020) on the languages inputs for NER, lemmatization, and mor-phological tags per word. Note that this is naturally run before ciphering. GEMBA-MQM is an LLM-based evaluation metric and is not guaranteed to be reliable, especially for all our languages on the generation side. While it is out-of-scope for us to provide meta-evaluation at scale for GEMBA-MQM for our languages, we perform a small analysis to sanity check the general signal provided by this metric. 

For →eng Firstly, we note that for →eng , the model is simply judging English outputs, given source texts in various languages. The model is quite capable of understanding source texts in these mid-to-high resource languages, and is consistent in its severity judgments for English outputs. 

For eng → For eng →, where it has to judge out-puts in various languages, GEMBA-MQM scores for all languages for the ciphered settings remain close to the worst possible score. This is very reasonable given their quality, as we judge in our manual anal-ysis. We look to the unciphered ( topline ) setting to get a sense of the reliability of GEMBA-MQM for these languages. We go through GEMBA-MQM judg-ments for 30 outputs for both comprehension and generation for Marathi and French, which are one of the lowest and highest-resource languages in our set respectively. In general, we find that the LLM is sometimes excessively strict, counting small devia-tions from style or wording as minor errors. In this regard, we find that scores for Marathi and Hindi are somewhat lower than those for French and Spanish, simply because they have less literal translations and accumulate more minor “errors” from GEMBA-MQM , despite being of similar quality. However, the model does so consistently for a given language, and relative scores are therefore reasonably reliable. Thus, we would caution against comparing scores across languages. However, we concur in the unusability judgments (i.e. critical errors, re-sulting in a score of 25) provided by GEMBA-MQM 

for most generation outputs, and believe that score differences from GEMBA-MQM for samples in our ap-proaches generally correspond to concrete and no-ticeable quality differences between outputs. We acknowledge that these are merely observa-tions based on a very small sample set, and heavier reliance on LLM-based evaluation metrics for par-ticular languages require at-scale meta-evaluations for those languages. Goal The goal of the analysis is to characterize the qualitative nature of the improvements made by each approach (if any), including all current and new strategies tried in the paper, and to present examples. 

Description of analysis Each of our approaches adds a resource or strategy incrementally to a previous approach. We therefore compare out-puts pairwise between two approaches differing only in the incremental addition in order to un-derstand the benefits of the addition. For ex-ample, we compare LELemM against LELem , and 

LELemMS against LELemM . We compare LELemMSI 

and Lcov ELemMS against LELemMS , since they add inflection paradigm descriptions and a lexicon change to LELemMS , and we compare Lcov ELemMS 

to CL cov ELemMS . We do so over four languages: Hindi, Marathi, French, and Turkish. The analy-sis was performed by the authors, who are native speakers of or fluent in the studied languages. 

Qualitative findings We obtain a qualitative sense of the effects of each approach, and provide our findings below. We provides examples accom-panying each of these points in Table 6. These examples are cherry-picked to illustrate the ben-efits of each approach, and we refer the reader to quantitative metrics instead to understand the over-all extent of translation quality improvement. • Baselines: Table 6 also contains representa-tive (non-cherry-picked) examples of outputs from topline and baseline approaches, show-ing that topline is fluent and accurate, that 

only-input produces random unrelated text, and word-for-word translation with L-str is nonsensical and invalid. • LE : As expected, lemmatizers help to increase coverage of the input words, when the lexicon may not cover an inflection but does have the lemma. • LELem : Exemplars, chosen with BM25 string similarity, generally help with particular over-lap strings in the input. They may contain a particular expression, or the usage of some function words correctly, that the model copies over and therefore improves over literal trans-lation. Occasionally, the exemplars also have more intangible effects, such as helping the model not deviate from the subject, if they share the same subject as the input. In gen-eral, in-domain exemplars help considerably with performance given overlap with the input in terms of topic and surface similarity. Note that this makes the exemplars quite an expen-sive resource to collect, i.e. bitext covering a diverse range of subjects and vocabulary. • LELemM : Added word features and POS tags help in targeted scenarios where the word meaning in the lexicon is ambiguous by part-of-speech. The model uses the tense or other information occasionally, but not consistently. • LELemMS : In the context of generation - the model uses the high-level syntactic informa-tion to reorder certain phrases, especially for languages with very different word from En-glish (like Hindi and Marathi). This is espe-cially true in simple phrases where, for exam-ple, there is only a very simple subject, ob-ject, and verb, or a noun and adjective. As sentences get longer, however, this informa-tion is no longer enough, and the model very quickly fails to apply the guidance even in sim-ple phrases within longer inputs. In general, word order remains very much mangled, even with some attempts to reorder as per the guid-ance. For comprehension, this information helps the model to correctly interpret events in certain cases by understanding, for example, subject and object ordering. • LELemMSI : The model attempts to inflect verbs as per the provided guidance. However, there remain several obstacles to perfect verb inflection. Firstly, given that the model often fails to correct interpret concepts and events, it often does not have the information (about sub-ject, object, gender, number, aspect, etc.) that it needs to perform correct verb agreement, even if it knows the correct inflection in every case. Secondly, the model displays inconsis-tent success in drawing from the guidance, and fails to do so as inputs gets longer. • Lcov ELemMS : Google Translate lexicons natu-rally help considerably and straightforwardly in providing coverage over missing words and morphological variants. • CL cov ELemMS : The related language cascade has perhaps the most success in mitigating many of the above problems. The related lan-guage in question often has similar idiomatic-ity and lexical usage patterns, that may differ from those of English. While English phrasing often results in nonsensical literal translations into Hindi, a fluent English-Marathi transla-tion transfers it into phrasing that is much likelier to result in much better Hindi output when literally translated into Hindi. This is also true for morphological paradigms: these often map much better between related lan-guages. Finally, this approach also naturally handles the word-order issue with much more success. Having said this, there are some im-portant caveats here. Firstly, in the case where the target ciphered language is already some-what close to English, the cascade may hurt by introducing noise and error propagation. Sec-ondly, literal translation even between related languages is very noisy and of poor quality, and heavily depends on the extent of their re-latedness. We observe that Hindi and Marathi are much more helped by this approach than French in all of the above aspects. In general, we find that the benefits of each ap-proach are by no means consistent and systematic over all inputs or even within a single input, and there is large scope of improvement beyond these.
