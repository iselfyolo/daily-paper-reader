Abstract 

The discovery of inorganic crystal structures with targeted properties is a significant challenge in materials science. Generative models, especially state-of-the-art diffusion models, offer the promise of modeling complex data distributions and proposing novel, realistic samples. However, current generative AI models still struggle to produce diverse, original, and reliable structures of experimentally achievable materials suitable for high-stakes applications. In this work, we propose a generative machine learning framework based on diffusion models with adaptive constraint guidance, which enables the incorporation of user-defined physical and chemical constraints during the generation process. This approach is designed to be practical and interpretable for human experts, allowing transparent decision-making and expert-driven exploration. To ensure the robustness and validity of the generated candidates, we introduce a multi-step validation pipeline that combines graph neural network estimators trained to achieve DFT-level accuracy and convex hull analysis for assessing thermodynamic stability. Our approach has been tested and validated on several classical examples of inorganic families of compounds, as case studies. As a consequence, these preliminary results demonstrate our framework’s ability to generate thermodynamically plausible crystal structures that satisfy targeted geometric constraints across diverse inorganic chemical systems. 

Keywords: Generative AI, Inorganic chemistry, crystal structure, diffusion models, con-straint guidance 21 Introduction 

Identifying and employing new functional materials has become critical to address pressing environmental (abundance, sustainability) and geopolitical challenges. 1 The discovery of new inorganic solids relies on both empirical and computational approaches. Empirical methods, combining chemical design rules with trial-and-error experiments, remain time- and resource-intensive. In contrast, computational methods—particularly first-principles approaches such as Density Functional Theory (DFT)—have enabled a deeper understanding of matter and, in some cases, prediction of atomic-scale ordering, albeit at high computational cost. 2

Over the past decade, data-driven science and machine learning (ML) have emerged as powerful paradigms for accelerating materials discovery, notably by guiding experiments. 3–6 

Generative models, including Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) 7,8 and more recently diffusion models, 9,10 have enabled the direct generation of novel structures rather than the screening of large candidate spaces. 11 In parallel, Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) incorporate physical laws into the learning process, ensuring consistency with fundamental principles. 12 Meanwhile, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Machine Learning Interatomic Potentials (MLIP) have become standard tools for efficient and accurate property prediction, leveraging the relational structure of crystalline materials without requiring expensive DFT calculations. 13 

Despite these promising opportunities, a critical gap has emerged: data alone cannot replace domain expertise and critical thinking in materials science. This became evident with large-scale models developed by major technology companies 14,15 announcing impressive claims, such as Google DeepMind’s GNoME predicting 2.2 million new crystal structures. 16 

Subsequent critical analyses revealed fundamental flaws: many predicted structures were merely combinatorial constructs of already known compounds, without proper experimental verification. 17 Subsequent analyses also raised concerns about the claims of 41 new inorganic compounds reported by the autonomous laboratory for the accelerated synthesis of novel materials (A-Lab), as several structures were found to be mischaracterized or already known 3in the literature. 18,19 These issues highlight that computational predictions, without chemical intuition and physical validation, can lead to physically implausible or trivial ”discoveries”. The purpose of this paper is to integrate classical diffusion models with critical human guidance grounded in chemistry, building on our prior work on diffusion-based models 11 

and on the state-of-the-art generative model MatterGen. 20 By introducing an additional module of chemical constraints (such as minimum interatomic distances or local chemical environments), we allow chemists, leveraging their physical intuition, to generate novel, functional, crystallographic structures that (i) satisfy a pre-defined set of constraints and (ii) are (meta)stable, meaning their energy lies below a pre-defined threshold above the convex hull. Rather than generating millions of unverified candidates, our approach emphasizes quality over quantity, ensuring that the generated structures respect fundamental chemical principles and domain-specific knowledge from the outset. We focus on constraints whose satisfiability can be estimated through differentiable functions, such as neural networks. Adding new constraints should then be straightforward and should not require additional fine-tuning. To this end, our approach relies on a foundation model: an unconstrained generative model for inorganic materials that has been pre-trained to generate stable and diverse crystal structures across the periodic table. Here, we employ MatterGen, 20 a robust diffusion-based model for inorganic materials. MatterGen has been pre-trained to generate stable structures by focusing on compounds within 0.10 eV/atom of the convex hull, ensuring diversity in crystal structures and supporting flexible property conditioning. While MatterGen currently handles unit cells with up to 20 atoms only, this limitation is outweighed in our setting by its maturity, optimization, and demonstrated performance, making it a strong backbone for our approach. At sampling time, we introduce a training-free guidance mechanism, 21 compatible with any off-the-shelf guidance function based on the Universal Guidance for Diffusion Mod-els. 22 This mechanism enforces user-defined physical and chemical constraints, as detailed 4in methodology Section 2. Building on this foundation, Section 3 of this paper presents our finetuning-free generative framework for materials, demonstrated through case studies of inorganic crystal structures generated under user-defined geometrical constraints such as interatomic distances and coordination numbers. These constraints are expressed and eval-uated through the analysis of their statistical distributions and energy profiles, leveraging GNN-MLIPs such as GRACE 23 as high-performing energy estimators.

The primary objective of generative models in materials science has traditionally been the generation of stable materials. However, in practice, researchers are often interested in specific materials that have to satisfy some well-defined properties and be experimentally reachable, even if they do not correspond to the most stable structures. For example, the synthesis of functional materials often involves strict constraints: the coordination number of metal centers can dictate optical properties, interatomic distances must be finely tuned to enable some catalytic mechanisms or provide mechanical properties, and the band gap must be adjusted to meet the requirements of optoelectronic applications. Incorporating mechanisms to control the generation process within such constrains is therefore essential to make these models more suitable to address actual properties. In the present work, we use guidance for this purpose. Guidance diffusion models aim to modify the reverse diffusion process so that a certain property C is satisfied. From a Bayesian point of view, the score function is modified to incorporate the condition C, yielding: 

∇zt log q(zt, C ) = ∇zt log q(zt) + ∇zt log q(C|zt).

The score is approximated by the score network ∇zt log q(zt), and an estimate of ∇zt log q(C|zt)has to be approximated. Different methods have been explored to compute this approxima-tion, and details about guidance for diffusion models are provided in SI C. 

2.2.1 Universal Guidance 

MatterGen proposed a classifier-free guidance (CFG) trained on set of selected stable com-pounds. The CFG allows guiding the backward diffusion process toward a desired result, such as a stable structure, without requiring an additional classifier layer (more details in the SI C.2). The main issue with the CFG method is that one needs to retrain the model 7for each specific new set of conditions. Otherwise the model is out-of-distribution, and can yield irrelevant results, especially if the conditions are far from those used for training. To overcome this issue, the Universal Guidance method 22 (the notations introduced in SI B) uses an off-the-shelf loss function ℓ. Although in the original report on this method, 22 only the Variance Preserving (VP) case was considered, here we need both VP and Variance Exploding (VE), so that we consider the general case. To integrate both VE and FP, we propose a different formulation of the approach: 

∇zt log q(C|zt) ≈ −∇ zt ℓ(C, f (zt)) .

Indeed, the negative log-likelihood is a measure of the model’s performance widely used in machine learning. We use here the cross-entropy loss ℓ that measures how strongly the condition C is violated by zt; f is an objective function that evaluates a specific property of zt

and compares it to the condition C. The estimation of the property is, however, challenging for noisy samples, especially if f is a black-box machine learning model trained on clean samples only. 14 3 Case studies of crystal structures generated under constraints 

Having introduced the general guidance procedure above, we now turn to its main applica-tion in this work, namely inorganic crystal structure generation. We show how physically motivated constraints can be incorporated into diffusion sampling in order to control the generation of crystallographic cells. Although the discovery of new materials is not the pri-mary objective here, we demonstrate that guidance can shift the statistical distributions of generated structures relative to non-guided sampling of the foundation model, while the gen-erated structures are still checked for chemical consistency in the postprocessing and analysis steps described below. We illustrate the methodology on several solid-state chemical systems taken as case stud-ies. For each case, structure generation is guided by specific geometric constraints related to the targeted structural features. A chemical system here simply refers to the set of crystalline materials formed from a given combination of chemical elements, and the corre-sponding guidance loss is defined according to the imposed constraints C.The effect of guidance is assessed by comparing the statistical distributions obtained with guided and non-guided generation on batches of 1000 generated crystal structures. The non-guided generation is obtained using MatterGen chemical system checkpoint, i.e. the fine-tuned model trained on chemical systems with a maximum of 20 atoms per unit cell and ener-gies less than 0.1 eV/atom above the convex hull. 27 Our diffusion-guidance implementation built on top of MatterGen is available at https://github.com/Inirius/mattergenbis ;additional details on the model, checkpoints, and code availability are provided in the SI D. For each case study, the guidance strengths k and g were chosen by testing a few candidate values so as to preserve reasonable structures while still producing a measurable effect on the targeted descriptors. This led us to retain relatively low values rather than enforcing the guidance constraints too strictly. 15 After sampling, the generated data are analyzed along two complementary directions. First, in order to assess the direct effect of guidance on the sampling procedure itself, de-scriptor distributions for the guided and non-guided generated structures are computed from the raw generated data, i.e. before any postprocessing. These distributions are represented by histograms or kernel-smoothed curves and compared with the corresponding reference distributions. The efficiency of guidance with respect to the targeted constraint is further quantified through a one-sided p-value from a two-proportion z-test comparing the fractions of generated structures that satisfy the constraint in the guided and non-guided samples. Small p-values provide evidence against the null hypothesis that guidance does not increase the constraint-satisfaction rate, suggesting that guidance may have a positive effect; values below 0 .05 are taken as statistically significant. Second, both the guided and non-guided structure sets are processed through the same postprocessing pipeline, described in detail in SI E. Among other steps, this pipeline includes symmetrization, deduplication, and the removal of structures that are chemically unreason-able from the outset. The latter screening is performed using interatomic-distance criteria and, more importantly, by estimating the height above the energy convex hull defined by a combined dataset from the Alexandria, 28 OQMD, 29 and Materials Project 30 databases. In the following, we refer to these known structures as the “references”. They provide a con-sistent comparison set for the generated distributions, but should not be interpreted as an exact reconstruction of the MatterGen training set, since the latter involves atomic-number and energy cutoffs. Energies are evaluated with the GRACE model, 23 using the “GRACE-2L-OMAT-large-ft-AM” checkpoint, 23 which proved to be of accuracy comparable to DFT for the systems considered here. We note that energy estimations are performed without crystal relaxation in order to directly assess the quality of generation and to preserve user-imposed geometric constraints, such as the target volume in our first case study. The postprocessed data are then used to assess the relationship between constraint satisfaction and physical plausibility, 16 measured here through the height above the convex hull, by means of Pareto fronts (see the first example below for definitions and details). Technical details of the proposed guidance functions for materials generation, including environment-dependent losses and equality constraints, are provided in SI D. There, we also show that multiple guidance objectives can be combined by treating C constraints as a multidimensional objective. More precisely, given losses ( ℓi)di=1 associated with properties computed by functions f = ( fi)di=1 , we derive the final scalar loss as detailed in SI D. The following case studies range from unary to quaternary systems, progressively illus-trating the scope of the proposed guidance strategy and how increasingly complex forms of structural control can be achieved. 

## 3.1 Case study 1: High-density boron 

In the first example we explore the dense high-pressure allotropes of boron, a system known for its structural complexity and diversity. 31 Our strategy consists in enforcing as single geometric constraint a low value of the per-atom volume, f (zt) = va = | det L|/N at , which is characteristic of the dense boron allotropes. The chosen target is C = va0 = 7 .0 ˚ A3 by atom, volume which corresponds to orthorombic γ-B, the most dense boron phase 32 known. Hence, the guidance loss function is ℓ(C, f (zt)) = ∥va − va0 ∥1.This allotrope has 28 atoms in the unit cell, which is relatively few compared to the most common boron phases ( cf. β-rhombohedral boron with 105 atoms), yet it is the most recent phase to be discovered. 31 The number of atoms per unit cell is close to but larger than MatterGen’s limit of 20 atoms, and it is not included in the training set by definition, which makes this system suitable to test our approach. In Fig. 2, we present the results of guided generation alongside the Pareto fronts analysis. Fig. 2.A, shows the distributions of the per-atom volume for the generated structures, together with the corresponding distributions for the 44 reference allotropes available in the databases. The curves show Gaussian-kernel-smoothed distributions, with smearing selected by Silverman’s rule of thumb. 33

The main result is the pronounced redistribution under guidance: about 60 % of the guided samples fall within ±0.25 ˚ A3 of the target value of 7.0 ˚ A3, compared to ∼ 20 % in the non-guided case. This difference is not only visually apparent in Fig. 2. These results provide strong quantitative evidence that guidance effectively concentrates sampling around the desired volume. To put these near-target samples in a materials-science context, we complement matching of structural features with a basic thermodynamic validity check by estimating the per-atom energy above the convex hull envelope formed by the reference structures. This value, ech ,compares a given crystal with the most stable competing mixture of reference entries of the same overall empirical formula. Non-positive ech indicates thermodynamic stability. For 18 elemental boron, ech is simply the per-atom energy relative to the lowest-energy known allotrope. To jointly consider target proximity and low ech , we rank structures using Pareto fronts, which are plots of energy as a function of the loss function. By definition, the first Pareto front contains solutions that are not dominated by any other solution in the population (i.e., no other solution is at least as good in all objectives and strictly better in at least one). For 

i > 1, the i-th Pareto front is the set of non-dominated solutions in the data after removing the solutions in fronts 1 , . . . , i − 1. The Pareto fronts for generated and reference data combined are shown in Fig. 2.B. The guided model successfully identified two very similar dense structures consisting of B 12 

clusters with interstitial B 3 units (Fig. 2.D), which closely resemble the γ-boron allotrope (Fig. 2.C). This outcome underscores the efficacy of our guidance mechanism in exploring crystal structures with densities near the target. While the identified structure is not an exact match to γ-boron, its proximity highlights the potential of our approach for refining and guiding materials prediction. 

## 3.2 Case study 2: Boron coordination in the Fe–Nd–B system 

The Fe–Nd–based compounds are widely studied for their properties as permanent magnets, particularly for data storage applications. To enhance the localization of d-electrons of the transition metal (Fe), one effective strategy is to introduce neutral elements such as boron. 34 

In fact, boron serves a structural role by increasing the Fe–Fe distance, which enhances the ferromagnetic moment and coercivity. The precise manner in which boron modulates Fe–Fe exchange interactions is critical to achieving superior magnetic hardness. Several stable com-pounds are known in this system, such as the boron-rich phases Nd(FeB) 4 and Nd 5(FeB 3)2,where B is coordinated by 3 to 6 Fe atoms depending on the site. Among the known iron-rich phases, and thus of particular interest for magnetism, is Nd 2Fe 14 B, where Fe atoms form continuous puckered hexagonal nets linked by short Fe—Fe bonds, separated by planes 19 containing both Nd and B atoms. 35,36 The compound adopts a tetragonal structure (space group P 42/mnm ), comprising 68 atoms per unit cell. Since this is too large to be reproduced by MatterGen generation, we address the boron coordination sphere as a proxy of the overall crystal structure: in Nd 2Fe 14 B, B atoms occupy the centers of trigonal prisms [BFe 6], with Fe–B bond distances of 2.09–2.14 ˚ A. These trigonal prisms are fundamental structural units that link the Fe layers perpendicular to the planes containing Nd, B, and some additional Fe atoms. In this case study, our aim is to generate structures that maintain these [BFe 6]building blocks, by defining the geometric constraint for the generation. This approach fa-vors the essential structural motifs that contribute to the exceptional magnetic hardness and energy density of the parent Nd 2Fe 14 B compound, while offering the opportunity to inves-tigate how systematic variations in local coordination geometry, especially when combined with Nb substitution, affect intrinsic magnetic properties and the mechanisms underlying ferromagnetic coupling.

## 3.3 Case study 3: the chemical environment in Li–Co–O 

The ternary Li–Co–O system is well-known for its prototypical compound, the lithium cobalt oxide LiCoO 2, a cornerstone cathode material in lithium-ion batteries. 38 Its layered structure enables reversible lithium intercalation, a critical feature for energy storage applications. LiCoO 2 exists in two primary crystalline forms: a high-temperature (HT) hexagonal phase and a low-temperature (LT) cubic spinel-related phase, with the HT-LiCoO 2 rhombohedral structure being the preferred choice for battery applications. 39 For this latter compound, Li +

cations are intercalated between sheets of edge-sharing CoO 6 octahedra, while cobalt in its +3 oxidation state achieves the charge neutrality with the 2 O 2−. The d6 low-spin electron configuration of Co 3+ favors octahedral coordination, splitting d-orbitals to stabilize the 21 structure through crystal field effects. Our aim in this case study is to deliberately induce metastable structures which are not dominant in the training database. By doing so, we seek to demonstrate that alternative coordination environments, such as reduced coordination numbers of 4, can be enforced, enabling in silico design of novel crystalline structures.

The Cu–Si–P chemical system is of particular interest due to its ternary compounds, which exhibit semiconductor properties and may serve as high-capacity anode materials in lithium-ion and redox flow batteries, thanks to low volume expansion compared to silicon-based anodes. 41 Compounds like CuSi 2P3 and CuSi 3P4 adopt distorted zinc-blende structures, 42 

making them relevant for studies in optoelectronics and energy band gap engineering. While experimental syntheses of CuSi 2P3 report a disordered arrangement of Cu and Si cations with largely covalent bonding in a tetrahedrally coordinated phosphorus environment, 42,43 DFT calculations from the Materials Project suggest a nearly stable ordered phase with CuP 4 and SiP 4 tetrahedra. 44 A similar chemical environment is observed in Cu 4SiP 8, where Cu atoms form Cu–Cu pairs, which are octahedrally coordinated to six P. 45 When combined with alkaline earth elements, other coordination environments appear, like e.g. in BaCuSi 2P3,where copper is bonded to 3 phosphorus and 2 silicon atoms. The Cu atoms are in a near trigonal planar environment with the 3 P atoms. 46 

Here we used the Cu–Si–P system as a controlled testbed to quantify how strongly our guidance mechanism can reshape local-environment statistics, independently of whether the targeted motif is thermodynamically plausible, by imposing three distinct Cu–P coordina-tion targets (3, 4, and 6 neighbors). In the reference data, fourfold Cu–P coordination is prevalent, whereas sixfold coordination is essentially absent; targeting the latter therefore provides a stringent stress test of the steering capability. As shown in Fig. 5, guidance produces clear, systematic shifts in the coordination-number peaks relative to non-guided sampling ( p-values < 10 −5 ), increasing the population of structures that satisfy the re-quested environment even in the extreme sixfold case. While such a coordination may be chemically “nonsensical” in this composition space, demonstrating that the generator re-sponds predictably to a local structural constraint is valuable: it establishes guidance as a reliable statistical control knob that can be used to generate counterfactual, deliberately imbalanced and out-of-the-box structure sets. These controlled distributions can, in turn, 23 support downstream machine-learning workflows, for example, augmenting training data for environment-conditioned generators, constructing hard negative examples for coordina-tion classifiers, or improving the robustness and calibration of surrogate property models and machine-learning interatomic potentials by exposing them to rare (or deliberately perturbed) local motifs.

## 3.5 Case study 5: multiple constraints in quaternary system: Cu– Si–P–Ca 

Following our previous case study on the ternary Cu–Si–P system, we extend the inves-tigation to the quaternary Cu–Si–P–Ca chemical space, where no quaternary compounds have been reported yet. We chose Ca to move beyond the reported ternary family into a less charted quaternary setting while remaining within the broader compositional family of alkaline-earth-containing Cu–Si–P phosphides, such as layered BaCuSi 2Pn 3 (P n = P , As). 46 

This provides a stringent setting in which to test whether motif-directed generation remains effective once the search is lifted into a broader compositional space. Within this quaternary system, we target a Cu–Cu dimer stabilized in a distorted octahe-dral phosphorus environment, as found in CuP 2 and Cu 4SiP 8. The interest of this motif lies 24 in the fact that it couples a direct metal–metal contact to a specific phosphorus coordination scaffold, so that successful generation requires recovering not only Cu–Cu pairing but also the local anion environment that supports it. In the previous examples, guidance was aimed directly at isolated coordination envi-ronments. Here, by contrast, the objective is a composite local motif rather than a single coordination feature. We show that such a motif can nevertheless be targeted using multi-objective guidance built from simple coordination-number constraints. Specifically, we im-pose CN(Cu −Cu) = 1 and CN(Cu −P) = 4 for each Cu atom. In this representation, the target Cu 2P6 motif is decomposed into two edge-sharing CuP 4 tetrahedra (Fig. 6A). The point is therefore not that the constraints themselves are more elaborate, but that their combination is sufficient to specify a structurally nontrivial local arrangement. The results of sampling are illustrated in Fig. 6B,C, where we compare the 2D kernel density estimates obtained without and with guidance. In the unguided case (Fig. 6B), the distribution is dominated by high-density regions corresponding to structures without direct Cu–Cu bonding, while the target appears only as a weak saddle-like feature. With multi-objective guidance (Fig. 6C), the dominant peaks are largely retained, but a pronounced ad-ditional local maximum emerges at the target, showing that the guidance selectively enriches the desired motif rather than simply redistributing the entire ensemble. This is supported by a p-value of 10 −6.Importantly, the appearance of a local maximum at the target in Fig. 6C demonstrates only that the combined coordination constraints are satisfied more frequently under guidance; it does not, by itself, establish recovery of the intended Cu 2P6 motif, since the same values of CN(Cu −Cu) and CN(Cu −P) may in principle also arise from alternative local geometries. For this reason, structural inspection is essential. The Pareto analysis in Fig. 7A identifies a set of low-loss, low-energy candidates, and the representative zero-loss structure shown in Fig. 7B, lying 0.175 eV/atom above the convex hull, confirms that the guided search can recover not only the formal coordination counts but also the intended arrangement of a Cu– 25 Figure 6: A. Figure 7: A. First three Pareto fronts in energy above convex hull vs loss coordinates, B Representative zero-loss structure found from guided generation. 26 4 Conclusions 

In this work, we have demonstrated that a foundational diffusion model for the generation of crystal structures, already trained on structures with energies on or close to the convex hull, can be equipped with an adaptive guidance mechanism that enforces explicit physico-chemical constraints during generation, without any re-training. By relying on differentiable cost functions, in particular those based on local environments, we show on several inorganic case studies (from unary to quaternary systems) that sampling can be concentrated around targeted atomic volumes, coordination numbers and nature of the near neighbors, while preserving thermodynamic plausibility as assessed by GNN/MLIP-based estimators. In the present work, our goal was to prove that chemical and geometrical constraints can be imposed in diffusion algorithms using Universal Guidance, which we have implemented on top of the MatterGen code (currently limited to unit cells with up to 20 atoms), in order to force the statistical distributions toward structural regimes that differ from those present in the reference datasets. This framework paves the way for a more directed exploration of materials space, where the statistical distributions of crystallographic cells can be oriented toward regions featuring local environments and densities compatible with desired physico-chemical properties, thereby providing a flexible tool for the assisted design of functional materials.

## B.3 Predictor-Corrector Sampling 

The predictor-corrector sampler was introduced in 26 to correct errors in the evolution of the discretized reverse-time SDE, so it is an amelioration of the simple denoising process that only uses the reverse diffusion formula 4. Having described more precisely each part of the diffusion models for material generation, we will focus more precisely on the one we will use: MatterGen. MatterGen exploits an SE(3)-equivariant graph neural network based on the GemNet architecture 49 to predict scores for the lattice, atom positions, and atom types during the denoising process. The model adapts GemNet-dT, the non-conservative force prediction vari-ant of GemNet, which efficiently captures directional, two-body, and three-body interactions to ensure SO(3)-equivariance. MatterGen employs four message-passing layers, a 7˚ A cutoff radius for neighbor list construction, and sets hidden node and edge feature dimensions to 512. Ideally, this cutoff radius should be dependent on the atom type, however we chose to keep it fixed so that the results can be compared. It is trained to predict Cartesian coordi-nate scores that are equivariant to rotation and permutation, invariant to translation, and subsequently transforms these scores into fractional coordinates. 

# C Guidance for diffusion models 

In this section, we describe the main ways to guide a diffusion process. 

## C.1 Classifier guidance 

This guidance introduced in 50 is based on a classifier fϕ that is trained to classify the noisy sample zt with respect to the property C. The term ”classifier” is employed here because usually the property C is a class label for zt. In this context: 

∇zt log q(c|zt) ≈ ∇ zt log fϕ(C, z t, t )The weakness of this method is that it needs an accurate and efficient classifier that ob-tains decent accuracy for noisy samples at each timestep. It is therefore generally impossible 38 to use off-the-shelf classifier network, as those are trained on existing stable materials, so for a noisy sample the classification error increases. With a bad classification, the guidance becomes erratic. 

## C.2 Classifier-free guidance 

To address the issue of designing a classifier, in 51 the authors came up with a way to avoid its use. Using the Bayes rules: 

∇zt log q(c|zt) = ∇zt log q(zt|c) − ∇ zt log q(zt)The model is therefore trained as a conditional score model with the condition C as an input so that: sθ(zt, t, c ) = ∇zt log q(zt|c). Therefore, with an empty condition, we retrieve the unconditional score model : sθ(zt, t, ∅) = ∇zt log q(zt). However, if this approximation is used directly, the term ∇zt log q(zt) vanishes and only the conditional model remains. Therefore, the guidance scale γ is used to control a trade-off between the conditional and unconditional models: 

∇zt log q(zt, c ) = sθ(zt, t, ∅) + γ(sθ(zt, t, c ) − sθ(zt, t, ∅)) = (1 − γ)sθ(zt, t, ∅) + γs θ(zt, t, c )

γ = 0 corresponds to the unconditional model, and γ = 1 to the conditional model. The innovation of the classifier-free guidance is to use γ > 1, which leads to significantly better alignment with the conditioning signal, but at the cost of a lack of diversity. 

## C.3 Training-Free Guidance 

The most recent improvement in guidance methods for diffusion models that do not require retraining is the unified Training-Free Guidance (TFG). 21 This work proposes a unified 39 framework that brings together various training-free guidance techniques used in diffusion models. Overall the method is almost exactly the same as the Universal Guidance, the only difference lies in the guidance function. In order to avoid local issues with its gradient, this method replaces f with a time-dependent smooth function ˜ft instead of: ˜ft(x) = Eδ∼N (0 ,I )[f (x + γσ tδ)] Called Implicit Dynamic , the expectation is approximated using a Monte Carlo method. However, reducing the number of Monte Carlo samples does not hurt performance much, suggesting that it is not the precise estimation that matters here, but the noise-driven dy-namics. We choose not to use this additional method, since, as shown in the analysis of the self-recurrence loop, the score is already averaged with a random dynamics in this self-recurrence loop by averaging the score over all the zit .

# D Details of the developed guidance functions for solid generation 

We have developed some guidance functions for solid generation. Simple guidance functions like a target cell volume, choice of the chemical environment (nature and number of first neighbors environment) have been implemented. The results of the tests on selected case-studies are presented in the main text. The guidance functions should be differentiable to be compatible with our algorithm. Because we used pytorch , the gradient is automatically calculated with torch.autograd as long as the function is rightly implemented. The parameters on which the derivative is computed should have requires grad = True , and every operations should be done so that the grad fn is kept all along. This last requirement is easy to satisfy when using machine learning predictor, only minor adaptations are needed. However for 40 already existing functions written with numpy , the code must be entirely rewritten using 

pytorch in order to be used efficiently as a guidance function. To support material generation within our framework, we have developed several ded-icated guidance functions. These functions address specific objectives, such as controlling local atomic environments, ensuring material stability, and handling multi-objective opti-mization. Indeed, the absolute continuity of the desired distribution with respect to the computed one is needed for the KL divergence. So it could not be used. Therefore, divergences like Wasserstein metric or Jensen-Shannon divergence should rather be used. 

## D.2 Energy above hull 

The convex hull is the lower envelope of formation energies over composition. For guidance, we directly use the energy above the convex hull (i.e., the deviation from the hull) as the guidance function: 

ℓ(C, f (ˆ z0|t)) = Ehull (ˆ z0|t)Indeed, the lower the energy, the more stable the solid is. The exact energy being really costly to compute, we use an off-the-shelf estimator from Microsoft: MatterSim. 52 Some adaptations were needed due to incompatibilities with the dependencies and also because it was designed for numpy . The energy on the convex hull is subtracted according to the composition of ˆ z0|t by using the known databases. The databases used were the classical DFT database used by the community: Alexandria, 28 Materials Project 30 and OQMD. 29

# E Postprocess pipeline 

To assess the quality of generated structures we developed a postprocess workflow as shown in the Fig.9. After parsing the data into the in-house data format (1), we perform a symmetrization procedure (2) using the spglib package. 53 This step is necessary because the generated struc-tures frequently appear with C1 symmetry, although they are in fact only slightly distorted variants of structures with higher symmetry. We then screen the structures for unphysically short interatomic distances (3). Subsequently, duplicate entries are removed by means of 44 Figure 9: Postprocessing workflow for generated samples. 45 a fingerprint-based similarity analysis (4), where the fingerprint is constructed from radial distribution functions. 54 

To place the results in the context of existing knowledge, the generated structures are compared against reference databases. In the present study, we use the Alexandria, 28 

OQMD, 29 and Materials Project 30 databases. The corresponding procedure (5) consists of sequentially querying these three databases and retaining only those structures that have not been encountered previously. This filtering is performed using the same similarity-analysis approach as in step (4). Prior to deduplication, we optionally exclude entries lying more than 0.5 eV/atom above the convex hull, based on the energies reported in the databases themselves. This is motivated by our primary interest in assessing how newly generated low-energy candidate structures are positioned relative to the reference convex hull. Finally, we perform joint energy estimation and calculate the energies above the reference-based convex-hull (7).
