Victor De Lima and Grace Hui Yang 

InfoSense Lab, Georgetown University

Abstract 

Most conversational agents (CAs) are designed to satisfy user needs through user-driven inter-actions. However, many real-world settings, such as academic interviewing, judicial pro-ceedings, and journalistic investigations, in-volve broader institutional decision-making processes and require agents that can elicit in-formation from users. In this paper, we in-troduce Information Elicitation Agents (IEAs) in which the agent’s goal is to elicit informa-tion from users to support the agent’s insti-tutional or task-oriented objectives. To en-able systematic research on this setting, we present YIELD , a 26M-token dataset of 2,281 ethically sourced, human-to-human dialogues. Moreover, we formalize information elicita-tion as a finite-horizon POMDP and propose novel metrics tailored to IEAs. Pilot exper-iments on multiple foundation LLMs show that training on YIELD improves their align-ment with real elicitation behavior and find-ings are corroborated by human evaluation. We release YIELD under CC BY 4.0. The dataset, project code, evaluation tools, and fine-tuned model adapters are available at: https: //github.com/infosenselab/yield .

Most conversational agents (CAs) are designed to satisfy user needs through user-driven interac-tions. In this paper, we propose to study and support Information Elicitation Agents (IEAs), a new class of conversational agents de-signed to meet these goals. The goal of this paper is to support the study and creation of agents that can behave like human elicitors. How-ever, little research has been done for this purpose. IEAs differ fundamentally from other conversa-tional agents in both whose goals they serve and how success is defined. Conversational agents are generally designed to help users accomplish user-initiated tasks, with the user controlling the agenda and direction of the interaction. IEAs, by contrast, act on behalf of an external institutional objective and use conversation as a means to extract informa-tion from the respondent. Crucially, at any point in the dialogue there is not a single optimal question or expected answer; instead, there are many possi-ble directions, each potentially yielding valuable information. The task of creating IEAs is challenging for several reasons. First, the agent must act in an extremely high-dimensional action space, where every utterance can shift the trajectory of the dia-logue. Second, defining what makes information “informative” is inherently nuanced and context-dependent, bringing subjectivity into both training and evaluation. These difficulties are further ampli-fied in long dialogues, where agents must maintain topic coherence over many turns while responding adaptively to unpredictable or even adversarial user replies. Additionally, the amount of publicly avail-able data for training IEAs is limited. To the best of our knowledge, no existing dataset is available to support the development of IEAs. In this paper, we make the following contributions: • We introduce IEAs as a distinct conversational paradigm and release YIELD: Information-Elicitation Learning Dialogues (YIELD), the first large-scale, ethically sourced IEA dataset. YIELD consists of real, human-to-human, English information-elicitation dia-logues sourced exclusively from repositories with clear public domain or Creative Com-mons licenses. YIELD contains four do-mains and is larger than existing task-oriented datasets (See Table 2). We publish both the standard, full dialogue version of the dataset and a rolling-window segmented version used for the experiments in this study. • We propose and operationalize a few IEA-specific evaluation metrics: Conformity , Pro-gression , and Turn-Length Ratio .• We further formalize information elicitation as a finite-horizon Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) and use offline reinforcement learning (ORL) with a minimal proxy reward to show that dialogue-level op-timization with YIELD is competitive with supervised fine-tuning (SFT). • Pilot experiments across multiple foundation LLMs show that fine-tuning on YIELD im-proves distributional and behavioral alignment with human elicitation dialogues. These find-ings are confirmed by human evaluations. The remainder of the paper is organized as fol-lows. Section 2 reviews related work. Section 3 de-scribes the YIELD dataset. Section 4 introduces the formalization of the information elicitation prob-lem. Section 5 presents IEA evaluation metrics. Section 6 details the pilot experiments and human evaluation; and Section 7 concludes the paper.

There is growing interest in role-aware CAs, which adopt defined personas and pursue struc-tured goals. These agents have gained popularity in a range of applications, from mental health sup-port (Fitzpatrick et al., 2017) to legal assistance (Thomson Reuters, 2025). In parallel, research on proac-tive CAs explores how conversational systems can drive interactions forward, such as initiating ques-tions, shifting topics, and helping users make deci-sions (Deng et al., 2024; Liao et al., 2023). Both role-aware and proactive agents benefit from curated dialogue datasets tailored to their specific domains and objectives. The Information Elicitation Agents (IEAs) pre-sented in this paper represent a natural extension of these trends. IEAs obtain information from hu-man respondents to support the agents’ information needs, such as information needs to serve for insti-tutional goals. Yet, despite increasing interest in these capabilities, no existing dataset has been de-signed to support IEAs. To our knowledge, YIELD is the first large-scale, ethically sourced benchmark created to enable the training and evaluation of IEAs across multiple domains. 

3 Dataset Construction 

Our dataset construction process consists of three stages: data acquisition, annotation, and dataset standardization. 

3.1 Data Acquisition 

We chose the data sources with the following aims: (1) to maximize coverage of elicitation settings and (2) to ensure legal usability through public domain or Creative Commons licensing. Many of the files, particularly PDF and DOCX transcripts, to the best of our knowledge, had not previously been pro-cessed into any dialogue dataset. We categorized the identified sources into four domains of elicita-tion dialogues. For a full description of the data sources, see Appendix A.1. 

3.2 Annotation 

The collected transcripts appeared in various for-mats, including HTML, TXT, PDF, and DOCX. Unifying these different formats and heterogeneous materials required extensive pre-processing. Au-tomated text extractors regularly introduced errors during conversion and sources contained individ-ual issues such as speaker names that appeared without distinguishing markers and often varied in form within the same document. These and other issues made automatic parsing and tagging unreli-able. Therefore, we manually corrected and anno-tated the transcripts over several weeks, removing extraneous text, standardizing speaker references, and marking turns and speaker roles. The development and test sets each contain 10% of the data (228 and 229 dialogues, respectively), while the training set contains the remaining 80% (1,824). We grouped the dialogue files into sets of 128 dialogues each. The last file in a set may contain fewer than 128 dialogues. The unzipped size of YIELD is 201.06 MB. All dialogues are organized around two roles (elicitor and respondent), but may include multiple speakers within a role (e.g., group interviews). In this work, all implementations are done at the role level, while the released dataset preserves speaker identities to enable future multi-speaker modeling. 

4 IEA Formulation and Implementation 

We formalize information elicitation as a finite-horizon POMDP and use offline reinforcement learning with Advantage-Weighted Regression (AWR) (Peng et al., 2019) to fine-tune the model. 

4.1 States, Actions, and Observations 

We model the process of elicitation as a sequence of interactions between an elicitor (interviewer) and a respondent (interviewee), where the elicitor is the agent and the respondent is part of the environ-ment. The respondent possesses information that is unobservable by the elicitor, such as knowledge, opinions, or preferences, that the elicitor aims to infer from conversational interaction. This setup aligns naturally with the framework of a POMDP. At each turn, the environment has a latent state 

Xt ∈ X that represents the respondent’s hidden information. The elicitor takes an action At ∈ A 

in the form of a natural language utterance, after which the environment responds with a respondent utterance (observation) Ot+1 ∈ O that depends on both the latent state Xt and the elicitor’s action At,leading to the next elicitor utterance At+1 . This dynamic continues until the conversation is over. The conversation history at time t is a sequence of elicitor and respondent utterances 

Ht = ( At−w, O t−w+1 , . . . , A t−1, O t), (1) where w is the window size in turns that is avail-able to the agent at time t when producing its next utterance. When the conversation history includes the elicitor’s latest utterance At, we denote it Hst .In the traditional POMDP formulation, the agent maintains a belief state St, which is a probabil-ity distribution over possible hidden environment states Xt. This distribution encodes the agent’s available information about the hidden variables governing the environment at time t and serves as the agent’s basis for decision making. In the dialogue setting, however, explicitly maintaining such a distribution is not feasible as Xt represents unbounded and continuously changing informa-tion (everything the respondent might know, think, or believe). To make the problem tractable, we represent the information available to the elici-tor at time t with token-level hidden representa-tions St = fθ(Hst ) produced by a causal language model, where fθ(Hst ) denotes the model’s forward pass over the full segment Hst parameterized by θ.Because the model is autoregressive, each token’s representation depends only on the tokens preced-ing it, meaning that the hidden states corresponding to the context Ht encode everything the model has “seen” in the current turn’s context. St provides the sufficient context from which the likelihood of At can be evaluated, functionally mirroring the role of the belief state in the traditional POMDP formulation.

4.3 Value Function and Advantage Estimation 

We follow Advantage-Weighted Regression (AWR) (Peng et al., 2019) to train the elicitor policy in an offline setting to bias learning toward elicitor utter-ances that yield higher long-term informational re-turn. AWR estimates a state-value function vψ(St)

and computes advantages as the difference between empirical returns-to-go and the value prediction. In our implementation, the value function is realized as a linear value head on top of the LLM’s hidden representations. Because dialogue windows are padded to a fixed length, we use the hidden state of the end-of-sequence token following the elicitor’s utterance At as input to the value head instead of the final hidden state.

5 Evaluation for Information Elicitation Agents 

In this section, we propose a set of key dimen-sions for evaluating IEAs. These dimensions go be-yond conventional dialogue evaluation approaches, such as BLEU (Papineni et al., 2001), task suc-cess (Walker et al., 1997), or response appropriate-ness (See et al., 2019), by focusing on the goals and dynamics of information elicitation. We release an open-source Python package for computing all pro-posed evaluation metrics (included in the project repository). 

5.1 Conformity 

Conformity measures whether an IEA agent could generate dialogue output that follows the distri-butional patterns typical of real human elicitation 5dialogues. We identify two dimensions that are particularly informative as to whether the agent ac-quired the data’s characteristics: (1) perplexity com-puted by evaluating real human utterances from the testing set under the trained IEA model and (2) Re-sponse length , measured as a simple token count. Perplexity reflects how well the model captures the linguistic content of human dialogue, while utter-ance length captures a critical stylistic property that varies significantly across large language models and strongly influences conversational behavior. For perplexity, we condition the models on the dialogue history and score the real human elici-tor utterance for each block in the test set. We restrict the loss calculation to elicitor tokens so that context tokens do not affect the score and com-pute perplexity in the standard manner as the ex-ponential of the token-level cross-entropy: PPL =exp

6 Pilot Experiments 

We conduct pilot experiments to evaluate model-generated elicitor utterances conditioned on fixed dialogue states and assess policy alignment with expert behavior without introducing live respon-dents, establishing necessary precursors to inter-active evaluation. We selected the (1) Meta AI Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (Meta Llama, 2024a) as our primary model due to computational considera-tions and its open-source status. To explore per-formance across related open-source variants, we also employed (2) DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B (DeepSeek-AI et al., 2025), a distilled variant of comparable size, and (3) Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct (Meta Llama, 2024b), a smaller-scale variant.

6.2 Reward Signal 

As a first-step reward formulation to validate the proposed elicitation framework, we operationalize rewards r using a factual novelty signal as proxy for informational gain, measuring how much pre-viously unmentioned content an elicitor action sur-faces. We apply a spaCy-based NER model (Hon-nibal et al., 2020) to each utterance and maintain a cumulative set of previously mentioned entities, events, or facts. After the elicitor’s turn, the re-spondent’s reply is scanned for entities not yet ob-served; the elicitor is rewarded in proportion to each newly introduced entity. Formally, the reward is Rt+1 = Et+1 \ E ≤t , where Et+1 denotes the set of entities extracted from the respondent’s response, 

E≤t the accumulated set from all prior turns, and 

\ denotes the set difference operator. To mitigate potential reward-gaming behaviors, we compute factual novelty under several constraints. Entities produced by the elicitor are also added to the cumu-lative entity set, but they do not contribute reward, so that entities subsequently repeated by the respon-dent are not misattributed. Repeated entities yield no additional credit, therefore, agents that allow conversations to linger on the same content accrue fewer reward. While entity count alone could be gamed in unconstrained settings (e.g., a model pro-viding a list of entities as utterance), this risk is mitigated here because rewards are computed on curated, real human–human dialogues. 

6.3 Experimental Dataset 

We adapted YIELD to fit the experimental setup by segmenting the dialogues into fixed length blocks 

using a sliding window of six turns. Each block ends with an elicitor utterance, with the preceding five turns serving as conversational context. We added a base system instruction to each block (“ Act as an information elicitation agent for {domain}. ”). We removed blocks exceeding 512 tokens to maintain computational feasibility and consistent utterance length. We removed segments ending with an elicitor utterance of two words or fewer as we found these generally impacted train-ing negatively by oversampling backchannel state-ments (e.g., “Okay”, “Hmm”, “Got it”). Then, we computed rewards and returns-to-go with a γ = 0 .9

discount factor on the full dialogues and annotated them. There are a total of 103,454 segments with 83,181 on the training set and 9,988 and 10,285 on the development and testing sets, respectively. Descriptive statistics are available in Table 5, while a sample line is available in Appendix A.4. 

6.4 Prompted and SFT Models 

We compare against two versions of the founda-tional models: (1) prompt-only and (2) supervised fine-tuning (SFT). Prompt-only models use the same base system instruction as ORL and SFT, plus an additional model-agnostic prompt (Ap-pendix A.5). For SFT, we apply PEFT with LoRA adapters using the SFTTrainer (von Werra et al., 2020). 

6.5 Evaluation 

For evaluation, each six-turn segment is truncated by removing the final elicitor turn, and the model is tasked with generating that turn given the preceding five turns. This is repeated for all segments in the 

testing set, which models have not seen yet. Sample outputs are demonstrated in Appendix A.7.

6.5.3 Turn-Length Ratio 

Table 8 presents turn-length ratio results, with the shuffled dataset also included. In the shuffled base-line, elicitor and respondent turns are randomly reassigned, causing answers to appear as questions and vice versa. This role mixing neutralizes the sys-tematic asymmetry present in real dialogues, lead-ing to ratios close to 1. Meanwhile, real dialogues show ratios well above 1 across domains, reflect-ing the elicitation dynamic in which elicitor turns prompt longer respondent utterances. Both SFT and ORL closely reproduce this asymmetry, learn-ing to limit elicitor verbosity in proportions similar to human interviewers, a behavior that prompted models largely fail to exhibit. As with response length, this gap cannot be reliably closed through additional prompting, since doing so does not make models internalize elicitor behavior. 

6.6 Human Evaluation 

We collected human ratings of elicitor question quality using Amazon Mechanical Turk. For each HIT, workers reviewed the five-turn dialogue con-text followed by corresponding real, prompted, SFT, and ORL responses. We randomized the order of the response sources across HITs to prevent or-der effects. Workers scored questions on five-point scales representing different aspects of elicitation. The full set of instructions given to participants is available in Appendix Table A.6. In total, 300 dialogue evaluations were collected (100 dialogue blocks sampled with stratified sampling, rated by three workers). Workers were required to have more than 500 prior HITs and approval rate above 90%. Workers were compensated at a rate consis-tent with U.S. minimum wage, based on estimated task completion time. The results in Table 9 show that fine-tuned mod-els more closely approximate the distribution of real utterances than prompted models, supporting automatic evaluation findings. Fine-tuned model scores track the real responses more consistently, even though their absolute ratings are lower. This outcome is particularly interesting, as it suggests that crowd workers rewarded fuller elicitor utter-ances, which prompted models reliably generate. However, this behavior departs from real dialogue as prompted responses tend to be more verbose, leading, and polished as shown in Section 6.5. By contrast, fine-tuned models, while receiving lower absolute scores, overwhelmingly align more closely with the distribution of real utterances. 

6.6.1 Potential Misuse and Mitigations 

Information elicitation agents raise ethical con-cerns due to their goal of guiding conversations toward information that serves external objectives, which can introduce risks of biased or leading ques-tioning, power imbalances, and undue influence, particularly in domains such as journalism, legal proceedings, or public opinion polling where neu-trality is critical. These risks are compounded in settings involving sensitive personal information (e.g., healthcare or counseling), where issues of data privacy, storage, and potential misuse arise. Mitigations include enforcing constraints on ques-tioning strategies (e.g., prompt-level guidelines or post-generation filtering) and extending evaluation protocols to detect and penalize biased behavior. Systems should also implement data minimization, secure storage, and clear user consent and trans-parency practices. In high-stakes or sensitive con-texts, IEAs should only be deployed under appro-priate institutional and regulatory oversight (e.g., IRB or clinical governance), with human monitor-ing and auditing to ensure compliance with ethical and legal standards, rather than relying solely on model-level safeguards. 

7 Conclusion 

Our evaluation demonstrates YIELD effectively supports the development of models for the elici-tation setting and our finite-horizon POMDP for-mulation with offline reinforcement learning shows that dialogue-level optimization is competitive with SFT even when trained with a minimal reward sig-nal. This work opens several directions for future research including extended reward formulations that capture domain-specific notions of informa-tional value, interactive evaluation with live respon-dents, and additional evaluation dimensions that capture a broader range of elicitation characteris-tics. 98 Limitations 

The reward formulation used in offline reinforce-ment learning is intentionally minimal and may not adequately capture informational value across elic-itation contexts. Additionally, evaluation is limited to offline, next-turn generation on fixed contexts rather than interactive settings. Moreover, some proposed metrics are sensitive to design choices (e.g., Progression depends on the choice of embed-ding model and hyper-parameters) and may not capture all aspects relevant to effective elicitation behavior. Finally, this work focuses on modeling the base elicitor agent and does not address conver-sation management. While a fine-tuned LLM can serve as the core of an elicitation system, it is likely insufficient on its own for sustaining structured and effective long-horizon interactions.
