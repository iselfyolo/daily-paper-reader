# Abstract 

When automated decision systems fail, organizations frequently discover that formally compliant governance infrastructure cannot reconstruct what happened or why. This paper synthesizes an operational governance evidence framework — structural accountability collapse diagnostics, decision trace schemas, evidence sufficiency measurement, and label-free monitoring — into an integrated chain and analytically assesses its transferability across four decision system architectures. The cross-architecture comparison reveals a governance coverage gradient: deterministic rule engines achieve full DES-property fillability, hybrid ML+rules systems achieve partial fillability, classical ML systems achieve only minimal fillability, and agentic AI systems encounter structural breaks. We introduce the cascade of uncertainty, showing how governance failures propagate through serial dependencies between framework layers. For agentic systems, we identify three structural breaks — decision diffusion, evidence fragmentation, and responsibility ambiguity — and propose corresponding analytical extensions. Four propositions formalize the gradient, cascade compounding, delegation-depth effects, and extension sufficiency, establishing boundary conditions for the framework’s valid operating envelope. 

Keywords: governance evidence, accountability, automated decision systems, agentic AI, decision trace, audit, cascade of uncertainty 

# 1. Introduction 

When automated decision systems fail, post-incident investigations repeatedly discover that formally compliant organizations cannot reconstruct the decision process from available evidence. The gap between formal compliance and operational accountability is well-documented: organizations that satisfy regulatory requirements on paper may nonetheless lack the evidentiary infrastructure to explain, after the fact, how a specific decision was reached and whether it was appropriate. Accountability requires not merely that rules exist but that decision processes are reconstructable from available governance artifacts (Novelli et al., 2024). Four prior studies by the authors developed an operational governance evidence framework to address this recurring pattern: the governance evidence framework (Solozobov, 2026b) diagnosed structural accountability collapse and its four modalities, the decision trace schema (Solozobov, 2026a) specified a Decision Event Schema for decision trace capture, the evidence sufficiency model (Solozobov, 2026c) defined evidence sufficiency metrics for environments with delayed ground truth labels, and the label-free monitoring layer (Solozobov, 2026e) completed the chain with detection of governance degradation without outcome data. Together, these contributions form what we term the N4 governance evidence chain (named for the four foundational papers) — a layered architecture progressing from diagnosis through evidence capture, sufficiency measurement, and continuous monitoring. However, the generalizability of this framework remains untested. The algorithmic accountability literature has produced a rich set of normative frameworks, audit methodologies, and impact assessment protocols (Moss et al., 2021), yet most of these operate at the level of organizational process or regulatory requirement rather than at the level of technical evidence architecture. The question of whether governance evidence can be systematically produced and evaluated across different decision system architectures — and what structural properties determine the limits of such governance — has received comparatively little attention. Existing audit-oriented approaches — including model cards, algorithmic impact assessments (Ada Lovelace Institute; AI Now Institute; Open Government Partnership, 2021), and ethics-based auditing frameworks (Mökander et al., 2021) — focus on what should be evaluated but do not specify how the underlying evidence for such evaluations is produced, validated, or maintained across system architectures. The N4 framework addresses this evidentiary substrate: it defines the minimum evidence requirements that any accountability mechanism — whether audit, impact assessment, or regulatory compliance — presupposes. This framework was developed and validated in the context of risk decision systems — specifically, financial fraud detection and regulatory compliance pipelines that combine machine learning models with business rules. The present paper asks three questions that extend beyond this origin domain. First, does the framework generalize across different decision system architectures? Automated decision-making in practice spans a wide spectrum, from deterministic rule-based systems with binary logic to machine learning systems with learned decision boundaries (Hackney & Huggins, 2023). If the framework addresses only one architecture type, its theoretical contribution is narrower than claimed. Second, how do governance failures at one framework layer affect other layers? The four components — diagnostic theory, trace schema, sufficiency measurement, label-free monitoring — were developed as modular contributions, but they may interact in ways that create compound vulnerabilities. Third, can the framework extend to agentic AI systems, where decisions are fragmented across multiple autonomous agents with delegated authority and emergent coordination? The third question is particularly urgent. Agentic AI systems are rapidly proliferating across enterprise decision environments (Mukherjee & Chang, 2025), and they challenge governance assumptions in ways that prior architectures do not. Governance happens in time, and AI decisions increasingly occur outside the window in which human intervention can reliably act (Driver, 2026). Where a classical ML system makes a bounded decision that can be captured in a trace, an agentic system decomposes goals into sub-tasks, delegates them to specialized agents, and produces composite outcomes through multi-step reasoning chains. The determination of decision rights in foundation-model-based systems is complicated by the diversity of roles: orchestration components, external APIs, plugins for specific tasks, and operational safeguarding components all contribute to decision outcomes (Liu et al., 2024). This architectural complexity creates what Mukherjee and Chang describe as an accountability vacuum — a void in which responsibility evaporates because agentic AI has its own operational autonomy (Mukherjee & Chang, 2025). The governance challenge posed by agentic systems is not merely an extension of the challenges posed by classical ML or hybrid systems. Where prior architectures introduce bounded opacity that can be mitigated through explainability artifacts and human oversight checkpoints, agentic systems introduce structural fragmentation — decisions are distributed across agent hierarchies, evidence is scattered across agent-local contexts, and responsibility cannot be attributed through existing organizational or technical mechanisms. Existing governance frameworks, including those designed for high-risk AI under the EU AI Act, were developed for systems with identifiable decision points and attributable decision-makers (Kaminski, 2019). Agentic systems challenge these assumptions at an architectural level. This paper makes three contributions. First, it synthesizes the N4 framework into an integrated governance evidence chain and analytically assesses its transferability across four decision system architectures: deterministic rule engines, classical ML with human oversight, hybrid ML+rules systems, and agentic AI. The cross-architecture comparison reveals a governance coverage gradient measured by DES-property fillability: deterministic systems achieve full fillability, hybrid systems achieve partial fillability, classical ML systems achieve only minimal fillability, and agentic systems encounter structural breaks with zero fillable properties. Second, it introduces the cascade of uncertainty — a structural property of decision systems in which governance failures at one layer propagate downward and compound at subsequent layers, connecting feature incorrectness through false negatives and untraceability to cumulative undetected losses. The cascade maps the four N4 framework components into a serial dependency chain, revealing compound failure modes that no single component detects in isolation. Third, it identifies three structural breaks that agentic AI systems introduce — decision diffusion, evidence fragmentation, and responsibility ambiguity — and proposes analytical extensions to the framework that address each break while preserving the governance properties established in the prior studies (Solozobov, 2026b; Solozobov, 2026a; Solozobov, 2026c; Solozobov, 2026e). The analysis is grounded in a purpose-built governance evaluation framework, which formalizes the cross-architecture comparison as a structured evaluation of six decision event properties across four architecture types. The benchmark produces feasibility matrices that quantify governance coverage as the proportion of properties that are fillable, partially fillable, unfillable, or opaque for each architecture — providing a structured analytical basis for claims about the governance coverage gradient that makes the comparison reproducible and auditable. Four propositions formalize the gradient, cascade compounding, delegation-depth effects, and extension sufficiency. P1 (the gradient) is an analytical derivation from the DES scoring scheme; P2 (cascade compounding) is empirically testable through instrumented pipeline experiments; P3 and P4 constitute analytical expectations derived from the framework’s structure — together establishing a research agenda for empirical validation. The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 synthesizes the four prior papers into an integrated framework. Section 3 applies the framework to four system architectures using the governance evaluation framework, establishing the governance coverage gradient. Section 4 introduces the cascade of uncertainty and maps it to the framework’s components. Section 5 examines the agentic structural breaks and proposes framework extensions. Section 6 defines boundary conditions and limitations. Section 7 discusses implications and future work. 

# 2. The Operational Governance Evidence Framework 

The operational governance evidence framework developed across four prior studies constitutes an integrated analytical chain that addresses a recurring failure pattern in automated decision systems: organizations that satisfy formal compliance requirements nevertheless cannot reconstruct the decision process when failures occur. Each paper contributes a distinct layer to this chain, and together they provide an integrated governance evidence infrastructure from diagnostic theory through operational measurement. This section synthesizes the four contributions into an integrated model that subsequent sections will test against different system architectures. 

## 2.1. Structural Accountability Collapse and Four Modalities 

The governance evidence framework (Solozobov, 2026b) identifies the root phenomenon: structural accountability collapse (SAC), defined as the condition in which architectural properties of automated decision systems — velocity, scale, and opacity — exceed the design capacity of governance infrastructure, causing governance evidence to degrade while compliance artifacts persist. The analytical contribution is a distinction between compliance evidence (demonstrating that a process was followed) and governance evidence (enabling reconstruction of whether the decision-making process was adequate to the risks it addressed). Three jointly necessary properties define governance evidence: reconstructability, evaluability, and contemporaneity. The paper establishes four modalities through which SAC manifests: evidence gaps (governance-relevant data is not captured), responsibility diffusion (accountability cannot be attributed to identifiable actors or roles), decision opacity (the decision logic cannot be reconstructed from available artifacts), and feedback failure (outcome signals do not reach the governance layer in time to support oversight). These modalities are structurally distinct — each produces a different type of governance evidence deficit, and each requires different remediation. The four modalities form the diagnostic foundation for the entire framework, providing a taxonomy that subsequent papers operationalize and measure (Cobbe et al., 2021). 

## 2.2. Decision Trace Schema 

The decision trace schema study Solozobov (2026a) operationalizes the governance-compliance distinction through a six-property Decision Event Schema (DES) that captures the provenance chain from input features through model inference to final decision. The schema defines the minimum evidence record that must be preserved for each decision event to enable post-hoc audit and reconstruction. As of DES v0.3.0, each event must declare an explicit schema_version , and the six property groups carry formal numbering: decision context (semantic completeness), decision logic (causal reconstructability), decision boundary (cross-system coupling), decision quality indicators (evidence of decision quality), human override record (independence of human judgment), and temporal metadata (temporal evidence and cryptographic integrity). Where compliance logging typically records that a decision was made and by which system, the DES captures the full decision context: what data was available, what model version processed it, what features were computed, what thresholds applied, and what the decision outcome was. The decision trace concept finds independent support in recent accountability frameworks. Huynh et al. describe provenance-based audit trails that enable tracing from a decision back to its input data while identifying responsibility for each activity in the chain (Huynh et al., 2020). Similarly, structured decision traces that create a chronological account of factors informing a given decision have been recognized as essential for transparent enterprise decision-making (Anumula, 2022). Natta proposes integrated data trails that capture all activity of an AI system in the decision-making process, providing step-by-step descriptions that can be audited at any stage (Natta, 2025). The convergence of these independent proposals toward trace-based accountability confirms that the decision trace schema addresses a structural need rather than an idiosyncratic design choice. The DES further specifies completeness criteria: a trace is governance-adequate only if it permits reconstruction of both the decision and the decision context. DES v0.3.0 operationalizes this through a tiered evidence model ( lightweight , sampled , full ) that defines progressive completeness requirements — lightweight traces capture required fields only, sampled traces add abbreviated logic and context, and full traces populate all six property groups completely. This distinction matters because many existing logging systems capture sufficient data to replay a decision (given the same model and inputs, produce the same output) but insufficient data to evaluate whether the decision process was appropriate (whether the right data was available, whether the model was fit for purpose, whether override conditions were met). 

## 2.3. Evidence Sufficiency Under Delayed Labels 

The evidence sufficiency study Solozobov (2026c) addresses a measurement problem that arises once decision traces exist: how to determine whether accumulated governance artifacts contain enough information to support accountability judgments. This problem is compounded by label delay — the condition in which ground truth outcomes (whether a decision was correct) arrive days, weeks, or months after the decision was made, or in some cases never arrive at all. The paper defines evidence sufficiency metrics that evaluate governance artifact quality independently of outcome labels. Rather than asking whether decisions were correct (which requires labels), sufficiency measurement asks whether the evidence archive is complete enough to support reconstruction and evaluation if and when accountability is required. This reframing separates the governance question (can we reconstruct?) from the performance question (was the decision right?) and enables continuous governance assessment even in domains with permanent label absence. Audit-ready logging architectures in clinical AI deployment illustrate this principle: logging systems that capture model versions, input data identifiers, and inference parameters enable reproducibility of AI determinations regardless of when or whether diagnostic ground truth becomes available (Joseph, 2023). 

## 2.4. Label-Free Monitoring 

The label-free monitoring study Solozobov (2026e) completes the framework with amonitoring layer that detects governance degradation without requiring outcome labels. Where the evidence sufficiency study (Solozobov, 2026c) measures the static sufficiency of governance artifacts at a point in time, the monitoring study addresses the dynamic problem: detecting when governance evidence quality is deteriorating over time. The monitoring approach uses distribution shift detection and evidence quality signals as proxies for governance health. If the statistical properties of decision traces change (feature distributions shift, decision time patterns alter, override rates diverge from baselines), this may indicate that the governance evidence being collected no longer represents the actual decision process. Modern logging architectures that treat individual executions as immutable historical events support complete system state reconstruction for any historical time point, providing the foundation for detecting temporal drift in governance evidence quality (Nallapu, 2025). Label-free monitoring is particularly important for systems where outcome feedback is structurally delayed or absent — precisely the systems where governance failures are most difficult to detect. By monitoring the evidence production process rather than decision outcomes, the framework provides continuous oversight capability that does not depend on external validation signals. 

## 2.5. The Integrated Chain 

The four contributions form a logical chain: the governance evidence framework (Solozobov, 2026b) diagnoses why governance evidence degrades (four modalities of SAC), the decision trace schema (Solozobov, 2026a) specifies what governance evidence must contain (the DES), the evidence sufficiency model (Solozobov, 2026c) measures whether enough evidence exists (sufficiency metrics), and the label-free monitoring layer (Solozobov, 2026e) monitors whether evidence quality is maintained over time (label-free detection). Each layer depends on the preceding one: sufficiency cannot be measured without a trace schema, monitoring cannot detect degradation without sufficiency baselines, and none of these operational layers function without the diagnostic theory that identifies what governance evidence failure looks like. This chain was developed and analytically assessed in the context of risk decision systems — specifically, financial fraud detection and regulatory compliance systems where automated decisions carry material consequences. The framework components have been disseminated as a series of companion papers, with the governance evidence framework available as a preprint (Solozobov, 2026b) and the remaining components as working papers under review. The DES property definitions, sufficiency metric formulas, and monitoring mechanism specifications that the present paper’s cross-architecture scoring depends upon are documented in the open-access governance evaluation framework repository (Solozobov, 2026d); anonymous reviewer copies of the companion studies are available as supplementary material accompanying this submission. The remaining sections of this paper test whether the chain generalizes to other system architectures, analyze the cascade of uncertainty that connects governance layers, and identify the structural breaks that emerge when the framework confronts agentic AI systems. 

# 3. Cross-Architecture Governance Comparison 

The operational governance evidence framework was developed in the context of risk decision systems — specifically, financial fraud detection pipelines combining learned models with business rules. A natural question is whether the framework generalizes beyond this origin domain. This section applies the N4 governance criteria (reconstructability, evaluability, contemporaneity, and the four SAC modalities) to four progressively more complex decision system architectures: deterministic rule engines, classical ML with human oversight, hybrid ML+rules systems, and agentic AI. The comparison reveals a governance coverage gradient measured by DES-property fillability: deterministic systems achieve full fillability, hybrid systems achieve partial fillability, classical ML systems achieve only minimal fillability, and agentic systems encounter structural breaks with zero fillable properties. 

## 3.1. Deterministic Rule-Based Systems 

Deterministic rule-based systems represent the strongest fit for the N4 governance framework. In these systems, decisions follow explicit, human-authored rules applied to structured inputs. Every decision can be reconstructed from the rule set and the input data alone, satisfying all three governance evidence properties by construction. Reconstructability is inherent: given the same inputs and rules, the same decision is produced. Evaluability follows from rule transparency — an auditor can read the rules and assess whether they are appropriate to the risk. Contemporaneity requires only that inputs are logged at decision time, since the rules themselves are versioned artifacts. Automation of administrative decision-making based on pre-defined algorithmic rules reduces room for discretion and supports consistent, predictable decision-making (Roehl & Hansen, 2024). Kroll et al. demonstrate that such systems can be designed to prove to oversight authorities that decisions were made under an announced set of rules consistently applied in each case, a condition they term procedural regularity (Kroll et al., 2017). Against the four SAC modalities, deterministic systems show minimal vulnerability. Evidence gaps are unlikely because the decision logic is fully specified. Responsibility diffusion is contained because rule authorship and deployment are discrete, attributable acts. Decision opacity is absent by definition — the rules are the explanation. Feedback failure remains possible (rules may be wrong, and outcome data may not reach rule authors), but this is an organizational rather than architectural problem. The N4 framework applies to deterministic systems with minimal adaptation. The Decision Event Schema natively supports this architecture through logic_type=rule_based , capturing rule version, input values, structured rule evaluation path, and decision output. DES v0.3.0 further requires that every event declare an override_occurred boolean and a cryptographic 

hash_chain for tamper evidence, both of which deterministic systems satisfy by construction. Sufficiency metrics confirm completeness trivially. Label-free monitoring detects rule drift through input distribution changes. This architecture type serves as the baseline against which governance coverage degradation in more complex systems can be measured. 

## 3.2. Classical ML Systems with Human Oversight 

Classical machine learning systems — supervised models (logistic regression, gradient-boosted trees, neural networks) deployed with human-in-the-loop oversight —introduce the first governance complications. The learned decision boundary is not directly readable as a rule set, creating a structural opacity that deterministic systems do not exhibit. Deep learning algorithms in particular are characterized by multiple hidden layers of artificial neurons relating inputs to outputs, and are often described as systems that hide their internal logic from the user (Busuioc, 2021). This opacity does not prevent governance evidence collection, but it changes what governance evidence must contain. Where a deterministic system’s trace need only record inputs and the rule version, an ML system’s trace must additionally capture the model version (trained weights), the feature engineering pipeline version, any pre-processing or post-processing transformations, and the confidence score or probability distribution that informed the decision. DES v0.3.0 provides native support for this through its model_inference object within decision_logic (capturing model_id , model_ version , feature_vector_hash , and confidence ) and through the logic_type=ml_inference 

classifier that distinguishes learned from rule-based decision paths. Human-in-the-loop design addresses the opacity gap by inserting human judgment at defined points in the decision pipeline to control risk, preserve contestability, and maintain operational accountability (Daruna, 2026). The human override creates a governance checkpoint: when a human reviews and approves or rejects a model recommendation, this creates an attributable decision event that the DES can capture. However, the governance value of human oversight depends on whether the override is substantive (the human independently evaluates the case) or ceremonial (the human rubber-stamps the model output). The framework cannot distinguish these from trace data alone — this is a known limitation. Against the SAC modalities, classical ML systems show moderate vulnerability. Evidence gaps emerge when feature engineering is not fully logged or when model training data provenance is incomplete. Responsibility diffusion increases because the decision involves both the model (trained by data scientists) and the reviewer (operating under time pressure). Decision opacity is the primary new challenge — the model’s learned boundary cannot be explained by citing rules, requiring additional explainability artifacts. Feedback failure risk increases because model performance degrades silently through data drift without triggering rule-level alerts. The N4 framework accommodates classical ML systems through the DES’s native decision_ logic property, which captures model metadata (version, inference parameters, confidence) alongside decision data, and through sufficiency metrics that incorporate model versioning and feature pipeline integrity. Label-free monitoring becomes particularly valuable here, as it can detect the distribution shifts that precede model degradation. 

## 3.3. Hybrid ML+Rules Systems 

Hybrid systems — the architecture most common in production risk decision systems — combine learned models with deterministic business rules at decision boundaries. A fraud detection pipeline, for example, might use an ML model to score transaction risk, then apply business rules to determine the action (block, review, approve) based on the score and contextual factors (customer tier, transaction amount, jurisdiction). This architecture creates governance seams: points where trace continuity and responsibility attribution must bridge two different decision paradigms. The ML component produces a score through opaque computation; the rules component applies transparent logic to that score. Decision boundaries that divide threshold logic from core processing allow adjustment without changing the pipeline, maintaining governance through documented decision criteria (Kasi, 2025). However, governance adequacy at the seam depends on whether the trace captures enough context to evaluate the interaction between the model score and the rule outcome. Consider a concrete failure scenario: a fraud detection system scores a transaction at 0.72 risk. The business rule threshold for review is 0.75, so the transaction passes without review. If this transaction later proves fraudulent, post-incident reconstruction must answer not only whether the model scored correctly (a model question) but whether the threshold was appropriate for this transaction type (a rule question) and whether the interaction between model uncertainty and rule rigidity created an evidence gap (a governance question). The DES must capture the model score, the applicable threshold, the rule evaluation path, and any contextual factors that informed the threshold setting. Against the SAC modalities, hybrid systems exhibit compound vulnerability. Evidence gaps can occur at the seam — the model trace and the rule trace may be logged by different systems with different schemas and retention policies. Responsibility diffusion compounds because model performance is owned by data science teams while rule configuration is owned by business or compliance teams, and neither may fully understand the governance implications of the interaction. Decision opacity is partial — the rules are transparent but the score that feeds them is not. Feedback failure is architecturally amplified: outcome labels (was this transaction actually fraudulent?) may take weeks to arrive, during which both model and rules continue operating on potentially degraded evidence. The N4 framework addresses hybrid systems through seam-aware trace design — the DES must explicitly capture the handoff between ML and rules components, including the score, the applicable rules, and the decision outcome. Sufficiency metrics must evaluate completeness across both paradigms. This is the architecture type for which the framework was originally designed, and it provides the most thorough governance coverage short of deterministic systems. 

## 3.4. Agentic AI Systems 

Agentic AI systems represent a qualitative departure from the preceding architectures. As used in this paper, “agentic AI” refers specifically to multi-agent systems in which an orchestrating agent decomposes goals into sub-tasks, delegates them to specialized sub-agents (which may themselves further delegate), invokes external tools, and synthesizes results through multi-step reasoning chains using foundation models with stochastic decoding. This definition encompasses the class of systems variously described as compound AI systems, LLM-based agent architectures, and autonomous multi-agent workflows. It does not encompass simpler single-agent systems with deterministic tool use or tightly constrained agent systems with fixed delegation patterns and pre-specified action spaces — such systems may exhibit some but not all of the structural breaks identified below, and their governance profiles require separate analysis. The zero-fillable profile in Table 2 applies to the multi-agent, foundation-model-based subclass defined here. Where deterministic, ML, and hybrid systems all share a common structural property — a bounded decision event that can be captured in a trace — agentic systems (as defined above) fragment decisions across multiple autonomous agents with dynamic tool use, delegated sub-tasks, and emergent coordination patterns. This fragmentation creates fundamental governance gaps that the N4 framework, in its current form, cannot fully address. The structural distinction is not merely one of complexity. Traditional governance models evolved during eras of human-operated systems with clear command hierarchies and discrete decision points, rendering them fundamentally misaligned with the continuous, distributed, and often opaque decision-making characteristic of modern autonomous systems (Barot, 2025). In an agentic system, an orchestrating agent may decompose a task into sub-tasks, delegate them to specialized agents, receive and synthesize results, and produce an output — all without a single identifiable decision point that the DES can capture. The decision is the emergent product of the entire delegation chain. Kroll et al.’s insight about machine learning — that learned rules raise problems because they do not have obvious decision rules fixed by the system’s designer (Kroll et al., 2017) — applies with greater force to agentic systems. Where ML systems at least have a fixed model that can be versioned and audited, agentic systems exhibit non-deterministic reasoning: prompt variation, reflection, delegation across agents, and stochastic decoding routinely produce different outputs even when semantic intent is unchanged (Fatmi, 2026). This non-determinism undermines reconstructability at an architectural level. Against the SAC modalities, agentic systems show severe vulnerability across all four. Evidence gaps are structural: execution logs record effects (API calls, state mutations) but do not capture the authorization rationale that allowed each event to occur (Fatmi, 2026). Responsibility diffusion reaches a qualitative threshold: the agentic AI has its own operational autonomy, creating an accountability vacuum in which responsibility evaporates, and the unstructured nature of its tasks, coupled with broad autonomy and inherently opaque training processes, defies traditional concepts of liability (Mukherjee & Chang, 2025). Decision opacity compounds because each agent in the chain may use different models, tools, and reasoning strategies. Feedback failure is amplified by the difficulty of attributing outcomes to specific agents or decisions within the chain. The N4 framework identifies these gaps but does not yet provide remediation. The DES assumes a bounded decision event; agentic systems require a trace protocol that captures delegation graphs. Sufficiency metrics assume a single decision context; agentic systems distribute context across agent-local states. Label-free monitoring assumes stable decision distributions; agentic systems exhibit inherent variability that is difficult to distinguish from degradation. Section 5 examines these structural breaks in detail and proposes extensions to address them. 

## 3.5. Summary: The Governance Coverage Gradient 

Table 2 formalizes the governance coverage gradient using a purpose-built governance evaluation framework (Solozobov, 2026d). The benchmark evaluates each architecture type against the six DES v0.3.0 decision event property groups. For each architecture-property pair, we assessed whether the property can be fully populated from available governance artifacts (fillable), only partially recovered through additional instrumentation (𝑝𝑎𝑟𝑡𝑖𝑎𝑙𝑙𝑦 𝑓𝑖𝑙𝑙𝑎𝑏𝑙𝑒 ), structurally unrecoverable given the architecture’s design constraints (unfillable), or fundamentally opaque to external inspection (opaque). The four rating categories are operationally defined as follows: • fillable : the property can be fully populated from governance artifacts that the architecture produces by construction, without additional instrumentation. • partially_fillable : the property can be recovered but requires supplementary artifacts (explainability tools, additional logging, or manual enrichment) that the architecture does not produce by default. • unfillable : the property cannot be populated given the architecture’s design constraints, regardless of instrumentation effort. • opaque : the property’s value is not merely missing but fundamentally inaccessible to external inspection — the architecture produces the behaviour but no artifact can expose the internal state that generated it. The decision rule for borderline cases between adjacent categories: a property is rated at the higher category only if the architecture’s structural properties guarantee artifact availability without dependence on optional instrumentation or organizational practice. regardless of how commonly such artifacts are used in practice. When the architecture makes the property structurally unrecoverable versus merely unobservable, the distinction between unfillable and opaque applies: unfillable means the evidence does not exist; opaque means the evidence exists internally but cannot be externalized. Assessments were derived analytically by mapping each architecture’s structural properties — determinism, trace availability, decision boundary explainability, intervention points, and temporal ordering guarantees — against the evidence requirements of each DES property. The fillable ratio represents the proportion of properties rated fillable out of six total. A coding decision on override_escalation_record warrants explanation. This property generalizes the original DES human_override_record (Solozobov, 2026a) to encompass any structured authority intervention — whether a human overriding a model recommendation or an orchestrating agent constraining a sub-agent’s mandate. DES v0.3.0 strengthens the governance basis for this property in two ways. First, the schema now requires override_occurred as a mandatory boolean at all evidence tiers and for all logic types, preventing silent falsification of the governance record — every event must explicitly declare whether an override was applied. Second, the schema introduces a two-path human judgment design: primary human decisions (logic_type=human_decision ) require actor attribution and rationale regardless of whether an override occurred, while algorithmic decisions require full override context only when override_occurred=true . This architectural enforcement means that override tracking is no longer dependent on organizational discipline alone. The fillability criterion is whether the DES can capture the escalation context: what triggered the intervention and under what authority conditions it occurred. For Hybrid ML+Rules, the property is rated fillable because the rules component generates structured escalation triggers: the system records which rule fired, what threshold was exceeded, and what risk category applied, providing a complete governance record of the override context — and the mandatory override_ occurred field ensures this record is always present. For Classical ML + HITL, the property is rated 𝑝𝑎𝑟𝑡𝑖𝑎𝑙𝑙𝑦 𝑓𝑖𝑙𝑙𝑎𝑏𝑙𝑒 because override triggers are discretionary — the system records that a human reviewed the case (and override_occurred is always populated) but not what structured criteria prompted the review. The distinction is between structured and unstructured escalation context, not between substantive and ceremonial cognition. The scoring logic is illustrated with two worked examples that clarify a distinction critical to the coding scheme. For decision_logic under Classical ML + HITL: the DES property asks what logic was applied to reach this decision. In a supervised model, the logic IS the trained weights plus the inference pipeline — these are versioned, stored, and deterministically reproducible given the same input. The logic is therefore fully recoverable from governance artifacts. Rating: fillable. By contrast, decision_boundary asks whether the decision surface can be explained in terms a human auditor can evaluate. For ML systems, explainability tools provide approximations (SHAP values, feature importance) rather than direct readings of the learned boundary. Rating: 𝑝𝑎𝑟𝑡𝑖𝑎𝑙𝑙𝑦 𝑓𝑖𝑙𝑙𝑎𝑏𝑙𝑒 . The distinction between these two properties — recoverable logic vs. interpretable explanation — is what allows Classical ML to score fillable on one while scoring only partially on the other. # 4. The Cascade of Uncertainty 

The cross-architecture comparison in Section 3 treated each governance challenge in isolation — the unfillable and opaque entries in Table 2 identify which DES properties each architecture cannot populate. In practice, however, these challenges do not arise independently. This section introduces the cascade of uncertainty: a structural property of decision systems in which governance failures at one layer propagate downward, amplifying evidence gaps at subsequent layers. The cascade connects the four N4 framework components into a dependency chain where upstream failures compound into downstream governance collapse. 

## 4.1. The Cascade Mechanism 

The cascade operates through five linked stages, each of which degrades a different governance property: 

Stage 1: Feature Incorrectness. The decision pipeline receives input data that is incorrect, stale, or incomplete. In a fraud detection system, this might mean that a customer’s transaction history is missing recent activity because of a data pipeline delay, or that a risk feature is computed from a table that has not been refreshed. The decision system processes these inputs without awareness of their deficiency — the features arrive in the expected format and pass schema validation, but their content does not reflect the actual state of the world. Stage 2: False Negatives. Incorrect features produce incorrect decisions. In risk-scoring domains where data degradation manifests as missing or stale risk-elevating signals (suspicious transaction patterns, adverse behavioral indicators), the directional bias is toward false negatives — cases that should have been flagged but were not, because the features that would have triggered detection were absent or wrong. A transaction that would score 0.85 risk with correct features might score 0.62 with stale data, falling below the review threshold. The cascade model is bounded to this false-negative path because false positives — which data degradation can also produce when risk-elevating features are corrupted — typically generate downstream governance artifacts (human reviews, customer appeals, case files) that make them traceable, preventing the cascade from reaching Stage 3. The governance-invisible path runs specifically through false negatives. 

Stage 3: Epistemically Indistinguishable Decisions. False negatives are structurally invisible to governance. True positives generate case files, review records, and outcome documentation — they leave rich governance evidence trails. False negatives, by contrast, produce no downstream artifacts. No case is opened, no reviewer is assigned, no outcome is recorded. The decision trace exists (the DES captured the inputs, model version, and score), but it is indistinguishable from a correctly scored low-risk transaction. The governance evidence archive is technically complete but epistemically empty — it contains a record of a decision that appears correct but is not. 

Stage 4: Undetectable Degradation. When false negatives accumulate, the decision system’s effective performance degrades without triggering monitoring alerts. Score distributions may remain stable (the system still produces scores in the expected range), decision volumes may be unchanged (the same number of transactions are processed), and override rates may hold steady (reviewers see approximately the same case mix). Label-free monitoring (Solozobov, 2026e) can detect distribution shifts in features and scores — but if the degradation operates through data quality rather than model drift, the monitoring signals may not fire. The degradation is real but the governance evidence does not register it. 

Stage 5: Cumulative Losses. Undetected false negatives accumulate over time into material financial, operational, or societal losses. In fraud detection, this manifests as undetected fraud that grows until an external signal (a customer complaint, a regulatory audit, a pattern detected by a different system) reveals the gap. By the time the loss is discovered, the governance evidence archive contains weeks or months of decisions that appeared correct at the time but were systematically biased by upstream data quality failures. Post-incident reconstruction using the DES can identify what decisions were made, but identifying which decisions were affected by feature incorrectness requires two distinct evidential channels: ground truth outcome labels (which confirm whether the resulting decision was wrong) and independent data provenance or source-system reconciliation (which identifies whether upstream feature data was corrupted). The former may never arrive; the latter requires cross-system infrastructure that most decision pipelines do not maintain. The cascade is not a hypothetical risk. Organizations attempting to construct complete lineages of decisions frequently discover that this requires matching events across different systems with dissimilar schemas, different time granularities, and varied data retention requirements (Bisht, 2026). The difficulty of reconstructing cross-system decision chains after the fact is a direct consequence of cascade-stage evidence loss. 4.2. Mapping the Cascade to the N4 Framework 

Each cascade stage corresponds to a specific component of the N4 framework, revealing that the framework’s four layers are not independent defences but a serial dependency chain: 

Stage 1 (Feature Incorrectness) maps to the decision trace schema (Solozobov, 2026a). The DES captures input features at decision time, creating a record that could, in principle, be used to detect feature incorrectness retrospectively. However, the DES records feature values as received by the decision system — it does not independently verify feature correctness against source systems. This is an inherent limitation: the trace is faithful to what the system saw, not to what the system should have seen. Evidence-driven governance systems require that documentation extend beyond high-level descriptions to include versioned configurations, feature selection rationales, and known limitations, supporting reproducibility under comparable conditions (Nwaodike, 2022). 

Stage 2 (False Negatives) maps to the evidence sufficiency model (Solozobov, 2026c). Sufficiency metrics evaluate whether the governance evidence archive contains enough information to support accountability judgments. For false negatives, the sufficiency question is whether the archive contains enough signal to distinguish a correctly scored low-risk transaction from an incorrectly scored one. In the absence of ground truth labels, this distinction may be unresolvable — the sufficiency metrics indicate the archive is complete, but completeness does not guarantee correctness. 

Stage 3 (Epistemic Indistinguishability) maps primarily to the decision trace schema and evidence sufficiency model (Solozobov, 2026a; Solozobov, 2026c). The trace records structurally valid but semantically incorrect decisions — the evidence exists but cannot distinguish correct from incorrect outcomes. The root problem is epistemic: the DES faithfully records what the system saw, and the sufficiency metrics report the archive as complete, but completeness does not guarantee correctness. The governance evidence archive is technically adequate yet epistemically empty. The label-free monitoring layer (Solozobov, 2026e) provides a secondary defence at this stage, but as discussed below, its detection capability depends on the degradation channel. 

Stage 4 (Undetectable Degradation) maps to the label-free monitoring layer 

(Solozobov, 2026e). This is the stage where the monitoring layer should detect the accumulating governance failure. Standard distribution-shift monitoring does catch many pipeline failures: stale data tables compress variance toward zero, and missing activity spikes zero-count features, both producing detectable distribution shifts. The cascade’s blind spot is narrower but more dangerous: distribution-preserving corruption, where semantic errors maintain identical statistical properties to correct data. Adversarial drift, systematic data entry errors that mirror historical patterns, or upstream logic changes that preserve marginal distributions while altering conditional relationships — these create governance failures that pass through distribution-based monitors undetected. This suggests that label-free monitoring should incorporate semantic data quality signals alongside statistical distribution signals — a framework extension identified as future work in the label-free monitoring study (Solozobov, 2026e). 

Stage 5 (Cumulative Loss) maps to the governance evidence framework 

(Solozobov, 2026b). The cascade’s terminal stage is precisely what the SAC diagnosis describes: a condition in which architectural properties exceed the design capacity of governance infrastructure. The cascade demonstrates that SAC is not merely a static vulnerability but a dynamic process — governance evidence degrades progressively through linked stages, each building on the failures of the preceding one. The cascade mapping reveals a critical insight: the N4 framework’s four components are necessary but may not be jointly sufficient. Each component addresses its own stage effectively in isolation, but the cascade creates failure modes that emerge from the interaction between stages. A trace that faithfully records incorrect features, a sufficiency metric that reports completeness for epistemically empty archives, monitoring that does not fire because the degradation channel differs from the detection channel — these are not individual failures but systemic ones that arise from the cascade structure itself. The cascade mechanism yields a testable proposition: P2: In risk-scoring decision pipelines where governance evidence flows through sequential processing stages (feature engineering, model inference, post-processing, human review), an evidence gap at stage k produces compounding governance degradation at downstream stages. The severity of the evidence deficit increases at each stage because downstream governance artifacts inherit the epistemic limitations of upstream records — a decision trace built on incorrectly recorded features is governance-deficient regardless of how completely the downstream stages log their own processing. This cascade is demonstrated here for the false-negative path in binary risk-scoring systems; whether analogous compounding occurs in other pipeline topologies (e.g., false-positive paths, multi-output classification, or non-sequential architectures) requires separate investigation. Empirical measurement of the compounding effect would require external ground-truth injection at each stage to distinguish inherited from locally introduced evidence gaps. 

## 4.3. Implications for Framework Design 

The cascade analysis has two practical implications. First, governance framework design must account for cross-layer dependencies. Testing each N4 component in isolation (does the DES capture all required fields? do sufficiency metrics report correctly? does monitoring detect drift?) may produce positive results while the cascade creates compound failures that no single component detects. End-to-end governance testing — deliberately introducing upstream data quality failures and measuring whether the full chain detects and reports them — is necessary but remains an open challenge. Current audit practices focus on individual model components rather than cross-layer evidence chains (Mökander et al., 2021). Second, the cascade intensifies as system architecture complexity increases, though no architecture is immune to its initial stages. In deterministic systems, feature incorrectness that produces syntactically valid but factually wrong inputs (e.g., an age of 25 instead of 65) causes the rules to fire correctly on incorrect data — the decision is procedurally perfect but factually wrong, and the error is silent rather than visibly anomalous. The cascade’s early stages (feature incorrectness through false negatives) therefore apply equally to deterministic systems; what deterministic systems avoid is the later-stage opacity that prevents reconstructing how the wrong output was reached. In hybrid ML+rules systems, the cascade operates as described above. In agentic systems, the cascade compounds further: features may be gathered by autonomous agents from multiple sources, processed through multi-step reasoning chains, and used to inform delegated sub-decisions, multiplying the stages at which incorrectness can enter and propagate. The next section examines how these compounding effects create structural breaks that challenge the framework’s applicability to agentic AI. 5. Agentic Extension 

The cross-architecture comparison (Section 3) identified agentic AI systems as a qualitative departure from deterministic, ML, and hybrid architectures — the only architecture type with zero fillable DES properties in Table 2. The cascade analysis (Section 4) demonstrated that governance failures compound through serial dependencies between framework layers. This section examines the specific structural breaks that agentic systems introduce and evaluates whether the N4 framework can be extended to address them. Agentic AI systems — autonomous agents that decompose goals into sub-tasks, invoke tools, delegate to other agents, and synthesize results — differ from prior architectures in three structural properties that directly challenge the framework’s assumptions: decision diffusion, evidence fragmentation, and responsibility ambiguity. Each property breaks a foundational assumption of the N4 governance chain. 

## 5.1. Decision Diffusion 

The N4 framework’s Decision Event Schema assumes a bounded decision event: a discrete moment at which inputs are processed, a model or rule set is applied, and an output is produced. This assumption holds for deterministic systems (rule evaluation), classical ML (model inference), and hybrid systems (score-plus-threshold). In agentic systems, this assumption fails. An agentic system does not make a single decision. An orchestrating agent receives a goal, decomposes it into sub-tasks, delegates sub-tasks to specialized agents, receives intermediate results, reasons about them, potentially re-delegates, and eventually produces a composite output. The decision is not a point but a directed acyclic graph (DAG) of delegated sub-decisions, each with its own inputs, reasoning, and outputs. In traditional decision-making contexts, responsibility can be traced to human actors with discrete roles and clearly defined obligations; with AI systems, accountability becomes distributed across multiple stakeholders, each with partial influence over the ultimate system behaviour (Tatipamula, 2025). The problem of opaque and unpredictable AI outcomes has consequences analogous to the many hands problem — a concept introduced to computer ethics by Nissenbaum (Nissenbaum, 1996), who identified the systematic difficulty of attributing responsibility in computerized systems where multiple actors and computational processes jointly produce outcomes. In the AI context, the many hands problem manifests as the impossibility of pinpointing individual responsibilities in systems that involve multiple actors and resources (Novelli et al., 2024). In agentic systems, the many hands problem extends beyond human stakeholders to the agents themselves. When an orchestrator delegates a risk assessment to Agent A, which invokes a data retrieval tool, passes results to Agent B for analysis, and Agent B’s output informs the orchestrator’s final recommendation, the decision graph contains at least four decision points — none of which individually constitutes the decision. The DES cannot capture this structure in its current form. A single decision trace record assumes a single decision context. Agentic systems require a trace protocol that captures the full delegation graph: which agent initiated each sub-task, what inputs were provided, what reasoning was applied, what outputs were produced, and how outputs were composed into the final result. Composition in agent chains does not weaken governance invariants but amplifies their necessity; without an execution-time boundary at each step, downstream effects become ungovernable and provenance becomes incomplete (Fatmi, 2026). 5.2. Evidence Fragmentation 

Even if a trace protocol could capture the delegation graph, a second structural break prevents governance evidence from being assembled: evidence fragmentation. In non-agentic systems, governance artifacts (decision traces, feature logs, model metadata) are produced within a bounded system perimeter — though even hybrid systems may log model traces and rule traces in different subsystems with different schemas and retention policies (Section 3.3), these fragments share a common decision event identity and can be assembled through schema mapping. In agentic systems, the fragmentation is qualitatively worse: each agent maintains its own local context, produces its own logs, and may use different tools, models, and data sources, with ephemeral and heterogeneous node schemas that lack a shared decision event identity. The governance evidence for a single composite decision is scattered across multiple agent-local contexts, tool invocation logs, and inter-agent message records. As established in Section 3.4, agent reasoning is non-deterministic and execution logs capture effects rather than authorization rationale. This compounds the fragmentation problem: even if all agent-local logs are collected, reconstructing the causal chain from logs alone is unreliable because the same semantic intent produces syntactically diverse outputs across invocations, and the logs record what happened without explaining why it was authorized. The distinction between execution logs and governance evidence is critical. An execution log answers what happened; governance evidence must answer why it was authorized to happen and whether the authorization was appropriate. In agentic systems, the authorization rationale is embedded in agent prompts, context windows, and reasoning chains that are ephemeral by default — they exist during processing and are discarded after the agent completes its task. The observability requirements for agentic systems must therefore extend beyond traditional logging to include monitoring of decision chains, action sequences, and environmental feedback loops (Joshi, 2025). The evidence sufficiency model (Solozobov, 2026c) assumes that evidence completeness can be evaluated against a known schema. In agentic systems, the schema itself is dynamic — different task decompositions produce different delegation graphs with different evidence requirements. A sufficiency metric for agentic governance must evaluate not just whether required fields are populated but whether the delegation graph has been fully captured and whether the causal links between agent-local evidence fragments are preserved. 

## 5.3. Responsibility Ambiguity 

The third structural break concerns responsibility attribution. The SAC framework (Solozobov, 2026b) treats responsibility diffusion as one of four modalities — an important but bounded governance challenge. In agentic systems, responsibility ambiguity becomes the dominant failure mode, qualitatively different from diffusion in non-agentic contexts. In human organizations, the many hands problem is mitigated by hierarchical authority structures: when multiple actors contribute to a decision, the chain of command determines who bears ultimate responsibility. In agentic systems, this mitigation is absent. Multi-agent architectures create accountability vacuums where control is distributed across autonomous agents, none of which individually determines the composite outcome (Joshi, 2025). When an orchestrating agent delegates a sub-task to a specialized agent, the orchestrator has nominal authority but limited visibility into the sub-agent’s reasoning. The sub-agent has operational control but limited understanding of the broader context. Neither bears clear responsibility for the composite outcome. At the human-system boundary, these accountability vacuums can create what Elish (2019) describes as moral crumple zones, where human operators absorb liability for failures they could not have prevented — but within the agent system itself, the problem is structural rather than moral: no attribution mechanism exists. The many hands problem in agentic AI extends beyond individual systems. Various agencies might be involved in the design and deployment of a single algorithmic system, making the attribution of responsibility and effective coordination challenging; this problem arises not only in legal liability attribution but also in coordinating accountability between different individuals and organizations who may have contributed to certain components (Ada Lovelace Institute; AI Now Institute; Open Government Partnership, 2021). In multi-agent systems, this organizational many-hands problem compounds with the agent-level many-hands problem: responsibility must be attributed across human designers, agent operators, the orchestrating agent, and multiple sub-agents, each of which contributed partial influence to the outcome. The responsibility diffusion modality identifies the problem but does not provide remediation for the agentic case. A governance framework for agentic systems requires a delegated responsibility attribution model that defines how responsibility flows through delegation chains — who is accountable when an orchestrator delegates poorly, when a sub-agent exceeds its mandate, or when the interaction between correctly functioning agents produces an undesirable composite outcome. 

## 5.4. Toward Framework Extensions 

The three structural breaks — decision diffusion, evidence fragmentation, and responsibility ambiguity — are not independent failures. They interact through the cascade mechanism described in Section 4: decision diffusion creates multiple points at which feature incorrectness can enter; evidence fragmentation prevents the monitoring layer from assembling a complete picture; responsibility ambiguity prevents post-incident reconstruction from assigning accountability even when the evidence is eventually gathered. Addressing these breaks requires three corresponding extensions to the N4 framework: 

Inter-agent decision trace protocols. The DES must be extended from a single-event schema to a delegation-aware protocol that captures the full delegation DAG. Each agent in a delegation chain must emit trace records that include: the task received, the reasoning applied, the sub-tasks delegated (if any), and the output produced. Correlation identifiers must link parent and child traces, enabling end-to-end reconstruction. DES v0.3.0 provides partial architectural support for this extension through its decision_boundary property, which now includes structured upstream_decisions and downstream_consumers arrays with per-interface boundary_contract objects (specifying protocol, schema version, SLA, data contract, and failure mode). These primitives capture inter-system coupling for non-agentic architectures; the agentic extension requires generalizing them from static boundary contracts to dynamic delegation graphs where coupling relationships are determined at runtime. The structural pattern of correlation-based distributed tracing is well established in software engineering: systems such as Dapper (Sigelman et al., 2010) and OpenTelemetry use span hierarchies and trace contexts to reconstruct request flows across microservices. The governance extension proposed here differs from observability tracing in a critical respect: it must capture not only execution state (what happened) but semantic authorization rationale (why the agent decided to act, what mandate it was operating under, and what constraints it evaluated). This additional semantic layer is what transforms an execution trace into governance evidence. Effective governance for agentic systems is not a monolithic layer but a distributed system requiring specialized components for multi-agent coordination protocols, real-time monitoring of action chains, and formal verification of safety guardrails (Joshi, 2025). 

Distributed evidence aggregation. Sufficiency metrics must be extended to evaluate evidence completeness across a delegation graph rather than within a single decision context. This requires a mechanism for assembling agent-local evidence fragments into a unified governance record, preserving causal links between fragments, and detecting gaps where agent-local evidence was not captured or was discarded. The aggregation mechanism must handle the heterogeneity of agent evidence — different agents may use different models, tools, and data formats, producing evidence in incommensurable schemas. 

Delegated responsibility attribution. The responsibility diffusion modality must be extended to capture the evidence required for accountability judgments in delegation chains. Specifically, governance traces must record: (a) the delegation parameters (what task was delegated, with what constraints), (b) mandate boundary conditions (what the sub-agent was authorized to do), and (c) outcome provenance (which agent produced which component of the composite result). Whether a principal-agent attribution model or an alternative accountability structure is adopted is a normative question beyond the framework’s scope — the framework’s contribution is specifying what evidence must be available to support any such attribution, ensuring that accountability remains determinable rather than structurally unresolvable. These three extensions address decision diffusion, evidence fragmentation, and responsibility ambiguity respectively. A fourth SAC modality — feedback failure — is amplified in agentic systems (Section 3.4) but is partially addressed by the proposed extensions rather than requiring a dedicated fourth extension. The delay aspect of feedback failure remains structural: outcome signals are delayed or absent regardless of how many agents produced the decision. However, the attribution aspect — the difficulty of tracing delayed feedback to the specific agent whose action produced the outcome — is directly mitigated by delegated responsibility attribution, which records the provenance chain linking outcomes to agent actions. The core distributional monitoring approach (Solozobov, 2026e) applies to the agentic case once inter-agent traces provide the evidence substrate, though the cascade analysis in Section 4 identifies a blind spot — data-quality degradation that does not manifest as distribution shifts — that would require extending the monitoring layer with data-quality signals regardless of architecture type. These extensions are proposed as analytical constructs, not implemented artifacts. Their feasibility, completeness, and practical implications require empirical investigation —developing and testing these extensions constitutes a primary direction for future work. The contribution of this analysis is identifying the structural breaks that necessitate such extensions and specifying the governance properties they must preserve. The structural breaks and proposed extensions yield two analytical expectations that follow from the framework’s structure and can guide empirical investigation: P3: In multi-agent decision systems, the complexity of responsibility attribution (as defined by the SAC responsibility diffusion modality) increases monotonically with the depth and branching complexity of the agent delegation graph, absent inter-agent trace protocols. For a given decision outcome, the number of agents whose contributions must be disentangled grows with delegation depth and branching factor — the precise growth rate depends on the DAG topology (whether subgraphs are reused, whether delegation is strictly hierarchical or allows cross-agent synthesis) and remains to be formally characterized. The analytical expectation is that deeper and more branched delegation structures make individual outcome attribution progressively less tractable. P4: Within a cooperative agent ecology — where all agents in the delegation graph are white-box or governance-trace-compatible (i.e., capable of emitting structured trace records as specified in Section 5.4; see Section 6.4 for the proprietary API boundary that limits this precondition in current commercial ecosystems) — if inter-agent trace protocols, distributed evidence aggregation, and delegated responsibility attribution are implemented as specified, they would improve four of six DES properties. # 6. Boundary Conditions and Limitations 

The preceding sections showed that the N4 framework, validated in hybrid ML+rules systems, can be analytically transferred to deterministic and classical ML architectures while encountering structural breaks in the agentic case. This section defines four boundary conditions where the framework’s governance coverage is structurally limited. These boundaries are not implementation gaps to be filled but inherent constraints that any post-incident governance framework must acknowledge. 

## 6.1. The Opacity–Determinism Spectrum 

The framework’s governance coverage is constrained by two distinct architectural properties: structural opacity (whether the decision logic can be inspected) and non-determinism (whether the decision can be reproduced). These properties are independent: classical ML inference is deterministic (given the same model version and inputs, the same output is produced) but structurally opaque (the learned decision boundary cannot be read as a rule set), which is why classical ML scores lower on governance coverage (0.17) than hybrid systems (0.50) despite having deterministic inference. At one extreme, deterministic rule engines are both transparent and deterministic — every decision is fully reconstructable, and the DES captures a complete governance record by construction. At the other extreme, foundation models with stochastic decoding produce different outputs for identical inputs across different invocations (Fatmi, 2026), and agentic systems compound both opacity and non-determinism through multi-step delegation chains with heterogeneous agent architectures. The practical implication is that governance evidence degrades along either dimension independently. Opacity limits evaluability: the DES can record what the system did, but auditors cannot assess whether the decision logic was appropriate without additional explainability artifacts. Non-determinism limits reconstructability: the DES can record what the system did on a particular occasion, but cannot guarantee that the system would produce the same output if re-run. For hybrid ML+rules systems — the framework’s primary analytically developed domain — the rules component compensates for ML opacity by providing transparent decision boundaries at governance seams, and non-determinism is bounded by the model version and feature inputs. For agentic systems, where both opacity and non-determinism are architectural rather than parametric, even weak reconstructability may require the inter-agent trace protocols proposed in Section 5. 

## 6.2. Counterfactual Unavailability 

The evidence sufficiency model (Solozobov, 2026c) evaluates whether governance artifacts contain enough information to support accountability judgments. This evaluation implicitly assumes that a correct judgment is possible in principle — that ground truth labels will eventually arrive, or that proxy signals can substitute. In some decision domains, this assumption fails permanently. Hiring decisions, welfare benefit determinations, and preventive policing interventions share a common property: the counterfactual outcome is structurally unobservable — a problem recognized in the algorithmic fairness literature as the selective labels problem, where observed outcomes exist only for cases that passed through the decision gate cf. (Schwartz et al., 2022). If a candidate is rejected, the organization cannot observe what would have happened had they been hired. If a welfare claim is denied, the outcome under approval is never realized. These domains have permanently absent ground truth, which means that the epistemic correctness of past decisions cannot be definitively assessed — sufficiency metrics can confirm that the evidence archive is structurally complete (all required DES fields are populated), but no external signal exists against which to evaluate whether the archived evidence reflects adequate decision-making. The distinction is precise: sufficiency metrics converge on completeness (was all available information captured?) but not on correctness (was the captured information adequate to the decision’s risks?). In counterfactual-unavailable domains, the framework provides verifiable evidence completeness but indeterminate epistemic value. The framework accommodates this boundary partially through label-free monitoring (Solozobov, 2026e), which detects governance degradation through distributional signals rather than outcome labels. However, label-free monitoring detects changes in evidence quality, not the absolute adequacy of evidence. In counterfactual-unavailable domains, the framework can detect that governance evidence is degrading relative to a baseline but cannot establish whether the baseline itself was adequate. 6.3. Adversarial Drift 

The label-free monitoring component assumes that governance degradation manifests in detectable distributional changes — feature distributions shift, score distributions alter, override rates diverge from baselines. A sophisticated adversary can exploit this assumption by preserving the statistical properties of governance artifacts while degrading actual decision quality. Concerns about gaming are well-documented in the audit literature: narrow audits that do not recognise such exploitation could amount to gaming, providing false assurance rather than genuine accountability (Costanza-Chock et al., 2022). In fraud detection, for example, an adversary who understands the monitoring signals can structure fraudulent transactions to maintain score distributions within expected ranges, avoid triggering override rate alerts, and preserve feature distribution statistics — while the underlying fraud rate increases. The governance evidence archive remains statistically normal; the monitoring layer reports no anomalies; yet the decision system is systematically failing. This adversarial drift boundary applies to all architecture types, including the hybrid systems for which the framework was originally designed. The framework does not claim to provide adversarial robustness. Its contribution is diagnostic: identifying when governance evidence has degraded, not defending against deliberate manipulation of governance signals. Adversarial robustness would require additional mechanisms — anomaly detection on governance metadata, cross-system correlation, or external audit — that are outside the framework’s current scope. 

## 6.4. Proprietary API Boundaries 

The inter-agent trace protocol proposed in Section 5 assumes that each agent in a delegation chain can be instrumented to emit structured governance traces — including reasoning applied, mandate constraints evaluated, and quality signals. In production multi-agent architectures, however, sub-agents are frequently third-party commercial LLM endpoints (e.g., proprietary foundation model APIs) that are structurally black-boxed by the vendor. The orchestrator cannot compel a proprietary endpoint to externalize its internal reasoning traces, constraint-checking logic, or confidence calibration. When delegation crosses a commercial vendor boundary, governance opacity re-emerges regardless of how thoroughly the orchestrator’s own traces are instrumented. This represents a structural limit to the proposed extensions: the inter-agent trace protocol achieves its governance improvements only to the extent that all agents in the delegation chain are white-box or cooperative — a condition that current commercial AI ecosystems do not guarantee. Addressing this boundary would require either vendor-side governance trace standards (analogous to how financial messaging standards mandate structured fields) or governance-preserving API wrappers that extract observable behavioral signals without requiring internal state access. 

## 6.5. Implications for Framework Scope 

These four boundaries define the framework’s valid operating envelope. The N4 governance evidence chain is most effective for hybrid ML+rules systems with bounded non-determinism, domains where ground truth eventually arrives (even if delayed), and environments where governance degradation is organic rather than adversarial. Beyond these boundaries, the framework’s diagnostic value persists — it can still identify what evidence exists and what is missing — but its ability to support definitive accountability judgments diminishes. Acknowledging these limits is itself a contribution: a governance framework that claims universal applicability without specifying boundary conditions provides less guidance than one that clearly delineates where it applies and where it does not. 

# 7. Discussion and Future Work 

## 7.1. Research Contributions 

This paper completes a research program that proceeds from diagnosis through engineering to measurement and synthesis. The governance evidence framework (Solozobov, 2026b) identified the problem: governance evidence degrades structurally in automated decision systems while compliance artifacts persist. The decision trace schema (Solozobov, 2026a) proposed a solution: a Decision Event Schema that specifies minimum governance evidence requirements. The evidence sufficiency model (Solozobov, 2026c) addressed measurement: sufficiency metrics that evaluate evidence completeness under label delay. The label-free monitoring layer (Solozobov, 2026e) closed the operational loop: detecting governance degradation without outcome data. This paper analytically tested the framework’s transferability across architectures, identified compound failure modes through the cascade of uncertainty, and mapped the structural boundaries that emerge when the framework confronts agentic AI. The combined contribution is an integrated governance evidence infrastructure — from diagnostic theory to operational measurement — analytically developed for hybrid ML+rules systems and analytically extended to agentic architectures. Empirical validation remains future work; the present contribution establishes the analytical foundations and identifies the structural constraints that shape possible governance solutions. The framework does not claim to solve governance for all decision systems. Its contribution is providing a structured way to ask governance questions: what evidence exists, whether it is sufficient, whether it is degrading, and where the governance architecture has structural blind spots. 

## 7.2. Relationship to the Accountability Literature 

The framework differs from the broader algorithmic accountability literature in two respects. First, it is operational rather than normative. Where much accountability scholarship asks who should be accountable and for what, the N4 framework asks what evidence is available to support accountability judgments regardless of how accountability is assigned. Evidence-driven governance transforms oversight from aspirational ethics and procedural compliance into an operational system capable of verification, audit, and enforcement (Nwaodike, 2022). This operational focus means the framework is compatible with different accountability regimes — it provides the evidentiary substrate that any regime requires. Second, the framework is post-incident rather than preventive. It does not prescribe how decisions should be made or what governance structures organizations should adopt. Instead, it addresses the reconstruction problem: when a decision system has produced an adverse outcome, can the decision process be reconstructed from available evidence? This distinguishes the framework from traditionally conceived accountability, “understood predominantly as a retrospective process through which designated accountors demand an account of prior conduct” (Eilstrup‐Sangiovanni & Hofmann, 2024) — the N4 framework provides the evidentiary infrastructure that such retrospective processes require. This framing is deliberately narrow — prevention is a broader challenge that requires organizational, regulatory, and technical interventions beyond evidence architecture. 7.3. Regulatory Implications 

The framework is potentially informative for emerging AI governance regulation. The EU AI Act requires high-risk AI systems to enable automatic recording of events over the system’s lifetime and mandates that logging capabilities facilitate post-market monitoring (European Parliament and Council of the European Union, 2024). The N4 framework’s DES provides a structured specification of what such logs must contain to support governance evidence requirements — going beyond generic logging mandates to specify the six decision event properties that enable decision reconstruction. For deterministic and hybrid systems, compliance with Article 12 logging requirements can be informed by DES field specifications, which provide a structured operationalization of what regulatory logging mandates require in practice. For agentic systems, however, the structural breaks identified in Section 5 reveal that Article 12 compliance faces the same challenges as the N4 framework itself: decision diffusion means no single log captures the complete decision, and evidence fragmentation means that the logs required for reconstruction are distributed across multiple agents. The inter-agent trace protocol proposed in Section 5 could potentially inform implementation approaches for Article 12 compliance in agentic deployments, though its formal specification remains future work. 

## 7.4. Future Research Directions 

The agentic extension proposed in Section 5 identifies three analytical constructs —inter-agent trace protocols, distributed evidence aggregation, and delegated responsibility attribution — that require empirical development. Three specific research directions follow. First, formal specification of inter-agent trace protocols using process algebra or temporal logic formalisms. The delegation graph structure proposed in Section 5 needs formal properties: completeness (all delegation events are captured), consistency (parent and child traces agree on delegated tasks and results), and minimality (no redundant trace information that creates storage or privacy burdens). TLA+ or similar specification languages could express these properties and enable model checking against protocol implementations. Recent work on policy-as-code frameworks for continuous AI assurance demonstrates the feasibility of expressing governance obligations as executable, reproducible criteria with deterministic acceptance gates (Muhammad et al., 2026). Second, empirical testing of the cascade of uncertainty using instrumented decision pipelines. The cascade model predicts specific failure propagation patterns — feature incorrectness producing false negatives producing untraceability producing undetectable degradation. These predictions are testable: deliberately introducing controlled data quality failures into an instrumented pipeline and measuring whether the N4 monitoring layer detects the cascade at each stage, and if so, how quickly. Third, privacy-preserving governance evidence. The DES requires capturing detailed decision context, which may include personal data subject to GDPR, CCPA, or similar regulations. A tension exists between governance evidence completeness (capture everything needed for reconstruction) and data minimization (capture only what is necessary for the specified purpose). Techniques such as differential privacy, zero-knowledge proofs, or purpose-limited access controls could reconcile these requirements, but their impact on governance evidence utility is unexplored. 7.5. Limitations of This Analysis 

This paper is a theory-building contribution. The cross-architecture comparison is analytical rather than empirical — it applies governance criteria to architecture types based on their structural properties, not based on measured governance outcomes in deployed systems. The cascade of uncertainty is a theoretical model that generates testable predictions but has not itself been tested. The agentic extensions are proposed as analytical constructs, not validated designs. These limitations are intentional: the contribution is identifying the right questions and the structural constraints that shape possible answers, not providing final answers. 

# 8. Conclusion 

This paper synthesized the operational governance evidence framework developed across four prior studies (Solozobov, 2026b; Solozobov, 2026a; Solozobov, 2026c; Solozobov, 2026e) and analytically assessed its transferability across four decision system architectures. Three findings emerged. First, the framework exhibits a governance coverage gradient. Deterministic rule-based systems satisfy all governance evidence requirements by construction. Classical ML systems require extended trace schemas but remain governable. Hybrid ML+rules systems — the framework’s analytically developed domain — demand seam-aware governance design at the boundary between learned models and business rules. Agentic AI systems, however, expose structural breaks that the current framework cannot address without extension. Second, governance failures propagate through a cascade of uncertainty. Feature incorrectness produces false negatives, which generate epistemically indistinguishable decisions, causing undetectable degradation that accumulates into silent losses. The cascade maps the framework’s four components — diagnostic theory, trace schema, sufficiency measurement, and label-free monitoring — into a serial dependency chain where upstream failures compound into downstream governance collapse. This cascade property means that the framework’s components, while individually sound, may fail collectively in ways that no single component detects. Third, agentic AI systems introduce three structural breaks: decision diffusion (no single decision point to trace), evidence fragmentation (governance artifacts scattered across agent-local contexts), and responsibility ambiguity (accountability cannot be attributed through delegation chains). These breaks require three corresponding framework extensions: inter-agent trace protocols, distributed evidence aggregation, and delegated responsibility attribution models. These extensions identify the structural requirements for agentic governance; the partial recovery of DES-property fillability they enable applies within cooperative, trace-compatible agent ecologies — where all agents can emit structured governance traces — rather than to all commercial deployments where proprietary API boundaries may re-introduce opacity. The extensions are proposed as analytical constructs requiring empirical development. The governance coverage gradient measures by-construction evidence availability, not end-to-end governance adequacy — the framework’s boundary conditions — the opacity–determinism spectrum, counterfactual unavailability, adversarial drift, and proprietary API boundaries — define the limits beyond which even architecturally complete evidence may be insufficient. Within these boundaries, the N4 governance evidence chain provides a structured approach to the question that initiated the entire research programme: when automated decision systems fail, what evidence is needed to reconstruct what happened and why?
