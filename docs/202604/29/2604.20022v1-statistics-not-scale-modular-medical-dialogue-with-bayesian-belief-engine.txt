## Abstract 

Large language models are increasingly deployed as autonomous diagnostic agents, yet they conflate two fundamentally different capabilities: natural-language commu-nication and probabilistic reasoning. We argue that this conflation is an architectural flaw, not an engineering shortcoming. We introduce BMBE (Bayesian Medical Belief Engine), a modular diagnostic dialogue framework that enforces a strict separation between language and reasoning: an LLM serves only as a sensor, parsing patient utterances into structured evidence and verbalising questions, while all diagnostic inference resides in a deterministic, auditable Bayesian engine. Be-cause patient data never enters the LLM, the architecture is private by construction; because the statistical backend is a standalone module, it can be replaced per target population without retraining. This separation yields three properties no autonomous LLM can offer: calibrated selective diagnosis with a continuously adjustable accuracy–coverage tradeoff, a statistical separation gap where even a cheap sensor paired with the engine outperforms a frontier standalone model from the same family at a fraction of the cost, and robustness to adversarial patient communication styles that cause standalone doctors to collapse. We validate across empirical and LLM-generated knowledge bases against frontier LLMs, confirming the advantage is architectural, not informational. 

## 1 Introduction 

Medical dialog is sequential decision-making under uncertainty. At each turn, a clinician must choose what to ask, update a differential diagnosis in light of the answer, and decide whether enough evidence has been gathered to commit, or whether to seek further context. Large language models are increasingly cast in this role, yet they conflate two fundamentally different capabilities: natural-language communication and probabilistic diagnostic reasoning. The result is systems that sound 

like doctors but lack the probabilistic grounding to reason like one: no explicit belief state, no information-theoretic question selection, uncalibrated confidence, and unauditable reasoning chains. We argue that these are not engineering shortcomings to be patched, they are consequences of a flawed architecture that asks a single model to do everything. What if instead, we could build an AI doctor that knows when it does not know , one that either produces a calibrated answer or defers, with a controllable tradeoff between diagnostic accuracy and patient coverage?

This idea is not new in spirit. Classical clinical decision-support systems demonstrated decades ago that structured probabilistic reasoning produces accurate diagnoses ( 1; 2; 3; 4 ; 5). These systems saw limited clinical adoption not because their statistics were wrong, but because of poor integration into clinical workflows, the “Greek Oracle” model that treated clinicians as passive data-entry operators ( 6). Modern LLMs ( 7; 8; 9 ) have greatly improved integration by joining the patient conversation in natural speech. However, in doing so, they have traded epidemiological rigor for fluency: no formal posterior, no principled stopping rules, and no mechanism for calibrated deferral. Recent work attempts to bridge this divide by injecting probabilistic structure into LLM-driven pipelines ( 10 ; 11 ; 12 ; 13 ; 14 ). In every case, however, the LLM still participates in probability estimation, which remains susceptible to hallucination and provides no formal guarantees. An orthogonal line fine-tunes LLMs on clinical data to internalise medical statistics ( 15 ), but this raises concerns about data safety and auditability (no guarantee the model faithfully represents the underlying distributions at inference time). We argue that the right response is not a better hybrid but a strict separation . We introduce BMBE , a modular diagnostic dialogue architecture in which the LLM’s role is confined to two tasks: parsing patient utterances into structured evidence triples and verbalising the questions chosen by an external statistical engine. All diagnostic reasoning, Bayesian belief updates, question selection via expected information gain (EIG) maximisation, stopping rules, and abstention decisions, resides in a belief engine that is fully deterministic, mathematically auditable, and replaceable per target population without retraining the language model. Because patient data never enters the LLM, the architecture is private by construction. Crucially, when the posterior confidence remains below a calibrated threshold, the system abstains rather than guessing, providing a principled, controllable tradeoff between accuracy and coverage. Our main contributions are: • Strict separation of language and reasoning. We propose BMBE, a diagnostic dialogue architecture where the LLM never participates in belief updates or clinical decisions. All inference is deterministic, auditable, and private by construction. The statistical backend can be replaced per target population without retraining. • Controllable selective diagnosis. A single threshold τ traces a continuous accuracy– coverage frontier, from high-throughput triage to safety-critical referral, without retraining. Standalone LLMs have no equivalent mechanism. • Statistical separation gap. Within matched model families, a cheap BMBE sensor outper-forms the corresponding frontier standalone doctor at a fraction of the cost. The advantage persists across adversarial patient personas and grows as the disease space scales. • Architectural validation. A fair-fight experiment on LLM-generated knowledge bases, where BMBE and the standalone doctor share the same clinical knowledge, confirms the advantage is architectural, not informational. 

## 2 Related Work 

Statistical clinical decision support. Early CDSS demonstrated that structured probabilistic reasoning produces clinically useful diagnoses across a range of formalisms. MYCIN ( 1) used certainty-factor rules for antimicrobial therapy, outperforming faculty specialists in blinded evalua-tion ( 16 ). de Dombal et al. (2) showed that even naïve Bayes over abdominal diagnoses surpassed senior clinicians when given structured input. Internist-I/QMR ( 3) scaled heuristic disease-profile scoring to general internal medicine, and DXplain ( 4 ) maintained a disease–finding matrix with modified Bayesian logic over thousands of diseases. Later systems introduced richer representations: Pathfinder ( 5) used full Bayesian belief networks for lymph-node pathology, and CASNET ( 17 )modeled causal pathophysiologic pathways for glaucoma management. Despite strong diagnostic accuracy, adoption remained minimal: all required structured, pre-coded input and treated clinicians as passive data-entry operators, the “Greek Oracle” model ( 6). The bottleneck was interfacing , not reasoning.

In multi-turn dialogue, AMIE ( 9) outperformed primary-care physicians on most evaluation axes via self-play training, DocCHA ( 18 ) decomposes diagnosis into modular subtasks, and Xu et al. However, all these systems share a fundamental limitation: no formal probabilistic state , no explicit posterior, no information-theoretic question selection, no calibrated stopping rules, and no principled abstention. The absence of structured reasoning also creates safety risks: LLMs exhibit poor calibration in clinical settings, with verbalized confidence showing minimal variation between correct and incorrect answers ( 20 ), and high hallucination rates on adversarial clinical vignettes ( 21 ). Their medical knowledge is further skewed toward Western populations, propagating race-based medicine (22; 23). 

Bridging probability and language. These shortcomings have motivated several lines of work that inject probabilistic reasoning into LLM-driven pipelines. On the active gathering side, MediQ ( 14 )shows that naive prompting to ask clarifying questions degrades diagnostic accuracy, and that reliable question-asking requires explicit abstention mechanisms the LLM does not possess by default. BED-LLM ( 10 ) embeds Bayesian Experimental Design for question selection but derives its probabilistic model entirely from the LLM’s own predictive distributions, no external knowledge base. APP ( 13 )combines medical guidelines with entropy-based active learning, yet the LLM still generates candidate questions before scoring. On the hybrid planning side, Sun et al. (24) use an RL-trained planner alongside an LLM parser, and general-purpose frameworks such as DeLLMa ( 11 ) and BIRD ( 12 )integrate decision theory and Bayesian inference respectively into LLM reasoning loops. In all cases, the LLM participates in probability estimation, which compromises mathematical guarantees. An orthogonal line fine-tunes LLMs directly on clinical data to internalize medical statistics ( 15 ), but adversarial attacks can extract training data, and there is no guarantee that learned distributions are faithfully represented at inference time. 

Our position: strict separation. No existing system achieves full separation between language and diagnostic reasoning. We propose that the LLM should touch only language, parsing utterances and verbalizing questions, while all belief updates, question selection, stopping rules, and abstention decisions are handled by an external, inspectable statistical engine. This design makes the inference engine replaceable per population without retraining the LLM, directly addressing the geographic bias problem, since adapting to a new disease prevalence requires only swapping the knowledge base, not re-aligning the model. The engine is fully auditable, and ensures that patient data never enters the language model. ## 3 Method 

We formulate diagnostic dialogue as a sequential Bayesian decision process. Let D = {d1, . . . , d K }

denote the set of K candidate diseases and F = {f1, . . . , f N } the set of N observable clinical features, where each feature fi takes values in a finite set Vfi . At each turn t, the system maintains a posterior belief bt ∈ ∆K−1 over diseases, selects the most informative feature to query, updates its belief from the patient’s response, and decides whether to terminate. The objective is to identify the correct diagnosis with calibrated confidence while minimising the number of questions asked. BMBE decomposes this into a language interface , an LLM that parses patient utterances and ver-balises questions, and a Bayesian reasoning engine that maintains beliefs, selects questions, and renders diagnostic decisions. 3.2 Bayesian inference engine 

Let C = {c1, . . . , c 5} ⊂ (0 , 1] denote the set of confidence weights and ϕ : L → C the mapping from linguistic labels L = {very_likely , likely , uncertain , unlikely , very_unlikely } to numerical weights. At each turn t, the patient’s response about feature ft is parsed into a tuple (vt, c t) ∈ V ft × C .5Evidence mapping. Patient responses often convey partial or hedged information (“I think I had a fever”). Rather than treating all observations as hard evidence, we apply Jeffrey’s conditioning ( 26 ). For unambiguous responses ( c = 1 ), the standard Bayesian update applies: 

bt+1 (d) ∝ bt(d) · P (Xf =v | d) (2) When patients express uncertainty, the effective likelihood mixes with a neutral vector: 

Leff (d) = c · P (Xf =v | D=d) + (1 − c) · 1 (3) At c=1 this recovers Eq. (2) ; at c→0 the evidence has vanishing effect. The mapping ϕ assigns 

ϕ(very_likely ) = 1 .0, ϕ(likely ) = 0 .8, ϕ(uncertain ) = 0 .5, ϕ(unlikely ) = 0 .25 , ϕ(very_unlikely ) = 0.05 ; see Table 4 in Appendix A.1 for trigger examples. The belief state is maintained in log-space:

3.3 Orchestration: ending the consultation 

The system must decide not only what to diagnose but whether to diagnose at all. After each belief update, termination conditions are evaluated in strict priority order. 

Warm-up and stopping. Early posteriors are dominated by the prior rather than evidence: a single shared symptom can cause a high-prevalence disease to spike before discriminative features have been queried. To prevent premature commitment, the system enforces a warm-up period of Tmin 

turns during which no stopping is permitted (default Tmin = 12 ; Appendix A.3). After the warm-up, if max d bt(d) ≥ τ , the system commits to d∗ = arg max d bt(d). A hard budget Tmax (default 20) guarantees termination; if no disease crosses τ by Tmax , the system abstains. 

Controllable selective diagnosis. The threshold τ is a deployment parameter that gives providers explicit control over the accuracy–coverage trade-off. A high τ yields near-perfect accuracy on committed cases at the cost of higher abstention; a low τ maximises coverage. Unlike autonomous LLM doctors, which offer no mechanism to tune this trade-off, BMBE allows deployers to set the operating point that matches their clinical risk tolerance, from high-throughput triage ( τ low, high coverage) to safety-critical referral ( τ high, near-zero error on committed cases). We characterise this trade-off in Section 5.4. 

3.4 Patient-facing interface 

The LLM serves as a pure language layer with no access to the posterior distribution or diagnostic state (prompt templates in Appendix B.1). 6Response parsing. Given a patient utterance ut and the queried feature ft with schema Sft =(Vft , C), the parser produces (vt, c t) ∈ V ft × C via a three-tier cascade. First, deterministic pattern matching maps clear affirmative, negative, and hedging expressions to (v, c ) pairs at zero API cost. When this tier abstains on ambiguous input, an LLM-based extractor classifies the response into the schema’s value set with a confidence label from L, mapped to a numerical weight via ϕ.The key design choice is that the LLM performs structured classification into Vf × L , a small, fixed label set, rather than continuous probability estimation. LLMs exhibit strong ordinal judgement ( 11 )but poor calibration for continuous probabilities ( 27 ); the discrete-to-numerical mapping ϕ isolates language understanding from probabilistic calibration. From the engine’s perspective, each (v, c ) pair is a noisy observation; the LLM is a sensor , not a reasoner. 

Question verbalisation. Given the EIG-selected feature f ∗, the LLM receives its name, an-swer schema Sf ∗ , and a discretised confidence indicator κt ∈ { low , medium , high } derived from 

max d bt(d). This signal modulates conversational tone only, the LLM never receives bt, H(bt), or any other internal engine state. 

## 4 Experimental Setup 

4.1 Datasets DDXPlus (empirical KB). DDXPlus ( 25 ) is a large-scale synthetic dataset of ∼1.3M patient records spanning 49 diseases and 314 features (binary and categorical). We aggregate co-occurrence counts from the training split to construct likelihood tables and set priors proportional to disease frequency. We hold out 50 patients stratified by disease for evaluation. The full conversion pipeline (evidence-type handling, multi-choice expansion, absent-filling, count normalisation) is detailed in Appendix B.3. 

LLM-generated KB. To isolate architectural from informational advantages, we construct a KB whose likelihoods are elicited entirely from GPT-5.4 rather than derived from patient records. This puts BMBE and the standalone doctor on equal informational footing: both reason from the same source of clinical knowledge (the LLM’s internal beliefs), differing only in how that knowledge is deployed. Construction follows a two-stage prompting pipeline, feature generation then distribution estimation, with structured JSON output, deduplication, and chunk-level validation; prompts are in Appendix B.1, the full procedure and configuration parameters in Appendix B.3. 

4.2 Patient simulation 

Evaluating a diagnostic dialogue system requires simulated patients that respond realistically to follow-up questions. Several frameworks address this challenge, from multi-facility settings ( 28 ; 29 )to diagnosis-focused simulators ( 30 ; 31 ). Drawing on the persona taxonomy of PatientSim ( 30 ), we parameterise patient behaviour along axes of personality, language proficiency, recall accuracy, and cognitive state to test robustness across diverse communication styles (details in Appendix B.4). Main experiments use a cooperative persona; adversarial persona results are in Appendix C.7. Each simulated patient receives a ground-truth clinical profile and responds accordingly; the simulator LLM never accesses the engine’s internal state or KB.

We compare two system types, selecting models in different spans (full specifications in Appendix B.5, Table 7). 

BMBE variants. Each variant pairs the Bayesian engine with a different LLM sensor for parsing and verbalisation. Since the LLM serves only as a sensor, extracting structured features from free text, medical-domain specialisation is unnecessary; general instruction-following suffices. ## 5 Results 

5.1 Main comparison 

Table 2 compares six BMBE sensor variants against six frontier standalone LLM doctors on DDXPlus. Every BMBE variant outperforms every standalone doctor on DHS. The best sensor achieves the highest DHS at a fraction of the per-token cost of the best standalone doctor. The advantage is architectural: the gap remains consistent across all six sensor backends, confirming it is not merely a matter of model quality. 

5.2 LLM-generated knowledge base 

A natural objection is that BMBE’s advantage stems from access to data-derived statistics unavailable to the standalone doctor. To control for this, we replace the empirical DDXPlus KB with ones generated entirely by LLMs (Section 4.1): one from GPT-5.4 and one from Gemini 3.1. In each ecosystem, BMBE and the standalone doctors operate from the same source of clinical knowledge, the generating model’s own internal beliefs, so any remaining performance gap is purely architectural. Table 3 reports both ecosystems. In both ecosystems, BMBE with an inexpensive sensor matches or approaches the frontier standalone doctor on top-1 accuracy despite costing 10–25 × less per token (Table 3). The advantage is most pronounced on rare diseases, where standalone LLMs struggle with low-prevalence conditions while the Bayesian engine leverages the KB equally regardless of prevalence. Why strict separation works. The central finding of this work is that confining the LLM to a sensor role, parsing and verbalising, nothing more, yields a system that outperforms frontier LLMs acting autonomously. The answer lies in the nature of diagnostic reasoning. Diagnosis is fundamentally a probabilistic inference problem: updating beliefs over diseases given sequential evidence, and deciding when enough evidence has accumulated. LLMs are not designed for this; they lack persistent belief states, cannot compute information-theoretic quantities, and produce uncalibrated confidence. By offloading these tasks to a Bayesian engine, the system gains exact posterior tracking, principled question selection, and calibrated abstention, capabilities that no amount of prompting or scaling can replicate. 

The LLM-generated KB experiment. The “fair fight” experiment (Section 5.2) provides direct evidence for the architectural thesis. When both BMBE and the standalone doctor operate from the 

same LLM-generated clinical knowledge, the only difference is how that knowledge is deployed: as a structured knowledge base with Bayesian inference, or as implicit knowledge accessed through free-form generation. BMBE matches or exceeds the frontier model in both ecosystems, confirming that the advantage is architectural. 

The statistical separation gap. Within matched model families, replacing a frontier standalone doctor with a cheap BMBE sensor consistently improves DHS while reducing cost (Figure 1b). We call this the statistical separation gap : the quality gain that comes from offloading diagnostic reasoning to a proper Bayesian engine. The gap persists across all three model families tested, confirming it is architectural rather than model-specific. From a deployment perspective, this means practitioners can achieve better diagnostic quality at lower API cost by pairing an inexpensive sensor with the Bayesian engine, rather than scaling up to a more expensive standalone model. 

Operating point control. Unlike standalone LLMs, which produce a single take-it-or-leave-it diagnosis, BMBE offers a continuous accuracy-coverage frontier controlled by the threshold τ

(Section 5.4). A deployer targeting high-throughput triage sets τ low (commit on most patients, accept some errors); a safety-critical referral system sets τ high (commit only when confident, abstain otherwise). No retraining is required to shift between these modes. Standalone LLMs have no equivalent mechanism: they either diagnose or abstain based on internal, uncalibrated confidence that cannot be tuned post-deployment. 

Robustness and scaling. The Bayesian backbone provides natural robustness along multiple axes. Across patient personas (Appendix C.7), BMBE maintains stable DHS even under adversarial commu-nication styles (distrustful, dazed), while standalone doctors collapse. Jeffrey’s conditioning (Eq. 3) provides a principled mechanism: uncertain or hedged responses receive lower confidence weights, limiting their impact on the posterior. Across the disease prevalence spectrum (Appendix C.8), BMBE outperforms standalone doctors on both rare and common diseases. As the candidate disease space grows (Appendix C.10), standalone LLMs plateau while BMBE degrades gracefully, because the engine’s complexity scales with the KB structure rather than with the LLM’s ability to juggle candidates in context. This suggests the architectural advantage will only increase as systems move toward real-world deployment with larger disease spaces. 

Limitations. Our evaluation has several limitations. First, BMBE operates under a closed-world assumption : it can only diagnose diseases present in the knowledge base. In practice, patients may present with conditions outside the KB entirely. Standalone LLMs, by contrast, can draw on open-ended medical knowledge and are not bound to a fixed candidate set. This makes KB coverage a critical deployment concern, though the system’s abstention mechanism provides a natural safety valve when no candidate reaches sufficient confidence, and the LLM-generated KB experiment (Section 5.2) shows that constructing a KB does not require clinical training data. Second, we rely on simulated patients rather than real clinical interactions. While the simulator provides diverse personas, it cannot capture the full complexity of real patient communication. Third, the current architecture cannot capture volunteered evidence that falls outside the asked question, a limitation that standalone LLMs handle naturally by reading the full conversation holistically. 11 Broader impact. BMBE is designed for clinical decision support , not autonomous diagnosis. The system’s selective abstention mechanism ensures it declines rather than guesses when uncertain, and the full decision trace (prior, evidence, posterior at each turn) is available for clinician review. The privacy-by-construction property, patient utterances are parsed into structured triples; raw text is never stored or used for belief computation, addresses a key concern in medical AI deployment. We note that the framework is KB-agnostic: deploying it in a new clinical population requires only a local knowledge base, not retraining the LLM, making it more practical for low-resource settings where clinical data may be available but compute infrastructure for LLM fine-tuning is not. 

## 7 Conclusion 

We presented BMBE, a diagnostic dialogue framework built on a strict separation principle: language models handle communication, while a Bayesian engine handles reasoning. The architecture is deterministic, auditable, and private by construction; its statistical backend is a replaceable module that can be swapped per target population without retraining. Calibrated selective diagnosis provides a controllable accuracy–coverage tradeoff, enabling deployers to set the operating point that matches their clinical risk tolerance. Evaluation across two knowledge-base modalities, empirical and LLM-generated, confirms that the advantage over autonomous LLM doctors is architectural rather than informational, and that it grows with diagnostic complexity. Our results suggest a broader principle for medical AI: rather than scaling language models and hoping they learn to reason probabilistically, we should build systems that separate what LLMs are good at , language, from what they are not :calibrated inference under uncertainty.

A.1 Jeffrey’s conditioning 

Standard Bayesian conditioning assumes evidence is observed with certainty. In conversational diagnosis, patient responses carry intrinsic uncertainty: “I think I had a fever” is not equivalent to a confirmed temperature reading. Jeffrey’s conditioning ( 26 ) generalises Bayesian updating to 

uncertain evidence by specifying a probability distribution over the evidence partition rather than a point observation. Formally, the confidence weight c induces a soft partition: P new (Xi=v) = c and P new (Xi̸ =v) = 1 −c.The resulting update (Eq. 3 in main text) mixes the standard likelihood with a uniform vector, dampening the evidence proportional to the parser’s extracted confidence. At c = 1 this recovers standard Bayes; at c = 0 the posterior is unchanged. 

Why ordinal labels? The five confidence labels ( very_likely through very_unlikely ) provide a coarse but principled discretisation of patient certainty. This avoids requiring the LLM to output calibrated continuous probabilities. The fixed mapping to numerical weights ( c ∈{1.0, 0.80 , 0.50 , 0.25 , 0.05 }) is auditable and eliminates a potential source of unchecked model influence on diagnostic outcomes. Effect of Jeffrey’s conditioning. In practice, evidence is soft: the effective likelihood Leff (d) =

Budget choice. B.1 Prompt templates 

All prompts use temperature 0.0 for deterministic output. The LLM never receives posterior values, entropy, or any internal engine state. 

Parsing prompt. The parsing prompt receives the patient utterance, the feature under inquiry, and the allowed schema values. A deterministic cascade (pattern matching for binary yes/no, emphatic affirmations, numeric ranges, and direct schema substring matches) is applied before the LLM call; the LLM is invoked only as a fallback for ambiguous utterances. The prompt enforces 13 confidence-assignment rules (e.g., prefer unknown over hard negatives when the response is partial or vague) to ensure conservative, faithful extraction. 

Parsing Prompt 

System: You are a medical data extractor. User Response: "{utterance}" Feature to Extract: "{feature}" (Allowed: {schema}) TASK: 1. Classify the user response into one of the Allowed Values. 2. Assess confidence: very_likely, likely, uncertain, unlikely, very_unlikely KEY RULES: – Direct answer ("Yes", "Not really"): map to closest value. – Unrelated response: return "unknown|likely". – Uncertain language ("I think so", "maybe"): use "uncertain". – Prefer "unknown" over hard negative when partial/vague. Return format: "value|confidence_level" Examples: "high|likely", "yes|uncertain", "no|very_likely" 

Verbaliser prompt. The verbaliser receives the feature name, answer schema, and a coarse system-confidence indicator derived from max d bt(d). It modulates conversational tone only; no internal state is exposed. 

Verbaliser Prompt 

System: You are a professional medical diagnostic assistant. Task: Ask the patient a question to check for: {feature}. CONTEXT: – Feature Name: {feature} – Expected Details: {schema} – System Confidence: {low|medium|high} CONSTRAINTS: 1. NEVER use technical IDs (e.g., "f_fever", "d_flu"). 2. Speak naturally and empathetically. 3. Do NOT mention probabilities or internal values. 4. If clarifying a previous confusion, keep it brief. 

Bulk intake prompt. At session start, a single bulk intake call maps the patient’s opening narrative to multiple (f, v, c ) triples simultaneously, reducing the number of follow-up questions needed.

B.2 Standalone doctor protocol 

Algorithm 2 summarises the standalone doctor inference loop. The standalone doctor outputs a comma-separated differential ( [DDX] ) or explicitly abstains ([ABSTAIN] ). The prompt requests the top 5 diagnoses, ranked by likelihood. Crucially, the doctor generates free-text disease names without access to the KB disease list , mirroring realistic deployment where a physician names conditions from memory. 

Post-hoc disease name normalisation. Because free-text predictions (e.g., “Acute exacerbation of COPD”) may differ lexically from KB entries (e.g., “Acute COPD exacerbation / infection”), a post-hoc matching step ( MatchToKB ) aligns predictions to the canonical KB disease names. A lightweight LLM (GPT-5.4-nano) receives the predicted names alongside the full KB disease list and maps each prediction to the closest match or NO_MATCH . Unmatched predictions are retained and counted as errors during evaluation, ensuring that predicting a disease outside the KB scope incurs a penalty. In our experiments, we observed no systematic matching failures: the LLM-based matcher reliably aligned free-text predictions to KB entries across all standalone doctors.

C.9 Failure mode analysis 

We classify every committed misdiagnosis (confidence ≥ τ ∗, top-1 incorrect) along three independent axes. A case may be tagged with multiple failure modes simultaneously. 1. KB Failure. We run an oracle test: all ground-truth features are supplied at confidence c=1 .0.We then measure the posterior gap between the oracle’s top-1 and top-2 diseases. If this gap falls below a threshold γ (γ=0 .80 ), the KB cannot reliably discriminate the disease pair. 2. LLM Failure. The LLM pipeline (verbaliser + patient simulator + parser) injected incorrect evidence into the engine. Two subtypes: • False Positive (FP) : the engine asks about a feature absent from the patient’s ground-truth profile; the pipeline returns yes . If more than 2 such turns occur in a session, the case is flagged. The threshold reflects the empirical separation between successful sessions (mean 1.3 FP) and failure sessions (mean 3.5 FP). • Wrong Evidence (WE) : the engine asks about a feature present in the ground-truth profile, but the extracted value contradicts the ground truth. Any occurrence triggers the flag. This taxonomy is conservative : it captures only detectable errors. Cases where the parser maps an uncertain response to no for an absent feature are not flagged, nor are confidence miscalibrations. 3. Inference Failure. The KB is adequate and the evidence pipeline introduced no detectable errors, yet the engine converged to the wrong diagnosis. Two subtypes: • Close : the ground truth remains in the top-3 posterior at session end, but the question budget or EIG policy did not resolve the differential. • Diverged : the ground truth is not in the top-3. The engine committed confidently to a wrong hypothesis.
