## Abstract 

Parent-AI collaboration to support real-time conversations with children is challenging due to the sensitivity and open-ended na-ture of such interactions. Existing systems often simplify collab-oration into static modes, providing limited support for adapting AI to continuously evolving conversational contexts. To address this gap, we systematically investigate the dynamics of parent-AI collaboration modes in real-time conversations with children. ## 1 Introduction 

Parent-AI collaboration in real-time conversational settings with children presents unique challenges. Unlike task-oriented interac-tions with predefined goals and structures, these settings involve sensitive and open-ended exchanges in which conversational con-texts can shift rapidly within a single interaction [ 27 , 69 , 79 , 86 ]. As a result, parents’ needs for AI support vary dynamically—for example, they may seek idea generation when fatigued [ 23 ], but expect emotional mediation in more sensitive moments [ 105 ]. This variability requires parents to continuously decide when and how AI should participate, making it difficult to predefine stable collab-oration strategies. However, existing AI systems for conversational support typi-cally rely on a limited set of static collaboration modes, such as gen-erating prompts or acting as a conversational agent [ 51 , 70 , 100 , 126 ]. While these approaches demonstrate the potential of AI to assist in conversations, they assume that the set of collaboration modes can be predefined for the entire interaction, providing limited support for adapting AI to continuously evolving conversational contexts. Despite the growing interest in AI-supported parenting tools, lit-tle is known about how parents’ preferred collaboration modes with AI emerge and evolve during ongoing conversations. To address this gap, we investigate how parents configure AI involvement in minimally constrained, real-time interactions. We introduce the concept of collaboration modes to describe how parents configure and engage AI scaffolding functions during interactions. We therefore explore the following research questions: 

• RQ1 : What modes of AI collaboration do parents prefer in parent-child conversations? 

• RQ2 : How do contextual factors influence parents’ pre-ferred modes of AI collaboration in parent-child conversa-tions? 

• RQ3 : What strategies do parents use to engage with AI support during parent-child conversations? To answer these questions, we conducted a co-design study with eight parents to elicit parental needs for AI support in parent-child conversations. The study revealed six frequently mentioned functional needs (F1-F6) and two key requirements for collaborating with AI: flexible decision authority and minimal mode-switching costs. Based on these findings, we developed COMPASS, a co llaborative m ediation and pa rental s caffolding s ystem, as a research probe to explore parent-AI collaboration in conversation. COMPASS allows parents to flexibly combine parental support functions during con-versations, enabling us to examine how their strategies adapt to shifting collaboration needs. Using COMPASS as a research probe, we conducted a lab-based study with 21 parent-child pairs in semi-structured conversational tasks. We found that parents developed preferred collaboration modes with AI, which moved towards a more partner-like collabo-ration mode and converged into recurring patterns (RQ1). Parents adjusted these modes systematically based on emotional intensity, emotion type, conversation stage, parental goals, and conversa-tion scale and structure (RQ2). Additionally, parents adopted dis-tinct strategies to engage with AI support across three dimensions: parent-oriented, child-oriented, and relationship-oriented (RQ3). Our study contributes to HCI in four ways. First, we provide em-pirical insights into the dynamic nature of parent-AI collaboration, showing how collaboration modes evolve over time, vary with con-textual factors, and are enacted through parental strategies. Second, we extend Parental Goal Theory by grounding its three established categories in our empirical findings of AI-assisted parenting. Third, we advance the understanding of human-AI collaboration in rela-tional, high-stakes domains. Finally, we offer design implications for parental support systems that enable flexible, context-adaptive human-AI collaboration. 

## 2 Related Work 2.1 Parent–Child Conversations in HCI 

Parent–child communication is critical to early childhood develop-ment, supporting children’s cognitive, social, and emotional growth through everyday interactions [ 10 , 36 , 40 , 44 , 76 , 108 ]. As children’s first social environment, families position parents as primary agents of socialization. In these interactions, parents continuously guide, respond to, and adapt to children’s needs, shaping the quality and outcomes of communication. Prior HCI research has explored supporting parent–child con-versations from multiple perspectives [ 121 ], including (1) content provisioning (e.g., prompts or games) [ 11 ]; (2) process guidance that analyzes conversation dynamics and provides feedback (e.g., tone or emotional cues) [ 19 , 23 ]; and (3) third-party mediators (e.g., social robots) that restructure interaction [ 50 , 51 , 95 ]. While these approaches provide valuable support, they largely assume stable interaction patterns, limiting adaptation to dynamically evolving conversational needs. In practice, parent-child conversations are highly dynamic and context-sensitive. Parents often balance multiple, shifting goals— such as guidance, protection, reciprocity, and control—while re-sponding to children’s changing cognitive, emotional, and atten-tional states [ 22 , 37 , 41 , 59 , 60 , 79 , 88 ]. This requires parents to continuously adjust how they engage in conversations. Prior work has explored parent–AI collaboration in conversa-tional settings [ 52 , 126 ]. However, how such collaboration evolves in response to changing goals and contexts has not been system-atically examined. Our work addresses this gap by investigating the dynamics of parent–AI collaboration in real-time conversations and showing how these dynamics can inform the design of adaptive AI support for parent–child communication. 

## 2.2 AI Systems for Parent-Child Interaction 

Prior AI systems for parent-child education typically predefine parent-AI roles before interaction. Existing work identifies three col-laboration modes. In the parent-led, AI-assisted mode, AI supports parents’ emotional guidance, story co-creation, and content selec-tion through analysis, generation, and feedback [ 57 , 98 , 119 , 127 ]. In Adapting AI to the Moment 

the AI-led, parent-assisted mode, AI acts as the primary interactive agent while parents supervise or intervene when needed [ 63 , 123 ]. A third mode emphasizes joint collaboration , positioning AI as a conversational partner in multi-turn interactions [96, 106]. While these systems explore different role allocations, they pri-marily focus on static collaboration structures [ 68 , 112 ], with less attention to how parents can adapt AI involvement during ongoing interactions. In practice, parent-child conversations are highly dy-namic, with emotions, goals, and engagement shifting in real time [6 , 79 ]. Supporting parents in responding to these changes requires more in situ control over AI behavior. Recent work has begun to explore more flexible forms of parental participation. For example, parents express a desire to adjust AI content generation, interaction depth, and collaboration structures according to contextual factors such as children’s learning stages, time availability, and energy levels [ 50 –52 , 126 ]. Other studies intro-duce configurable parental control tools that support differentiated permissions, interaction modes, and intervention intensity across developmental stages [29, 120]. However, these approaches largely assume relatively static col-laboration modes throughout the interaction, offering limited sup-port for adapting AI behavior to rapidly changing conversations. In addition, prior work provides limited theoretical grounding for understanding how and why parents adjust collaboration with AI in real time. Parental Goal Theory [ 46 ] suggests that parents simulta-neously pursue parent-, child-, and relationship-oriented goals that shift during interaction. Building on this perspective, we extend the theory to explain how parents regulate collaboration with AI under changing contextual factors. 

## 2.3 Collaboration Dynamics in Human-AI Interaction 

HCI research has long examined how humans and AI share roles in collaboration. Prior work often frames AI through static roles. As a tool or assistant, AI improves efficiency while humans retain primary control [ 67 , 109 , 117 ]. As a coach or mentor, AI provides guidance without taking over decisions [ 1, 35 ]. As a teammate or partner, AI collaborates with users toward shared goals through complementary capabilities [ 7, 91 , 122 ]. However, these roles are typically fixed at design time, limiting collaboration to stage-based arrangements and overlooking interaction dynamics. Recent work instead frames collaboration as “agency negotia-tion”, where initiative and control shift with task stages and contex-tual factors [ 73 ]. Adjustable autonomy has been shown to improve perceived usefulness, competence, and satisfaction [ 53 , 124 ], and to enhance team performance when AI adapts during collaboration [ 91 ]. While prior work has begun to highlight interaction-level dynamics, most systems examine these dynamics at coarse-grained task units (e.g., storytelling or search sessions) [97, 126], with lim-ited work systematically capturing fine-grained interaction changes or validating them through system design. Recent systems explore finer-grained collaboration mechanisms, including proactive intervention timing [ 61 , 85 , 129 ], proactive task collaboration [ 77 , 89 ], shared representations for alignment [55, 125], and role fluidity at the micro-task level [71, 116, 128]. However, these studies primarily address general task collabo-ration and rarely examine parent-child communication, a setting characterized by multiple concurrent goals [ 59 , 60 ], strong emo-tional fluctuations [ 22 ], and asymmetric roles [ 34 ]. Moreover, they lack a theoretical account explaining how users select collaboration modes under such conditions. Building on this literature, we examine collaboration dynamics in emotionally sensitive parent–child conversations, tracing how parents adjust control and support in real time and extending prior frameworks from macro-level autonomy to micro-level interaction. 

## 3 Formative Study 

To understand parents’ needs and preferred modes of collaborating with AI in parent–child conversations, we conducted a formative co-design study to inform the design of our research probe. 

## 3.1 Setup 

3.1.1 Protocol. The study consisted of a pre-task and a co-design session. Before the session, parents engaged in a 10-minute con-versation with their child about daily challenges (e.g., peer conflict, teacher criticism), audio-recorded the interaction, and shared it with the research team. During the co-design session, parents reviewed their record-ings with two HCI researchers and identified moments where AI support could be helpful using an online whiteboard (Figure 2). Conversations were structured into four phases (before, during, af-ter, and throughout), and parents annotated desired AI support for each phase. They then organized, prioritized, and combined these functions, and discussed feature-switching preferences and usage requirements. Participants were encouraged to think aloud, and facilitators used follow-up questions to elicit detailed reflections.

This age group was selected because children in the early primary school years still rely on parental guidance [ 38 , 101 ] while developing conversational abilities that allow them to engage in meaningful conversations [3, 75]. ## 3.2 Findings 

3.2.1 Scaffolding Functions Aligned with Parental Goals. From the co-design sessions, we identified six frequently requested AI scaf-folding functions (F1-F6), which we group into two categories based on Parental Goal Theory [24]. 

Parent-focused goals include Scenario Provision (F1), Utter-ance Suggestion (F2), and Reflective Feedback (F3), supporting par-ents in framing topics, expressing themselves, and reflecting on their strategies. Parents preferred contextualized storytelling over fixed scripts (F1; P6:“ making it easier for children to understand and accept ”), concise and directly usable utterances at critical moments (F2; P8:“ could read aloud directly ”), and non-judgmental, heuristic feedback (F3; P2:“ I need it to inspire me on how to think ”). 

Relationship-focused goals include Conversation Summariza-tion (F4), Agent-as-Speaker (F5), and Multimodal Enrichment (F6), supporting mutual understanding and engagement. Parents ex-pected real-time summaries to track emotions and perspectives (F4; P1:“ so that everyone can understand each other ”), occasional AI participation as a lightweight third party when conversations stalled (F5; P4:“ third-party friend ”), and multimodal content (e.g., images) to help children understand the current conversation and sustain engagement (F6; P5:“ he will be more willing to continue the conversation ”). Child-focused goals were excluded, as our study focused on parent-centered collaboration needs in conversations. Details of the six functions (F1–F6), including their purposes and use contexts, are provided in Appendix A. 

3.2.2 Flexible Function Combination with Retained Decision Au-thority. In the co-design sessions, parents emphasized the need to dynamically adapt scaffolding functions to rapidly changing conversational contexts. Preferences varied. Some parents preferred full manual control, advocating “ free combination ” to maintain flexibility and natural interaction: “ The freer, the better... the more fixed its feedback format is, the less natural it feels. ” (P4). Some parents favored preconfig-ured function combinations to reduce effort: “ I think fixed system functions would be better, so I don’t have to switch frequently. ” (P7) Most parents preferred a hybrid approach, where AI suggests an initial set of combinations while leaving final control to parents. P6 noted, “ You can offer suggestions for them to adopt ,” and P8 suggested “it could prompt whether I need certain functions and I can just select ‘yes’. ”Across these views, parents consistently stressed retaining de-cision authority in choosing functions (P4: “ Different parents may have different needs ”), as needs vary across situations and conversa-tions are inherently unpredictable (P8). 

3.2.3 Minimizing Interactional Costs of Collaboration Mode Switch-ing. Parents noted that frequent manual switching could disrupt conversational flow, suggesting a preference for minimally intru-sive interaction (P7: “ when talking to children...keeping a fixed mode might be better ”).

## 4 COMPASS Design and Implementation 

Building on insights from the formative study, we designed and implemented COMPASS as a research probe to support flexible parental scaffolding in parent–child conversations. Rather than functioning as a full intervention system, COMPASS is intended to investigate how parents’ collaboration needs and behaviors with AI evolve during such conversations. To preserve natural decision-making, we minimized system-induced bias that could influence parents’ collaboration choices. To support analysis of parent-AI collaboration dynamics, we incorporated an annotation interface to capture in-situ reflections and strategies. 

## 4.1 User Interface Design 

The COMPASS interface consists of two components: Parent–Child Conversation and Parent Annotation (Figure 1). 

4.1.1 Parent-Child Conversation Interface. This interface helps par-ents conduct topic-based conversations with their children. Based on the findings from the formative study, we derived a set of design requirements (Table 1). To address the identified functional needs (F1–F6), COMPASS includes six modules: Scenario Provision , Utterance Suggestion , Re-flective Feedback , Conversation Summarization , Agent-as-Speaker ,and Multimodal Enrichment . Each module provides contextual sup-port during the conversation. Before the conversation, parents configure an initial set of func-tions (a1 in Figure 1). They can describe their communication needs in natural language, based on which COMPASS suggests a set of functions via LLM-based reasoning. Parents can also manually ad-just the collaboration mode. During the parent–child conversation (a2 in Figure 1), COMPASS provides support through the enabled modules. Parents can enable or disable modules at any time, enabling flexible adaptation to evolv-ing interactions. To balance responsiveness and minimal disruption, Adapting AI to the Moment

## 4.2 COMPASS Workflow 

We describe the workflow of COMPASS as a research probe sup-porting parent–child conversations (Figure 3). During the parent-child conversation, the Audio Processor per-forms automatic speech recognition (ASR) and speaker diarization on the live audio stream. The resulting transcripts, annotated with speaker labels, are passed to the Conversation Monitor , which main-tains a sliding window of recent utterances. Meanwhile, parents can enable or disable functions (F1-F6) through the Parent–Child Conversation interface, which updates the Function State Manager in real time. Parents may select a predefined topic from the Scenario Manager to initiate a conversation. The scenario set includes eight situations identified in the formative study as challenging for children aged 6-8 (e.g., peer conflict). The current context, including recent utterances, function states, and the selected scenario, is passed to the Function Library to gen-erate context-aware support for the parent. The Function Library 

contains six modules (Section 3.2.1) that operate independently. These modules leverage LLMs, text-to-speech, and text-to-image models to provide multi-channel support. Implementation details are provided in the Appendix B. 

## 5 User Study 

We conducted a lab-based study with 21 parent-child pairs using COMPASS as a research probe to examine (1) parents’ preferred AI scaffolding modes, (2) how conversational contextual factors shape these preferences, and (3) strategies parents use to engage with AI during parent–child conversations. The study was IRB-approved, and informed consent/assent was obtained from all participants. 

## 5.1 Participants 

We recruited 21 parent-child pairs via social media. Each pair re-ceived $20 or equivalent compensation. Parents (P1-21) included 4 fathers and 17 mothers ( 𝑀 = 39 .05 , 𝑆𝐷 = 4.02 ). Children (C1-21) included 11 boys and 10 girls aged 6-8 ( 𝑀 = 7.00 , 𝑆𝐷 = 0.76 ); the rationale for this age group is provided in Section 3.1.2. Detailed demographics are provided in Appendix C. All sessions were recorded (video, audio, screen) with anonymiza-tion. Throughout the study, system outputs were monitored re-motely to prevent harmful content. Mei et al. 

## 5.2 Procedure 

Each parent-child pair participated in a continuous two-hour lab session using a touchscreen laptop. 

Introduction (25 min). Parents were introduced to COMPASS and its six modular functions, followed by a 10-minute free con-versation to familiarize themselves with the system. They were encouraged to explore different feature combinations and collabo-ration strategies. 

Parent–child conversation (20 min). Parents and children dis-cussed one of two randomly assigned challenging topics (e.g., peer conflict, teacher criticism), selected based on formative interviews with five parents. Parents interacted with the system while guiding their children. 

Co-annotation (45 min). Using the Parent Annotation Inter-face, parents and an HCI researcher jointly reviewed conversation segments. Parents recalled interactions via replay, annotated con-textual factors (Table 2), and explained their decisions through researcher probing. Children stayed in a supervised adjacent area during this and the next session. 

Final interview (15 min). We conducted semi-structured inter-views to elicit parents’ overall strategies for engaging with AI. 

## 5.3 Data Analysis 

For quantitative analysis, we pre-processed COMPASS logs and questionnaire data. Numeric features (e.g., turn count, utterance length) were z-standardized. For each conversation slice, collabora-tion modes were encoded as six-digit binary vectors indicating the activation of each function. Questionnaire responses (e.g., stage, emotion type) were one-hot encoded. We then analyzed function usage over time and its correlations with contextual factors. For qualitative analysis, we transcribed co-annotation and in-terview sessions and conducted thematic analysis [ 8, 9 , 104 ]. Four HCI researchers performed iterative coding: initial open coding, collaborative codebook development with one participant (P1), and independent coding with consensus discussions. Codes were then clustered to identify recurring parental strategies and collaboration patterns. All analyses were conducted in Mandarin to preserve original meanings.

## 6.3 Three Types of Strategies Parents Employ to Engage with AI (RQ3) 

Based on interview data, we identify three strategy types guiding parent-AI engagement: parent-oriented , child-oriented , and relationship-oriented . These extend Parental Goal Theory [ 26 , 46 ] to AI-supported parent-child interaction. 

6.3.1 Parent-Oriented Strategies. Parents adopted self-focused strate-gies to (1) compensate for their limitations and (2) regulate their own cognitive and emotional states. 

(1) Compensating for limitations and maintaining conver-sational control. 11 of 21 parents used AI for real-time feedback and information retrieval to compensate for communication lim-itations, with eight reporting that AI helped identify deficiencies and support self-correction. As P11 noted, “ it can point out some parts of my speech that are not quite right... I can adjust my direction accordingly. ” Parents also relied on AI to address knowledge gaps (e.g., explaining “new moon” to children) and to provide psycho-logically appropriate responses. In emotionally sensitive situations, AI’s phrasing suggestions were used to avoid potentially harmful expressions. 9 parents further leveraged AI to improve interaction quality. Seven described using emotional insights to “shift from evalua-tive to empathetic communication” (P18), while six relied on AI’s suggestions to maintain conversational flow when unsure how to initiate a topic (P3). Under high stress or uncertainty, some parents adopted an “all-round reliance” strategy by enabling multiple functions simulta-neously to maintain conversational control. As P4 noted, “ I was worried that after my child expressed something, I would not know how to respond. So I kept Functions 2, 3, and 4 enabled to make sure that after my child spoke, I could immediately pick up her words. ”

(2) Managing parents’ emotional and cognitive states. Par-ents reported using AI to regulate their internal states during con-versations. Three described using reflective prompts to manage agitation and prevent negative emotions (P14: “ parents’ emotions can affect the conversation, and AI can help me with that ”). ## 7 Discussion 7.1 Evolving Dynamics of Parent-AI Collaboration with COMPASS 

Our findings reveal how parent–AI collaboration unfolds over time. We highlight three representative themes that extend prior work and offer new design insights. 

7.1.1 From Backstage Support to Partner-style Co-performance. Par-ents initially relied on backstage support to maintain conversational flow, then gradually granted COMPASS more visible roles (F5, F6), moving toward partner-style collaboration. Extending prior work on flexible role division [ 43 , 51 , 126 ], we identify a risk-perception-driven shift : parents incrementally delegate control to assess AI controllability before incorporating it into the “safe zone” of par-ent–child interaction. This suggests that systems should support progressive trust-building rather than assuming immediate readi-ness for partnership. It extends HCI trust research into parenting contexts [16, 56, 83]. 

7.1.2 From Experimentation to Stable Collaboration Modes. In the user study, only a subset of collaboration modes was consistently used, while others appeared as short-lived transitional states. Be-yond prior work treating collaboration styles as stable preferences [ 50 , 52 ], our findings show that stable and transitional modes co-exist. Transitional modes reflect parents’ experimentation and fa-miliarization with the AI system, echoing exploratory processes in other HCI domains (e.g., exploratory search) [ 58 , 92 ]. This sug-gests that systems should scaffold low-risk experimentation to help families converge on stable strategies suited to their conversational settings. 

7.1.3 Context-Aware Restraint in Sensitive Parenting Moments. Par-ents adapted AI use to context: in calm, cognitively oriented mo-ments, they preferred structured support, whereas in emotionally intense situations, they avoided directive scaffolds and relied on alignment and mediation. While prior HCI work highlights context-aware scaffolding [ 17 , 18 , 84 ], we further identify the need for 

context-aware restraint . AI should not only know when to step in but also hold back in “no-go” moments (e.g., avoiding rigid re-sponses when children are sad). This aligns with “calm technology” [113 , 114 ] and reframes deliberate AI silence as a form of positive intelligence that protects sensitive parent–child interactions. 

## 7.2 From Parental Goal Theory to AI-Engaged Parental Goal Theory 

Parenting goals are typically classified as parent-oriented, child-oriented, and relationship-oriented goals [ 26 , 46 ]. Grounded in our findings, we revisit these categories and show how they are reshaped in AI-engaged parenting contexts. 

7.2.1 Orchestrating AI Support for Parent-Oriented Goals. Prior work shows that parents aim to maintain competence, bridge knowl-edge gaps, and regulate their own states when interacting with children [ 20 , 21 , 47 ]. Our findings support this view, but further show that these goals are increasingly achieved through AI collab-oration rather than solely individual effort. Parents treated AI as a just-in-time collaborator that compensated for gaps in knowledge and experience, supported conversational flow, and reduced un-certainty in stressful situations. When facing high cognitive load or emotional instability, they dynamically adjusted collaboration modes to obtain appropriate support, often drawing on familiar technology-use habits. This suggests a shift from individual reg-ulation to parent-AI collaborative regulation in parent-oriented goals. 

7.2.2 Extending AI-in-situ Feedback to Support Child-Oriented Goals. 

Child-oriented goals are typically framed around long-term cogni-tive and socio-emotional development [ 12 , 25 , 49 , 102 ], and existing AI systems largely follow this orientation by supporting children’s knowledge learning, emotion recognition, and self-expression [ 14 ,15, 102]. Our findings extend this view by showing that child-oriented goals also involve attending to children’s moment-to-moment states 

(e.g., emotional type, emotional intensity, and expressive state), which is underexplored in prior theory and system design. While parents are known to regulate children’s emotions during parent-child interactions [ 31 , 32 ], we find that AI reshapes this process: parents rely on AI to identify and summarize children’s current states, such as emotional type and emotional intensity, reducing the cognitive burden of continuously monitoring children’s states during parent–child conversations. By activating state-reflective AI features, parents gained a clearer understanding of children’s moment-to-moment states, which di-rectly informed their selection of AI collaboration modes. This suggests that AI expands child-oriented goals from a sole focus on long-term development to a combination of developmental goals and moment-to-moment regulation . Future work should examine how AI can support and balance these two aspects without displacing parental judgment. 

7.2.3 AI-Mediated Parent-Child Conversation to Support Relationship-Oriented Goals. Prior work frames relationship-oriented goals as maintaining and enhancing the quality of parent–child relation-ships [ 64 , 65 ]. Our findings extend this view by showing that AI actively reshapes relational dynamics, as it supports interaction continuity, encourages children’s disclosure, and buffers emotional tension. This is particularly important under high emotional load, where parents often struggle with real-time relationship regulation, leading to escalation and conflict [2, 80]. With AI introduced as a new participant in parent–child con-versations, relational maintenance is no longer solely the parent’s responsibility. Parents positioned AI as both a conversational medi-ator and a relational scaffold: as a neutral third party that encour-aged children’s disclosure and helped de-escalate tension, and as a support for alignment and intimacy within the interaction. This transforms relationship-oriented goals from a dyadic par-ent–child structure to a triadic parent–child–AI collaboration , ex-tending the theoretical boundary of relational goals. Moreover, parents’ trust and attitudes toward AI shaped how much relational authority they delegated, requiring a balance between AI participa-tion, parental authority, and children’s autonomy. Together, these findings suggest that AI functions as a collaborative social actor in family interaction, fundamentally reshaping the structure of relationship-oriented goals. Mei et al. 

## 7.3 Towards More Fine-grained and Dynamic Human-AI Collaboration Framework 

Prior research on human-AI collaboration largely focuses on task-driven contexts, where success is defined by performance metrics such as accuracy and efficiency [ 13 , 103 , 111 ]. Such work often assumes that collaboration goals are clearly defined and relatively stable, and that the division of labor between humans and AI can be specified in advance of interaction. Recent studies introduce human-centered factors (e.g., trust, transparency) to collaboration frameworks [ 28 , 107 ], yet often treat these goals in isolation, over-looking how multiple goals interact in practice. Our findings extend this view, showing that parent–AI collaboration in our conversation setting involves navigating multiple, sometimes conflicting goals. In parent-child conversations, parents simultaneously balance three goals: parent-oriented, child-oriented, and relationship-oriented. The priority of these goals shifts across situations, and parents’ on-going trade-offs across these goals lead to more fine-grained and dynamic parent-AI collaboration. This goes beyond prior work, which assumes a stable collaboration mode throughout interaction [51, 126]. In our study, parents switched collaboration modes 7.90 times per conversation on average. With only six functions, they produced 41 of 64 possible combinations, indicating that collaboration modes are not predefined but constructed in situ. Through frequent switching and recombination of AI functions, parents continuously adjust AI’s role, form, and level of involvement in response to conversational progress, the child’s state, and their own judgments. These results show that collaboration modes are emergent, fine-grained, and dynamically reconstructed across time and context. Together, our findings reframe collaboration success from static performance optimization to the ability to manage multi-objective trade-offs through dynamic adaptation. Parenting represents a high-stakes, relational, and asymmetric-agency context [ 42 , 99 , 105 ]. In such contexts, parents must simultaneously assume responsibility while preserving children’s autonomy, which further intensifies the tensions among collaboration goals. Thus, our work also provides a new lens for understanding human-AI collaboration in other high-stakes, relational domains such as healthcare, education, and counseling [5, 93, 118]. 

## 7.4 Design Implications 

Grounded in our findings, we derive design implications for flexible and effective parent–AI collaboration. 

7.4.1 Context-aware Explainability without Disrupting Interaction. 

AI interventions in sensitive contexts can appear intrusive without explanation, undermining parental trust [ 4 , 62 ]. Our results show that parents’ collaboration preferences shift systematically with emotional intensity, conversation stage, and parental goals. Systems should therefore provide context-aware explanations that clarify strategy–context links [ 30 , 110 ] in family communication settings. However, continuous explanations risk cognitive overload and interrupt the smooth flow of parent-child conversation. Explain-ability should be treated not as a constant stream of information, but as an adaptive interactional feature, where timing, modality, and granularity are dynamically adjusted to balance transparency with conversational continuity. 

7.4.2 Distinguishing Exploration from Stable Usage Patterns. Par-ents engaged with the system through an iterative process of trial and adjustment, often experimenting with new functions or revisit-ing previously dismissed ones. This means that system logs do not always reflect stable preferences. Therefore, when modeling user preferences [ 54 , 87 ], systems should distinguish exploration from stable usage patterns. Moreover, rather than treating exploration as noise, it should be recognized as a valuable meta-strategy that reflects how parents probe AI capabilities. Future systems may leverage such patterns to personalize support based on users’ exploration styles. 

7.4.3 Rethinking AI Literacy in Parenting. Collaboration flexibility varied with parents’ AI literacy: in our study, high-literacy parents dynamically reconfigured functions, whereas low-literacy parents tended to rely on fixed collaboration modes. This suggests that parent–AI collaboration systems may impose excessive complexity and cognitive burden on low-literacy parents, highlighting the need for literacy-aware designs that adapt system complexity to parents’ capabilities. However, in parenting contexts, AI literacy extends beyond tech-nical proficiency [ 72 , 81 ] to include relational sensitivity—knowing when and how to involve AI without disrupting the parent–child relationship. At the same time, overly simplified designs may constrain par-ents’ agency and hinder long-term learning. This underscores the importance of building on prior work on fostering AI literacy [ 66 , 82 ], and balancing immediate usability with the gradual devel-opment of parents’ AI literacy. 

## 7.5 Limitations and Future Work 

First, participants were limited to Chinese parents and children, with most parents being mothers, reflecting common caregiving patterns [ 48 , 74 , 94 ]. Future work should examine broader cultural contexts and parental roles, including mother–father differences in AI collaboration under similar structured interactions. Second, the lab-based setting ensured controlled observation of moment-to-moment collaboration shifts but may not reflect everyday home communication routines. Longitudinal in-home de-ployments are needed to understand how parent–AI collaboration evolves in natural family settings. Third, we did not assess children’s outcomes, as COMPASS was designed as a probe to surface parental strategies rather than a full intervention. Our contribution lies in theorizing and understanding parent-AI collaboration. Future research should further investigate how different parent–AI divisions of labor influence children’s communication and development. Finally, COMPASS focused on six common scaffolding functions identified in our formative study, which do not cover the full design space. While this scope is sufficient for our purpose of examining parents’ shifting collaboration needs, future work could extend par-ent–AI collaboration to provide richer, more personalized support. ## 8 Conclusion 

We developed COMPASS, a research probe designed to provide flexible scaffolding for parents during parent-child conversations. Using COMPASS as a probe in a study with 21 parent-child pairs, we find that parent–AI collaboration evolves from backstage support to partner-style co-performance, shifting with contextual factors to balance cognitive and emotional regulation. We identify three parental strategies, namely parent-oriented, child-oriented, and relationship-oriented strategies, extending Parental Goal Theory and informing the design of future parental support systems that enable flexible and effective partnerships.

## A Details of the Six Parent Support Functions 

This section presents the six AI support functions (F1-F6) identified from parents’ requests during the formative study. 

Scenario Provision (F1). Parents preferred that the AI introduce topics through contextualized storytelling or illustrative examples to evoke children’s empathy and understanding, rather than di-rectly providing fixed utterances. The ability of the AI to generate stories naturally aligns with everyday communication and chil-dren’s cognitive development stages, making it “ easier for children to understand and accept ” (P6). 

Utterance Suggestion (F2). Seven out of nine parents expected the AI to provide concise, contextually appropriate utterance sug-gestions that they “ could read aloud directly ” (P8) at critical moments to help them overcome difficulties in phrasing. This highlights par-ents’ need for immediacy and contextual adaptability rather than reliance on generic templates. 

Reflective Feedback (F3). Some parents emphasized their pref-erence for heuristic rather than judgmental feedback: “ I don’t want the AI to criticize me in a harsh tone... I need it to inspire me on how to think ” (P2). Parents were more inclined to view the AI as a gentle thinking partner rather than an authoritative instructor, and therefore, the reflection function should focus on guidance and empathy. 

Conversation Summarization (F4). Six parents reported that they often found it difficult to track their children’s emotions and perspectives, and thus hoped that the AI could summarize both parties’ positions and feelings in real time. One parent expressed a desire for the AI to analyze family members’ thoughts and reason-ing, “ so that everyone can understand each other, and I can respond to my child better ” (P1). This reflects parents’ expectations for the AI to function as an information filter and emotional mediator, helping maintain smooth conversation and promote understanding. 

Agent-as-Speaker (F5). Although most parents preferred the AI to act as an assistant, some welcomed its brief involvement as a “ third-party friend ” or “ story character ” when conversations stalled (P4). The premise was that “ the AI should be a good listener ”, intervening naturally and gently without interrupting parent–child interaction (P2). 

Multimodal Enrichment (F6). Some parents noted that chil-dren aged 6-8 have difficulty understanding abstract vocabulary, and therefore suggested that the AI provide multimodal feedback such as picture book illustrations, enabling children to “ be more will-ing to continue the conversation ” (P5). This reflects parents’ desire for the system to reduce comprehension burden through concrete and vivid forms while maintaining engagement in the conversation. 

## B Implementation of the COMPASS Research Probe 

We implemented the main system workflow on a Flask server, while the frontend interface is built in Vue and communicates with the server via WebSocket. The frontend runs on a touchscreen-enabled laptop to provide an intuitive and interactive interface for parents. For the Audio Processor , we utilized the TINGWU API, with audio sliced and compressed on the frontend prior to transcription. We also employed GPT-4o-mini to map each diarized speaker index to the corresponding role (parent or child). For all six functions in the Function Library, we leveraged the GPT-4o API to balance speed and output quality. For the Multimodal Enrichment Module ,we employed the Stable Diffusion XL (SDXL) model due to speed considerations, whereas for the Agent-as-Speaker Module , we used Alibaba Cloud’s streaming synthesis API configured with a gentle female voice profile.
