Abstract 

Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown remarkable capabilities across various tasks but remain prone to hallucinations in knowledge-intensive scenarios. Knowledge Base Question Answering (KBQA) mitigates this by ground-ing generation in Knowledge Graphs (KGs). However, most multi-hop KBQA methods rely on explicit edge traversal, making them frag-ile to KG incompleteness. In this paper, we proposed a novel graph-based soft prompting framework that shifts the reasoning paradigm from node-level path traversal to subgraph-level reasoning. Specifically, we employ a Graph Neural Network (GNN) to encode ex-tracted structural subgraphs into soft prompts, enabling LLM to reason over richer structural context and identify relevant entities beyond immediate graph neighbors, thereby reducing sensitivity to missing edges. Furthermore, we introduce a two-stage paradigm that reduces computational cost while preserving good per-formance: a lightweight LLM first leverages the soft prompts to identify question-relevant entities and relations, followed by a more pow-erful LLM for evidence-aware answer gener-ation. Experiments on four multi-hop KBQA benchmarks show that our approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on three of them, demonstrating its effectiveness. 1

KBQA frameworks improve answer reli-ability by explicitly retrieving and reasoning over relevant entities and relations on KGs, enabling more accurate and evidence-grounded responses (Song et al., 2023). Addressing complex questions often requires multi-hop reasoning, as the answer nodes are typ-ically distant from the starting topic nodes in the Knowledge Graph. To bridge this gap, repre-sentative methods start from topic entities and progressively expand along KG edges under the guidance of LLMs (Sun et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024). Building on this paradigm, subsequent works further make some improvements by en-hancing search efficiency via Monte Carlo Tree Search (Luo et al., 2025) or by strengthening rea-soning coherence through explicit memory of ex-plored paths (Jiang et al., 2025; Chen et al., 2024). paradigm suffers from an inherent limitation: they are strictly confined to explicit KG edges. In practice, knowledge graphs are often incomplete, making the reasoning process highly sensitive to missing relations, which can easily disrupt multi-hop inference. As illustrated in the Figure 1, answering “ What language do the people in the area where the newspaper Knews is circulated speak? ” requires identifying the newspaper’s cir-culation region and then inferring the predominant language spoken in that region. The absence of a direct edge between Knews and Cyprus blocks further forward expansion, causing the reasoning chain to break. To mitigate these bottlenecks, Wan et al. (2025) attempt to enrich the context by extracting sub-graphs and converting them into textual descrip-tions via LLMs. While this approach increases information redundancy to hedge against missing edges, such graph-to-text transformation inherently obscures the underlying structural topology, hin-dering the model’s ability to discern and exploit implicit correlations between seemingly discon-nected entities. Furthermore, this verbalization process is highly susceptible to semantic noise, as the generated narratives often incorporate extrane-ous or irrelevant information that can distract the LLM from the core reasoning task. Our key insight is that even in the absence of explicit edges, the surrounding subgraph structure often provides sufficient evidence to bridge rea-soning gaps. The topological structure of KGs often contains essential information (Sun et al., 2019). For example, as illustrated in the Figure 1, although Knews and Cyprus are not directly connected, their local subgraph exposes conver-gent associative paths, such as (Knews, owned by, SPP Media Group) → (SPP Media Group, operates in, Cyprus). Such structural patterns offer strong structural cues for inferring the missing relation 

circulated in between Knews and Cyprus . Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are well suited to exploit this information by capturing complex entity de-pendencies through message-passing mechanisms (Wu et al., 2020). Unlike previous methods that make isolated, step-by-step decisions, using GNNs allows candidate entities to be evaluated within their local structural contexts, enabling the LLM to reason beyond immediate neighbors and reach entities without direct connections. In this paper, we proposed a graph-based soft prompting ( GraSP ) framework for multi-hop KBQA. GraSP first employs a GNN to encode the retrieved subgraph, capturing its rich structural dependencies as dense entity representations. We then used a transformation layer to map these rep-resentations into soft prompts, which provides the LLM with global structural context. Inspired by the two-stage paradigm of Chen et al. (2023), we first used a small LLM to identifies question-relevant entities in the subgraph with the help of the soft prompts. Then we further injected the selected en-tities together with their associated relations into another larger LLM to generate accurate and high-quality answers. This two-stage approach, using a lightweight LLM to generate a draft and then a larger LLM to refine and verify, improves overall performance while reducing computational cost. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • We propose a novel subgraph-level reasoning paradigm that encodes structural subgraphs into graph-based soft prompts using a GNN, enabling more robust reasoning under knowledge graph incompleteness. • We propose a two-stage reasoning paradigm in which a lightweight model performs soft-prompt-based reasoning, and a powerful LLM gener-ates evidence-aware answers, reducing computa-tional cost while enabling seamless integration with off-the-shelf LLMs. • Our framework achieves state-of-the-art perfor-mance on three out of four multi-hop KBQA benchmarks, validating its effectiveness in com-plex knowledge-intensive tasks. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Knowledge Graph Reasoning 

Multi-hop knowledge base question answering (KGQA) requires reasoning over multiple relations to find answers. Recent work increasingly treats LLMs as reasoning agents that explore knowledge graphs. Methods such as Think-on-Graph (Sun et al., 2024) perform structured traversal over en-tities and relations, while Plan-on-Graph (Chen et al., 2024) and Debate-on-Graph (Ma et al., 2025) introduce step-wise verification or role-based col-laboration to reduce spurious reasoning. Other ap-proaches enhance exploration via auxiliary mech-anisms, including memory augmentation (Jiang et al., 2025) and iterative query decomposition (Wang and Yu, 2025; Huang et al., 2023). Learning-based methods further guide multi-hop reasoning, including transition modeling (Zhao et al., 2024) and reinforcement learning–based decision pro-cesses (Zhang and Zhao, 2025). To improve ef-ficiency, LightPROF (Ao et al., 2025) compresses reasoning graphs into compact embeddings. How-ever, these approaches still rely on explicit KG edges at inference time. LMP (Wan et al., 2025) alleviates this by linearizing subgraphs into text, but with a loss of graph structure. 

2.2 Incomplete Knowledge Graphs 

Incomplete knowledge graphs are traditionally ad-dressed by knowledge graph completion (KGC), which employs embedding-based, structure-aware, or large pretrained models to predict missing links (Chao et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021; Saxena et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024). Some works further ex-plores inductive and foundation-model-based KGC to improve transferability (Galkin et al., 2023; Sax-ena et al., 2022; Hua et al., 2025). Despite method-ological advances, these methods are primarily de-signed to explicitly complete the KG before down-stream use. There are also some work focuses on reason-ing over incomplete KGs. To mitigate missing knowledge at inference time, Generate-on-Graph (Xu et al., 2024) employs LLMs to generate im-plicit facts, but such generation may suffer from hallucination. Alternatively, Guo et al. (2023) es-timate the likelihood of missing relations in the retrieved subgraph, but this approach still relies on explicit edge prediction and makes limited use of the global graph structure. In contrast, our work perform multi-hop KGQA directly over incomplete graphs, without requiring prior graph completion. 

2.3 Soft Prompt Reasoning 

Soft prompting enables parameter-efficient adap-tation of frozen language models by optimizing continuous prompt vectors (Li and Liang, 2021; Lester et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022). Recently, this paradigm has been extended to latent reasoning (Xu et al., 2025; Hao et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025) as well as structured reasoning, like graph-based reasoning (Liu et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024). However, existing methods typ-ically require access to model internals, which re-stricts their applicability to open-source LLMs. In contrast, our proposed two-stage paradigm applies soft prompting to a lightweight open-source model and then translates the learned representations into natural language, enabling seamless integration with any off-the-shelf closed-source LLMs. 

3 Task Definition 

We consider the problem of Knowledge Base Ques-tion Answering (KBQA) over a knowledge graph 

G ⊆ E × R × E , where E and R denote the sets of entities and relations, respectively. Each fact in the graph is represented as a triple (e, r, e ′), indicating that a relation r holds between entities e and e′.Given a natural language question q and a topic entity et ∈ E identified from the question, the goal is to determine the corresponding answer entity 

ea ∈ E by reasoning over G. This process typi-cally involves traversing the graph starting from 

et. However, due to the sparsity and incomplete-ness of real-world knowledge graphs, the answer entity often cannot be reached via direct one-hop relations. Consequently, effective KBQA systems must support multi-hop reasoning, where ea is con-nected to et through a sequence of intermediate entities and relations. 

4 Method 

Our proposed method is illustrated in Figure 2. It consists of three components: (1) Subgraph Extrac-tion , (2) Graph-based Soft Prompting , and (3) Two-Stage Reasoning and Answering . Given a complex query, we first extract a subgraph centered on the topic entities and encode its structure using a GNN with message passing. The encoded entity repre-sentations are then used to construct soft prompts. Finally, we decouple reasoning and answering via a two-stage LLM framework, reducing computa-tional cost while enabling flexible integration with off-the-shelf LLMs.

4.4 Optimization 

Overall, the trainable components of our GraSP framework include the GNN and the FFN in Sec-tion 4.2. For the LLM used in entity selection (denoted as LLM select in Section 5.3), we allow two training strategies: either fine-tuning the model or keeping it frozen. Since the primary objective of this com-ponent is to identify question-relevant entities, we optimize it using a cross-entropy loss: 

L = − log P (y | Iinstr , Q, E, ˆH), (9) where y denotes the ground-truth target entity. Gra-dients are backpropagated through the graph-based soft prompt ˆH to update the parameters of both the GNN and the FFN. In contrast, for LLM ans in the answer genera-tion stage, once sufficient information has been retrieved, it answers the question does not rely on task-specific modeling assumptions. Therefore, the LLM used for answer generation can be any off-the-shelf LLM and does not require fine-tuning.

5.1 Datasets 

We evaluated our approach on four widely-used datasets for KBQA, leveraging a large-scale general-purpose knowledge graphs Freebase: Com-plexWebQuestions (CWQ) (Talmor and Berant, 2018), WebQuestionsSP (WebQSP) (Yih et al., 2016), WebQuestions (Berant et al., 2013), and GrailQA (Gu et al., 2021). The characteristics of all datasets are provided in Table 6 in Appendix B. Following prior work (Sun et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2024), we used Hit@1 score as the evaluation metrics. The implementation details are provided in Appendix A. 

5.2 Comparative Methods 

We compared our approach with the following state-of-the-art methods: • ToG (Sun et al., 2024): A step-by-step reasoning framework based on LLMs. We re-implement this method using GPT-5.2. • KBQA-o1 (Luo et al., 2025): Enhances search efficiency on knowledge graphs by training a Monte Carlo Tree Search strategy, which re-quires model fine-tuning. We re-implement this based on LLaMA3.3-70B. • KG-Agent (Jiang et al., 2025): Stores previously explored reasoning paths in a memory module and leverages this memory to guide subsequent explorations. • iQUEST (Wang and Yu, 2025): Performs knowl-edge graph reasoning by iteratively generating and answering sub-questions. • LMP (Wan et al., 2025): Extracts entity-centered first-order subgraphs and converts them into nat-ural language explanations using an LLM, pro-viding textual evidence for reasoning. 5.3 Main Results 

The main experimental results of our method are re-ported in Table 1. As shown, our approach achieves the best performance on three multi-hop datasets. On the generalization dataset, our results are also comparable to the strongest baseline. Among the compared methods, ToG exhibits the poorest per-formance, primarily because it relies solely on node-level semantic information at each reason-ing step. In contrast, KBQA-o1, iQUEST, and KG-Agent show consistent improvements, indicat-ing that incorporating sub-questions or memory mechanisms can effectively enhance reasoning per-formance. Notably, KG-Agent extracts entities from both the training and test sets in advance, sig-nificantly reducing the search space for the LLM. This substantially lowers the difficulty of evaluat-ing generalization on GrailQA, leading to relatively strong results. We also observe that LMP, which also uses sub-graphs to enhance reasoning, achieves the best per-formance among the baselines, as it can utilize richer contextual information at each reasoning step. However, this approach incurs substantial computational overhead due to repeated subgraph extraction and transformation. Also, the transfor-mation process is less effective because introduce noises, and overly large subgraphs can exceed the LLM’s effective context window, negatively affect-ing performance. In contrast, our method adopts a soft-prompt-based design and employs a GNN to efficiently compute graph representations in par-allel, resulting in a more effective and scalable utilization of graph structure. 

5.4 Ablation on Graph-based Soft Prompt 

To investigate in detail the effect of the Graph-based Soft prompt, we designed several variants for comparison. (1) Without Soft Prompt , we removed the soft prompting module and allowed the LLM to directly select question-relevant entities. (2) 

Triplet-based Representation , where the retrieved subgraph was linearized into a list of knowledge triplets, which were then provided to the LLM as input, discarding the explicit graph structure. (3) 

Text-based Soft Prompt , in which all triplets were encoded into semantic vectors using BERT, and the resulting embeddings were used as soft prompts instead of graph-structured representations. These variants enable us to isolate and analyze the contri-bution of graph-structured soft prompts to overall model performance. The experimental results are reported in Table 2. Overall, our GraSP achieves the best performance across all settings. Removing graph information entirely ( Without Soft Prompt ) leads to a substan-tial performance drop, highlighting the importance of structured knowledge for multi-hop reasoning. 

Triplet-based Representation yields noticeable im-provements over the no-graph baseline, indicating that incorporating graph-derived information bene-fits reasoning. Furthermore, compared to Triplet-based Representation , Text-based Soft Prompt con-sistently performs better, demonstrating that soft prompts effectively enhance the reasoning capabil-ity of LLMs. Notably, Graph-based Soft Prompt 

further outperforms Text-based Soft Prompt , con-firming that GNNs can more effectively encode and exploit structural information. These trends are highly consistent across experiments with two dif-ferent LLMs, providing strong evidence for the ef-fectiveness and robustness of the proposed GraSP.

5.7 Impact of Subgraph Structure 

As described in Section 4.1, subgraph extraction starts from a topic entity and collects its l-hop neighbors to construct an induced subgraph. In-creasing the hop number introduces more multi-hop neighbors, resulting in a structurally more com-plex subgraph. To examine the impact of the hop number on subgraph construction, we evaluate our method using 1-hop, 2-hop, and 3-hop subgraphs, with the results reported in Table 4. The results show that 2-hop subgraphs achieve the best overall performance. With 1-hop extrac-tion, missing edges cannot be effectively compen-sated, making it difficult to infer potential con-nections between entities; besides, the resulting subgraph is often too simple to fully exploit the proposed method. In contrast, 2-hop subgraphs can partially recover missing relations through inter-mediate entities, as the example in Figure 1. How-ever, increasing the hop number to 3 introduces excessive entities and edges, which complicate in-formation passing and introduces substantial noise, leading to little benefit. Therefore, we adopt 2-hop subgraphs as they strike a favorable balance be-tween structural expressiveness and noise, achiev-ing effective encoding of graph-based soft prompts.

6 Conclusion 

In this paper, we proposed GraSP, a novel subgraph-level reasoning framework that encodes subgraphs into soft prompts using GNNs. By re-ducing reliance on explicit knowledge graph edges, GraSP effectively mitigates the impact of missing relations and alleviates issues caused by incom-plete knowledge graphs. We further introduced a two-stage reasoning paradigm, where a lightweight LLM identifies question-relevant entities guided by soft prompts, followed by a more powerful LLM for evidence-aware answer generation. This modular design allows GraSP to be seamlessly in-tegrated with off-the-shelf LLMs. Experiments on four benchmarks show state-of-the-art perfor-mance on three datasets, while extensive analyses further validate the effectiveness of our approach. Limitations 

While our approach demonstrates promising effec-tiveness, it still has several limitations. First, our method relies on the structural information of the underlying knowledge graph. When the knowl-edge graph is severely incomplete, contains sparse information, or exhibits overly simple structures, the extracted subgraphs may provide limited use-ful evidence, which in turn constrains the effec-tiveness of our approach. Second, our framework employs soft prompts, which are continuous repre-sentations without explicit semantic grounding. As a result, the reasoning process becomes less inter-pretable, making it difficult to precisely understand how specific structural signals influence the LLM’s decision-making.

A Implementation Details 

In our main experiments, we use GPT-5.2 as the answer LLM and GPT-OSS-20B as the selection LLM. Specifically, we apply LoRA to the query and value projection matrices of each self-attention layer, with the LoRA rank set to r = 8 and the scaling factor set to α = 16 . A dropout rate of 0.1 is applied to the LoRA layers. During fine-tuning, only the LoRA parameters are updated while all original model parameters remain frozen. During subgraph construction, we extract 2-hop subgraphs, and accordingly employ a two-layer attention-based GNN for subgraph encoding. We adopt bert-base-uncased as the text encoder, which has a hidden size of 768, while the hid-den dimension of the GNN is set to 128. Since GPT-OSS-20B has a hidden size of 2880, the feed-forward mapping layer that projects GNN represen-tations into soft prompts has a dimension of 128 × 2880. All models are trained on 8 NVIDIA A100 GPUs (80GB each). In addition, we conduct exten-sive comparisons using LLMs of different scales, including Qwen3-0.6B, Qwen3-1.7B, Qwen3-4B, Qwen3-8B, Qwen-30B-A3B, LLaMA-3.3-7B, and GPT-4o. For constructing ground-truth labels for question-related entity selection (i.e., y in Func-tion 9), we treat the answer entity as the most relevant entity for 1-hop questions. For multi-hop questions, all entities along the shortest path between the topic entity and the answer entity are considered relevant.
