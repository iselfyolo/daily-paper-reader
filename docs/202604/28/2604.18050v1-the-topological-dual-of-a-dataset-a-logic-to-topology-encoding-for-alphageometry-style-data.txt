Abstract. AlphaGeometry represents a milestone in neuro-symbolic rea-soning, yet its architecture faces a log-linear scaling bottleneck within its symbolic deduction engine that limits its efficiency as problem com-plexity increases. Recent technical reports suggest that current domain-specific languages may be isomorphic as input representations to natural language, interchanging them acts as a performance-invariant transfor-mation, implying that current neural guidance relies on superficial en-codings rather than structural understanding. This paper addresses this representation bottleneck by proposing a logic-to-topology encoding de-signed to reveal the structural invariants of a model’s latent space under a transformation of its input space. By leveraging the Logic of Observation, we utilize the duality between provability in observable theories and topologies to propose a logic-to-topology encoder for the input space. We introduce the concept of the “topological dual of a dataset”, a transformation that bridges formal logic, topology, and neural processing. This framework serves as a Rosetta Stone for neuro-symbolic AI, providing a principled pathway for the mechanistic interpretability of how models navigate complex discovery paths. 

Keywords: Representation learning · Logic-to-Topology encoder · Al-phaGeometry · Scaling Law · Mechanistic interpretability · The Logic of Observation · Lean theorem prover.

# 1 Introduction: Beyond Superficial Representations 

1.1 AlphaGeometry 

AlphaGeometry (AG) represents a milestone in neuro-symbolic reasoning, achiev-ing gold-medalist performance on International Mathematical Olympiad (IMO) geometry problems by overcoming the chronic scarcity of human data through massive synthetic proof generation [13,7]. Central to AG’s architecture is the synergy between a neural language model and a specialized symbolic deduction engine. This neural guidance is most vital during auxiliary constructions: the creative act of proposing new points, lines, or circles that act as the keys to unlocking a proof. Despite its success, the domain-specific language (DSL) of AlphaGeometry remains a specialist’s tool. While its coverage has expanded in recent iterations, it is still architecturally bounded by a specific subset of geometry, covering roughly 88% of IMO geometry problems from 2000–2024. In [3], we addressed this limi-tation by proposing a cross-domain language based on the logic of observation. By embedding AlphaGeometry’s reasoning into this fragment of first-order logic, we provided a mathematically principled path toward a cross-domain reasoning engine. However, a fundamental question remains: Why does the specific syntax of the domain-specific language matter (or not matter) to the neural model? 

1.2 The “Superficial Encoding” Fallacy 

In the AlphaGeometry2 (AG2) technical report, the authors conducted an exper-iment to investigate the role of their DSL in the model’s performance [7, App. A]. They translated all synthetic data into natural language, trained a new model, and compared it against the original DSL-trained model. Somewhat surprisingly to AG2’s authors, the models achieved identical performance on 2000-2024 IMO geometry problems. We contend that this result is expected. The AlphaGeometry DSL, while structured, remains isomorphic to natural language as input representations for the description of geometric problems. Both are human-legible serializations of the same underlying logical steps. 1 If the mapping between natural language and DSL is merely a superficial translation –a change of “font” rather than a more structural change– it is logical that a sufficiently high-capacity transformer (such as the Gemini-based MoE models in AG2) would find no functional difference between them. If the model can map AG’s DSL idiom perp(A, B) directly to “line A is perpendicular to line B”, the neural network has not learned a new way to reason, and the model’s latent space remains essentially unchanged, explaining why the transition from natural language to a domain-specific language fails to provide a meaningful shift in reasoning capability. # 2 The Logic of Observation 

This section introduces the logic of observation, a symbolic framework designed to provide a formal grounding for AlphaGeometry and its successors. While traditionally known as geometric logic[9], we frame it here through the lens of observability to emphasize the structural constraints of human and machine reasoning. The logic of observation respects the fundamental asymmetry of the “testable”: a property is confirmed by a finite number of successful observations (conjunction), even if it could be satisfied by an infinite number of distinct con-figurations (disjunction). This section distills the formal machinery –signatures, formulas, and observable theories, e.g., Euclidean geometry– necessary to treat the central theme of Section 3: a new representation of the input space of Alpha-Geometry through a logic-to-topology encoder, and its potential advantages. For a machine-checked formalization of this logic in Lean 4, the reader is referred to [3]. 

2.1 Signatures: The Alphabet of Language 

A signature Σ provides the non-logical symbols of the language: the specific vocabulary used to describe a mathematical domain. Formally, it consists of three components: 

Sorts: A set of base types (e.g., S, T, . . . ) representing the distinct categories of objects under observation, such as Point or Line . These define the fun-damental ontology of the system. 

Function Symbols: A set of mappings f : S1 × · · · × Sn → T . These represent constructions, such as the midpoint of a segment, which generate new terms from existing ones. 

Relation Symbols: A set of predicates R ⊆ S1 × · · · × Sn used to state ob-servable properties, such as Collinear( p1, p 2, p 3).Collectively, the signature determines the granularity of the representation, es-sentially defining what the model can perceive in a single step. Other observable theories include, for instance, affine geometry, algebraic theories (groups, rings, modules, fields), the theory of small categories, the theory of graphs etc. 

2.4 Meta-Properties and the Reliability of Reasoning Provability: The Mechanics of Deduction. The logic of observation pro-vides a formal deduction system: a rigorous mechanism to derive new knowledge and theorems. This system consists of inference rules that govern how obser-vations can be transformed into conclusions. These include structural rules for managing sequents, rules for logical connectives ( ∧, W, ∃), and the properties of equality. 3 A proof is always conducted relative to an observable theory T. We say a sequent σ is T-provable (written T ⊢ σ) if there exists a finite proof tree –built from the core logical rules and the axioms of T– that concludes with σ.This deduction system is what ensures that a proof’s trajectory from premises to a conclusion follows a valid logical path.

Satisfiability and Models. While provability is purely syntactic, it is grounded in a robust semantic foundation. Indeed, we can interpret a signature Σ within a mathematical structure M over a category C (typically C is a category with sufficient structure, such as the category of sets or a category of more general spaces). We say M is a model of a theory T over Σ, if M satisfies every axiom in T. This notion of satisfaction ( M |= T) bridges the gap between abstract symbols and the objects they represent for a human mathematician. 

Soundness: Bridging the Trust Gap. The connection between syntax (prov-ability) and semantics (satisfiability) is established through meta-properties such as soundness. Formally, soundness states that if a sequent is provable within a theory T, it is necessarily true in every model M of T:

T ⊢ σ =⇒ (M |= T =⇒ M |= σ).

In [3], we provided a mechanized proof of soundness for our implementation of the logic of observation in Lean 4. This property is particularly relevant given the current state of geometry solvers like AlphaGeometry. While AG’s symbolic engine is highly performant, it lacks a formal proof of soundness. This creates a trust gap: there is no rigorous guarantee that its symbolic deductions are free from error, often necessitating human expert review for final verification. Furthermore, because soundness is a property of the logic itself rather than of a specific theory, it scales automatically. Once the logic is proven sound, that guarantee extends to Euclidean geometry, affine geometry, or any future domain the system might explore. This provides a scalable path to building verifiable AI as the range of mathematical tasks increases.

At this point, the reader familiar with AlphaGeometry should sense how its underlying architecture relates to the formal framework of the Logic of Obser-vation. While AG is presented as a specialized solver for International Mathe-matical Olympiad (IMO) geometry, its internal mechanics are deeply rooted in the logical foundations we have discussed in the previous Section 2. The present section will be devoted to a more thorough dialogue between AlphaGeometry and the logic of observation. 

3.1 From AlphaGeometry to the Logic of Observation AlphaGeometry’s Language as a Specific Signature. As we have seen, AG’s Domain-Specific Language (DSL) , meticulously tailored for Euclidean plane geometry, is fundamentally a particular instantiation of the more general notion of a first-order signature Σ.

– Sorts: In AG, the domain’s objects (points, lines, circles . . . ) correspond directly to the sorts of a signature. The Topological Dual of a Dataset 9

– Relations: AG’s geometric predicates like perp(a b c d) (meaning AB ⊥

– Functions: Constructions such as X = midpoint (A, B ) for finding the mid-point of a segment act as the function symbols of the said signature. 

Signature Extension and Abstraction Layers. If the logic of observation starts with basic sorts like points and lines, how does it account for the rich library of objects found in AlphaGeometry –and further extended by AG2 [7, 2]– such as circle , trapezoid , tangent or quadrilateral ?The answer lies in the extensibility of a first-order signature. A signature can always be extended to include new sorts, new function and relation symbols. 

Why Extend the Signature? For the authors of AG/AG2, extending their “sig-nature” in this way provides two massive advantages for their neuro-symbolic architecture: 

– Heuristic Efficiency: The neural language model can communicate using these higher-level primitives, which is far more efficient than describing ev-erything in terms of raw coordinates or basic incidence axioms. 

– Search Space Reduction: By treating, e.g. , eq_trapezoid (a trapezoid with two equal sides) as a primitive sort, the symbolic engine can skip many low-level deduction steps. In the Lean framework of [3], these extensions are cost-free in terms of soundness. Even for a future generalized iteration of AlphaGeometry, which could dynami-cally swap signatures depending on the problem domain while maintaining the same verified logical core, our soundness theorem would still apply. 

Information Density and Semantic Compression. The finite context window of transformers and the O(n2) complexity of vanilla attention place a premium on the efficiency of representation. This raises a fundamental question regard-ing the semantic compression of mathematical thought. Because the AG team lacks a formal conceptual framework for this phenomenon, they can only phrase the question of factors affecting a model’s mathematical ability in binary terms: their DSL versus natural language [7, A.]. Through the notion of a signature, we turn a switch into a dial: by utilizing the notion of a signature and its ex-tensions, our framework allows us to investigate whether, and to which extent, the performance of LLMs is affected, through the management of the limited context window and token consumption, by the level of abstraction at which a problem is posed. In this view, an extended signature does not merely add vocabulary; it allows derived notions to become first-class citizens, increasing the information density per token. AlphaGeometry’s Symbolic Engine: A Domain-Specific Deduction Sys-tem. Starting from a set of generated premises, the symbolic engine in Alpha-Geometry (the DD+AR system) operates by alternately applying a Deductive Database (DD) and Algebraic Rules (AR) to compute the deductive closure of the problem state. The DD component consists of a fixed set of 75 deduction rules (AG1) complemented by human-designed heuristics. Both categories are manually encoded as forward-chaining rules in a structured format analogous to Datalog. These rules encapsulate expert-level geometric knowledge that human contestants utilize to navigate the search space of IMO-style problems. Cru-cially, these are not elementary logical inference rules (such as the cut rule or 

∧-introduction); those logical operations are relegated to the solver’s imperative host code (Python/C++). Instead, the DD rules are high-level geometric the-orems; for instance, Thales’s theorem or the detection of concyclic points via equal angles. These rules act as curated deductive shortcuts; they are not de-duced or verified by the system, but are hard-coded as axiomatic truths. With the framework presented in Section 2 and implemented in Lean 4 in [3], we provide the formal logical substrate of AlphaGeometry’s symbolic engine. This substrate corresponds to the explicit construction of proof trees within a specified observable theory T (e.g. , Euclidean geometry). Specifically, within the Lean-based representation of the logic of observation [3], we constructed for each consequent ψ in the deductive closure a term of type T.Proof (ϕ, ψ ) ,where ϕ represents the antecedent premises. This approach could allow us to state high-level theorems and subsequently prove them as lemmas using standard mathlib-style tactics or by using custom sequent-style “chase” tactics developed with Lean’s metaprogramming features. By postulating these theorems (using Lean’s sorry mechanism in the initial stages), we can emulate the efficiency of AlphaGeometry’s deductive closure while maintaining a pathway to total formal verification. 

AlphaGeometry’s Deductive Database. Actually, the DD portion of AG’s engine is powered by Horn clauses that serve as a fact base. Horn clauses take the form of simple implications 

P1 ∧ P2 ∧ · · · ∧ Pn =⇒ Q

where Pi and Q are atomic formulas. These Horn clauses are secretly Horn sequents, a particularly tractable subclass of observable sequents. Interestingly, Horn sequents do not contain existential quantifiers. This limitation is not a problem in the case of AG, because its domain-specific language contains the constructs corresponding to the objects of discourse, but this would make AG ineffectual at speaking about the mere existence of mathematical objects so widespread in more advanced mathematics.

A richer logic, such as the logic of observation, will face this creative gap all the same and, as a result, will also need the support of a compensatory component such as a transformer language model.

3.2 The Topological Dual of a Dataset 

The asymmetry in observable logic, where we permit only finite conjunctions but allow arbitrary (infinite) disjunctions, is not a mere formal constraint. It reflects a physical and mathematical reality. 

A Duality Between Logic and Topology. On one hand, even if a property could be satisfied in an infinitely diverse set of ways (infinite disjunction), in practice it can only be confirmed through a finite number of observations (finite conjunctions). This is the logic of observation. On the other hand, this logical structure mirrors the defining axioms of a topology: open sets of a topology are stable under arbitrary unions and finite intersections. This correspondence suggests a duality between logic and topology: these fields are two projections of the same underlying system. 

Provability as Covering. The notion of provability in observable logic lies at the heart of the duality between logic and topology. Indeed, provability in ob-servable theories can be rephrased through the so-called Grothendieck topologies and the membership of sieves. This insight is due to the Canadian mathematician André Joyal and traces back to his influential lectures in the early 1970s (though largely unpublished, Joyal’s original work was disseminated via his lectures). In his later published work with André Boileau (1981), Joyal presented a “Théorème d’adéquation” (soundness), a “Théorème de complétude” (completeness), and a “Théorème d’engendrement” (generation of a particular kind of categories from logical theories), framing these results as a “geometrization of algebraic logic” [2] (what we call the logic of observation or observable logic ). The first comprehen-sive presentation of this geometric logic appeared in the seminal monograph of Makkai and Reyes published in 1977.[11] They introduced the so-called syntactic site of a theory T: a category B(T) composed of formulas and provably func-tional relations, equipped with a Grothendieck topology JT. Within this site, the link between coverings (a covering is a family of maps that generates a covering sieve in a Grothendieck topology) and provability is explicitly made. As Makkai and Reyes famously noted [11, Intro.]: If asked what is the most immediate point of contact between the Grothendieck theory and logic, we could point to the notion of a site , a category with a Grothendieck topology. It seems to us that it is most natural to iden-tify a site with a theory [. . . ] The point is that the notion of a covering The Topological Dual of a Dataset 13 

has the same arbitrary nature as an axiom ; and in fact, each covering is considered an axiom in the precise identification we will consider below [. . . ] 

A Proof System Rooted in Topology. Joyal’s insight is remarkably precise: under the correspondence he unearthed, a sequent σ := ( ϕ ⊢ ψ) is provable in the theory T if and only if the sieve Cσ generated by the monomorphism ϕ ∧ ψ , → ϕ

of B(T) is a covering sieve for the topology JT (or in other words, if and only if the monomorphism ϕ ∧ ψ , → ϕ constitutes a covering family). Some of the details of this correspondence are worked out in [5] (see also [1] for a broader perspective). Formally, this equivalence is expressed as: 

(T ⊢ σ := ϕ ⊢ ψ) ⇐⇒ Cσ ∈ JT(ϕ)

This suggests that provability in the logic of observation is governed by the three axioms of a Grothendieck topology, which serve as the exact counterparts to classical rules of inference: 

– Identity: Corresponds to the reflexive property of proofs ( ϕ ⊢ ϕ). 

– Stability: Corresponds to the substitution and pullback of variables (chang-ing context). 

– Transitivity: Corresponds to the Cut Rule or the chaining of implications. Indeed, Joyal realized that the covering sieves of the site ( B(T), J T) governed by the axioms of Grothendieck topologies form a formal proof system which is the exact counterpart of the deduction system formed by the rules of inference in our logic. Following their sequence of fundamental theorems (soundness, complete-ness, and generation), Joyal and Boileau conclude with a striking statement [2, §4.]: It is now clear that a category is often nothing more than a generalized formal system [. . . ] 

The Topological Dual of a Dataset. Given an observable theory T and an observable sequent σ over the same signature Σ, we recall that we denote by (T ⊢ σ) the statement that σ is provable within T. Assuming the theory 

T is clear from context, we define σ∗ as the dual statement of (T ⊢ σ). This represents the T-provability of σ reformulated in the language of Grothendieck topologies (specifically through the membership of covering sieves, as previously explained). As a result, given a dataset of observable sequents –batched under their respective theories T1, . . . , Tn (potentially over different signatures)– we can compute the dual of every individual datapoint (Ti ⊢ σi,j ). We call the resulting collection the topological dual of the dataset (see Fig. 1). Crucially, this mapping extends beyond a mere translation of provability: it is not simply a conceptual shift from “provability of sequents” to “membership in a topology”. We can also compute the dual of the proofs themselves. When a proof of a sequent 14 A. Bordg 

σ is available, it can be systematically transformed into a proof of its dual σ∗

that operates directly on covering sieves using the axioms of a Grothendieck topology. This transformation shifts the focus from a chain of inferences to a topologically driven proof leveraging a calculus of sieves. This could constitute a complete change in the input space of a language model trained on math data framed in the logic of observation. 

The Branching Problem: Efficiency of the 3 over the 16? At first glance, it seems that replacing the sixteen rules of inference in observable logic with the three axioms of Grothendieck topologies (Identity, Stability, and Transitivity) should drastically simplify the proof search. However, as is common in automated reasoning, complexity often shifts rather than disappears. In this framework, the computational burden could shift to calculating the covering sieves of the Grothendieck topology, or a refinement thereof; and if it is not the case, the complexity could migrate into the underlying category B(T) of the Grothendieck site (B(T), J T). One should note that an observable theory can be presented through many different sites (akin to how a group in mathematics admits various presentations via generators and relations). In particular, one can choose to simplify the topology at the cost of complicating the category, or vice versa. The sixteen rules of observable logic are, in effect, an unpacked version of the various faces of Grothendieck sites. 

The Problem is the “Infinite” Branching. The fundamental challenge in automated reasoning is not primarily the number of inference rules; to suggest otherwise would be naive. Rather, the true bottleneck lies in the requirement for auxiliary constructions: the creativity inherent to the mathematical activity. In geometry, if a diagram contains n points, the number of potential new objects (e.g., midpoints, lines) explodes combinatorially. For n = 5 , there are  52

The strategic advantage here is not that the problem of proof search is solved by having fewer rules, but that the input space of the math data processed by a language model can be dramatically refactored. As a result, we expect training data to remain an existential challenge, an essential fuel. This leads us to a critical question: where do the data σi,j come from in the dataset of Figure 1? 

3.3 A New Input Space for AlphaGeometry-Style Data The Infrastructure. We must address the fuel for the engine of a neural prover: where would the training sequents σi,j and their proofs come from? A naive initial approach would be to ingest Mathlib (Lean’s extensive mathemat-ics library) into the framework of observable logic implemented in [3]. How-ever, this strategy faces a significant hurdle of scale. Even without account-ing for the limitations in expressive power of observable logic, and assuming a The Topological Dual of a Dataset 15 

successful translation of Mathlib’s formalized material into topological terms, the resulting dataset would likely remain several orders of magnitude smaller than the volume required to train a high-performing neural prover. Mathlib is quality-dense but volume-sparse relative to machine learning requirements; its human-curated libraries simply do not provide on their own the sheer vol-ume necessary to saturate a transformer-based model. However, we can lever-age Mathlib’s sophisticated infrastructure for category theory (specifically the 

CategoryTheory.Sites.Grothendieck module on Grothendieck topologies) to formally express the dual statements σ∗ and their corresponding proofs in the language of Grothendieck topologies, provided we have the initial sequents 

σ and their proofs from which to translate. 

The Source of Data: Strategic Re-basing. Given that our work provides a rigorous mathematical home for AlphaGeometry, it should be possible to re-base and reuse AG’s synthetic data-generation process. The instance in which it should be the most direct to do so is Euclidean geometry. The core of AG’s pipeline –a domain-specific language, a sampling mechanism for (consistent) theorem premises, the DD+AR symbolic engine, and a traceback algorithm– can be formally interpreted through our framework. Indeed, the for-malization presented in [3] provides a dictionary between AG’s engineering and our logical framework: 

– Domain-specific language → A signature instance Σ.

– Symbolic deduction engine → Inference rules of observable logic + axioms of a theory T over Σ (e.g., Euclidean geometry), ideally augmented with high-level theorems in a dedicated library (see 3.1), potentially sourced from human-curated data. 

– Synthetic triples (premises, conclusion, proof) → Synthetic formal pairs (ob-servable sequent, derivation in observable logic). By utilizing this dictionary, AG’s synthetic generator could be re-based. 

Proof Synthesis: Topology as a High-Level Language. Using Joyal’s en-coding expressing the duality between logic and topology, we can translate the synthetic pairs (σ, p ) output by our generator into their topological duals (σ∗, p ∗).These pairs, consisting of a sequent and its derivation, provide the ground truth for training a language model to prove topological statements. Effectively, the model learns to predict token sequences that describe the proofs of topological statements of a very specific form: “the sieve generated by this family of mor-phisms is a covering sieve for the given topology” (cf. Section 3.2). While this duality allows us to perform logic via topology, it is equally powerful in reverse. For any provable sequent σ in an observable theory T, a topological proof p∗ of its dual σ∗ can be used to extract a formal derivation p in the original observable logic. If the initial mapping (σ → σ∗) is viewed as an input space encoding, then the reverse mapping (p∗ → p) acts as a compilation process or a lowering map-ping. This process translates a high-level topological proof (structured around 16 A. Bordg 

the three axioms of Grothendieck topologies) into a lower-level logical derivation (structured around the sixteen rules of observable logic). By utilizing Mathlib’s category theory library on one side and the internal representation, given in [3], of observable logic within Lean on the other, this entire process occurs within Lean as the ambient language. This ensures that every generated proof is correct by design: if the derivation type-checks, it is formally valid. We anticipate that a future iteration of AlphaGeometry, grounded in this implementation, could adopt this inference loop: 1. Map: A given sequent σ is mapped to its dual σ∗.2. Predict: A neural prover outputs a topological proof p∗.3. Compile: p∗ is compiled (lowered) into a formal logical proof p of σ.An overview of this synthesis process is provided in Figure 2. 

Representational Shifts: Investigating the Latent Space. Once a neural prover has internalized the “grammar” of covering sieves and the statistical dis-tribution of topological proofs, a fundamental research question emerges: How does this input space mapping alter the model’s internal representation of Eu-clidean geometry? Testing the sensitivity of AlphaGeometry’s latent space to this change in symbolic representation could yield significant insights. Specifically, we are interested in whether this topological encoding results in a measurable rep-resentational shift: 

– Latent space geometry: Does the transition from logical sequents to topo-logical sieves produce a more structured or flatter latent manifold? 

– Clustering density: Would some geometric concepts exhibit higher clus-tering density in the latent space, indicating a higher resolution of their relationships, while other might become more diffuse? 

– Feature emergence: Would patterns previously obscured by the raw syn-tax of logic become more readily learnable for the transformer’s attention heads? Evaluating AlphaGeometry’s performance through this lens would allow to move beyond simple pass/fail metrics on IMO problems and would provide a window into its interpretability. 

# 4 Conclusions and Future Work 

In this paper, we have identified a log-linear scaling bottleneck in the symbolic deduction engine of AlphaGeometry: a limitation that, while obscured in the original publications [13,7], presents a significant barrier to solving problems of increasing complexity. This observation led us to scrutinize the current repre-sentation of mathematical data and the superficial nature of the encoding from natural language into AlphaGeometry’s domain-specific language. Ultimately, we addressed a fundamental question for the next generation of neuro-symbolic systems: What would a deep encoding-decoding mapping look like? The Topological Dual of a Dataset 17 

4.1 The Goldilocks Zone 

We have proposed that a non-trivial transformation of the input space is found not in these representationally isomorphic surface syntaxes, but in the logic-to-topology encoding enabled by the logic of observation. By embedding this encoding into the neuro-symbolic architecture of Alpha-Geometry, we transform the input space from a collection of syntactic attire into a more thorough structural mapping. In particular, we have described how to use this encoding to transform the input space of AlphaGeometry by introduc-ing the “topological dual of a dataset”. This topological dual acts as a Rosetta Stone, bridging the gap between formal logic, topology, and neural processing. Ultimately, we hope this framework could open the black box of neural geomet-ric reasoning and shed light on the mechanistic interpretability of latent spaces, by allowing us to better understand how a latent space changes, or remains un-changed, under a transformation of the input space representation, and to which extent this change dictates the path to a discovery. 

4.2 Toward an AI Scientist 

The implications of the logic-topology duality extend far beyond Euclidean ge-ometry, since the logic of observation encompasses a wealth of theories. While the foundations established in [3] could be extended to full first-order or higher-order theories, the observable fragment provides a new lens for mechanistic in-terpretability. However, one may want to expand this framework via full first-order logic or some higher-order logic. A cross-theory reasoning system founded on these principles could eventually encompass many domains with a known axiomatization. This includes not only pure mathematics but also the fun-damental laws of physics: ranging from Newtonian mechanics and Maxwell’s electromagnetism to Special Relativity and Thermodynamics. This would al-low systems like AlphaGeometry, AlphaProof [8], and systems tackling the Put-namBench [14,6,15,4,10,12,16] to move beyond competition-level mathematics toward a more ambitious horizon: AI systems that possess a deep, structural understanding of the physical universe.
