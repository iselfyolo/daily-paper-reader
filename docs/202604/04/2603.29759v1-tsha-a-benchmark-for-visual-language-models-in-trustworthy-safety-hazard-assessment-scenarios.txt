Abstract. Recent advances in vision-language models (VLMs) have ac-celerated their application to indoor safety hazards assessment. However, existing benchmarks suffer from three fundamental limitations: (1) heavy reliance on synthetic datasets constructed via simulation software, cre-ating a significant domain gap with real-world environments; (2) over-simplified safety tasks with artificial constraints on hazard and scene types, thereby limiting model generalization; and (3) absence of rigorous evaluation protocols to thoroughly assess model capabilities in complex home safety scenarios. To address these challenges, we introduce TSHA (Trustworthy Safety Hazards Assessment), a comprehensive benchmark comprising 81,809 carefully curated training samples drawn from four complementary sources: existing indoor datasets, internet images, AIGC images, and newly captured images. This benchmark set also includes a highly challenging test set with 1707 samples, comprising not only a care-fully selected subset from the training distribution but also newly added videos and panoramic images containing multiple safety hazards, used to evaluate the model’s robustness in complex safety scenarios. Exten-sive experiments on 23 popular VLMs demonstrate that current VLMs lack robust capabilities for safety hazard assessment. Importantly, models trained on the TSHA training set not only achieve a significant perfor-mance improvement of up to +18.3 points on the TSHA test set but also exhibit enhanced generalizability across other benchmarks, underscoring the substantial contribution and importance of the TSHA benchmark.

The versatility of VLMs has cat-alyzed significant research interest, particularly in the emerging domain of auto-mated indoor safety inspection [12, 15, 17, 29, 41], where they offer the potential to augment or replace traditional human assessments. However, current VLM-based approaches exhibit critical limitations in home safety hazard assessment, primarily stemming from inadequate domain knowledge about potential domes-tic risks. Addressing this knowledge gap requires a robust evaluation framework that serves as both a challenging testbed and a rich source of domain knowledge. Existing evaluation benchmarks for safety inspection fall into two categories: text-based approaches [29, 41] that convert visual data into textual representa-tions (e.g., object relationship graphs) for processing by pure LLMs, and vision-based methods [12, 15, 17] that construct virtual environments using Unity 3D for VLM training. These paradigms suffer from three fundamental limitations: (1) over reliance on synthetic data from simulators, causing a substantial reality gap that impedes practical deployment; (2) constrained scenario diversity with artificially limited hazard categories, failing to capture the broad spectrum of real-world safety concerns; and (3) simplistic evaluation protocols using static images with high training-test overlap, providing inadequate assessment of model generalization. To address these limitations, we introduce TSHA (Trustworthy Safety Haz-ards Assessment), a comprehensive benchmark for trustworthy safety hazard as-sessment. TSHA offers three key advantages: (1) Diverse and High-Quality Data . TSHA aggregates a large-scale dataset curated from four complementary domains: existing indoor datasets, internet images, AIGC images, and newly captured images. It integrates a substantial volume of realistic videos, synthetic images, and authentic photographs, effectively mitigating the reality gap in-herent in prior simulators and the real world. Beyond common hazards such as fires and falls, it includes more complex, hard-to-assess risks, for instance, unattended children in proximity to dangerous objects or clutter obstructing emergency escape routes. Our main contributions are summarized as follows: 

– We introduce TSHA, the first comprehensive, realistic, and large-scale bench-mark for indoor safety hazard assessment. Unlike prior works, it imposes no artificial restrictions on hazard categories, aiming to advance the hazard recognition capabilities of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) and establish a standardized training resource for both academic and industrial communi-ties. The full dataset and its construction methodology will be made publicly available. 

– We construct a rigorously curated test set incorporating video sequences and panoramic images with multiple embedded safety hazards. This suite is designed to enable comprehensive and stringent evaluation of model perfor-mance in identifying diverse and co-occurring hazardous situations.

Fur-thermore, by training a baseline model on TSHA using reinforcement learn-ing, we demonstrate a notable performance improvement on our TSHA test sets. Enhanced results on external benchmarks further confirm the gener-alization capability gained through TSHA-based training, underscoring its effectiveness as both an evaluation framework and a training resource. 

2 Related Work 

VLM for Safety. Visual-Language Models (VLMs) are increasingly being in-tegrated into safety-critical applications, where their capacity to accurately in-4 Authors Suppressed Due to Excessive Length 

terpret visual content, reason about real-world physical scenarios, and generate actionable insights is pivotal to preventing potential hazards and mitigating as-sociated risks. This transformative potential has been most prominently realized in large-scale, public, and industrial domains, where reliable perception and rea-soning capabilities directly impact operational safety and human well-being. For instance, in the field of autonomous driving, VLMs serve as a core component for robust situational awareness, enabling vehicles to perceive complex road en-vironments, identify vulnerable road users, and navigate dynamic scenarios to avoid collisions [40, 43]. In industrial automation, VLMs power intelligent mon-itoring systems that continuously detect unsafe working conditions, equipment malfunctions, or operational anomalies, thereby ensuring worker safety in high-risk manufacturing and industrial environments [38]. Furthermore, in disaster response scenarios, recent research explores leveraging VLMs to guide embodied agents in making real-time, safety-aware decisions within dynamically changing and hazardous environments, such as post-earthquake or fire-affected areas [46]. However, while these applications demonstrate the potential of VLMs in di-verse scenarios, the nuanced and highly complex domain of private home safety remains significantly underexplored. Our work addresses this critical gap, focus-ing on adapting VLM capabilities for automated inspection of potential domestic hazards. 

Home Hazard Assessment Benchmarks. Existing benchmarks for home hazard detection adopt either text-based or vision-based methodologies. Text-based benchmarks, such as SafetyDetect [29] and Safe BeAl [17], pioneered this area by converting visual scenes into structured textual representations for anal-ysis. While foundational, this text-centric approach creates an unavoidable in-formation bottleneck. Specifically, nuanced visual details that are critical for accurate safety assessment are often lost or oversimplified during the image-to-text conversion process. This information loss directly impairs the ability to identify ambiguous or low-visibility hazards, which are particularly common in real-world home environments. Moreover, their scale remains limited, with Safe-tyDetect offering 1,000 samples across only 3 hazard categories. Vision-based methods directly process visual inputs through VLMs. M-CoDAL [15] pioneers this path with 2,000 Reddit-sourced images, while SafeAgentBench [41] contributes 750 samples covering 10 hazard categories. The latest bench-mark, HOMESAFEBENCH [12], introduces 12,900 samples across 5 categories with interactive environment capabilities. To sum up, these approaches are still constrained by a narrow scope of predefined hazard categories, and large-scale studies rely on synthetic data, leading to critical gaps between simulation and reality. In contrast to prior works, TSHA is fundamentally designed for greater real-ism and complexity. Unlike simulation-dependent work, TSHA prioritizes real-world data sources and constructs safety scenarios closer to everyday domestic environments, presenting significantly greater discrimination challenges for cur-rent models. 3 TSHA 

In this section, we introduce TSHA, a comprehensive benchmark specifically designed to evaluate the ability of vision-language models (VLMs) to assess safety hazards. Sec. 3.1 details the data sources of the training and test sets, as well as the rigorous filtering and quality control procedures implemented to ensure dataset reliability. Sec. 3.2 presents detailed statistics of the dataset, providing insights into its coverage and representativeness. Sec. 3.3 elaborates on the pipeline for generating text descriptions for each sample, ensuring consistent and accurate annotation of safety hazards. Finally, Sec. 3.4 explicitly defines the task formulation of TSHA, clarifying the evaluation objectives for VLMs’ safety hazard assessment capability. 

3.1 Data Source TSHA Training Set. We curate images from four complementary sources: Existing Indoor Datasets, Internet images, AIGC-generated images, and newly captured images. The entire training set was developed with the participation of 20 volunteers and took two months to complete. For existing indoor datasets, we selected NYU v2 [33] and MIT Indoor Scenes [30] due to their rich indoor scene coverage, encompassing 67 indoor categories and over 10,000 real-world images (no simulator or virtual content). For Internet images, we carefully selected and downloaded 63 high-quality videos of everyday home life from plat-forms such as YouTube and Bilibili, covering diverse domestic scenarios. These videos were split into static frames at 3 FPS, with irrelevant opening/closing frames (prologues/epilogues) manually removed. For AIGC-generated images, we employed state-of-the-art closed-source text-to-video and text-to-image large models, including Jimeng [13], Hunyuan [35], PixVerse, and HailuoAI. Specif-ically, we first used text-generation LLMs (Doubao [14], Tongyi Qianwen [4], and Deepseek [32]) to generate prompts describing safety-hazard-containing sce-narios. These prompts then guided the aforementioned video/image generation models to produce corresponding content. Videos were again decomposed into static frames at 3 FPS. For newly captured images, 20 volunteers recorded videos of real-world home and work scenes (bedrooms, kitchens, living rooms, toilets, studies, and offices). TSHA Test Set. To rigorously evaluate the safety hazard assessment capabili-ties learned from the TSHA training data, we constructed a comprehensive test suite designed specifically to assess models’ generalization to challenging, real-world scenarios. Unlike traditional benchmarks with limited hazard diversity, our test set introduces greater complexity by integrating three complementary components: video sequences, panoramic images, and a carefully selected subset of the training data. For the newly added video sequences, we leveraged Sora2’s [5] advanced video generation capabilities to create dynamic indoor scenes with time-evolving safety hazards—challenging models to interpret risks across se-quential frames, as required in real-world applications. For the newly added panoramic images, we used the Tencent Hunyuan World model [16] to generate comprehensive panoramic scenes containing multiple concurrent hazards. This enables a holistic assessment of hazard detection capabilities, rather than limit-ing evaluation to local scene analysis. The data generation pipeline is illustrated in Fig. 2. 

Filtering and Quality Control. To ensure the reliability and quality of the dataset, particularly the AIGC-based samples, two PhD students in computer science manually inspected each data entry individually. All training and test samples were verified to satisfy three essential criteria: physical plausibility, re-Title Suppressed Due to Excessive Length 7

quiring that image or video content adheres to real-world physical laws; real-ism of danger, ensuring that the depicted hazards are consistent with real-life safety scenarios; and semantic consistency, meaning that images and videos ac-curately represent the intended safety risks. This manual filtering step effectively eliminated all AI-generated images that violate physical constraints or contain excessively exaggerated content. 

3.2 Data Statistics Overall Data Scale and Distributions. As illustrated in Fig. 3a and Fig. 3b, the final training set comprises a total of 80,102 question-answer (QA) pairs, derived from four data sources: existing datasets (28,172 samples, 35.2%), newly captured images (8,724 samples, 10.9%), internet images (25,942 samples, 32.4%), and AIGC-generated images (17,264 samples, 21.6%). Each image is paired with two QA pairs, resulting in 40,051 training images—broken down into 14,086 (ex-isting datasets), 4,362 (newly captured images), 12,971 (internet images), and 8,632 (AIGC images). The final test set contains 1,707 QA pairs, with contri-butions from six sources: existing datasets (392 samples, 23.0%), newly cap-tured images (102 samples, 6.0%), internet images (296 samples, 17.3%), AIGC-generated images (210 samples, 12.3%), Sora2-generated videos (400 samples, 23.4%), and Tencent Hunyuan world model panoramic images (307 samples, 18.0%). Notably, existing datasets, newly captured images, internet images, and AIGC images each map to two QA pairs per image, while Sora2 videos and panoramic images correspond to one QA pair per video/image. This results in 196, 51, 148, 105, 400, and 307 images/videos for existing datasets, newly captured images, internet images, AIGC images, Sora2, and panoramic images, respectively.

Compared to prior work, the TSHA dataset covers a substantially broader and more diverse set of safety hazards, including blocked escape routes and electrical shock. The most prevalent categories are tripping hazards (3,633), cluttered floors (2,933), fire risk (1,737), and blocked escape routes (1,327).

Building on this description, the Preliminary Hazards Identification stage performs an initial risk scan across multiple predefined safety dimensions—namely, electrical safety, fire hazards, trip or fall risks, clutter and obstruction hazards, and other conditions that could lead to personal injury or property damage. The model systematically enumerates potential hazards, each accompanied by a con-cise rationale linking the observation to its associated risk, thereby constructing a preliminary hazard profile. The Final Hazards Identification phase enforces rigorous, evidence-based val-idation of each candidate hazard. For every identified risk, the model must: (1) cite specific visual evidence from the image; (2) assign a confidence level (high/medium/low); (3) differentiate genuine hazards from benign everyday sce-narios; and (4) apply domain-specific rules—particularly for clutter and ob-struction—to ensure consistent interpretation. This stage further incorporates misidentification analysis and confidence calibration to suppress false positives while preserving sensitivity to true hazards. The fourth step consists of human verification. To ensure that GPT-4o can accurately identify and describe safety hazards within images, the preceding steps adopt a chain-of-thought-based self-correction mechanism, enabling GPT-4o to serve as an automatic quality assessor for self-inspection and revision. To further validate the quality of the resulting dataset, we conduct human veri-fication performed by two PhD students in computer science. Specifically, we employ a random sampling strategy, where 10% of the total samples are ran-domly selected for manual inspection. The results demonstrate that the textual annotations in the sampled samples are highly consistent with the actual safety hazards depicted in the images, which effectively guarantees the reliability and high quality of the final dataset. This four-stage pipeline maintains strict contextual continuity: each reason-ing step explicitly builds upon the prior output, forming a coherent and traceable analytical chain. 3.4 Task Formulation. 

To systematically evaluate the capability of vision-language models in identifying safety hazards, we design two complementary inference tasks based on carefully curated image–question pairs. The first task, Choice Question, comprises two subtypes: binary true/false questions and four-option single-choice questions. In the true/false variant, each question presents a statement about the presence or absence of a hazard in the image, and the model must select “true” or “false” ac-cordingly. The single-choice variant evaluates the model’s ability to recognize and categorize specific hazard types. Each question provides four mutually exclusive options covering distinct hazard categories or the option “no hazard”, and the model is required to select the label that best describes the risk depicted in the image. The second task, Open-ended Question Answering, requires the model to comprehensively identify and describe all potential safety hazards in an image or video without any predefined answer choices. Unlike the constrained formats above, this task demands spontaneous generation of natural language responses that are not only accurate but also concise, coherent, and semantically com-plete. Consequently, it constitutes a more challenging evaluation setting, jointly assessing hazard detection accuracy, descriptive fidelity, and reasoning clarity.

Metrics. To evaluate model performance, we use accuracy as the metric for True/False and choice questions, as it effectively captures correctness in these constrained formats. For subjective question-answering (QA), however, evalu-ation is more challenging: traditional NLP metrics (e.g., BLEU-4, CIDEr) are insufficient due to their limited semantic understanding and inability to model long-range contextual dependencies. Recent work has demonstrated the promise of large language models (LLMs) as evaluators, leveraging their robust semantic comprehension. Building on this, we develop a specialized evaluation framework for indoor safety hazard QA pairs. Specifically, we employ ChatGPT-4o [18] to assess model responses against reference answers across three dimensions: (1) 

Accuracy (factual correctness and completeness of hazard identification), (2) 

Conciseness (efficiency and redundancy of the response), and (3) Coherence 

(structural organization and logical flow of the explanation). Each response is evaluated in multiple rounds, with scores averaged to ensure reliability. The final benchmark score is the mean of all sample-level scores, and the overall QA score is a weighted sum, yielding a comprehensive and statistically sound evaluation, which is shown as: 

SQA = 0 .7 · Saccuracy + 0 .2 · Sconciseness + 0 .1 · Scoherence , (1) where Saccuracy , Sconciseness , and Scoherence denote the respective scores for accu-racy, conciseness, and coherence. We adopt such a weighting scheme based on the practical significance of each metric in safety hazard detection. Accuracy is assigned the highest weight since it directly determines the reliability of hazard identification, which is closely related to personal safety and property protection and thus represents the most critical factor. Conciseness is given the second-highest weight, as timely model responses are essential in safety-critical scenar-ios to enable rapid intervention and countermeasures. Coherence characterizes whether the model can provide logically consistent and complete descriptions of all identified safety hazards. For multiple-choice questions, we directly used answer accuracy as the score SCQ . The final overall performance is reported as the average of the QA and choice-question scores:

In this work, we introduce TSHA, the comprehensive benchmark for evaluat-ing safety hazard assessment capabilities in VLMs. TSHA aggregates training data from four complementary sources: existing indoor datasets, internet images, AIGC images, and newly captured images.
