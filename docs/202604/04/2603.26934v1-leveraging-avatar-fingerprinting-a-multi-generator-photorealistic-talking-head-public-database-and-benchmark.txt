# Leveraging Avatar Fingerprinting: A Multi-Generator Photorealistic Talking-Head Public Database and Benchmark 

Laura Pedrouzo-Rodriguez, Luis F. Gomez, Ruben Tolosana, Ruben Vera-Rodriguez, Roberto Daza, Aythami Morales, Julian Fierrez 

> BiometricsAI, Universidad Autónoma de Madrid, Madrid, Spain

Abstract 

Recent advances in photorealistic avatar generation have enabled highly re-alistic talking-head avatars, raising security concerns regarding identity im-personation in AI-mediated communication. To advance in this challeng-ing problem, the task of avatar fingerprinting aims to determine whether two avatar videos are driven by the same human operator or not. How-ever, current public databases in the literature are scarce and based solely on old-fashioned talking-head avatar generators, not representing realistic scenarios for the current task of avatar fingerprinting. To overcome this situation, the present article introduces AVAPrintDB, a new publicly avail-able multi-generator talking-head avatar database for avatar fingerprinting. AVAPrintDB is constructed from two audiovisual corpora and three state-of-the-art avatar generators (GAGAvatar, LivePortrait, HunyuanPortrait), representing different synthesis paradigms, and includes both self- and cross-reenactments to simulate legitimate usage and impersonation scenarios. Building on this database, we also define a standardized and reproducible benchmark for avatar fingerprinting, considering public state-of-the-art avatar fingerprinting systems and exploring novel methods based on Foundation Models (DINOv2 and CLIP). Also, we conduct a comprehensive analysis un-der generator and dataset shift. Our results show that, while identity-related motion cues persist across synthetic avatars, current avatar fingerprinting sys-tems remain highly sensitive to changes in the synthesis pipeline and source domain. The AVAPrintDB, benchmark protocols, and avatar fingerprinting systems are publicly available to facilitate reproducible research.

1. Introduction 

Recent progress in photorealistic avatar generation has enabled the syn-thesis of highly realistic talking-head avatars from a single picture, signifi-cantly advancing applications in telepresence [1], virtual reality [2], digital hu-mans [3], and AI-mediated communication [4]. Large technology companies 1

and startups 2 are actively investing in avatar-based interfaces, with applica-tions ranging from virtual assistants and customer service agents to remote collaboration platforms and personalized digital identities [4]. Industry re-ports 3 estimate that the global market for digital humans and avatar-based communication systems is expected to grow rapidly in the coming years, driven by advances in generative AI and increasing adoption of immersive communication technologies. At the same time, the widespread availability of realistic avatar genera-tion tools introduces new security and privacy risks. Recent incidents have demonstrated that synthetic media can be exploited for impersonation at-tacks, social engineering, and identity fraud. For instance, DeepFake-based scams 4 have been used to impersonate executives in video calls and fraudu-lently authorize financial transactions, leading to losses of millions of dollars in real-world cases 5. As avatar-based communication becomes more preva-lent, these risks extend beyond static DeepFakes to interactive, real-time impersonation scenarios, where an attacker can control a synthetic avatar that visually appears to be a legitimate user. In such settings, traditional authentication methods based on appearance (e.g ., biometric face recognition [5, 6]) become unreliable, since the avatar’s visual identity is fully synthetic and can be arbitrarily chosen or manipu-lated. 2. Related Work 

Avatar fingerprinting addresses the problem of determining whether a synthetic avatar video is driven by its legitimate user or not, focusing on iden-tity cues preserved in facial motion dynamics rather than visual appearance alone. The problem is also related to behavioral biometrics [27, 28], where identity is inferred from human dynamic patterns such as gait, keystroke, or facial motion. Unlike traditional face recognition, which relies heavily on appearance cues, avatar fingerprinting must operate under conditions where appearance is synthetically generated and therefore unreliable. This section provides a revision of the state of the art in avatar finger-printing. Sec. 2.1 summarizes recent advances in photorealistic talking-head avatar generation. Sec. 2.2 describes publicly available databases for avatar fingerprinting. Sec. 2.3 reviews avatar fingerprinting approaches. 42.1. Photorealistic Talking-Head Avatar Generation 

Photorealistic talking-head avatar generation aims to animate a target identity using the facial motion and head pose extracted from a driving sig-nal, typically a video sequence. This task is also commonly referred to as por-trait animation [29], motion transfer [30], or talking-head reenactment [31]. It is important to remark that this task differs from related problems such as novel-view synthesis [32], full 3D head reconstruction [33], or audio-driven animation [34], as the goal is to preserve the appearance of a reference iden-tity while faithfully transferring motion dynamics from another source. Early approaches to facial reenactment relied on parametric face models and 3D morphable models to retarget expressions and head pose, enabling real-time portrait manipulation and reenactment [35, 36]. The emergence of deep generative models enabled realistic reenactment from minimal input, particularly through motion transfer methods that animate a single portrait using learned keypoint dynamics [13, 37]. Subsequent advances in neural rendering and implicit scene representations improved realism and view con-sistency [38, 39], while recent diffusion-based video generation techniques have further increased photorealism and temporal coherence [24, 40]. These methods can be broadly categorized according to their underlying representation and synthesis strategy as follow: 

• Neural rendering approaches , such as NeRF-based (Neural Radi-ance Field) models, represent the head as a continuous radiance field learned from images [38, 41]. While they achieve high visual fidelity and view consistency, they typically require multi-view data and are computationally expensive, which limits their applicability in real-time or one-shot scenarios. 

• Gaussian splatting methods provide a more efficient alternative by representing the head as a set of 3D Gaussian primitives [16, 42, 43]. These approaches enable real-time rendering and controllable anima-tion through parametric face models, although they may exhibit minor geometric inconsistencies. 

• Image-based warping methods operate directly in the image do-main by transferring motion using learned keypoints or flow fields [37, 44]. These methods are computationally efficient and well-suited for 5real-time applications. Recent works such as LivePortrait [23] demon-strate that large-scale training and improved architectures can yield high-quality and stable animations despite the lack of 3D modeling. 

• Diffusion-based methods represent the current state of the art in visual quality. By conditioning video diffusion models on identity and motion signals, they generate temporally coherent and highly realistic avatar videos [24]. These approaches offer strong generalization capa-bilities but typically incur higher computational costs. Across these paradigms, different design choices lead to variations in mo-tion transfer fidelity, temporal consistency, and artifact characteristics. These differences are particularly relevant for avatar fingerprinting, as they can in-fluence how identity-related facial dynamics are preserved or distorted. Con-sequently, evaluating avatar fingerprinting systems across multiple avatar generation methods is essential to assess their robustness under realistic syn-thesis variability, which is the purpose of the present article. 

2.2. Avatar Fingerprinting Databases and Benchmarks 

To date, the number of public databases and benchmarks specifically de-signed for avatar fingerprinting is very limited. The NVFAIR database intro-duced by NVIDIA [7] is currently the largest collection of reenacted talking-head videos, with more than 600,000 videos. NVFAIR comprises scripted and free-form monologues from 46 captured subjects (real data) as well as synthetic reenactments generated with three methods (FaceVid2Vid [13], TPS [14], and LIA [15]), although they are considered old-fashioned nowa-days in comparison to state-of-the-art avatar generators, as discussed in Sec. 2.1. In total NVFAIR covers 161 unique identities (by including ad-ditional CREMA-D [20] and RAVDESS [21] speakers) with self- and cross-reenactments. Access to NVFAIR is restricted via a request form. Also, benchmarking is not possible as the proposed avatar fingerprinting system is not publicly available. To the best of our knowledge, the only fully public avatar fingerprinting database and benchmark so far is the GAGAvatar-Benchmark [12]. How-ever, it only covers a single talking-head avatar generator (GAGAvatar [22]), limiting diversity in appearance and motion dynamics. The proposed AVAPrintDB and benchmark is publicly available and cov-ers multiple up-to-date photorealistic avatar generators (GAGAvatar [22], 6LivePortrait [23] and HunyuanPortrait [24]) and varied source domains ( e.g ., generator and dataset shift), allowing to evaluate state-of-the-art avatar fin-gerprinting systems in realistic scenarios. Finally, as already mentioned in Sec. 1, it is important to highlight that the task of avatar fingerprinting differs from the task of DeepFakes detec-tion [8, 9, 10], where the main goal is to distinguish real from synthetic content. As a result, public databases in the topic of DeepFakes detection cannot be used for avatar fingerprinting. 

2.3. Avatar Fingerprinting Systems 

The task of avatar fingerprinting is formulated as a pairwise decision prob-lem: given two avatar videos, the goal is to determine whether they are driven by the same underlying identity or not. This differs from authentication, which evaluates a sample against a claimed identity ( e.g ., access control sce-narios), and from identification, where a sample is matched against a gallery of known identities. Avatar fingerprinting constitutes the most fundamental setting, as it isolates the ability of a model to capture identity-dependent behavioral signals without relying on predefined identity classes. Early works on avatar fingerprinting demonstrated that such behavioral cues persist through avatar generation. In [7], identity was modeled through facial landmark dynamics using temporal convolutional networks, showing that user-specific motion patterns can be discriminative even in highly re-alistic avatars (they achieved around 87% AUC in their best setting). Sim-ilarly, [11, 12] proposed a graph-based approach that captures structured relationships between facial landmarks over time, obtaining similar results in their respective experiments (86.6% and 83% AUC respectively). A common characteristic of existing approaches is their reliance on landmark-based representations, which provide invariance to appearance and mitigate the influence of rendering artifacts. However, this design choice may also limit the ability to capture fine-grained visual cues present in the pixel domain, particularly those related to texture-dependent motion or subtle temporal inconsistencies. For this reason, in the present work, we explore for the first time the use of Foundation Models (DINOv2 [25] and CLIP [26]) for avatar fingerprinting, which can encode richer spatio-temporal information without explicit geometric modeling. 73. 3.1. Real Databases: Source Videos 

We select two widely used video datasets in the literature as they consider realistic scenarios for avatar fingerprinting: CREMA-D [20] and RAVDESS [21]. Our selection is guided by three main considerations. First, both datasets are distributed under permissive licenses that allow redistribution and deriva-tive use, making them suitable for constructing and releasing a reproducible benchmark. Second, they have been employed in prior work on avatar fin-gerprinting and behavioral biometric analysis, including [7, 12, 45], which facilitates continuity. Third, they include structured variations in emotional expression and scripted utterances. These characteristics enable controlled experimental protocols in which different subjects can be evaluated while producing identical sentences and emotional expressions. Such control is es-sential for avatar fingerprinting studies, as it allows identity-related motion cues to be isolated from linguistic content, affective expression, and other confounding factors. For example, the datasets make it possible to compare avatars of different individuals speaking the same sentence with similar affect, or the same individual rendered through different avatar pipelines, enabling fine-grained evaluation of biometric consistency. Initially, we also considered the real videos from the NVFAIR dataset [7], which includes additional videoconferencing recordings from 46 participants. Unfortunately, its licensing terms restrict redistribution and limit demo-graphic analysis ( e.g ., ethnicity, age, and gender), so we could not included in the proposed benchmark. Consequently, CREMA-D [20] and RAVDESS [21] provide the best balance between legal permissibility, experimental control, and comparability with prior avatar fingerprinting research. 

3.1.1. CREMA-D Dataset 

CREMA-D [20] (Crowd-sourced Emotional Multimodal Actors Dataset) is a large-scale audiovisual corpus designed to study emotional speech and 8multimodal expression. It contains 7,438 video recordings of 91 actors (48 male, 43 female) between 20 and 74 years, representing a demographically diverse group with respect to race and ethnicity. Actors were recorded under controlled indoor conditions with frontal viewpoints, consistent lighting and high-quality audio capture. Each participant delivers 12 semantically neutral sentences, which are performed using multiple emotional expressions: anger, disgust, fear, hap-piness, neutral, and sadness. The recordings exhibit natural variation in intensity and articulation while preserving consistent linguistic content. All videos were recorded on 460 × 360 pixels at 30 FPS. 

3.1.2. RAVDESS Dataset 

RAVDESS [21] (Ryerson Audio-Visual Database of Emotional Speech and Song) is a high-quality audiovisual corpus designed for research on emo-tional expression and multimodal speech. It includes recordings from 24 professional actors (12 female, 12 male) speaking and singing with a range of emotional expressions. In this article, we use the speech subset, which con-tains 1,440 video recordings corresponding to two repetitions of 60 scripted utterances produced by each actor. Recordings were captured under tightly controlled studio conditions, in-cluding uniform lighting, frontal framing, neutral backgrounds, and high-fidelity audio and video. Actors deliver semantically neutral sentences using multiple emotional states (neutral, calm, happy, sad, angry, fearful, surprised, and disgust) with two intensity levels for all emotions but neutral. All videos are 1280 × 720 pixels and were recorded at 30FPS. 

3.1.3. Post-Processing Stage 

We discard the four videos reported by the creators of CREMA-D [20] as corrupted, following the recommendations in their official repository 7. We further filter the dataset to retain only the 85 identities that contain at least 72 valid samples each. This result in a subset of 6,120 videos, ensuring a minimum number of samples per identity and reducing class imbalance. In addition, where metadata are available, the selection is stratified to preserve diversity in gender, age ranges, and ethnicity.

3.2. Photorealistic Talking-Head Avatar Generators 

A key aspect of our proposed AVAPrintDB is the inclusion of multiple state-of-the-art avatar generators, each relying on different modeling assump-tions and motion transfer mechanisms: one from the Gaussian splatting fam-ily (GAGAvatar [22]), one from the warping-based family (LivePortrait [23]), and one from the diffusion-based family (HunyuanPortrait [24]). These three generators are selected as they represent distinct synthesis paradigms and 10 therefore introduce different types of motion transfer artifacts that may in-fluence the avatar fingerprinting task. They are specifically selected due to practical considerations of availability, efficiency, and quality. GAGA-vatar [22] is a one-shot 3D Gaussian head avatar method that reconstructs a controllable 3D head from a single image in a single forward pass. It is lightweight in GPU memory and offers real-time reenactment speeds thanks to its efficient 3D Gaussian representation, making it easy to use for our purposes. LivePortrait [23] provides a fast, efficient 2D animation solution with minimal memory overhead, and the code/models are publicly avail-able. LivePortrait’s ease of use and high speed, combined with qualitatively good visual results, made it a practical choice for our comparisons. Finally, HunyuanPortrait [24] considers a state-of-the-art diffusion model. Although diffusion models are heavier, HunyuanPortrait is open-source and produces great visual fidelity and temporal consistency. Its inclusion allows to evaluate a high-quality diffusion approach against the faster lightweight methods. From a biometric behavioral perspective, the generation process can be in-terpreted as a transformation that maps real facial motion into a synthetic do-main. Ideally, this transformation should preserve identity-specific temporal patterns while altering appearance. However, in practice, avatar generators may introduce biases, artifacts, or normalization effects that partially sup-press or modify these cues. As a result, models trained or evaluated on a sin-gle generator may inadvertently learn generator-specific artifacts instead of robust behavioral patterns. To address this limitation, our proposed bench-mark incorporates multiple generators with diverse characteristics, enabling systematic evaluation under intra- and cross-generator conditions. This de-sign allows us to analyze whether avatar fingerprinting systems generalize across synthesis pipelines or rely on artifacts tied to a specific generator.

4. Proposed Avatar Fingerprinting Benchmark 

Beyond the proposed AVAPrintDB itself, we also define a standardized experimental benchmark, including publicly available avatar fingerprinting systems, identity-disjoint development and evaluation splits, predefined veri-fication trial lists, and evaluation procedures. This design ensures that differ-ent avatar fingerprinting systems can be compared under identical conditions, facilitating reproducible research and fair benchmarking. This section is or-ganized as follows: the details of the proposed experimental protocol are included in Sec. 4.1 whereas in Sec. 4.2 we describe the avatar fingerprinting systems considered in the benchmark, including the exploration for the first time of Foundation Models (DINOv2 [25] and CLIP [26]).

Using the above protocol, we define three main experimental scenarios: 

• Intra-dataset, intra-generator : the development and evaluation of the avatar fingerprinting systems are performed on avatar videos generated from the same source dataset and with the same avatar generator. These are the baseline results. 

• Intra-dataset, cross-generator : the development and evaluation of the avatar fingerprinting systems are performed on the same source dataset, but the avatar generator used for development differs from the one used at the final evaluation. This evaluates robustness to avatar synthesis-model shift while keeping the underlying source domain fixed. 

• Cross-dataset, intra-generator : the development and evaluation of the avatar fingerprinting systems are performed using the same avatar gen-erator, but the source dataset differs between development and the fi-nal evaluation. This setting measures generalization across acquisition conditions, subject populations, and dataset-specific biases. All these experimental scenarios allow us to disentangle three impor-tant axes of variation in avatar fingerprinting: identity generalization (un-seen subjects), generator generalization (unseen avatar synthesis pipelines), and dataset generalization (unseen source domains). This benchmark pro-vides a controlled framework for understanding whether avatar fingerprint-ing systems learn robust identity-related motion cues or overfit to dataset-or generator-specific artifacts. 

4.1.3. Performance Metrics 

Similar to previous approaches in the literature [7, 11, 12], we assess avatar fingerprinting performance using the Area Under the Receiver Oper-ating Characteristic Curve (AUC-ROC). This metric measures the ability of the system to separate target from non-target trials across all possible deci-sion thresholds, and is particularly appropriate here because it is threshold-independent and robust to moderate class imbalance in the number of gen-uine and impostor pairs. Unless otherwise stated, all results in the article are reported in terms of AUC (%). 17 4.2. Avatar Fingerprinting Systems

4.2.1. Deep Learning Methods 

To advance in the topic of avatar fingerprinting under realistic synthe-sis conditions, we require systems that are reproducible, fully specified, and publicly accessible. Although recent studies have been presented in the lit-erature [7, 11], none of them have released their implementations, training pipelines, or pretrained weights, making it impossible to reproduce their re-sults or conduct fair comparative evaluations. For this reason, in the proposed benchmark we evaluate our publicly available graph-based avatar fingerprinting system 8 presented in [12], which, to the best of our knowledge, is currently the only fully reproducible imple-mentation. This system, as depicted in Fig. 2, takes as input a set of 109 facial landmarks per frame in an avatar video, structured as graphs. Then a Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) generates a graph embedding for each graph (frame), and finally the aggregation mechanism (temporal attention pooling) yields a single embedding for the video. By evaluating the only pub-licly available avatar fingerprinting system on a more diverse dataset, we aim to provide insights into the robustness and limitations of current technology and to establish a foundation for future comparative studies once additional methods become publicly accessible. In addition, we explore for the first time the use of public Founda-tional Models and attention-based neural networks for avatar fingerprinting. Foundation Models provide a complementary perspective, analyzing whether general-purpose visual representations can capture behavioral biometric cues in avatar videos. 6. Discussion and Conclusion 

This article introduces AVAPrintDB, a publicly available multi-generator photorealistic talking-head avatar database and benchmark for the task of avatar fingerprinting. Its main strength is that it enables, for the first time in a fully reproducible way, controlled evaluation of avatar fingerprinting sys-tems across multiple state-of-the-art synthesis pipelines and source datasets. This makes it possible to study whether current avatar fingerprinting systems truly capture identity-related behavioral cues or instead rely on generator-or dataset-specific artifacts. The proposed benchmark and results achieved using two state-of-the-art public avatar fingerprinting systems highlight two main findings. First, avatar fingerprinting is feasible under controlled conditions, supporting the hypothesis that identity-related motion cues can persist after avatar synthe-sis. Second, current avatar fingerprinting systems remain sensitive to both generator and dataset shift. These results indicate that present methods do not yet learn fully robust and invariant behavioral representations. Overall, AVAPrintDB provides a reproducible and extensible benchmark for studying avatar fingerprinting under diverse synthesis and data condi-tions. We hope it facilitates the development of more robust and practically useful avatar fingerprinting systems for authentication in avatar-mediated communication.
