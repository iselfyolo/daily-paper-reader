## Abstract 

Connectivist MOOCs (cMOOCs) rely on learner-driven interac-tion, yet their intentionally light facilitation makes it difficult to design generative AI participation that is both scalable and edu-cationally productive. This design-based research study examined how human–GenAI interaction can be designed for discussion fa-cilitation through a collaborative AI-in-the-loop workflow. Across two iterations in a five-week cMOOC ( 𝑁 = 606 ), we designed, de-ployed, and evaluated a facilitation system that combined network-structure-driven target selection, discourse-adaptive response roles, and mandatory human review before AI participation became visi-ble in the community. Iteration 1 (Weeks 1–2) focused on refining the interaction design, showing that the most sustainable facilita-tion patterns were Guide (70.4%) and Amplifier (28.5%) responses and yielding explicit moderation standards for publishable AI par-ticipation. Iteration 2 (Weeks 3–5) examined how different forms of AI-mediated interaction related to social and cognitive presence. AI participation selectively enhanced Open Communication ( 𝑟 = 0.188 ,

𝑝 = .006 ), Networked Cohesion ( 𝑟 = 0.274 , 𝑝 < .001 ), and overall so-cial presence ( 𝑟 = 0.162 , 𝑝 = .015 ), while cognitive presence showed no overall improvement. More importantly, direct learner–agent interaction was associated with significantly higher social pres-ence ( 𝑟 = 0.186 , 𝑝 = .004 ) and higher-order cognitive indicators— Integration ( 𝑟 = 0.206 , 𝑝 = .001 ) and Resolution/Creation ( 𝑟 = 0.350 ,

𝑝 < .001 )—than mere co-presence in AI-involved threads. The find-ings suggest that effective GenAI-supported discussion depends less on AI presence alone than on interaction design: reciprocal exchange, discourse-adaptive facilitation roles, and collaborative human review appear to be key conditions for productive AI par-ticipation in online learning communities.

## 1 Introduction 

Massive Open Online Courses (MOOCs) have substantially broad-ened access to learning across geographic regions and learner pop-ulations, becoming an established modality within open and contin-uing higher education [ 18 , 20 ]. However, sustaining meaningful en-gagement over time remains a persistent challenge. Research consis-tently demonstrates that social, cognitive, and teaching presence— the three pillars of the Community of Inquiry (CoI) framework [22 ]—are strongly associated with participation quality, learner sat-isfaction, and perceived learning in online environments [ 24 , 49 ]. In large-scale MOOCs, these presences often remain underdevel-oped due to limited instructor capacity and high learner attrition [1, 8, 34, 45]. This challenge is particularly acute in connectivist MOOCs (cMOOCs), which emphasize learner autonomy, distributed knowledge produc-tion, and self-organized participation [ 14 , 29 , 55 ]. Unlike content-centered xMOOCs, cMOOCs intentionally maintain minimal fa-cilitation to preserve learner agency. This design choice, however, creates a practical dilemma: while peer interaction can be substan-tive, discourse often remains at surface levels without consistent facilitative support [ 2, 8]. What is needed is a facilitation mecha-nism that is scalable, unobtrusive, and compatible with peer-driven discourse dynamics. Pedagogical Conversational Agents (PCAs), enabled by recent advances in large language models (LLMs), offer a promising solu-tion. PCAs can engage in dialogue, provide socio-emotional support, and scaffold reasoning processes [ 19 , 28 , 42 ]. In cMOOCs, a PCA designed as a learning partner rather than an authoritative tutor may address facilitation gaps while preserving the learner-driven nature of discourse. In the present study, the PCA served as the con-crete form of GenAI participation. However, deploying LLM-driven PCAs in authentic educational settings raises legitimate concerns about content quality, role consistency, and the necessity for human oversight [ 30 , 70 ]. More fundamentally, the design problem is not simply whether GenAI should participate in discussion, but how human–GenAI interaction should be configured so that PCA par-ticipation becomes educationally useful rather than merely visible [6, 38]. Current research offers only partial guidance on this question. Most PCA studies examine structured courses or small-group set-tings [ 25 , 57 , 68 ], often treating GenAI support as a unitary inter-vention rather than distinguishing among interaction designs [ 11 ]. Yet several design decisions are likely to matter in large-scale online discussion: when GenAI should intervene, how it should respond, 

whether its participation should be filtered through human review, and what kind of learner–agent interaction is educationally conse-quential. In community settings, mere PCA co-presence may not produce the same outcomes as reciprocal learner–agent exchange, and publishable PCA participation may require governance mecha-nisms that are integral to interaction design rather than secondary quality-control procedures.

To address these gaps, this study employed a design-based re-search (DBR) approach [ 3] to iteratively design, deploy, and evaluate a collaborative AI-in-the-loop facilitation workflow in a five-week cMOOC with 606 learners. The workflow, implemented through 

FaciHub , combined network-structure-based focal post selection, emergent facilitation roles, and mandatory human review prior to publication. Iteration 1 focused on refining these interaction design decisions in authentic deployment. Iteration 2 examined how different forms of AI-mediated participation—with-PCA ver-sus without-PCA discussion and, within intervention threads, direct interaction versus co-presence—were associated with social and cognitive presence. This study makes three contributions. First, it reframes GenAI-supported facilitation in cMOOCs as an interaction design problem and develops a collaborative AI-in-the-loop workflow grounded in networked discussion dynamics. Second, it provides deployment evidence showing which facilitation roles and moderation criteria made PCA participation publishable and contextually appropriate. Third, it offers empirical evidence that discussion outcomes depend less on PCA presence alone than on the form of learner–agent interaction, thereby yielding evidence-based design implications for GenAI participation in online learning communities. 

## 2 Theoretical Foundations 2.1 Community of Inquiry (CoI) Framework 

The CoI framework [ 21 ] conceptualizes online learning through the interaction of three interdependent presences. Social presence is “the ability of participants in a CoI to project their characteristics into the community, thereby presenting themselves to other partic-ipants as ‘real people’ ” [ 22 ]. It encompasses affective expression, open communication, and group cohesion—dimensions that estab-lish relational trust and psychological safety as prerequisites for sustained dialogue. Cognitive presence refers to “the extent to which the participants in any particular configuration of a community of inquiry can construct meaning through sustained dialogue” [ 21 ]. It unfolds through a four-phase practical inquiry cycle: triggering event, exploration, integration, and resolution [ 22 ]. Teaching pres-ence includes design and organization, facilitation of discourse, and direct instruction. Rather than functioning solely as content deliv-ery, teaching presence structures participation and helps discussion advance from idea exchange to deeper inquiry [53]. These three presences are mutually reinforcing: social presence maintains open exchange, cognitive presence reflects the depth of collective meaning-making, and teaching presence provides direc-tion and coherence [ 1 , 35 ]. A meta-analysis by Richardson et al .[49] demonstrates robust links between social presence and learner satisfaction. Teaching presence—through task design [ 51 ], support strategies [6], platform affordances [32], and interaction structure [62, 64]—can shape presence trajectories. 

## 2.2 CoI in cMOOCs: Key Differences 

xMOOCs and cMOOCs differ in instructional design and in how teaching presence is enacted, which in turn shapes social and cog-nitive presence [ 2 ]. xMOOCs typically follow a structured, content-centered model with instructor-led delivery, predetermined learn-ing paths, and formal assessment [ 31 ]. This structure provides clear teaching presence, but social and cognitive presence may remain peripheral when peer interaction is weakly integrated. In contrast, cMOOCs are grounded in connectivist principles [ 29 , 55 ]. They emphasize distributed content creation, peer-assisted learning, and minimal instructor intervention. Knowledge is co-constructed through networked interaction, and teaching presence is intention-ally light to preserve learner agency [ 2 , 14 , 15 ]. Table 1 summarizes how the three presences manifest differently across the two MOOC types. MOOC-specific validation studies suggest that CoI presences operate differently at scale. Large-sample analyses have supported the original three-factor structure but also reported improved fit for an expanded six-factor model, in which design and organization, affective expression, and resolution emerge as distinct dimensions [ 8, 34 ]. These findings indicate that scale, participation diversity, and reduced facilitation visibility render certain presence dimen-sions more salient than in smaller online courses. Although cMOOCs can stimulate active peer discourse, facilita-tion quality is often uneven. Existing cMOOC research has exam-ined content production [ 9 , 69 ], social capital [ 29 , 37 ], and network interaction patterns [ 36 , 59 , 63 ], yet CoI-oriented analysis of social and cognitive presence remains limited. Two questions are there-fore central to this study: how can facilitative support be provided when teaching presence is distributed, and how should presence be measured to fit cMOOC interaction patterns? The following subsections address these questions. 

## 2.3 Implications for the Current Study 

2.3.1 Distributed teaching presence and alternative facilitation. In cMOOCs, teaching presence is spread across the community rather than held by a single instructor [ 2]. Facilitation emerges from peer contributions and collective sense-making. However, this dis-tributed model often results in uneven and inconsistent support. Many learners share content, but few take on facilitative roles such as guiding discussion, asking follow-up questions, or synthesizing ideas [ 45 , 63 ]. Without stable facilitation, discussions may remain shallow or stall before reaching deeper inquiry [71]. This gap points to the need for a lightweight, scalable mechanism that can provide consistent facilitative support without replacing learner agency. Prior work has shown that instructional interven-tions can be evaluated through their effects on social and cognitive presence [ 51 ]. Following this approach, a PCA can serve as one way to operationalize teaching presence in cMOOCs. The goal is not to replace peer discourse but to enact facilitative functions that stabilize and extend it. Therefore, this study designs FaciHub as a form of distributed teaching presence. By taking on multiple facilitative roles within networked discussions, FaciHub aims to support social and cogni-tive presence while preserving the learner-driven nature of cMOOCs. 

2.3.2 Adapting social and cognitive presence for cMOOCs. Fur-thermore, in professional learning communities, the endpoint of inquiry frequently involves the creation of original knowledge arti-facts rather than task resolution alone [ 14 , 15 ]. Two adaptations are therefore necessary—not cosmetic terminological adjustments, but theoretically motivated reconceptualizations fitted to the structural and functional conditions of cMOOC learning. 

From Group Cohesion to Networked Cohesion. Group cohesion in the original CoI framework refers to the interpersonal bonds that form within a closed, stable group: the shared sense of commu-nity that motivates sustained commitment to dialogue [ 22 ]. This conception is grounded in small-group psychology, where cohe-sion accrues through repeated interaction, mutual familiarity, and deepening affective trust. In cMOOCs, no such stable group exists. Participation is open, continuous, and structurally fluid; learners navigate a distributed network of peers rather than a fixed cohort. Social network theory provides an alternative framing: in networks characterized by weak ties and open participation, community be-longing is expressed not through dyadic closeness but through shared language, collective norms, and identification with the pro-fessional community as a whole—markers that signal membership in a broader learning network rather than a bounded group [ 23 , 55 ]. This reconceptualization is supported empirically: large-sample CoI validation studies in MOOCs report that cohesion dimensions operate differently at scale, with network-level belonging emerg-ing as structurally distinct from interpersonal affiliation [ 8, 34 ]. Networked learning research further confirms that community identification—rather than dyadic bonding—is the characteristic sociality mechanism in cMOOCs, manifested in learners’ use of community-specific terminology, shared referential frames, and col-lective professional language [ 29 , 45 ]. Accordingly, Group Cohesion 

is reconceptualized as Networked Cohesion (NC), with coding indi-cators reoriented toward network-level expressions of belonging. 

From Resolution to Resolution and Creation. In the original prac-tical inquiry model, resolution denotes the application of newly constructed understanding to the problem that triggered the in-quiry cycle [ 22 ]. While this captures an important endpoint for academic task contexts, it does not adequately represent a theo-retically and empirically prominent outcome in professional learn-ing communities: the production of original knowledge artifacts. Knowledge creation scholarship distinguishes between knowledge 

use and knowledge production : professional learning communities generate new professional tools—lesson designs, teaching frame-works, assessment instruments—that externalize tacit expertise and contribute to a shared knowledge base [ 43 ]. The trialogical model of learning [ 43 ] explicitly identifies artifact creation as a third mode of knowing, analytically distinct from knowledge transmission and from social construction through dialogue, and particularly salient in expert communities engaged in sustained professional practice. In teacher professional development specifically, this dimension manifests as practitioners’ production of shareable professional knowledge that advances both individual competence and com-munity capability [ 58 , 60 ]. Empirical work in cMOOC contexts confirms that artifact creation constitutes an observable, analyti-cally distinct endpoint of cognitive engagement that the original resolution category cannot adequately capture [ 16 , 69 ]. Accordingly, 

Resolution is expanded to Resolution and Creation (RC), encompass-ing both knowledge application (RC-1: Applying Solutions) and original artifact production (RC-2: Artifact Creation) as distinct higher-order cognitive indicators [63]. These adaptations are operationalized in the Iteration 2 coding framework (Section 7.2.3). 

## 3 Related Work 

This section reviews empirical work relevant to the design and eval-uation of collaborative GenAI-supported facilitation. Two themes structure the review: (1) how PCAs can operationalize teaching pres-ence in cMOOCs through intervention targeting, facilitation roles, and human oversight; and (2) why different forms of learner–PCA interaction may produce different social and cognitive outcomes. 

## 3.1 PCAs as Operationalization of Teaching Presence 

3.1.1 Network-Structure-Driven Intervention in Online Learning. 

Network analysis has been widely employed to understand interac-tion patterns in online learning communities [ 51 , 72 ]. Centrality metrics can identify influential posts, active participants, or emerg-ing discussion clusters [ 37 ]. In MOOCs, network-based approaches have been applied to analyze learner engagement, knowledge flow, and community structure [29]. However, most studies employ network analysis for post-hoc description rather than real-time intervention [ 66 ]. Few have ex-plored how network structure can guide where and when a PCA should participate [ 17 ]. In cMOOCs, where discussions are decen-tralized and non-linear, targeting high-centrality posts may help PCA responses reach more learners while aligning with the emer-gent nature of community discourse [ 12 ]. This study employs a Xiao et al. 

hypergraph-based approach to identify focal posts for PCA inter-vention based on closeness centrality, which captures a node’s average distance to all other nodes and thus indicates potential reach within the discussion network. 

3.1.2 Facilitator Roles in Online Discussions. Research on online facilitation identifies several key roles that support productive dis-cussion: guiding conversation, amplifying valuable contributions, providing socio-emotional support, and prompting critical reflec-tion [ 7, 52 , 71 ]. In instructor-led courses, a designated facilitator typically performs these roles. In cMOOCs, such roles must emerge from peer contributions or platform-mediated features. Recent research on conversational agent design has explored how different agent roles influence learning outcomes. Nguyen [41] demonstrated that role design affects how learners engage with agent-generated content. Studies on human facilitators indicate that when facilitative behaviors appear—such as summarizing ideas, posing follow-up questions, or affirming contributions—discussions tend to be more sustained and substantive [ 71 ]. Yet such behaviors are unevenly distributed in peer-driven communities [ 2 , 54 ]. This suggests that effective PCAs should enact multiple facilitative roles matched to different contribution types, rather than adopting a single instructor persona. 

3.1.3 Content Quality and Human Oversight in LLM-Driven Agents. 

LLMs enable PCAs to generate fluent and context-responsive dia-logue at scale. However, deployment in authentic educational set-tings introduces quality risks extending beyond linguistic fluency [ 30 , 70 ]. LLM-generated responses may hallucinate—fabricating facts, personal experiences, or instructional details absent from the discussion context. Responses may also drift from the assigned role, creating tone or intent mismatches with the pedagogical purpose of the interaction. These concerns are amplified in cMOOCs, where discussions are learner-generated, topically diverse, and rapidly evolving. These risks raise a fundamental governance question: how should authority be allocated between AI systems and human profession-als? Two paradigms are commonly distinguished: Human-in-the-Loop (HITL), where AI leads with human oversight, and AI-in-the-Loop (AITL), where human decision-making remains primary and AI provides computational support [ 40 ]. In education, AITL is particularly relevant because pedagogical decisions require profes-sional judgment that current AI systems cannot reliably provide independently. Prior work on AI-assisted facilitation has predom-inantly adopted HITL designs [ 6, 56 ], but practical frameworks for human–AI collaborative review—including criteria, workflow, and decision rules—remain underdeveloped. This gap is not only a governance issue but also an interaction design issue: human re-view influences which PCA utterances become socially visible and therefore shapes the educational interaction that learners actually encounter. 

## 3.2 Effects of PCAs on Social and Cognitive Presence 

3.2.1 Empirical Evidence from MOOC Contexts. Empirical CoI stud-ies in MOOCs indicate that cognitive presence tends to cluster in early inquiry phases (triggering and exploration), while higher-order phases (integration and resolution) occur less frequently [ 24 , 39 ]. Social presence is similarly uneven, with active partic-ipants demonstrating higher levels than peripheral participants [ 45 , 72 ]. Strengthening teaching presence is consistently identi-fied as a key lever for improving both presences [ 53 , 72 ], but how to provide such support in cMOOCs—where formal facilitation is intentionally minimal—remains an open question. PCAs offer one potential approach. They can support social presence through self-disclosure prompts, empathic responses, and personalized addressing [ 26 , 27 , 42 ], and support cognitive pres-ence through content-based scaffolding that encourages idea de-velopment and synthesis [ 6, 32 , 41 ]. Recent research demonstrates that design choices—such as knowledge-oriented versus emotion-oriented scaffolding—produce differential effects across presence dimensions [ 38 ], highlighting the need to examine how specific agent designs map to specific outcomes. However, most PCA stud-ies are conducted in structured courses or small groups, with agents positioned as tutors or feedback tools [ 25 , 57 ]. Evidence from large-scale, learner-driven cMOOCs remains limited. 

3.2.2 Interaction Mode as a Moderating Factor. Different learner– PCA interaction modes may shape presence in distinct ways. Ba et al . [6] found that idea-oriented AI feedback enhanced cognitive presence but also triggered negative emotions, while task-oriented feedback promoted social interaction. Two modes are particularly relevant in cMOOC discussions: direct interaction , where a learner exchanges replies with the agent, and co-presence , where the agent participates in the same thread without direct exchange with a given learner. The distinction parallels research on social pres-ence in computer-mediated communication, which differentiates between interacting with others and merely perceiving their pres-ence [ 47 ]. Research on human–AI group interaction suggests that agent positioning can shift interaction patterns [ 17 ], but compara-tive evidence on how these two modes affect social and cognitive presence in cMOOCs remains scarce. This distinction is central for evidence-based interaction design because it tests whether ed-ucational value derives from reciprocal exchange with the PCA or simply from the PCA’s ambient participation in the discussion space. 

## 3.3 Research Questions 

The preceding review identifies two gaps. First, how to design scal-able, discourse-adaptive human–GenAI interaction for cMOOC facilitation remains unclear, especially when human review is re-quired before PCA participation becomes visible in the community. Second, whether different learner–PCA interaction modes (direct interaction vs. co-presence) produce differential presence outcomes has not been empirically examined. This study addresses both gaps through two research questions: 

• RQ1 (Iteration 1): How can a collaborative AI-in-the-loop interaction design for network-embedded facilitation be iteratively refined to support publishable and pedagogically appropriate PCA participation in cMOOCs? 

• RQ2 (Iteration 2): How are different forms of PCA-mediated interaction—PCA participation and learner–PCA interac-tion mode (direct interaction vs. co-presence)—associated with social and cognitive presence in cMOOC discussions? Designing Human–GenAI Interaction for cMOOC Discussion Facilitation 

## 4 Interaction Design of the Collaborative AI-in-the-Loop Workflow 

This section presents the initial interaction design of the collabo-rative AI-in-the-loop workflow, which was subsequently refined through the two DBR iterations. The workflow was designed to structure how GenAI would participate in learner-driven discussion rather than merely adding a generic AI presence to the forum. It comprised three design decisions: (1) targeting design , which deter-mined which posts or comments would receive PCA attention; (2) 

response-role design , which determined how the PCA would partic-ipate once a target was selected; and (3) participation governance design , which determined how human facilitators would review PCA responses before they became publicly visible. To implement this workflow reliably in authentic cMOOC prac-tice, and to make it reusable for facilitators, we developed Faci-Hub —a Human–AI Collaboration Hub for Facilitators. As shown in Figure 1, FaciHub operationalized the three design decisions through corresponding modules: the focal post/comment selec-tion component (module B) supported the targeting design, the role-based content generation component (module C) supported the response-role design, and the facilitator review interface (mod-ule A) supported the participation governance design. FaciHub is introduced here as the platform through which the collaborative AI-in-the-loop workflow could be enacted consistently and examined empirically. 

## 4.1 Participation Governance Design 

The workflow was integrated into the cMOOC platform via a Chrome extension that supported facilitator review and publica-tion decisions. As shown in Figure 2, facilitators could inspect candidate PCA replies together with the focal post, thread context, assigned role, and moderation status, then either approve or reject the response before publication. This design made human review a constitutive part of the learner-visible interaction: only approved PCA responses entered the discussion space. This governance layer served two functions. Pedagogically, it ensured that PCA participation remained contextually appropriate and aligned with facilitation goals. Methodologically, it generated the acceptance and rejection records later used in Iteration 1 to refine moderation standards and prompt instructions. Thus, the interface was treated as the mechanism through which human judgment shaped publicly visible PCA participation. 

## 4.2 Targeting Design 

The targeting design identified posts and comments warranting PCA responses through two daily mechanisms. The first mechanism is network-structure triggering . Based on platform behavioral logs, the system constructs a hypergraph from the most recent two days of activity. In this representation, hyperedges represent interaction events, and nodes represent users (U), posts (P), and comments/replies (C)–that is "who interacts with whom and what the interaction entails." Figure 3 illustrates an example network. The system ranks nodes by closeness centrality and selects the top 5% of P-type and C-type nodes as candidate intervention targets. These targets represent active discussion areas, enabling PCA responses to reach more learners while respecting the emergent, non-predetermined pathways characteristic of cMOOCs. The second mechanism is learner-reply triggering . When a learner directly replies to a PCA comment, that reply is automati-cally added as a target in the next scheduled processing run. Together, these mechanisms linked PCA participation to network position and learner behavior rather than to a predetermined post-ing schedule. Candidate sets from both mechanisms were merged and used to construct the prompt context sent to the LLM ( kimi-k2-turbo-preview , 𝑡𝑒𝑚𝑝𝑒𝑟𝑎𝑡𝑢𝑟𝑒 = 0.6). ## 4.3 Response-Role Design 

The response-role design specified how the PCA should participate once a target had been selected. Four roles were initially configured to address different discourse conditions: Guide, Amplifier, Em-pathizer, and Critical Inquirer. The Guide and Amplifier roles draw on established facilitation practices in cMOOCs. The Empathizer 

and Critical Inquirer roles were included to address affective support and reflective questioning, respectively. Each role was matched to target comments based on textual characteristics and followed a distinct interaction objective and linguistic style. The Guide role scaffolded deeper reflection and invited further discussion. The Amplifier role recognized the value of practice-based sharing and encouraged broader peer exchange. The Empathizer role provided emotional support and psycholog-ical safety. The Critical Inquirer role used gentle questioning to promote critical reflection and boundary awareness. In this sense, the role system functioned as an interaction logic rather than as a set of independent personas: it defined what kind of PCA partic-ipation would be attempted under different discourse conditions. Technical details on multi-agent role generation are provided in Appendix 10.2. Taken together, these three design elements established the ini-tial workflow examined in the DBR process. Iteration 1 evaluated whether the targeting, role-selection, and human-review arrange-ments produced publishable and pedagogically appropriate PCA participation. Iteration 2 then examined whether different forms of PCA-mediated interaction within this workflow were associated with different discussion outcomes. 

## 5 Research Design 5.1 Design-Based Research Approach 

To address the two research questions, this study adopted a DBR approach [ 48 ] to iteratively design, deploy, and evaluate a collabo-rative AI-in-the-loop facilitation workflow in a five-week authentic cMOOC setting. Rather than treating GenAI participation as a single intervention, the DBR process focused on refining three interaction design decisions: (1) targeting design —which discussion contribu-tions should receive AI facilitation, (2) response-role design —how the AI should participate in discourse, and (3) participation governance design —how human review should regulate which AI responses be-come publicly visible. ## 5.2 Research Context 

The study was conducted in a Chinese cMOOC titled Front-line Teachers Explore AI Teaching 2.0 (November 24 to December 29, 2025), hosted on a university-developed open learning platform by a Chinese university. Designed with reference to UNESCO’s AI Competency Framework for Teachers , the course aimed to strengthen participants’ understanding of AI in education and enhance their practical competencies. The five-week course comprised four the-matic modules: (1) AI Attitude and Ethics, (2) AI Knowledge and Tools, (3) AI Teaching Methods, and (4) AI Professional Develop-ment. Participants engaged in discussions, experience sharing, and reflection on applying AI-supported pedagogical strategies in pro-fessional practice. 

FaciHub was embedded in discussion threads to augment facilitator– learner interaction. As illustrated in Figure 5, the PCA participated alongside learners by generating context-aware replies to partici-pant posts. All replies underwent human facilitator review prior to publication, making human review part of the interaction design rather than a post-hoc audit step. This arrangement positioned PCA contributions within natural peer discussion while ensuring that only pedagogically appropriate AI participation became visible in the community.

## 6.2 Methodology 

6.2.1 Procedure. During Weeks 1–2, the facilitation workflow was deployed in the cMOOC forum. The targeting design identified candidate discussion targets daily based on the hypergraph repre-sentation of community interactions, and the response-role design generated replies through four emergent roles—Guide, Amplifier, Empathizer, and Critical Inquirer. All generated responses were routed to the facilitator review interface rather than being published directly. Human facilitators reviewed each response and made accept/reject decisions. This participation-governance process served dual pur-poses: quality control and empirical grounding for the moderation standards that determined which PCA utterances entered the visi-ble community discourse. Responses were evaluated along three predefined dimensions: role and task alignment, interactional ap-propriateness, and factual plausibility with contextual coherence. Although these dimensions were specified prior to deployment, the specific acceptance and rejection criteria were iteratively refined through facilitator–researcher discussion during the review process (see Section 6.3.2). 6.2.3 Analytical Approach. The analysis proceeded in two stages. First, role analysis examined response frequency for each of the four roles and assessed how the response-role design aligned with observed discourse characteristics. This stage identified which forms of PCA participation predominated during deployment and informed role refinement decisions for Iteration 2. Second, content quality analysis calculated overall acceptance and rejection rates from facilitator decisions and examined tem-poral trends in acceptance rates across the deployment period. The review process also supported refinement of the participation-governance protocol, translating the initial review dimensions into explicit acceptance and rejection criteria for publicly visible PCA interaction. 

## 6.3 Results 

6.3.1 Role and Discourse Characteristics Analysis. The role distri-bution revealed a pronounced concentration in two roles: Guide accounted for 440 responses (70.4%) and Amplifier for 178 responses (28.5%). Empathizer and Critical Inquirer generated only 5 (0.8%) and 2 (0.3%) responses, respectively. Figure 6 presents the daily distribution of PCA-generated responses, facilitator decisions, and role composition throughout the deployment period. In interaction-design terms, the workflow primarily stabilized around two recur-rent forms of PCA participation: prompting deeper discussion and amplifying valuable peer contributions. The dominant patterns reflected the discourse characteristics of the community. Most learner posts were either (a) brief or generic, triggering the Guide role to scaffold deeper reflection, or (b) detailed practice-based narratives, triggering the Amplifier role to recognize and extend these contributions. Posts expressing emotional distress or polarized viewpoints—which would activate the Empathizer and Critical Inquirer roles—were nearly absent. Based on the distribution and discourse analysis, we retained only the Guide and Amplifier roles for Iteration 2, removing Em-pathizer and Critical Inquirer from the active role configuration. Empathizer and Critical Inquirer generated insufficient responses (5 and 2, respectively) to constitute a meaningful or consistent in-tervention, and their low frequency reflected authentic discourse characteristics rather than a design limitation. The four-role frame-work remains theoretically grounded; however, its full deployment is better suited to communities exhibiting different discourse pro-files. This refinement exemplifies the DBR approach: the initial interaction design was tested against authentic community data, and the active role configuration was adapted based on empirical evidence prior to the effectiveness evaluation phase. 

6.3.2 Content Quality Analysis and Moderation Standards. The overall acceptance rate was 71.4% (446 of 625 responses). Among the 179 rejected responses, facilitators identified three primary failure modes aligned with the review dimensions: 

• Non-native language expression : Responses exhibited exces-sive emoji use, redundant punctuation, or phrasing patterns inconsistent with native Chinese professional discourse. 

• Interactional inappropriateness : Responses appeared tem-plated without engaging the specific content of the focal post, or misattributed ideas to incorrect participants in multi-turn threads. 

• Factual implausibility : Responses fabricated specific teach-ing experiences, student reactions, or institutional details that the PCA could not authentically possess. These failure patterns informed iterative prompt refinement throughout the deployment period. When facilitators identified re-curring issues—such as excessive emoji use or templated openings— the research team revised corresponding prompt instructions to address these patterns. This iterative refinement contributed to observed improvements in acceptance rates: beginning at 46.9% on the first day and stabilizing between 65% and 88% in subsequent days (Figure 6). This trajectory reflects the progressive refinement of both prompts and review criteria during early deployment, and shows that collaborative AI-in-the-loop facilitation requires on-going adjustment of the interaction design rather than one-time prompt specification. The final content moderation standards, refined through facilitator– researcher discussion during Weeks 1–2, are presented in Table 2. These standards operationalize the three review dimensions into explicit acceptance and rejection criteria, providing a replicable protocol for governing which AI responses become part of visible educational interaction. 

## 7 Iteration 2: Evaluating Interaction Effects 7.1 Research Goals 

Iteration 2 addressed RQ2 by examining whether the refined inter-action design—configured with the Guide and Amplifier roles and governed through human review—enhances social and cognitive presence in cMOOC discussions, and whether interaction mode moderates these effects. Building on the moderation standards and role configurations established in Iteration 1, this phase analyzed discussion data from Weeks 3–5 with two specific goals: (1) To evaluate whether PCA participation itself is associated with social and cognitive presence by comparing with-PCA versus without-PCA conditions. ## 8.1 Discourse-Adaptive Role Distribution in Guide and Amplifier Functions 

The pronounced concentration of PCA activity in the Guide (70.4%) and Amplifier (28.5%) roles during Iteration 1 reflects an adaptive response to authentic community discourse characteristics rather than a design deficiency. The in-service teacher community was characterized by predominantly professional, solution-oriented ex-changes with minimal expressions of emotional distress or ideolog-ical divergence—the very discourse patterns that would otherwise activate the Empathizer and Critical Inquirer roles. That the work-flow weighted facilitation toward roles congruent with observed community norms indicates that the interaction design successfully instantiated a discourse-responsive mechanism. This finding carries substantive implications for interaction de-sign in professional learning communities. A fixed role repertoire— one that deploys empathic or adversarial facilitation regardless of community context—risks misalignment and may undermine com-munity trust. Effective GenAI facilitation should therefore incorpo-rate discourse-monitoring mechanisms that dynamically modulate role activation based on emergent interaction patterns. The present workflow grounds role selection in observed network dynamics rather than predetermined schedules, thereby operationalizing the connectivist principle that meaningful learning emerges from, and should be responsive to, the evolving structure of networked inter-action [55]. 

## 8.2 PCA Selectively Enhanced Open Communication and Networked Cohesion 

Iteration 2 Goal 1 results demonstrated that PCA participation selectively enhanced two dimensions of social presence—Open Communication (SP_OC) and Networked Cohesion (SP_NC)—with SP_total increasing as a composite reflection of these gains, and without producing uniform improvements across all indicators. This specificity is theoretically informative rather than a limitation, and aligns with the role distribution observed in Iteration 1. The Guide role, by scaffolding reflection in brief or generic posts, likely encouraged sustained engagement with others’ contributions (OC-1: Continuing a Thread) [ 71 ]. The Amplifier role, by explicitly recognizing and extending substantive contributions, may have reinforced community-building behaviors such as use of inclusive language and shared terminology (NC-2: Community Building) [ 63 ]. The absence of improvement in Affective Expression (SP_AF) is con-sistent with previous research on human-GenAI interactions (with ChatGPT) [ 44 ], where emotional expression is less accustomed. Previous research indicates that as cMOOC learning communities evolve, participants increasingly prioritize cognitive engagement over socio-emotional interaction [ 61 ], thereby diminishing the in-fluence of affective exchanges on the community’s ongoing devel-opment [ 13 ]. Taken together, these patterns suggest that presence Designing Human–GenAI Interaction for cMOOC Discussion Facilitation 

enhancement is not a unitary outcome but is shaped by the align-ment between PCA role design and the communicative conventions of the target community. These findings extend prior work on PCAs in educational set-tings. Whereas previous studies have documented PCA effects on social presence in small-scale contexts [ 6 , 26 , 27 ], the present study demonstrates that comparable effects are achievable at the commu-nity level in cMOOCs through network-structure-driven facilita-tion. Prior research has also shown that specific network dynamics exert differential influences on the sustainability of learner interac-tions; for instance, reciprocity can amplify the persistence of social wayfinding interactions [ 66 ]. The observed selective enhancement pattern further suggests that evidence-based GenAI interaction design should target specific discourse functions rather than as-sume that PCA participation will produce broad gains across all educational outcomes. 

## 8.3 Interaction Mode as a Moderator in PCA-Supported cMOOC Presence Development 

A key finding from Iteration 2 was the clear differentiation between the direct-interaction and co-presence groups (Goal 2). Prior re-search has shown that different PCA designs yield varying effects on social and cognitive presence [ 6 ]. Consistent with this line of inquiry, our results indicate that learners who directly engaged with the PCA demonstrated significantly higher levels of social presence (SP_OC, SP_NC, SP_total) and higher-order cognitive indicators (CP_IN, CP_RC) than those who did not directly partic-ipate in PCA-mediated discussions. These findings indicate that interaction mode is a meaningful moderating variable in cMOOC contexts. This pattern aligns with the CoI framework’s emphasis on the reciprocal relationship between teaching, social, and cognitive presence [ 22 ]. PCA-mediated social exchanges may function as scaf-folding mechanisms that prompt learners to elaborate, synthesize, and apply knowledge, thereby deepening cognitive engagement. In this respect, the study responds to the longstanding challenge of “distributed teaching presence” in cMOOC research [ 2]. By provid-ing targeted cognitive scaffolding, the PCA partially compensates for structural limitations inherent in distributed environments, fa-cilitating learners’ transition from distributed exploration toward more centralized knowledge construction and problem resolution [10, 33]. Problem Triggering (CP_PT) did not support the directional hy-pothesis, with descriptive means favoring the co-presence group. This suggests that early-phase cognitive activity may be more sen-sitive to environmental climate than to direct interaction. The re-sult is consistent with prior assumptions about cMOOC learners, who are characterized by relatively strong cognitive autonomy and the capacity to engage proactively within open-ended exploratory environments [ 14 , 29 ]. Accordingly, from a practical perspective, GenAI interaction design should balance mechanisms that sup-port direct learner–agent interaction with those that leverage co-presence effects. In the present study’s network-embedded inter-vention threads, 54.8% of learners (114 of 208) directly engaged with the PCA, indicating a moderate yet substantial level of reach. The findings suggest that such a design effectively supports presence while also pointing to opportunities for refinement—such as more strategic selection of focal posts or the use of explicit invitational prompts—to further amplify community-level impact [66]. 

## 8.4 Complementary Human and PCA Support Across Discussion Contexts 

Subgroup analyses revealed that PCA effects varied systematically by discussion initiator type. In teacher-initiated threads, PCA partic-ipation primarily enhanced social interaction dimensions (SP_OC, SP_NC), consistent with the overall pattern. In learner-initiated threads, however, the most pronounced effect was on Resolution and Creation (CP_RC), suggesting that PCA scaffolding was particu-larly effective in supporting learners’ progression from exploratory discussion to knowledge application and artifact creation when dis-cussions originated from their own questions or practical concerns. This differential pattern likely reflects the distinct pedagogical structures of the two thread types. Teacher-initiated threads typi-cally embed predefined learning objectives and discussion frame-works [ 50 ]; in these contexts, PCA may function primarily to sustain peer interaction and reinforce community cohesion, complement-ing the teaching presence already structurally embedded in the thread design. Learner-initiated threads, by contrast, represent self-organized inquiry arising from authentic practice-based puzzlement [ 15 ]. The relative absence of pre-established facilitative structure in such threads creates a more substantive role for PCA: guiding learners through the cognitive progression from problem identifi-cation through exploration to resolution and creation—a trajectory that might otherwise stall without targeted scaffolding. This distinction has implications for understanding the com-plementary relationship between human teaching presence and AI-facilitated support [ 17 , 26 ]. Where human pedagogical structure is present, PCA appears to reinforce the social dimensions of learn-ing; where such structure is absent, PCA may more directly scaffold cognitive advancement. In this sense, it helps address concerns about support gaps in MOOCs. Accordingly, strategies for deploy-ing PCA should be calibrated to the type of discussion initiator and the extent of existing instructional structure within each discourse context. 

## 9 Conclusion, Implications, Limitations, and Future Directions 9.1 Conclusion 

This study examined how human–GenAI interaction can be de-signed for scalable discussion facilitation in a cMOOC. Across two DBR iterations in a five-week cMOOC with 606 registered learn-ers (primarily in-service teachers), we investigated (RQ1) how a collaborative AI-in-the-loop workflow could be refined to produce publishable, pedagogically appropriate PCA participation and (RQ2) how different forms of PCA-mediated interaction related to learner social and cognitive presence. Xiao et al. 

Three contributions follow. First, we reframed GenAI-supported facilitation as an interaction design problem and refined a work-flow that combines network-structure-based targeting, discourse-adaptive response roles, and human review before publication. Sec-ond, we established a replicable collaborative AI-in-the-loop mod-eration protocol with explicit criteria for role/task alignment, in-teractional appropriateness, and factual plausibility, showing how human governance shapes the PCA participation learners actually encounter. Third, we provide quasi-experimental evidence that PCA participation selectively enhances dimensions of social presence, while reciprocal learner–agent interaction is more strongly associ-ated with higher-order social and cognitive outcomes than mere co-presence. Overall, effective GenAI facilitation in cMOOCs appears to de-pend less on generic AI presence than on interaction design. In this study, productive PCA participation required discourse-adaptive role configuration, structured human oversight, and opportuni-ties for reciprocal exchange with learners. These findings sup-port a bounded claim: collaborative AI-in-the-loop facilitation may strengthen discussion in large-scale online learning communities when PCA participation is carefully targeted, publicly governed, and designed to invite interaction rather than simply occupy space in the thread. 

## 9.2 Implications 

9.2.1 Extending the CoI Framework to cMOOC Contexts. This study contributes to ongoing efforts to adapt the CoI framework for large-scale open online learning environments [ 34 , 45 ]. The reconcep-tualization of Group Cohesion as Networked Cohesion, and the expansion of Resolution to Resolution and Creation, proved ana-lytically productive in capturing presence dimensions specific to professional learning communities operating at scale. The finding that PCA enhanced Networked Cohesion—via inclu-sive language, shared terminology, and community-specific references— suggests that social presence in cMOOCs can be cultivated through network-level interventions that reinforce collective identity, not only small-group bonding [ 2]. This extends CoI by showing that teaching presence (here instantiated through PCA) can strengthen social presence through symbolic and linguistic mechanisms that deepen identification with the broader learning network. 

9.2.2 Operationalizing Connectivist Facilitation. The present study extends connectivist facilitation into an evidence-based human– GenAI interaction design framework. Connectivism emphasizes learning through pattern recognition, connection-forming, and navigation across distributed knowledge resources [ 15 , 55 , 69 ]. In this study, those principles were operationalized not through un-restricted PCA participation, but through a governed interaction design that determined where the PCA should intervene, how it should respond, and which responses should become visible in the community. The network-embedded targeting approach positioned the PCA as a responsive participant in the learning network rather than a broadcaster of predetermined content, while the stronger out-comes associated with direct interaction suggest that the PCA can function as a connectivist “node” most productively when learners enter reciprocal exchange with it [16, 66]. 

9.2.3 Evidence-Based Human–GenAI Interaction Design. Across both iterations, four design implications emerge. First, mere PCA presence is not enough . The contrast between Goal 1 and Goal 2 shows that simply inserting the PCA into a thread yields only selec-tive gains, whereas reciprocal interaction is associated with stronger and more robust improvements. Designers should therefore prior-itize structures that invite reply exchanges rather than assuming that visible PCA participation will diffuse benefits across all thread participants. Second, PCA roles should be discourse-adaptive rather than fixed . The dominance of Guide and Amplifier responses indi-cates that productive PCA participation depended on alignment with the communicative needs of the community. Effective interac-tion design should therefore match facilitation roles to discourse conditions instead of enforcing a static persona repertoire across contexts. Third, human review is part of interaction design, not only quality control . In this study, accepted versus rejected responses determined which PCA utterances became part of the visible com-munity discourse. The moderation workflow therefore shaped the interaction learners actually experienced. For educational settings where trust, authenticity, and contextual appropriateness matter, collaborative AI-in-the-loop review should be treated as a constitu-tive design mechanism [ 40 , 67 ]. The improvement in acceptance rates from 46.9% to 65–88% during Iteration 1, and the sustained 77.9% acceptance rate in Iteration 2, further indicates that such governance can be refined empirically rather than imposed as a static rule set. Fourth, designers should expect trade-offs rather than uniform gains . PCA participation strengthened some social dimen-sions and, under direct interaction, some higher-order cognitive outcomes, but it did not improve all indicators. Evidence-based GenAI design should therefore focus on which outcomes are being targeted, under what discourse conditions, and with what level of human governance, rather than presuming a general improvement effect. These principles extend beyond professional development cMOOCs. University-run MOOCs, teacher education programmes, and fac-ulty development initiatives face comparable challenges of scalable facilitation and governed AI participation; the present workflow’s emphasis on targeted intervention, discourse-adaptive roles, and human-reviewed reciprocal exchange offers a transferable design template for these higher education contexts. 

## 9.3 Limitations and Future Directions 

Several limitations should be acknowledged. The study was con-ducted in a single cMOOC in China, and transferability to other populations, disciplines, or cultural contexts requires empirical ver-ification. The quasi-experimental design limits causal inference: although a predefined deterministic allocation rule was used, focal post selection was also shaped by network structural criteria, and the interaction-mode classification for Goal 2 was observational, introducing potential self-selection bias. In addition, potential inter-ference or carryover across conditions cannot be ruled out because learners could participate in multiple threads over time, and PCA exposure in one thread might influence subsequent behavior in another. The adaptation of coding categories (Networked Cohesion, Resolution and Creation) for the cMOOC context, while theoreti-cally motivated, has not been independently validated. Finally, the study focused on short-term presence outcomes within a five-week Designing Human–GenAI Interaction for cMOOC Discussion Facilitation 

course and did not capture learner perceptions of PCA, including concerns about authenticity or the appropriate role of AI in learning communities. These limitations point to productive future directions: repli-cation across diverse online learning communities; experimental isolation of specific interaction design features such as review in-tensity, invitation prompts, and role configuration; longitudinal examination of effects on learning and practice transfer; integration of learner modeling for personalized facilitation; and qualitative investigation of learner experiences and ethical considerations in collaborative AI-in-the-loop discussion environments.

## 10 Appendix 10.1 Focal Post Selection 

The focal post selection engine runs once daily, processing behav-ioral logs from the most recent two days to identify high-centrality discussion targets. The process consists of two steps: hyperedge construction and closeness centrality computation. 

10.1.1 Hyperedge Construction. Each log record is transformed into a relational event hyperedge that captures the multi-way re-lationship among actors and artifacts involved in the interaction event. The hyperedge construction follows these rules based on action type: 

• For posted actions: the hyperedge includes the actor, the post, and the post author. 

• For commented or liked_comment actions: the hyperedge additionally includes the comment and comment author. 

• For replied or liked_reply actions: the hyperedge fur-ther includes the reply and reply author. This construction ensures that each action is represented as a single hyperedge connecting all relevant participants and content nodes, preserving the higher-order relational structure of forum discussions [12]. 

10.1.2 Closeness Centrality Computation. Given the set of hyper-edges, the system constructs a hypergraph 𝐻 = (𝑉 , 𝐸 ) where 𝑉 is the set of nodes (actors, posts, comments, and replies) and 𝐸 is the set of hyperedges. The system computes 𝑠 level-closeness central-ity metrics using the HyperNetX Python library [ 46 ], with 𝑠 = 1

(indicating adjacency through shared hyperedges). Closeness cen-trality measures the extent to which a node is proximal to all other nodes in the hypergraph. In this study, it serves as a ranking crite-rion for focal post/comment selection. Nodes with higher closeness centrality are structurally more accessible within the interaction network and are therefore more likely to reach a broader segment of the community. Accordingly, posts or comments with higher centrality scores are prioritized as focal posts/comments for PCA intervention. 

10.1.3 Target Selection. After computing centrality metrics, nodes are ranked by closeness centrality in descending order. The top 5% of P-type (post) and C-type (comment/reply) nodes are selected as candidate targets for PCA intervention. This threshold balances coverage of active discussions with manageable review workload for human facilitators.

## 10.5 Quasi-Experimental Assignment Diagnostics for Iteration 2 

Iteration 2 used a rule-based quasi-experimental assignment rather than strict randomization. The assignment rule was fixed a priori :focal posts were sorted by timestamp and alternately assigned to 

Definition with PCA versus Definition without PCA according to odd–even sequence parity. This deterministic mechanism is fully reproducible and reduces researcher discretion, but it can still leave comparability gaps and temporal interference risk. To transparently bound this risk, we added two diagnostics: (1) post-level balance checks and (2) within-user-week permutation sensitivity analysis. 

10.5.1 Post-level Balance (Focal Posts). Distributional differences were small for time structure and centrality under the data corpus; this indicates improved comparability under the deterministic rule, while still not equivalent to strict randomization. 

10.5.2 Time-trend Sensitivity via Permutation. Observed social-presence gains were concentrated in SP_total and SP_NC (both exceeding the null distribution), while SP_OC lay in the upper tail but did not reach conventional two-tailed significance. Other effects remained inside expected random fluctuation ranges. This indicates that part of the Goal 1 signal is plausibly robust to assignment-rule randomness, but temporal allocation risk cannot be fully ruled out.
