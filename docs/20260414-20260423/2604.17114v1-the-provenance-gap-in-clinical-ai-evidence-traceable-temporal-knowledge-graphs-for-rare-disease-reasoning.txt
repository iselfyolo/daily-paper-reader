# The Provenance Gap in Clinical AI: Evidence-Traceable Temporal Knowledge Graphs for Rare Disease Reasoning

Abstract 

Frontier large language models generate clinically accurate outputs, but their citations are often fabri-cated. We term this the Provenance Gap . We tested five frontier LLMs across 36 clinician-validated sce-narios for three phenotypically confusable rare disease pairs (myasthenia gravis/Lambert-Eaton, Duchen-ne/Becker muscular dystrophy, CIDP/Guillain-Barr´ e). No model produced a clinically relevant PubMed identifier (PMID) without prompting. When explicitly asked to cite, the best model achieved 15.3% relevant PMIDs; the majority resolved to real publications in unrelated fields, so a spot-check finds real papers, just not the right ones. We present HEG-TKG (Hierarchical Evidence-Grounded Tempo-ral Knowledge Graphs), a system that grounds clinical claims in temporal knowledge graphs built from 4,512 PubMed records and curated sources. HEG-TKG structures evidence with quality-tier stratification (GOLD/SILVER/BRONZE reflecting cross-source consensus) and 1,280 disease-trajectory milestones -temporal anchoring absent from existing biomedical KGs. In a controlled three-arm comparison (Vanilla, Guideline-RAG, HEG-TKG; same synthesis model), HEG-TKG matches baseline clinical feature cov-erage while achieving 100% evidence verifiability: every output is generated from an average of 181 PMID-backed knowledge graph edges per scenario, with 203 inline citations. Guideline-RAG, given over-lapping source documents as raw text, produces zero verifiable citations, which is identical to vanilla. The graph structure is what creates traceability. LLM judges cannot distinguish fabricated from verified citations without PubMed audit data. Independent clinician evaluation confirms the verifiability advan-tage: clinicians rated HEG-TKG higher on Verifiability (D1: ∆ = +1 .64, Cohen’s d = 1 .81, p < 0.001) and actionability with no degradation on safety or completeness. A counterfactual experiment shows 80% resistance to injected clinical errors with 100% detectability via citation trace. The system deploys on-premise via open-source models, so patient data never leaves institutional infrastructure. The Provenance Gap should be a standard reporting metric for clinical AI. 

Keywords: clinical AI, knowledge graphs, evidence provenance, rare diseases, LLM evaluation, citation verification, temporal reasoning 1

# 1 Introduction 

Frontier LLMs produce competent differentials for myasthenia gravis versus Lambert-Eaton syndrome, and it is the same for other pairs of diseases as well. Med-PaLM 2 achieves 86.5% on MedQA[1], GPT-4 ex-ceeds passing scores on all three parts of the United States Medical Licensing Examination[2], and clinical deployment appears imminent across specialties[3]. But correctness alone does not make it useful in clinics. A neurologist presented with an AI-generated differential diagnosis needs to verify certain aspects, like why 

that answer was reached, trace each claim to its evidentiary source, and potentially confirm that the cited evidence actually supports the recommendation. Without traceability, clinicians don’t trust the output. A misdiagnosis of MG as LEMS that leads to incorrect immunotherapy, or a DMD/BMD confusion that affects steroid timing, delays treatment initiation by months, and is not acceptable. The EU AI Act[4], FDA clinical decision support guidance[5], and WHO[6] all mandate independent verification of AI-generated clinical recommendations. At the same time, patient scenarios constitute pro-tected health information under GDPR and HIPAA[7], meaning that cloud API calls expose data outside institutional control. A clinical AI system needs verifiability and data protection. Current LLMs deliver neither. HEG-TKG resolves both: structured evidence provenance for verifiability, open-source synthesis models behind institutional firewalls for data protection. Citation failures in LLMs are worse than most studies acknowledge. SourceCheckup found 50-90% of LLM responses unsupported by their cited sources[8]; VeriFact showed automated fact verification can achieve 92.7% clinician agreement for EHR claims[9]. Autoregressive generation produces hallucinations by design[10, 11]. For example, what LLMs know and what they can prove are different things. Using a second LLM to verify citations from the first does not work. Our experiments show LLM judges rate fabricated citations as valid because they lack external verification data. The Provenance Gap cannot be solved within the LLM ecosystem itself. This matters most in rare diseases, where the clinician diagnosing you has probably never seen your condi-tion before. Over 6,000 rare diseases affect approximately 300 million patients globally[12], most diagnosed by non-specialists after years-long diagnostic odysseys. We selected three phenotypically confusable disease pairs - myasthenia gravis versus Lambert-Eaton[13], Duchenne versus Becker muscular dystrophy[14], and CIDP versus Guillain-Barr´ e[15] - because all three are clinically similar at presentation but diverge over their longi-tudinal course, requiring temporal reasoning that existing knowledge graphs do not support. PrimeKG[16], UMLS[17], and DrugBank[18] encode static relationships: dystrophin is associated with DMD, but not that Gowers’ sign appears at age 3-5 while ambulation loss follows at 9-13. We define Provenance Gap as the gap between a system’s clinical feature coverage and its reliable evi-dence traceability (adjusted for citation quality; see Methods). We tested five frontier LLMs spanning three providers across 36 clinician-validated scenarios and verified every PubMed identifier against the E-utilities API. In vanilla mode, all models produced zero clinically relevant PMIDs. The best model, when explicitly told to cite, managed 15.3%. We present HEG-TKG, a system that grounds clinical reasoning in temporal knowledge graphs with a two-tier architecture: a curated Tier 1 backbone from GeneReviews[19], OMIM[20], Orphanet[21], and CDC guidelines[22], augmented by Tier 2 extraction from 4,512 PubMed records (987 unique source PMIDs), and anchored to 1,280 disease-trajectory milestones. To our knowledge, no existing biomedical knowledge graph captures these temporal trajectories[23]. Where standard retrieval-augmented generation[24, 25] hands the model a bag of text chunks, HEG-TKG maps every claim to a verified PMID, as-signs a quality tier reflecting cross-source consensus, and places it within a disease timeline. As our three-arm comparison confirms, this structured grounding maintains clinical feature coverage (Table 2). The following 2two works essentially highlight this gap. Zhao et al.[26] cover 2,919 diseases using an agentic framework, whereas Song et al.[27] build a graph-RAG system for rare genetic diseases (6,143 nodes). But they do not verify reasoning against primary literature and lack temporal anchoring and citation verification. We take a depth-first approach: six diseases with fully traceable temporal reasoning, showing that the architecture generalises. In this work, we make five contributions: (1) a formal definition and empirical measurement of the 

Provenance Gap across five frontier LLMs; (2) HEG-TKG, a temporal knowledge graph architecture with fully verified citations, quality-stratified evidence (GOLD/SILVER/BRONZE), and disease-trajectory anchoring; (3) the first demonstration that LLM judges cannot detect fabricated citations without external audit data; (4) a counterfactual safety test showing 80% error resistance with 100% detectability; (5) the temporal KG as a reusable resource with comprehensive coverage of six rare neuromuscular diseases (5,481 nodes; 1,280 temporal anchors).

The most intuitive question is: would better prompts fix this? We tested this directly by adding an explicit prompt (“You must cite specific PubMed PMIDs for every clinical claim”) to three models (Table 1, citation-prompted rows). Claude Opus 4.6 produced 577 unique PMIDs, the highest among all models. Of those, 78.9% were published in unrelated fields, and only 15.3% were clinically relevant. Claude Sonnet 4.6 achieved 11.8%, and DeepSeek-v3 managed 10.4%. These models do not generate random digit strings, but they generate plausible PubMed identifiers that happen to exist in the database. A clinician who spot-checks a cited PMID will find a real paper and may not notice it discusses a different disease entirely. That’s worse than citing nothing if a clinician who spot-checks will find a real paper and assume the citation is correct. The Provenance Gap is an intrinsic limitation of how parametric memory works, not a prompt engineering problem. 

## 2.3 Structured KG grounding halves the Provenance Gap without sacrificing coverage 

Given that prompting alone fails to produce verifiable citations, we next asked whether structured evidence grounding could close the gap. We evaluated HEG-TKG’s clinical outputs against two baselines: Vanilla (parametric knowledge only) and Guideline-RAG (overlapping source documents as raw text passages) - in a controlled three-arm design where all arms use the same synthesis model (GPT-4.1) to isolate the contribution of evidence grounding (see Methods). HEG-TKG achieves comparable clinical feature coverage to both baselines (bootstrap 95% CIs overlap: HEG-TKG [0 .652 , 0.779], Vanilla [0 .688 , 0.796], Guideline-RAG [0 .633 , 0.741]; Mann-Whitney p > 0.10) while halving the Provenance Gap (0.366 vs 0.743). The ETS of 0.372 requires careful interpretation. It measures the inline citation rate : 37% of output claims carry an explicit [PMID:X] tag in the generated text. But HEG-TKG’s actual verifiability is 100%. Every output is generated entirely from a PMID-backed evidence context, on average 181 knowledge graph edges drawn from 199 unique PMIDs per scenario, and the full evidence manifest is stored per query. A clinician can trace any claim, cited or uncited, back to the specific KG edges and PMIDs that informed it. The 63% without inline tags are not ungrounded: they are synthesised from the same auditable evidence, but the model did not attach a citation (e.g., combining two cited facts into a differential reasoning statement). The inline citation rate is a lower bound on verifiability, not a measure of it. The actual verifiability gap is therefore: HEG-TKG 5= 100% (every claim traceable to PMID-backed evidence), vanilla = 0% (no evidence trail at any level), Guideline-RAG = 0% (raw text passages with no structured identifiers). The ETS-based Provenance Gap (0.366) is a conservative, automatable proxy for this fundamental difference. Per-disease-pair performance varies: HEG-TKG outperforms vanilla on MG/LEMS (0 .814 vs 0 .747) but underperforms on DMD/BMD (0 .659 vs 0 .771), reflecting the higher proportion of treatment-specific scenarios where parametric knowledge is stronger (Supplementary Table S28). A worked example comparing outputs side by side is provided in Supplementary S29. The Guideline-RAG receives overlapping source material : GeneReviews, OMIM, and the same PubMed abstracts used to build the knowledge graph, arguably more raw text than a clinician would consult, but delivered without graph structure, PMID citations, or quality tiers. It nonetheless produces zero verifiable citations, identical to vanilla, suggesting that the volume of source text is not the bottleneck; rather, it is the structured citation metadata in the graph that enables traceability. ## 2.4 Quality-tiered knowledge graphs provide 6,316 PMID-backed edges 

The three knowledge graphs collectively contain 5,481 nodes, 6,316 edges, 987 unique source PMIDs, and 1,280 temporal anchors (Supplementary Table S30). The temporal anchors are disease-trajectory milestones (e.g., Gowers’ sign at P3Y-P5Y, ambulation loss at P9Y-P13Y for DMD), not publication dates. Our automated keyword-matching scorer reports Clinical Feature Coverage of 71.7% (range 65.9-81.4% across disease pairs). However, manual inspection reveals this substantially underestimates true coverage: 70% of features scored as “missing” are in fact discussed in the output but fail the rigid substring matcher (e.g., “reading frame rule 90-95% accuracy” is marked absent despite the output stating “fulfilled in ∼90% of DMD cases”). Of the 80 feature instances flagged as missing, 76% have corresponding concepts present in the KG evidence manifest. The true KG coverage gap: features genuinely absent from the knowledge graph is approximately 5-10%, confined primarily to treatment specifics available only in full-text articles. Across all 36 scenarios, HEG-TKG retrieved 26,139 knowledge graph edges. Of these, 562 (2.2%) are GOLD tier (cross-tier confirmed: Tier 2 extraction independently confirmed a Tier 1 curated fact, confi-dence = 0.95), 2,271 (8.7%) are SILVER (multi-model or multi-document consensus, confidence = 0.85), and 23,306 (89.2%) are BRONZE (single model, single source, confidence = 0.70). Temporal edges account for 65,802 (22.2%) of all retrieved evidence. The quality tier distribution is clinically meaningful: GOLD edges represent guideline-confirmed facts (e.g., “corticosteroids delay ambulation loss in DMD” confirmed by both GeneReviews and independent literature) that a clinician can treat as high-confidence anchors. SILVER edges reflect claims corroborated across multiple sources or extraction models, suitable for clinical reasoning with standard verification. BRONZE edges flag single-source claims warranting additional scrutiny before acting - analogous to a case report versus a systematic review. This stratification gives clinicians a built-in confidence signal absent from both vanilla LLMs and text-based RAG, where every claim carries equal (zero) provenance weight.

The table doesn’t capture what this actually looks like in practice. Consider a pediatrician who has just received genetic confirmation that a 4-year-old boy has a DMD-causing dystrophin mutation. The immedi-ate clinical question is not “what is DMD?” but “what happens next, and when?” HEG-TKG produces a structured disease timeline: “Gowers’ sign at age 3–5 years [PMID:29395989, GOLD]; loss of independent ambulation at 10–12 years [PMID:41391909]; scoliosis onset after loss of ambulation [PMID:20301670, SIL-VER]; cardiac surveillance should begin by age 10 [PMID:37240001, SILVER]; dilated cardiomyopathy by end of second decade [PMID:37240001, SILVER]; respiratory decline (FVC <1L) by late teens [PMID:28981889, SILVER].” Each milestone carries a PMID and a quality tier. The paediatrician can plan: cardiac screening at age 10, respiratory function monitoring from age 12, orthopaedic referral at first signs of ambulation loss. Vanilla GPT-4.1 produces “onset of symptoms (delayed motor milestones, Gowers’ sign, calf pseudohypertro-phy)” with no age windows and no citations. It tells the clinician what happens but not when - and “when” is what drives clinical management. The same pattern holds across disease pairs. For GBS/CIDP, HEG-TKG provides the 7-14-day nadir window and 2-4-week recovery onset with PMIDs, the timeline a GP needs to distinguish GBS from CIDP. For MG/LEMS, the 2-4-year latency between neurological onset and LEMS-associated small-cell lung cancer makes the screening timeline safety-critical. PrimeKG[16] tells you that dystrophin is associated with DMD; HEG-TKG tells you when each consequence arrives and backs it with a PMID. 72.6 Clinicians confirm the verifiability and actionability advantage

This evaluation philosophy that citation of established facts is redundant, produces a flat D1 profile despite the structural difference in citation presence. It represents a legitimate clinical perspective (experienced specialists do not need citations for consensus knowledge) and highlights that verifiability is most valuable for non-specialists and for claims at the frontier of evidence. The 72 clinician cases are drawn from the same base scenarios used in the LLM judge evaluation, enabling direct LLM–human correlation analysis (Section 2.8). 

## 2.7 LLM judges cannot assess verifiability without ground truth 

Most clinical AI studies rely on automated judges. Can LLM judges assess citation quality? We deployed a three-model judge panel (GPT-4o-mini, DeepSeek-V3, Claude 3 Haiku) across all 108 cases (36 scenarios 

× 3 arms) using five clinician-aligned Likert dimensions: Verifiability (D1), Actionability (D2), Temporal 8Precision (D3), Non-Expert Safety (D4), and Clinical Completeness (D5). Cases were blinded and randomly ordered. In v1 (blind), D1 Verifiability showed no meaningful separation: HEG-TKG 4 .59, Vanilla 4 .34, Guideline-RAG 4 .58 (all pairwise deltas < 0.5, non-significant after BH correction). Inter-rater reliability for D1 was negative (Krippendorff’s α = −0.09), confirming judges fundamentally disagree about what “verifiable” means without ground-truth citation data. On D2-D5, all three arms scored comparably: HEG-TKG showed slight advantages on Actionability (+0 .03 to +0 .17), Temporal Precision ( −0.08 to +0 .28), and Completeness (+0 .11 to +0 .31) vs Vanilla, with no degradation on any dimension. Guideline-RAG scored lower on Temporal Precision (D3: 3 .47-4 .28) than both other arms, reflecting the absence of structured temporal data. The D1 failure was specific and structural: judges score citation plausibility (does the text look well-referenced?) rather than citation validity (are the references real?). To confirm this, we deployed a second evaluation round (v2) with one addition: each judge received a PubMed E-utilities citation audit report documenting which PMIDs existed, their publication titles, and clinical relevance to the disease pair. This three-arm design (N = 108 cases, 36 per arm, 3 judges each) tests whether the citation blindness extends to Guideline-RAG, which has access to overlapping source texts but without structured citations. Ungrounded LLMs and text-based RAG offer neither parametric resistance nor citation traceability.

# 3 Discussion 

The Provenance Gap doesn’t shrink with bigger models or better prompts, but it’s structural. We propose a four-level citation quality hierarchy to contextualise this finding (Table 9). Table 9: Four-level citation quality hierarchy. The Provenance Gap measures a system’s distance from Level 3. All tested frontier LLMs remain below Level 2. 

Level Type Description 

0 No citation Vanilla LLMs. Claims with no trail to follow. 1 Source attribution URLs or text passages indicating where the system read a claim. Subject to link rot (25% within two years[33, 34]). No structured identifiers, evidence grading, or claim-level mapping. 2 Identifier-based Agentic systems with PubMed tool access retrieve real PMIDs at inference time. Evidence set reconstructed de novo per query; not reproducible across sessions. No quality stratification or temporal context. 3 Evidence provenance Every claim maps to a verified PMID, carries a quality tier (GOLD/SILVER/BRONZE) reflecting cross-source consensus, and sits within a structured temporal context. 

Text-based RAG (including our Guideline-RAG baseline) operates at Level 1. Agentic architectures with PubMed tool access could reach Level 2, but reactive retrieval produces a different evidence set per query, undermining clinical audit reproducibility[5]. We did not benchmark against an agentic baseline because no reproducible agentic system for rare disease citation currently exists; this comparison is planned for future work. HEG-TKG operates at Level 3, and its two-plane architecture, public knowledge graph separated from locally executed synthesis that allows deployment behind institutional firewalls (Supplementary Tables S25-12 S26). In preliminary tests, open-source models achieved 75–80% of cloud citation coverage on a single institutional GPU. 

Clinical implications. For a typical output containing 28 claims, verifying a vanilla LLM requires manually searching PubMed per claim ( ∼2.5 minutes each[35]), totalling ∼70 minutes, which is impractical in a 15-minute consultation. With HEG-TKG, 37% of claims carry inline citations verifiable in seconds, and the remainder can be traced via the stored evidence manifest, reducing verification effort by an estimated 50-64%. This enables concrete workflows: a GP can attach the PMID-backed output to a specialist referral letter; a neurologist can check age-anchored milestones during a consultation; and when a clinician disagrees with a recommendation, they can trace exactly which PMID informed it, fundamentally different from disputing a black-box output. The barrier to clinical AI adoption is not essentially accuracy[1, 2] but rather that clinicians will not act on recommendations they cannot verify. 

The role of structure. The three-arm design isolates why raw text access is insufficient: Guideline-RAG receives overlapping source material (GeneReviews, OMIM, PubMed abstracts) yet produces zero verifiable citations, identical to vanilla. Compared to FPKG[27] (6,143 nodes), HEG-TKG adds temporal anchoring, programmatic citation verification, and counterfactual safety testing, the capabilities absent from existing biomedical knowledge graphs[23]. The counterfactual experiment reveals a nuanced picture: the model resisted 80% of injected errors through parametric knowledge, but deferred to planted evidence in 13% of cases involving treatment recommendations[36]. The safety argument rests not on error prevention alone, but on the fact that all 15 cases, including the two failures, remained traceable to their cited source, which enabled a clinician to catch the error. 

Methodological implications. The v1/v2 judge comparison makes this concrete that LLM judges cannot evaluate citation quality without external ground truth[37–39]. In blind assessment, inter-rater agree-ment on Verifiability (D1) was negative (Krippendorff’s α = −0.09); with PubMed audit data, the same judges reached α = 0 .89 and Cohen’s d up to 8 .78. This finding should inform the design of future clinical AI benchmarks: LLM-as-judge evaluation of citation quality without verification data measures surface plausi-bility, not validity. The clinician evaluation (Table 4) confirms this from the opposite direction: the senior neurologist (C1) rated HEG-TKG D1 at 4 .00 versus Vanilla at 2 .36 ( d = 1 .81, p < 0.001), while a second neurologist (C2) who evaluated citation utility rather than presence found no D1 difference, underscoring that verifiability is most valuable for non-specialists navigating unfamiliar disease territories. 

Limitations. We tested three neuromuscular disease pairs. That’s not enough to claim this works everywhere; metabolic disorders or cancers with thinner PubMed coverage could break differently. The system also currently lacks graceful degradation for out-of-scope queries. Our automated keyword-matching scorer reports 66-81% feature coverage across disease pairs, but manual inspection suggests this is a lower bound: 70% of features scored as “missing” are discussed in the output but fail the rigid substring matcher. The true knowledge graph coverage gap is approximately 5-10%, confined to treatment specifics available only in full-text articles. In our subsequent work, we extend this pipeline to full-text with wide disease coverage. At the same time, we did not benchmark against agentic retrieval systems (models with PubMed tool access) because no reproducible agentic baseline for rare disease citation currently exists. This comparison, along with formal verification-time studies, is planned. The 36 clinician-validated scenarios reflect the practical ceiling for board-certified evaluator time. The clinician panel ( n = 3; two neurologists, one medical informatician) is small and drawn from a single institution. C1’s strong D1 signal ( d = 1 .81) survives at n = 18 per arm, but D3–D5 deltas would require larger panels to detect. C2’s flat profile may reflect evaluator philosophy (citation of consensus knowledge viewed as unnecessary) rather than absence of system difference. Inter-rater 13 reliability between C1 and C2 could not be computed because they evaluated different case sets. Multi-site clinician evaluation with larger panels is needed to confirm these preliminary findings. We did use a limited number of tier-1 authoritative sources and didn’t check their update status over time. We will address multi-authoritative source verification in our future work. 

# 4 Methods 

## 4.1 Knowledge graph construction 

Two-tier architecture. HEG-TKG employs a hierarchical design that separates authoritative clinical facts (Tier 1) from literature-extracted knowledge (Tier 2). Tier 1 relationships were programmatically extracted from GeneReviews[19], OMIM[20], Orphanet[21], and clinical care guidelines[22] by the first author using disease-specific extraction scripts, then tagged as protected edges ( is protected=True ) that are preserved even when literature sources present conflicting data. We acknowledge single-author initial curation as a limitation; multi-annotator verification with formal inter-annotator agreement is planned for the expanded TemporalAtlas pipeline. 

Corpus selection. Tier 1 entities inform PubMed queries via the E-utilities API[28]. For each disease pair, we retrieved abstracts from PubMed using disease-specific MeSH[40] terms and free-text queries, yielding 651 (MG/LEMS), 706 (DMD/BMD), and 654 (CIDP/GBS) abstracts after relevance screening. 

Multi-LLM consensus extraction. Abstracts are processed through a six-step pipeline (Figure 6; formal pseudocode in Supplementary Algorithm S27): 1. Relevance screening. Each abstract is assessed by the first available extraction model (Claude Haiku 4.5 or GPT-4.1-mini) using a structured prompt that checks for disease-specific extractable content against include/exclude criteria defined in the YAML configuration (Supplementary S15). Ab-stracts must receive extract: true with confidence ≥ 0.85 to proceed. This filtered 40-60% of non-informative abstracts (e.g., 293/651 passed for MG/LEMS). 2. Schema-guided triplet extraction. Two models (Claude Haiku 4.5, GPT-4.1-mini/Gemini 2.0-flash) independently extract subject-predicate-object triplets against a disease-specific schema of 34 predicates across six categories (Supplementary S15). Every triplet must include an evidence quote from the source abstract. The architecture supports additional models and scales consensus voting accordingly. 3. Entity normalization. Entities are mapped to UMLS CUIs via cascading resolution: dictionary lookup, SapBERT entity linking[41], and ScispaCy fallback[42], followed by 18 semantic correction rules (Supplementary Table S10). 4. Temporal anchor resolution. Time-dependent predicates are anchored to structured ISO 8601 durations at four precision levels: exact age (e.g., P13Y), range (P13Y-P16Y), fuzzy qualifier (“late teens” → P17Y-P19Y), and developmental stage (“early childhood” → P2Y-P6Y). 5. Multi-model consensus voting. Triplets extracted by multiple models from the same document are deduplicated via MD5 hashing of normalized subject |predicate |object. Quality tiers are assigned: GOLD (Tier 1 curated knowledge or cross-tier confirmation where Tier 2 extraction independently con-firms a Tier 1 fact; confidence = 0.95), SILVER ( ≥2 extraction models agree, or ≥2 independent PMIDs support the same triplet; confidence = 0.85), BRONZE (single model, single source; confidence = 0.70). 6. Cross-tier integration. Tier 2 extractions are merged with the Tier 1 curated backbone, with Tier 1 protected edges preserved in case of conflict. The unified graph is serialized to Neo4j via Cypher import 14 scripts. The pipeline is configuration-driven: each disease pair is defined by a YAML configuration specifying disease entities (with UMLS CUIs), extraction schemas, temporal predicates, and few-shot examples (Sup-plementary S15). Adding a new disease pair requires only this configuration; no code changes are needed. 

Figure 6: HEG-TKG system architecture. ## 4.2 Clinical scenario design 

Thirty-six clinical scenarios (12 per disease pair) were authored as realistic neurological vignettes by a biomed-ical AI researcher with clinical domain training (Supplementary S1). Scenarios span four reasoning types: differential diagnosis ( n = 15, including 3 safety/diagnostic-pitfall scenarios), temporal comparative ( n = 6), temporal trajectory ( n = 6), and treatment rationale ( n = 9). A clinical collaborator (J.Z., neuromuscular specialist, University Medical Center G¨ ottingen) reviewed and refined 22 of 36 scenarios. The 36-scenario set reflects the practical ceiling for board-certified evaluator time (each neurologist scores 72 blinded cases across 5 dimensions). For automated LLM-as-judge evaluation, the same 36 scenarios were evaluated across all 3 arms (108 cases total). 

## 4.3 Three-arm evaluation design 

All three arms use GPT-4.1 (temperature=0.0) as the synthesis model to isolate the contribution of evidence grounding. Prompts are identical except for the evidence context (Supplementary S2): 

• Vanilla : System prompt + clinical scenario only 

• Guideline-RAG : System prompt + clinical scenario + retrieved text passages from GeneReviews, OMIM, and the same PubMed abstracts used in Tier 2 extraction (overlapping with HEG-TKG’s source corpus, but delivered as raw text without graph structure or citation metadata). Source documents were chunked into 500-character segments with 80-character overlap, embedded using all-MiniLM-L6-v2[43], and indexed per disease pair (3,160–3,960 chunks each). At query time, the top-k (k = 10) most similar chunks were retrieved by cosine similarity and concatenated into the prompt context. Chunk size and k were not tuned; we selected standard defaults to provide a reasonable baseline rather than an optimised competitor, since the comparison tests the effect of structure (graph vs. raw text), not retrieval engineering. 17 • HEG-TKG : System prompt + clinical scenario + structured evidence from the knowledge graph (entities, relationships, quality tiers, temporal anchors, PMID citations) Graph retrieval uses disease-specific Cypher queries against Neo4j 5[44] (Supplementary S3), returning relevant subgraphs formatted as structured evidence blocks with inline PMID citations and quality tier in-dicators. End-to-end inference latency for HEG-TKG comprises Cypher subgraph retrieval ( <1 s), evidence formatting ( <1 s), and LLM synthesis. Total wall-clock time is ∼8 s with local open-source models (Supple-mentary S27) and 20–50 s with cloud frontier models, dominated by the synthesis model’s generation time. The retrieval overhead is negligible relative to vanilla inference. 

## 4.4 Multi-model citation verification 

Vanilla mode. Four frontier LLMs (GPT-4.1[45], GPT-5.4, Claude Sonnet 4.6[46], DeepSeek-v3[47]) gener-ated clinical outputs for all 36 scenarios using their default behavior (no citation instruction). Outputs were parsed for PMID patterns (regex: PMID: \s* \d+ ), author–year references, and other citation formats. 

Citation-prompted mode. Three models (Claude Opus 4.6[46], Claude Sonnet 4.6, DeepSeek-v3[47]) received an additional instruction: “You must cite specific PubMed PMIDs for every clinical claim you make.” 

Why PMIDs. We use PubMed identifiers as the verification standard because they provide unambiguous resolution (one integer maps to exactly one publication), machine verification (E-utilities API lookup in <10 seconds), and scalability (automated audit of thousands of citations). Author–year references (e.g., “Gilhus et al., 2016”) require manual PubMed search, disambiguation among multiple matching publications, and verification that the specific paper supports the claim, requiring approximately 2–5 minutes per reference. DOIs present similar problems: in preliminary tests, frontier models generated plausible DOI strings (e.g., 

10.1016/j.nmd... ) that either did not resolve or pointed to unrelated publications, mirroring the PMID fabrication pattern. The identifier type does not matter; the failure is in parametric generation of structured identifiers. 

PubMed verification. Every extracted PMID (1,147 unique across all experiments) was verified against the PubMed E-utilities API[28] ( esummary endpoint). For each PMID, we retrieved the publication title, journal, and MeSH terms, then classified clinical relevance by keyword matching against 30 neuromuscular disease terms in publication titles (Supplementary S4). 

## 4.5 Clinician evaluation protocol 

Seventy-two cases (36 scenarios × 2 systems: Vanilla and HEG-TKG) were generated, each presenting a single clinical output without system identification. Guideline-RAG is excluded from clinician evaluation to conserve evaluator time; its three-arm comparison is covered by the LLM judge evaluation. Cases were shuffled using a deterministic seed and assigned sequential case IDs. Three evaluators—two neurologists (C1, senior; C2) and one medical informatician (MI)—scored cases independently on five Likert-scale dimensions (1–5). C1 scored the reduced 36-case pack (18 per arm); C2 scored the full 72-case pack (36 per arm); MI scored 36 of 72 cases but was excluded from primary analysis due to ceiling effects (Section 2.6). The five dimensions are: 

• D1: Verifiability - Can each clinical claim be traced to a specific, identifiable published source? 

• D2: Actionability - Is the information sufficient to inform a clinical decision without additional literature search? 18 • D3: Temporal Precision - Does the output provide specific time windows for disease onset, progres-sion milestones, and clinical trajectories? 

• D4: Non-Expert Safety - How safe is this output if a non-specialist GP followed it without additional expert consultation? 

• D5: Clinical Completeness - Does the output cover key differential diagnoses, therapeutic options, contraindications, and monitoring? The five dimensions were designed to test HEG-TKG’s specific contributions: D1 measures the Provenance Gap directly; D3 measures the temporal anchoring that distinguishes HEG-TKG from static knowledge graphs; D4 addresses the GP-to-specialist referral use case where AI-assisted triage must be safe without specialist oversight (Supplementary S8). 

## 4.6 LLM-as-judge validation 

A three-model judge panel (GPT-4o-mini, DeepSeek-V3, Claude 3 Haiku) evaluated 108 cases (36 scenarios 

× 3 arms) using the same five clinician-aligned Likert dimensions (D1–D5). We selected fast, cost-efficient models from three different providers to maximise provider diversity while keeping the 648-evaluation budget (108 cases × 3 judges × 2 rounds) feasible; the v1/v2 comparison tests whether any LLM judge can assess verifiability, not whether frontier judges perform better. Temperature was set to 0 for all judges. Cases were blinded with randomly generated IDs and shuffled to prevent order effects. 

Citation-aware judging (v2). A second evaluation round augmented each prompt with a PubMed citation audit report (Supplementary S6.4). This design tests whether LLM judges can assess citation quality without external verification data. 

LLM–human correlation. The 72 base cases evaluated by clinicians were also scored by the LLM judge panel using the same five-dimension rubric. This shared-case, shared-rubric design enables per-dimension Spearman ρ between human panel means (C1 + C2) and LLM panel means on each dimension, separately for v1 (blind) and v2 (citation-aware). 

## 4.7 Counterfactual faithfulness experiment 

Fifteen clinically incorrect evidence statements (5 per disease pair) were injected into the HEG-TKG evidence pipeline. Errors were clinically meaningful: swapped antibody targets, reversed age thresholds, incorrect first-line treatments. For each injection, we measured: (1) parametric resistance, (2) faithfulness to wrong evidence, and (3) detectability via citation trace. 

## 4.8 Statistical analysis 

Pairwise comparisons between independent arms were tested using the Mann-Whitney U test[48] (two-sided). Effect sizes are reported as Cohen’s d[49]. 95% confidence intervals were computed via non-parametric bootstrap (10,000 resamples, seed=42). Multiple testing across six pairwise comparisons (3 disease pairs × 2contrasts: HEG-TKG vs Vanilla, HEG-TKG vs Guideline-RAG) was corrected using the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure[50] (FDR α = 0 .05). Inter-rater reliability was assessed using Krippendorff’s alpha[39] ( α ≥ 0.80 = excellent). 

Provenance Gap. For each scenario, PG = max(FC − ETS × r, 0), where FC is clinical feature coverage, ETS is the evidence traceability score, and r is a citation reliability coefficient: r = 0 .97 for HEG-TKG (verified PMIDs, checkable in <10 s), r = 0 .80 for vanilla (author–year references, 88% point to 19 wrong papers), and r = 0 .50 for Guideline-RAG (vague source names, ∼50% independently verifiable). The max( ·, 0) clamp prevents negative PG when citations exceed feature coverage. Pooled PG is the mean across all per-scenario values. 

## 4.9 PHI-compliant deployment architecture 

HEG-TKG separates two data planes: (1) the knowledge graph (public PubMed/GeneReviews/OMIM/Or-phanet data, deployable without privacy constraints) and (2) the clinical synthesis step (potential PHI, requiring privacy controls). The synthesis model is configurable between cloud APIs and locally served open-source models via Ollama[32]. A validate privacy config(strict=True) function raises an error if any component routes data externally (Supplementary S25).

# S4: Citation Verification Protocol 

## Verification Process 

Every PMID extracted from clinical outputs was verified against the PubMed E-utilities API using the following protocol: 1. PMID Extraction : Regex pattern PMID[: \s]*( \d{6,9 }) applied to all clinical outputs 2. API Query : Each PMID queried via https://eutils.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/entrez/eutils/esummary.fcgi?db=pubme 

3. Metadata Extraction : Publication title, journal name, and MeSH terms extracted from the XML response 4. Relevance Classification : Automated keyword matching against disease-specific terms: 5. Three-way Classification :

• Clinically relevant : PMID exists AND title/MeSH contains disease-specific keywords 

• Real, wrong field : PMID exists BUT title/MeSH contains no disease-specific keywords

# S6: LLM Judge Prompts (v1 Blind + v2 Citation-Aware) 

## S6.1: Five Evaluation Dimensions 

All judges (both v1 and v2) score on the same five clinician-aligned Likert dimensions (1–5), matching the clinician evaluation rubric: 

ID Dimension Rubric 

D1 Verifiability Score 1–5 on whether clinical claims can be traced to specific published evidence. Does the output cite specific PMIDs, studies, or guidelines? Can a clinician verify each claim within 1 minute? Score 5 if most claims have verifiable citations; 1 if no citations. D2 Actionability Score 1–5 on whether the output provides actionable clinical guidance. Does it specify concrete next steps, dosing, monitoring, or referral cri-teria? Score 5 if immediately actionable; 1 if only general information. D3 Temporal Precision Score 1–5 on whether the output provides specific time points for dis-ease milestones, treatment response windows, and monitoring intervals. Score 5 if precise temporal anchoring; 1 if only vague temporal language. D4 Non-Expert Safety Score 1–5 on whether the output is safe for a non-specialist (e.g., GP) to act on. Are red flags clearly flagged? Are dangerous diagnostic pitfalls highlighted? Score 5 if safe for non-expert use; 1 if serious safety risks without specialist oversight. D5 Clinical Completeness Score 1–5 on whether the output covers ALL clinically important fea-tures for this scenario: key differentiating features, relevant investiga-tions, treatment options, prognosis, and red flags.

## S6.6: Case Design 

The 36 base scenarios were evaluated across all 3 arms (Vanilla, Guideline-RAG, HEG-TKG), yielding 108 cases total (36 per arm). Each case was assigned a blinded random ID and shuffled using a deterministic seed (seed=42) to prevent order effects. No demographic or severity expansion was applied; each case corresponds to exactly one base scenario and one system arm, avoiding pseudo-replication. The evaluation key mapping blinded IDs to systems and scenarios is stored per disease pair.

Single-blind independent-cases evaluation. Each evaluator reviews 24 clinical cases per disease pair. Each case presents a clinical scenario followed by a single AI-generated response. Evaluators are fully blinded to which system produced each output.

# S27: Algorithm 1 - HEG-TKG Construction and Clinical Inference Pipeline 

The following pseudocode formally specifies the complete HEG-TKG pipeline, from corpus acquisition through clinical output generation. The algorithm is divided into two phases: Phase I constructs the knowledge graph offline (run once per disease pair), and Phase II performs clinical inference online (run per query).

# S29: Worked Example - From Query to Verifiable Output 

A GP who suspects Lambert-Eaton syndrome in a patient with proximal weakness and autonomic dysfunc-tion. The GP asks: “Differentiate MG from LEMS given these findings.” 

Vanilla GPT-4.1 produces a correct but often unverifiable response: “LEMS is associated with P/Q-type voltage-gated calcium channel antibodies and frequently occurs as a paraneoplastic syndrome. Autonomic features and areflexia help distinguish it from MG.” No citation, no way to verify any claim. 

HEG-TKG retrieves a disease-specific subgraph from the knowledge graph and produces structured out-put with inline citations: “MG: fluctuating fatigable weakness affecting ocular and bulbar muscles [PMID:30470270, SILVER]; symptoms worsen with exertion [PMID:32057206, SILVER]; reflexes normal [GeneReviews, GOLD]. LEMS: proximal limb weakness precedes ocular involvement [GeneReviews, GOLD]; autonomic symptoms (dry mouth, constipation) [GeneReviews, GOLD]; reflexes reduced but increase after brief exercise [GeneRe-views, GOLD]; decremental pattern on repetitive nerve stimulation differs from MG [PMID:24332471, SIL-VER].” Every claim maps to a PMID or curated source. The GP can look up PMID:30470270, confirm it is a 2019 

Neurologic Clinics article on myasthenia gravis, and verify the fatigability claim in under 30 seconds. The quality tier (GOLD = confirmed by both curated sources and literature extraction; SILVER = supported by multiple independent PMIDs) tells the GP how much weight to place on each claim. None of this is available from the vanilla output.

# S31: PHI-Compliant Local Deployment Details 

Patient information cannot leave institutional infrastructure under HIPAA (US), GDPR Article 9 (EU), and equivalent regulations in most jurisdictions. HEG-TKG addresses this through a dual-architecture deploy-ment model. The knowledge graph itself contains no patient data. It stores only disease-level relationships extracted from public PubMed abstracts and authoritative sources. At inference time, the clinical sce-nario (which may contain PHI) is processed by the synthesis model. We validated that this synthesis step can run entirely on-premise using open-source models (Qwen 3 14B, Gemma 3 27B) served via Ollama, achieving 75-80% citation coverage on a single institutional GPU (Supplementary Table S25). The system’s 

validate privacy config() function enforces deployment-time checks: in strict mode, any configuration routing patient data to cloud APIs raises an error. A regulatory compliance mapping across EU AI Act Article 14, FDA CDS Criterion 4, and GDPR Article 9/HIPAA requirements is provided in Supplementary Table S26.
