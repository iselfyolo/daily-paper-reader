## Abstract 

It is crucial to explore the impact of different teaching methods on student learning in educa-tional research. However, real-person experi-ments face significant ethical constraints, and we cannot conduct repeated teaching experi-ments on the same student. LLM-based gener-ative agents offer a promising avenue for simu-lating student behavior. Before large-scale ex-periments, a fundamental question must be ad-dressed: are student agents truly credible, and can they faithfully simulate human learning? In this study, we built a Big Five Personality-based student agent model with a full pipeline of student-teacher interaction, self-study, and examination. To evaluate behavioral fidelity, we collected 13 empirical studies on Big Five traits and learning, and distilled them into 14 criteria. We found that the 71.4% of the stu-dent agents’ behavior was aligned with human learners. 

## 1 Introduction and Related Work 

As LLMs become increasingly prevalent in edu-cation, research has shifted from simple question-answering toward complex pedagogical agent in-teractions (Chu et al., 2025). Rooted in social con-structivism, Scaffolding Theory holds that mentors provide temporary and targeted support to help learners traverse their Zone of Proximal Develop-ment (Wood et al., 1976; Vygotsky, 1978; Vygot-sky et al., 2011). More broadly, human experiments face ethical constraints and cannot expose the same student to repeated interventions, making simula-tion an alternative. Agent-Based Modeling (ABM) is a powerful simulation approach for complex systems. In the first work on generative agents, Park et al. (2023) instantiated LLM-based generative agents in an interactive sandbox environment. Building on this work, Xiao et al. (2023) demonstrated the reliability of generative ABMs in simulating social events. Research on LLM-based math instruction shows that agent personality traits significantly change learning outcomes — students favor complemen-tary personalities (Li et al., 2025), while conscien-tious or neutral personas best promote cognitive engagement (Lyu et al., 2025a,b). However, these studies only use agents as teachers and humans as students; there is limited research using agents as students with a memory system. Constructing a full-pipeline teaching agent is technically challeng-ing, yet the value of building such agents is substan-tial: they enable simulation of learning trajectories over time, which is a genuine gap in the literature, and allow systematic testing of how different per-sonality types respond to different teaching styles, something nearly impossible in human-participant studies. They also offer cost-effective alternatives to human experiments and may yield unexpected findings. Nevertheless, researchers cannot directly use stu-dent agents for experiments before validating their effectiveness and fidelity. In this paper, we address this question by constructing ABMs grounded in the Big Five personality traits, evaluating their learning and exam performance across four mathe-matical domains: Algebra, Number Theory, Count-ing and Probability, and Geometry. We analyzed the results of student agents with different person-alities after rounds of teacher interaction or self-study 1. We further collected 13 prior studies on the influence of Big Five personality traits on hu-man students, distilled them into 14 criteria to as-sess how well LLM-based agents align with human learning characteristics. We found that most agents improved after learning, though over-learning tends to worsen performance. High-extraversion stu-dent agents performed best on average, while high-neuroticism student agents performed worst. Over-

## 2 Pipeline 

NuminaMath-CoT (LI et al., 2024) consists of 860k math problems with chain-of-thought so-lutions. We used the bert-finetuned-math-prob-classification model (Devlin et al., 2018) 2 to clas-sify and select questions across four domains: Al-gebra, Number Theory, Counting and Probability, and Geometry. We then filtered and retained 3,044 questions with classification confidence above 0.95. To improve evaluation accuracy, we prepared two formats of answers: LaTeX and plain text. The questions are split into development and test sets, and pre-embedded to vector space for retrieval us-ing the inf-retriever-v1-1.5b retriever (Yang et al., 2025). All agents are based on the Large Language Model gpt-oss-120b (OpenAI, 2025). We extracted personality descriptions of the big five personality traits from research of Goldberg (1990) to con-struct the personality basis of the student agent. All types of student agents will experience two identical stages: the learning round and the exam round. During the learning round, student agents decide among three actions: asking the teacher, self-studying, or resting. When asking the teacher, the student selects a topic of interest, and the teacher agent explains the relevant knowledge, re-trieves a closely related question from the question bank, and walks through the solution. The teacher agent doesn’t have a memory system, but is in-formed to explain the question adaptively to the student’s personality. During self-study, the stu-dent selects a topic to review and independently retrieves a related question to study. Resting skips the round entirely. At the end of each learning round, newly acquired knowledge is encoded into memory and merged with prior memory, with all entries vectorized for retrieval. During the exam round, student agents retrieve relevant memories to inform their responses to exam questions. The entire pipeline is more like a real person than a machine learning model, where the agent decides what to learn and what to recall during the exam.

## 3 Results and Discussion 

We configured the teaching system to run for 10, 20, and 50 rounds, with each set of rounds run in-dependently for 3 times. We also set up a control group without learning. Each exam is single-topic; a round consists of 100 questions. We then mea-sured and recorded the learning outcomes of the student agents. The F1 (macro F1) results from figure 2 show that for all four math types, all agents showed im-provement after some rounds of learning. However, fewer rounds of learning often outperformed 50 rounds, suggesting that exam performance tends to drop after over-learning — possibly because once memory becomes saturated, the retriever struggles to retrieve useful information to assist with answer-ing. We can also observe that agent performance varies across different math types: even though all math problems came from the same original dataset, agents’ understanding of geometry was far weaker than algebra, resulting in an average score difference of nearly 30%. Another notable finding is that learning significantly reduced the number of blank answers, though high-neuroticism student agents were an exception compared to other agents. The bottom of Figure 2 illustrates interac-tion choices across personality types: high-conscientiousness agents interacted least with the teacher, while those high-agreeableness and open-ness showed a greater tendency to do so. Most notably, high-neuroticism agents engaged with the teacher in every single learning round — the high-est rate among all five personalities. Regarding time cost, high-conscientiousness agents consumed the fewest timestamps during both learning and examination, while high-neuroticism agents con-sumed the most, consistent with procrastination patterns documented in the literature (Schouwen-burg and Lay, 1995; Lee et al., 2006). In terms of final average score rankings, high-extraversion student agents performed best overall. To validate that agent behavior aligns with hu-man learners, we collect 13 empirical studies in Ta-ble 1, across five dimensions and 14 scoring criteria. Overall, student agents perform similarly to human learners across general dimensions, with minor discrepancies in the math do-main. High-extraversion agent rather than high-conscientiousness achieved the best scores — though this is not entirely inconsistent with the literature: Meyer et al. (2023) reported that ex-traversion is associated with more effective student interactions, and Mammadov (2022) notes that ex-traversion is a stronger predictor of academic per-formance in early education, with conscientious-ness becoming dominant only at higher levels. Af-ter analyzing the saved agent memory, we found that high-extraversion agents get an average longer teacher’s reply during learning rounds, while high-conscientiousness agents interacted least with the teacher and tend to study by themselves, which may partly explain their underwhelming performance. The most anomalies also emerged in teacher-student interaction: agents high in neuroticism in-teracted most frequently, contrary to expectations. Karabenick and Knapp (1991) mentioned that anx-iety leads to executive help-seeking. Thus, we hy-pothesize that the high-neuroticism student agent’s model captured the anxiety-driven help-seeking tendency, but failed to simulate the help-avoidance mechanism in real-world humans. Low-quality interactions also diluted the agent’s learning mem-ories, ultimately resulting in the weakest academic performance. A key limitation of this study is the lack of a well-defined quantitative framework for evaluating how closely agent behavior mirrors that of human learn-ers. Our current evaluation relies on manual scor-ing against findings from prior literature, partly because exam scores are straightforward to mea-sure while learning behaviors are not, and partly because fine-grained behavioral data from human studies is rarely available. For instance, while the literature may report what proportion of students seek teacher help, it seldom captures the quality of those interactions or their specific content, leaving us to infer interaction quality indirectly from exam performance. Personality prompts are static, but real human personalities change dynamically during learning, and agents cannot simulate these situational fluc-tuations in personality. Furthermore, it’s difficult to determine whether personality has a direct or indirect impact on the agent. While we can confirm a correlation between academic and exam perfor-mance and the established personality traits, the mechanism remains unclear. Potentially, we could explore whether personality prompts influence the model’s token probabilities. On the other hand, these limitations point to promising directions for future work. One particu-larly interesting avenue is studying the interaction between student and teacher personality: are simi-lar personalities more effective, or complementary ones? This kind of crossover design is difficult to run with human participants, but well-suited to agent-based simulation.

## A Computational Cost and Runtime 

We conducted all experiments using one NVIDIA L4 GPU alongside the gpt-oss-120b API, where the GPU was used to embed the question bank into vectors and to perform retrieval for memory and teacher-student interactions. We used the GPT-oss-120b API as the backbone model. Running 50 learning rounds took approximately 20 minutes, while completing a 100-question exam took ap-proximately 90 minutes. The exact time varied by agent personality: students high in Neuroticism consumed more time, while those high in Consci-entiousness consumed less. 

## B Timestamp Rules 

During each learning round, the student agent au-tonomously selects one of three actions: 

Self-Study: The agent first decides what content to review, then the retriever fetches a relevant ques-tion (with answer) from the question bank based on the agent’s stated intent, which is subsequently stored in memory. This action consumes 2 times-tamps .

Ask Teacher: The agent first decides on the topic or direction of the question. The teacher agent, who has been informed of the student’s per-sonality profile prior to the interaction, responds accordingly and adapts their instruction to the stu-dent’s personality traits. The teacher then retrieves a relevant question from the question bank to illus-trate the explanation, which the student stores in memory. This action consumes 3 timestamps .

Take a Rest: The agent skips the current round. This action consumes 1 timestamp .During the examination round, for each question, the student agent first reads the problem, then de-cides what memories to retrieve, and the retriever fetches the most relevant entries from the mem-ory store. The agent then answers the question following the prompt using the retrieved memo-ries. Under normal circumstances, answering one question consumes 2 timestamps . If the mem-ory retrieval step returns no response, the agent is granted one additional attempt to reconsider and re-retrieve, consuming an additional 1 timestamp ,for a total of 3 timestamps for that question. 6C Question Domains 

We do not rely on a large-scale dataset. Instead, we curate a question bank of several hundred prob-lems per mathematical domain, each with detailed solutions and worked examples to support both self-study and teacher-agent explanations. The question bank is split into a development set and a test set. Not every development problem is encountered dur-ing learning — how much a student agent engages with the material depends on its personality — but we ensure no overlap between the problems seen during learning and those used in the exam. The statistical data for the question bank is shown in Table 2. Exam scoring is based on final answers only, with no assessment of intermediate steps. Since answers may be formatted differently by differ-ent agents, a response is counted as correct if it matches either the LaTeX or plain-text version of the reference answer. Per-question F1 is taken as the maximum across both formats, and the overall score is reported as the macro F1.
