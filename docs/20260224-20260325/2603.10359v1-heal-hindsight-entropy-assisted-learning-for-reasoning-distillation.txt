Abstract 

Distilling reasoning capabilities from Large Reasoning Models (LRMs) into smaller mod-els is typically constrained by the limitation of rejection sampling. Standard methods treat the teacher as a static filter, discarding com-plex "corner-case" problems where the teacher fails to explore valid solutions independently, thereby creating an artificial "Teacher Ceiling" for the student. In this work, we propose Hind-sight Entropy-Assisted Learning (HEAL), an RL-free framework designed to bridge this rea-soning gap. Drawing on the educational theory of the Zone of Proximal Development(ZPD), HEAL synergizes three core modules: (1) Guided Entropy-Assisted Repair (GEAR), an active intervention mechanism that detects crit-ical reasoning breakpoints via entropy dynam-ics and injects targeted hindsight hints to repair broken trajectories; (2) Perplexity-Uncertainty Ratio Estimator (PURE), a rigorous filter-ing protocol that decouples genuine cognitive breakthroughs from spurious shortcuts; and (3) Progressive Answer-guided Curriculum Evo-lution (PACE), a three-stage distillation strat-egy that organizes training from foundational alignment to frontier breakthrough. Extensive experiments on multiple benchmarks demon-strate that HEAL significantly outperforms tra-ditional SFT distillation and other baselines.

where a teacher model generates multiple reason-ing trajectories for a given problem, and only the correct trajectories are retained for student train-ing. As illustrated in Figure 1A, this process ef-fectively treats the teacher model as a static filter. While the teacher can effortlessly generate valid paths for simpler problems, it often struggles with complex, "corner-case" problems due to limited exploration capabilities. Consequently, when the teacher model fails to independently derive the cor-rect answer, these valuable hard problems are la-beled as "unsolvable" and discarded. This leads to a significant data efficiency problem: the stu-dent model is trained primarily on easy-to-medium samples, creating an artificial "Teacher Ceiling", where the student model is deprived of the oppor-tunity to learn from the most challenging segment of the problem distribution. Our empirical probing shows that even when scaling the sampling budget to N = 64 , the teacher model still fundamentally fails to generate any valid trajectory for 13% of hard problems 1. This indicates that the "Teacher Ceiling" is a probabilistic barrier rather than a mere budget limitation. In this work, we argue that the teacher’s failure to solve a hard problem independently does not imply a lack of latent reasoning capability. Sometimes, the model simply needs a "nudge" to navigate the correct search space. As depicted in Figure 1B, instead of discarding the failed hard problems, we introduce a Hindsight Hint mechanism (symbolized by the golden key). By conditioning the generation on the ground-truth answer or specific intermediate steps, we guide the teacher to reconstruct valid rea-soning trajectories for these previously intractable problems. This process transforms what was once "waste data" into valuable training signals. The incorporation of these additional reasoning trajec-tories for challenging problems facilitates further improvements in the student model’s distillation performance. To address the "Teacher Ceiling" problem, we propose Hindsight Entropy-Assisted Learning (HEAL). Our framework is inspired by a pedagog-ical evolution where a teacher refines their self-study and lesson plans to better mentor a student. Our framework primarily consists of three main components. The first component, Guided Entropy-Assisted

Repair (GEAR), mimics an expert teacher’s self-repair process: when encountering a problem be-yond their immediate grasp, they attempt to solve it independently and only consult the answer at the exact moment they become "stuck." This aligns with Vygotsky’s Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) (Vygotsky, 1978) and Scaffolding theories (Wood et al., 1976), which define the optimal learn-ing space as the gap between unaided and guided capability. In HEAL, we monitor entropy gradients to detect "reasoning dead-ends"—the boundaries of the ZPD. By injecting the ground-truth answer as a local hint only during entropy spikes, we provide the necessary scaffolding to bridge the logic gap while preserving the authenticity of the teacher’s original reasoning. A great teacher, however, does not deliver raw scratchpads to students; they refine them into struc-tured "lesson plans". We introduce Perplexity-Uncertainty Ratio Estimator (PURE), a Ratio-based Filtering Protocol for pedagogical quality control. This step ensures that the teacher’s newly mastered solutions are logically sound and explain-able, rather than being "shortcuts" created by force-fitting a logic to a known answer. By contrasting step-wise perplexity (PPL) with step-wise answer uncertainty, we prune incoherent "shortcuts" where logic jumps abruptly to the conclusion. This re-finement ensures the student is taught via clear, step-by-step derivations. Finally, knowledge transfer is organized through Progressive Answer-guided Curriculum Evolution (PACE), a three-stage curriculum learning based distillation, mimicking a structured educational pro-gression. The student model first consolidates foun-dational knowledge from standard correct paths be-fore tackling the "entropy-repaired" trajectories of complex corner cases. This ensures a robust cog-nitive foundation, preventing noise and enabling the student model to more easily digest the most challenging knowledge. Our main contributions are as follows: 1. We propose HEAL, an interventionist frame-work that applies the ZPD principles to LRM distillation, actively repairing failed reasoning trajectories via GEAR. 2. We design PURE, a ratio-based filtering met-ric to perform pedagogical refinement, decou-pling genuine reasoning breakthroughs from spurious shortcuts to ensure high-quality les-son plans for distillation. 3. We introduce PACE, a three-stage curriculum distillation strategy and conduct extensive val-idation experiments across multiple datasets with models of varying parameter scales. The experimental results demonstrate that the pro-posed approach significantly improved the dis-tillation performance of LRMs. 

2 Related Work 

Large Reasoning Model Distillation. Knowledge distillation serves as an effective alternative, trans-ferring reasoning capabilities from strong teacher models to smaller student models via Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) (Shridhar et al., 2023). Rep-resentative works include DeepSeek-R1-Distill se-ries (Guo et al., 2025) and recent data-efficient approaches like s1 (Muennighoff et al., 2025) and LIMO (Ye et al., 2025), which demonstrate that a small number of high-quality reasoning trajectories can yield substantial performance gains. Unlike Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR), distillation has been shown to improve both accuracy and capability, provided that the teacher traces introduce new knowledge or supe-rior reasoning patterns not present in the student model (Kim et al., 2025; Yue et al., 2025). Fur-thermore, distilled models often exhibit more "flex-ible reasoning" traits, such as multi-perspective thinking, anthropomorphic internal monologues, and metacognitive awareness, which are crucial for solving complex problems but are difficult to emerge solely through Zero-RL on smaller archi-tectures (Hu et al., 2025). Despite its efficiency and effectiveness, existing reasoning distillation methods face the Teacher Ceiling challenge: The capability of the student model is constrained by the teacher model and can never solve problems beyond the scope of the teacher model’s abilities. In this work, we enhance the distillation effect of the student model by elevating the "ceiling" of the teacher model through hint-guided sampling. 

Knowledge Guided Reasoning. Prior studies (Li et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025; Zhang et al., 2025) have incorporated guidance prompts during the training of reasoning models to reduce exploration along erroneous paths. However, these approaches are primarily RL-based and require full ground-truth reasoning processes. In contrast, our method targets the distillation scenario, relying solely on the final correct answer. Eliciting reasoning paths by conditioning on the final answer has been ex-plored in works like STaR (Zelikman et al., 2022) and RAVR (Lin et al., 2025). STaR bootstraps rea-soning by prompting a model to rationalize ground-truth answers, while RAVR employs prompt en-gineering to simulate independent solving. How-ever, these paradigms face two critical limitations when applied to complex Large Reasoning Mod-els (LRMs). First, they rely entirely on global answer hints, which are often insufficient for ex-treme corner-case mathematical problems. Second, blindly conditioning on answers inevitably induces severe shortcut learning, where rationales logically "drift" toward the solution without valid derivation. This paper designs a specialized module (PURE) specifically to address such spurious shortcuts. 

Self-Distillation Paradigms. Very recently, self-distillation has emerged as a promising paradigm for enhancing reasoning models (Hübotter et al., 2026; Shenfeld et al., 2026; Zhao et al., 2026). While these concurrent works primarily focus on on-policy self-improvement loops within a sin-gle model architecture, HEAL fundamentally tar-gets cross-model distillation. Our primary ob-jective is to explicitly demonstrate how a com-pact student model can break the "Teacher Ceil-ing" caused by standard rejection sampling from a stronger, distinct teacher. Furthermore, HEAL’s core mechanisms—entropy-guided local repair and ratio-based shortcut filtering—are model-agnostic, making them highly complementary and can be seamlessly integrated into future self-distillation pipelines.

4 Methodology 

We propose HEAL, a unified framework designed to unlock the latent reasoning capabilities of teacher models for efficient distillation. As illus-trated in Figure 2, HEAL synergizes three core modules to address the challenges of exploration, quality control, and knowledge transfer: • Synthesis via GEAR: An active intervention module that detects reasoning dead-ends via entropy dynamics and repairs them with tar-geted hindsight hints 2, synthesizing valid tra-jectories for previously intractable problems. • Filtering via PURE: A rigorous quality con-trol metric that decouples genuine reasoning breakthroughs from spurious shortcuts, ensur-ing the logical integrity of the synthesized data. • Training via PACE: A stagewise distillation strategy that organizes training data from foun-dational to complex, enabling the student model to robustly absorb the repaired reason-ing paths. The formulation and implementation of these three modules is elaborated in following subsec-tions.

4.1 Synthesis via GEAR 

Standard rejection sampling is often prohibitively inefficient for "hard" instances, where the probabil-ity of autonomously sampling a correct trajectory is vanishingly small. The goal of GEAR is to amplify the yield rate of valid reasoning paths for these chal-lenging samples for the teacher model by actively intervening at critical moments of uncertainty. 

Theoretical Grounding. Our approach is grounded in the educational theory of the ZPD (Vygotsky, 1978). We posit that for hard prob-lems, the correct reasoning path lies within the teacher model’s ZPD—it cannot be traversed independently but is accessible with appropriate scaffolding (Wood et al., 1976). GEAR opera-tionalizes this by identifying the exact boundary of the ZPD (where the teacher’s reasoning confidence wavers) and providing a temporary scaffold (hindsight hint) to bridge the cognitive gap. 

Identifying Critical Reasoning Breakpoint. 

We identify the precise boundary of independent ca-pability by analyzing the entropy dynamics within the foundational phase of reasoning. Rather than reacting to minor fluctuations, we seek the moment of maximal cognitive dissonance. 4.2 Filtering via PURE 

A critical challenge in answer-conditioned gen-eration is the phenomenon of "logical shortcuts". When provided with the ground-truth answer, lan-guage models are prone to generating "cheating" rationales that are syntactically coherent but log-ically disconnected (e.g. " Since the reference an-swer is 36, the Final Answer is 36 "), often jump-ing abruptly to the final conclusion without valid derivation. Such trajectories act as noise during distillation. To rigorously sanitize the synthesized datasets, we introduce PURE to decouple genuine reasoning breakthroughs from spurious shortcuts. PURE identifies shortcuts by examining the relationship between a step’s perplexity and the model’s confidence in the reference answer after that step. We define the Suspicion Ratio Rt as: 

Rt = PPL (st|q, s <t )

NLL (a|q, s ≤t) + ϵ (4) where: PPL (st|q, s <t ) denotes the step-wise per-plexity evaluated by the teacher model πT , quan-tifying the surprisal of generating the current step given the preceding context. NLL (a|q, s ≤t) is the Answer Uncertainty, defined as Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL) of the ground-truth answer a

given the current step st. ϵ is a smoothing term to prevent division by zero. To operationalize this metric, we process each repaired trajectory by first excluding the final two steps, as these typically contain the concluding statement (e.g., " The answer is X "), which natu-rally exhibits high information gain but is logically valid. For the remaining steps, we compute the Suspicion Ratio Rt sequentially, generating a ratio curve for the entire reasoning path. We then iden-tify all local peaks within this curve and designate the global maximum peak value as the Trajectory Anomaly Score (Atraj = max( Rt)). This single scalar serves as a proxy for the worst-case logical violation within a trajectory. Finally, to construct the clean Drepair dataset, we rank all synthesized trajectories by their anomaly scores and prune the top λ% (e.g., λ = 20 ), effectively filtering out the most egregious instances of shortcut learning while preserving the valid reasoning trajectories. 

4.3 Training via PACE 

While GEAR and PURE provide high-quality rea-soning trajectories for complex problems, directly exposing a student model to these advanced sam-ples can lead to training instability or catastrophic forgetting of foundational skills. To mitigate this, we propose PACE, a three-stage distillation strategy designed to align the student’s learning trajectory with the complexity of the data. We categorize the training data into three tiers of complexity: • Dbase (Independent Reasoning): Standard trajectories obtained via rejection sampling where the teacher solves problems without assistance. • Dhint (Global Hindsight): Trajectories gener-ated by providing the ground-truth answer as a global hint in the prompt 3, filtered by PURE. These represent problems within the teacher’s latent capability but requiring goal-oriented guidance. • Drepair (Local Repair): Trajectories synthe-sized via GEAR’s entropy-guided interven-tion and filtered by PURE. These represent the "hardest" corner cases where the teacher faces reasoning dead-ends even with global hints. PACE organizes the distillation process into three progressive stages: 

Stage I: Foundation Alignment. The student is trained exclusively on Dbase .

LI = LSF T (θ, Dbase ) (5) This phase ensures the student masters funda-mental arithmetic rules and the teacher’s baseline reasoning patterns, establishing a stable instruction-following capability.

Dhint to unlock reasoning paths that are valid but require answer-conditioned navigation. To prevent catastrophic forgetting, we mix these with the foun-dational data (e.g., Dbase ∪ D hint ). 

LII = LSF T (θ, Dbase + Dhint ) (6) This phase bridges the gap between indepen-dent solving and complex repairs, expanding the student’s solution space to medium-difficulty prob-lems.

into the mix, typically upsampling them to empha-size handling cognitive impasses.

5.1 Experimental Settings 5.1.1 Benchmarks 

We evaluate models on four mainstream and highly challenging reasoning benchmarks: • MATH 500 (Hendrycks et al., 2021; Light-man et al., 2023): It is a representative subset of the MATH dataset, comprising 500 prob-lems that span diverse mathematical disci-plines and difficulty levels. • AIME 2024 & AIME 2025 (MAA., 2024, 2025): They consist of 30 problems from the American Invitational Mathematics Examina-tion, respectively. • OlympiadBench (He et al., 2024): It is a comprehensive and rigorous benchmark fea-turing elite-level problems from international Olympiads. We evaluate on the subset of 675 English math problems. 

5.1.2 Teacher Model and Student Models 

For reasoning distillation, we employ Qwen3-32B as the teacher model to generate high-quality rea-soning trajectories. For the student models, we utilize Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct and Qwen3-4B-Base. This selection allows us to evaluate the efficacy of our method across varying parameter scales and model types. 

5.1.3 Baseline Methods 

We compare our proposed approach with three rep-resentative baselines: • SFT : Full-parameter supervised fine-tuning is the most commonly used distillation method for LRM. • LIMO (Ye et al., 2025): LIMO employs hand-crafted rules to select the reasoning paths sam-pled from the teacher model. Following this methodology, we reproduced a comparable data selection pipeline for our training dataset. • Curriculum SFT (Wen et al., 2025): This method aims to enhance Long-CoT reasoning capabilities through multi-stage training pro-cess characterized by progressive difficulty. 5.1.4 Training Details 

We implement the training pipeline via the MS-SWIFT 4 framework using 919 problems from the AIME 5 spanning 1985 to 2023. To construct the distillation dataset, we employ a dynamic sampling strategy with a temperature of 0.7 and top_p of 0.8. The data elicitation process proceeds in three stages based on problem difficulty: 1. Initial Sampling ( Dbase ): We first sample 30 reasoning trajectories per problem using the teacher model. Problems with a pass rate (Pass@30) below 0.5 are identified as "hard" instances. 2. Global Hindsight Sampling ( Dhint ): For these hard instances, we perform an additional round of sampling (30 trajectories per problem) using answer-conditioned prompts to elicit valid paths. 3. Local Repair Sampling ( Drepair ): For "ex-tremely hard" problems—defined as those yielding 

≤ 1 correct trajectory in the initial phase—we se-lect 10 incorrect paths and apply GEAR to repair them, generating 20 candidate repaired trajectories for each error path. All sampled trajectories undergo rigorous fil-tering via PURE. The final distilled dataset con-sists of 5,777 samples for Dbase , 1,108 samples for Dhint , and 2,544 samples for Drepair . In the implementation of PURE, we segment the reason-ing trajectory into steps using " \n\n " as the de-limiter. λ is set to 0.2, and the smoothing term ϵ

is set to 0.01. Our training was conducted over 5 epochs with a learning rate of 1 × 10 −5 and a global batch size of 8. Experiments are executed on 8×NVIDIA H100 GPUs, leveraging DeepSpeed

6.2 Ablation Study 

To rigorously validate the individual contributions of each component within the HEAL framework, we conducted a component-wise ablation study on the Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct model. Table 2 summa-rizes the performance across four benchmarks. 

Impact of Entropy-Guided Synthesis (GEAR). 

We first analyze the synthesis strategy. Replac-ing the entropy-guided breakpoint detection with a Random Breakpoint strategy results in a noticeable performance drop (e.g., from 53.63% to 50.27% on AIME 2024). This validates that reasoning dead-ends are not randomly distributed; utilizing entropy dynamics to pinpoint the exact moment of cognitive dissonance is crucial for effective re-pair. Furthermore, completely removing the local repair mechanism (w/o GEAR), which relies solely on global answer hints, leads to a drastic regres-sion (-11.33% on AIME24). This confirms that for intractable corner cases, simple global guidance is insufficient; fine-grained, step-wise repairs are essential to bridge the reasoning gap. 

Impact of Quality Control (PURE). Removing the filtering protocol (w/o PURE) results in the most severe performance degradation alongside w/o GEAR, plummeting AIME 2024 accuracy to 42.10%. This highlights the detrimental impact of "shortcut learning". Without PURE, the student model overfits to spurious trajectories leaking the answer without valid derivation. The significant gap between HEAL and w/o PURE underscores that the quality of hindsight data is as critical as its quantity. 

Impact of Curriculum Training (PACE). Fi-nally, discarding the three-stage curriculum in favor of monolithic data mixing (w/o PACE) causes a sharp decline in performance (43.27% on AIME24). Despite having access to the same high-quality data, direct mixing fails to stabilize the learning process. This demonstrates that PACE is pivotal: it allows the student to consolidate foundational capabilities before tackling the high-complexity, entropy-repaired trajectories, thereby preventing catastrophic forgetting and ensuring ro-bust knowledge transfer. In summary, the synergy of all three mod-ules—GEAR, PURE, and PACE—is required to achieve state-of-the-art distillation performance.

7 Conclusion 

In this work, we introduced HEAL, a distillation framework designed to overcome the "Teacher Ceil-ing" by recovering valid reasoning trajectories for previously intractable problems. Grounded in the educational theory of the ZPD, HEAL synergizes three key innovations: GEAR, which actively re-pairs broken logic chains at critical reasoning break-points via entropy-guided hints; PURE, which rig-orously filters out spurious shortcuts to ensure peda-gogical quality; and PACE, which organizes knowl-edge transfer through a progressive curriculum. Empirical results across challenging benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness of the HEAL frame-work. Furthermore, HEAL offers a complementary advantage to Reinforcement Learning: by recov-ering valid reasoning trajectories for intractable problems, it provides a promising "cold-start" ini-tialization to further RL training. Limitations 

Despite its effectiveness, HEAL has three primary limitations. First, the GEAR module relies on the availability of high-quality ground-truth answers to construct hindsight hints. This restricts the frame-work’s applicability to tasks with definitive solu-tions (e.g., math, coding) and makes it less suitable for open-ended generation or unlabeled datasets. Second, our approach assumes the target problem lies within the teacher model’s ZPD. For problems that require domain knowledge or reasoning prim-itives completely absent from the teacher’s pre-training distribution, even active intervention may fail to elicit valid reasoning paths. Another po-tential limitation is the additional computational overhead incurred by the step-wise calculation of Perplexity and NLL in PURE module. However, we emphasize that this is strictly an offline cost and does not impose any latency during inference.

B Impact of Truncation Percentage Threshold 

To investigate the impact of the truncation per-centage threshold λ for PURE, we conducted grid search validation on OlympiadBench with Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct. As illustrated in Figure 5, evaluation results across different λ values demon-strate that the model achieves optimal performance when λ = 0 .2. We attribute this to the delicate trade-off between data purity and diversity. When 

λ is too low, the filter is overly permissive, allowing a significant fraction of "shortcut" trajectories that degrade the performance. Conversely, an overly aggressive threshold risks discarding valid high-perplexity steps that represent genuine cognitive breakthroughs, thereby reducing the diversity of reasoning patterns available for distillation.
