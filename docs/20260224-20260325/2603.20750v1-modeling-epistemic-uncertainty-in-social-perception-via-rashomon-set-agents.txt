# Modeling Epistemic Uncertainty in Social Perception via Rashomon Set Agents 

Jinming Yang 1,∗, Xinyu Jiang 2,∗, Xinshan Jiao 1,† and Xinping Zhang 3

Abstract —We present an LLM-driven multi-agent probabilistic modeling framework that demonstrates how differences in students’ subjective social percep-tions arise and evolve in real-world classroom settings, under constraints from an observed social network and limited questionnaire data. When social information is incomplete and the accuracy of perception differs between students, they can form different views of the same group structure from local cues they can access. Repeated peer communication and belief updates can gradually change these views and, over time, lead to stable group-level differences. To avoid assuming aglobal ”god’s-eye view,” we assign each student an individualized subjective graph that shows which social ties they can perceive and how far information is reach-able from their perspective. All judgments and interac-tions are restricted to this subjective graph: agents use retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) to access only local information and then form evaluations of peers’ competence and social standing. We also add structural perturbations related to social-anxiety to represent consistent individual differences in the accuracy of social perception. During peer exchanges, agents share narrative assessments of classmates’ academic perfor-mance and social position with uncertainty tags, and update beliefs probabilistically using LLM-based trust scores. Using the time series of six real exam scores as an exogenous reference, we run multi-step simulations to examine how epistemic uncertainty spreads through local interactions. Experiments show that, without re-lying on global information, the framework reproduces several collective dynamics consistent with real-world educational settings. The code is released at https: //anonymous.4open.science/r/Rashomonomon-0126. 

Index Terms —Multi-agent systems, large language models, epistemic uncertainty, social perception, Rashomon set, cognitive social structures. 

I. Introduction 

In educational psychology and computational social sci-ence, academic achievement is commonly understood as an outcome jointly shaped by individual ability, effort, and contextual factors [1], [2]. Accumulating empirical evi-dence, however, suggests that students’ learning behaviors and academic performance do not develop in isolation; rather, they are embedded in classroom social networks

Peer interactions, informa-tion exchange, and social comparison can influence how individuals assess their own competence and others’ levels, and these shifting perceptions may in turn affect learning-related decisions. As such processes unfold, performance differentiation and structural disparities can gradually emerge at the group level. Explaining how macro-level learning outcomes arise from micro-level social interaction remains a long-standing concern at the intersection of educational psychology and social computing. From a modeling perspective, this problem is inherently multi-actor, network-structured, and temporally evolv-ing. Analyses that rely on single-time-point prediction or purely static descriptions are therefore limited in their ability to capture how student behavior changes within authentic social contexts [5]. A more appropriate view is to treat learning as a dynamic system composed of multiple mutually influencing individuals and to char-acterize its evolution under the constraints imposed by real social structure [6]. Existing approaches to modeling learning behavior and cognitive diffusion in social net-works largely fall into two categories: rule-based multi-agent models and data-driven predictive methods. Tradi-tional rule-based multi-agent models describe interaction mechanisms via hand-crafted behavioral rules [7]. While such designs can be conceptually transparent, the rules are often simplified and struggle to represent the complexity and heterogeneity of cognition and decision-making in realistic educational settings [8]. Moreover, they are not always directly constrained by observed classroom network structure or psychometric measurements [9]. In contrast, data-driven predictive models emphasize learning a static mapping—often requiring large-scale longitudinal labels that are scarce in education—and they do not naturally support sustained multi-step interaction with evolving cognition [10], [11]. Recent large language models have demonstrated strong capacity for generating complex be-haviors and producing narrative explanations [12], [13], which opens a new avenue for decision modeling. Yet using supervised fine-tuning to deploy such models in multi-agent interaction typically demands substantial data and may still have difficulty enforcing constraints from real social structure without inadvertently introducing global information [14]. This leaves an open challenge: how to incorporate the expressive power of language models into multi-agent systems without additional model training, while simultaneously representing social cognition under agents’ limited visibility.

Building on these considerations, this paper introduces a large-language-model-driven multi-agent probabilistic modeling framework that models and simulates how het-erogeneous subjective social cognition propagates and evolves within an authentic classroom social network. The central premise is that, under incomplete and unevenly distributed social information, different individuals can arrive at distinct subjective interpretations of the same group structure. As localized interactions continue, these interpretations are repeatedly revised and, at the collective level, may accumulate into stable and structurally differ-entiated patterns. In contrast to graph modeling approaches that implic-itly assume a global, god’s-eye view, we construct an individualized subjective graph for each student agent to represent the social relations the agent can perceive and the corresponding reachability of information. All judgments and interactions are confined to the local view defined by this subjective graph: an agent can access only reachable evidence via a retrieval-augmented generation mechanism and, on that basis, form evaluations of peers’ academic capability and social standing. To reflect system-atic differences in perceptual accuracy across individuals, we further introduce structural perturbations associated with social anxiety. During interaction, agents exchange narrative assessments annotated with uncertainty and per-form probabilistic belief updates using credibility weights produced by the language model. Embedded in a multi-step interaction environment, these mechanisms enable the proposed framework to simulate the network-level diffusion of subjective cognition and to analyze the group-level evolutionary patterns that emerge over time. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:  

> •

We propose a large-language-model-driven multi-agent probabilistic framework to characterize how subjective cognition propagates and evolves in class-room social networks.  

> •

We model agents’ judgments and interactions under limited social visibility by introducing individualized subjective social graphs together with a constrained information-access mechanism.  

> •

Using multi-exam observations as an exogenous ref-erence in multi-step simulations, we analyze group-level evolutionary dynamics and show how cognitive uncertainty shapes ranking-related judgments over time. 

II. Related Work 

Multi-agent social simulation has long served as a foun-dation for studying collective behavior and cognitive evo-lution. Early work was largely built on rule-driven agent models or classical opinion-dynamics frameworks, where information diffusion over networks is described through pre-specified interaction rules [15], [16]. Such formulations are analytically interpretable, yet they often rely on styl-ized cognitive assumptions and consequently struggle to capture heterogeneous perception in real social environ-ments or to represent uncertainty as something agents can explicitly express [17]–[19]. Subsequent studies introduced probabilistic representations or stochastic processes to account for fluctuations in judgment and the impact of noise. Even so, many of these models operate on abstract network structures and provide limited constraints from concrete social context [20], [21]. As deep learning ma-tured, data-driven approaches were increasingly adopted to model social behavior and group-level outcomes—for instance, using regression or sequence models to predict individual actions or aggregate indicators [22]–[24]. While these methods can deliver strong predictive accuracy, they typically emphasize static input–output mappings and offer only limited support for explaining the mechanisms of cognitive evolution under sustained, multi-step interaction [10], [11]. More recently, the natural language understand-ing and generation capabilities of large language models have accelerated their adoption in multi-agent systems. A growing body of work employs language models as agents’ decision or generation modules to simulate phe-nomena such as dialogue, cooperation, and social bias [12], [13], [25]. These results suggest that language models can produce socially plausible behavior patterns with internal consistency and contextual dependence. At the same time, many existing designs assume that agents operate in a global—or near-global—information environment, leaving perceptual heterogeneity induced by social structure com-paratively underexplored [14]. In parallel, belief propaga-tion and group cognition research has long examined how individual judgments accumulate through interaction and shape collective cognitive structure. Classical formulations typically update beliefs via linear or weighted aggregation rules, and some extensions introduce probabilistic beliefs to represent uncertainty [26]. Within language-model-based multi-agent frameworks, however, belief updating is often embedded implicitly in generation behavior or implemented through heuristics, which makes it difficult to systematically characterize uncertainty as an explicit state and to study how its effects compound over time [27]– [29]. Psychological and affective factors further complicate this picture, as they are widely regarded as influential drivers of information processing and social judgment [30], [31]. Empirical findings indicate that emotional state, anxiety level, and self-perception can substantially alter how individuals receive, interpret, and evaluate informa-tion [32], [33]. Several multi-agent models have begun to incorporate emotion- or psychology-related variables as agent attributes to simulate information distortion and judgment volatility observed in real social settings. In most cases, however, these factors are treated as static perturbations, and the coupling between affective states, perceived social structure, and belief diffusion remains insufficiently explored [21], [34]. A. Subjective Graph 

Students do not share a unified understanding of the classroom social structure [36]. To capture such perceptual heterogeneity, we define for each student agent i an indi-vidualized subjective social graph Gi = ( V, E i, π i). Here, 

Ei ⊆ V × V denotes the set of social ties that student 

i believes to exist or to be reachable, and πi(e) ∈ [0 , 1] 

assigns a subjective reachability (or credibility) weight to an edge e [16]. Importantly, Gi is not intended to recon-struct the true classroom network; instead, it specifies the social structure that the agent can perceive and utilize when forming judgments and engaging in interactions [37]. The construction of the subjective graph is fully con-strained by observable social-relational and psychometric information from the questionnaire. Explicitly reported friendships define the agent’s base visible neighborhood, while subjective judgments about peer relations and social standing provide a prior modulation of edge reachability weights. To represent individual differences in the stability of social perception, we further introduce a noise parameter 

αi mapped from questionnaire scales. This parameter controls the probabilities of missing edges and spurious connections in the subjective graph. A larger αi indicates higher uncertainty when assessing whether a social tie exists; consequently, even within the same classroom envi-ronment, different individuals may form subjective graphs with substantially different structures. Beyond the subjective graph itself, we impose an explicit constraint on information access. We treat all classroom-related structured information as an external knowledge base D. IV. Experiments and Results 

This section systematically evaluates the proposed 

Rashomon Set Agents framework on real-world middle-school classroom data. The experiments are de-signed to address a central question: when agents are restricted to local visibility and engage in noisy social interaction, how does micro-level cognitive uncertainty propagate over time and give rise to stable, observable patterns of collective misperception at the group level? We present the experimental settings, the main results, key ablations, comparisons with external baselines, and an analysis of classroom-level heterogeneity. 

A. Experimental Settings a) Data: We study 12 middle-school classrooms in which all students have complete score records across 6

consecutive examinations, yielding a total of 482 students. Based on the availability of friend information in the ques-tionnaire, we construct two subsets: (i) Full-Temporal 

(n = 482 ), used to define the ground-truth score trajecto-ries and ranking benchmarks; and (ii) Social-Observed 

(n = 419 ), consisting of students who reported at least one friend ID and are therefore eligible for subjective-graph construction and interaction. Students not included in 

Social-Observed are treated as weakly observed bound-ary nodes: their beliefs are updated primarily via the exogenous self-score anchor, while they can still appear as passive targets of others’ beliefs. 

b) Protocol: The system evolves over 6 discrete time steps (epochs), each corresponding to a real examination. At the beginning of each time step, every agent first calibrates its self-belief using its own exam score, after which the system executes R = 2 rounds of social inter-action. In each round, an agent samples up to K = 3 

interaction partners from the visible neighborhood of its subjective social graph. All stochasticity is controlled by fixed random seeds. Fig. 2. Baseline temporal trend: DPAE increases and Spearman ρ

c) Metrics: We report three primary metrics: (1) group-perceived ranking error DPAE = 1 − ρ (where ρ is the Spearman rank correlation; lower is better); (2) Top-

B. Temporal Evolution of Collective Misperception 

Baseline results are averaged over five seeds; all other ablation settings are run under the shared seed set 

{42 , 43 , 44 } to enable paired comparisons with the Base-line. Under the Baseline configuration, we examine how the deviation between the group-perceived ranking and the ground-truth ranking evolves over time across five seeds (seeds = {42 , 43 , 44 , 45 , 46 }). The results reveal a stable and reproducible accumulation of ranking misperception: DPAE increases from 0.066 ± 0.008 at the first exam to 

0.124 ± 0.009 at the sixth exam, while the Spearman cor-relation decreases from 0.934 ±0.008 to 0.876 ±0.009 . This pattern indicates that, even with an exogenous anchor provided by real exam signals at every time step, noise and uncertainty propagated through localized interactions can accumulate into a structured, time-amplifying deviation at the group level, rather than manifesting as transient random fluctuations. As shown in Fig. 2, DPAE increases monotonically with exam index and the Spearman correlation declines in tan-dem, with consistent directionality across random seeds. Fig. 3 further reports bootstrap 95 % confidence intervals computed over the seed dimension. The entire interval shifts upward over time, supporting the interpretation that the observed “accumulated misperception” is not driven by an outlier run, but instead reflects a statistically consistent temporal dynamic under repeated trials. Inspec-tion of single-seed trajectories (e.g., seed=42) confirmed consistency with the population-averaged trends shown in Fig. 2, indicating that the accumulated misperception is a systematic effect rather than an artifact of averaging.

V. Conclusion 

This paper proposed a large-language-model-driven multi-agent probabilistic modeling framework to charac-terize how heterogeneous subjective cognition emerges, propagates, and accumulates in real classroom social net-works. Departing from conventional assumptions of global visibility or shared cognition, we start from a premise that is closer to everyday social reality: cognitive access is intrinsically limited. Each agent is therefore endowed with an individualized subjective social graph, which con-strains information acquisition, social interaction, and be-lief updating throughout the simulation. By integrating retrieval-augmented generation, trust gating, and proba-bilistic belief fusion into a multi-step interaction system, the proposed framework provides a tractable way to simu-late collective cognitive evolution without relying on global information. Experiments on real middle-school classroom data show that, even when each individual receives an exogenous signal in every time step—namely, the observed exam outcome—localized and noisy social exchange can still sustain the diffusion of cognitive uncertainty and produce a time-accumulating structure of collective misperception at the population level. that heterogeneity in subjective visibility and LLM-based trust gating play a critical role in suppressing long-horizon amplification of misjudgment; removing either component noticeably weakens the group’s ability to identify high-performing individuals. Compared with classical opinion-dynamics baselines, our approach achieves more stable long-term ranking performance while preserving cognitive diversity, thereby avoiding the information collapse that can accompany rapid consensus formation. Methodologically, the contribution of this work is not to introduce a stronger predictor, but to demonstrate a modeling paradigm in which a language model acts as a structurally constrained cognitive module embedded within a multi-agent system. In our design, the language model is not treated as an omniscient oracle; instead, it is explicitly restricted to each agent’s subjective view and locally retrievable evidence, making its outputs inter-pretable as approximations of situated human judgment. This perspective opens a principled path for incorporating language models into research on social simulation, cogni-tive diffusion, and collective decision-making. Several limitations remain. First, our experiments focus on classroom-scale social systems, and the scalability of the framework to larger or more complex network struc-tures has not yet been validated. Second, the modeling of psychological variables relies on questionnaire-based mappings and parametric noise; future extensions could incorporate more fine-grained behavioral traces. Finally, the present study emphasizes descriptive and mechanis-tic analysis, leaving intervention- or optimization-oriented applications for future work.

Overall, this work offers a new computational lens for under-standing how cognitive differentiation arises in realistic social environments, and it argues for a more cautious and controllable role for large language models in social computing.
