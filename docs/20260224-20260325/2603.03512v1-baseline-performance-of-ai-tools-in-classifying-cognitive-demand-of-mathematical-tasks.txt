Baseline Performance of AI Tools in Cl assifying Cognitive Demand of Mathematical Tasks 

Danielle S. Fox 1, Brenda L. Robles 2, Elizabeth DiPietro Brovey 2, Chris tian D. Schunn 1, 2

> 1

Learning Research and Development Center, University of Pittsburgh 

> 2

Institute for Learning , University of Pittsburgh Abstract 

Teachers face increasing demands on their time, particularly in adapting high -quality 

mathematics curricula to meet individual student needs while maintaining cognitive rigor. 

This study evaluates whether artificial intelligence tools can accurately classi fy the 

cognitive demand of mathematical tasks , which we consider a foundational ability for 

creat ing or adapt ing tasks that support student learning . We tested eleven AI tools: six 

general -purpose tools (ChatGPT, Claude, DeepSeek, Gemini, Grok, Perplexity) and five 

education -specific tools (Brisk, Coteach AI, Khanmigo, Magic School, School.AI), on their 

ability to categorize mathematics tasks across f our levels of cognitive demand using a 

widely -used, research -based framework for defining cognitive demand . The goal wa s not to 

determine whether prompts can be optimized to achieve better performance, but rather to 

approximate the performance teachers will achieve with a relatively straightforward 

prompt. On average, AI tools accurately classifi ed task cognitive demand in only 63% of the 

cases . Education -specific tools overall were not more accurate than general -purpose 

tools, and no tool exceeded 83% accuracy. Notably, all tools struggled with tasks at the 

extremes of cognitive demand ( Memorization and Doing Mathematics ), exhibiting a 

systematic b ias toward middle -category classifications ( Procedures with /without 

Connections ). The tools often gave plausible -sounding explanations for th eir decisions ,

which are likely to be persuasive to novice teachers. Detailed error analysis of instances in 

which AI tools misclassified the broad level of cognitive demand (high vs. low) revealed that 

the AI tools consistently over -weighted surface textual features rather than analyzing 

underlying cognitive processes. Further, unpacking AI tools ’ ability to reason about the 

factors that mak e a task higher vs. lower cognitive demand showed weaknesses across the 

board . That is, errors were made not by ignoring relevant dimensions of the task , but rather 

by incorrectly reasoning about multiple aspects of the task . These findings have important 

implications for the integration of AI into teacher planning workflows and highlight the need 

for improved  prompt engineering  and tool development for educational applications. 

Keywords: artificial intelligence, mathematics education, cognitive demand, task analysis, 

teacher planning, educational technology 1. Introduction 

## 1.1 Context and Motivation 

Teachers are increasingly stretched thin, managing multiple responsibilities that extend far 

beyond classroom instruction. Even when provided with high -quality curricula, teachers 

must adapt materials to meet individual student needs, accommodate time constraints, 

and maintain pedagogical rigor. 

The cognitive demand of mathematical tasks has been shown to significantly influence 

student learning : Tasks requiring deeper conceptual understanding and mathematical 

reasoning promote more robust learning than those focusing on memorization or 

procedural application without connections to underlying concepts ( Stein & Lane, 1996; 

Stein et al., 1996 ). It is critical that teachers are supported in plan ning lessons that regularly 

involve high -quality tasks, regardless of the adaptations that are made for their students .

However, identifying and modifying tasks to achieve appropriate cognitive demand requires 

substantial pedagogical expertise and time, with time being an increasingly scarce 

resource for in -service teachers. 

Recent advances in artificial intelligence, particularly large language models (LLMs), have 

generated interest in their potential to reduce teacher workload. While existing research 

has primarily focused on student -facing AI applications such as intelligent tutoring systems 

(Hwang & Tu, 2021) and perspectives of pre -service teachers (e.g., Montero -Izquierdo et 

al., 2025 ), there is growing recognition that AI tools might support teachers in the process 

of planning and curriculum adaptation. Indeed, a number of such tools have been created. 

However, we know surprisingly little about whether current AI tools can perform 

fundamental pedagogical tasks such as accurately assessing the cognitive demand of 

instructional materials. 

## 1.2 Literature Review and Research Gaps 

The emerging literature on AI in education reveals several critical gaps. First, most studies 

focus on pre -service teachers experimenting with AI in artificial settings rather than 

experienced teachers integrating AI into authentic planning workflows (Aqazade et al., 

202 5; Kim et al., 2025; Lee & Yeo, 2022; Lee et al., 2025; Pelton & Pelton, 2024 ; Son et al., 

2024 ; Uygun et al., 2025; Wijaya et al., 2025 ). Second, research has overwhelmingly 

focused on ChatGPT (e.g. , Fock et al., 2025 ; Demir & Çimen, 2024 ; Lestari et al., 2025 Lee & 

Zhai, 2024; Küchemann et al., 2023; Pelton & Pelton, 2023; Sapkota & Bondurant, 2024; 

Şimşek , 2025; Tun ç, 2024) , leaving other AI tools largely unstudied despite their incr ease d

use in educational contexts. Third, there is insufficient subject -specific depth in existing 

research (Awang et al., 2025; Hwang & Tu, 2021) , particularly in mathematics , an important 

subject for success in future STEM (Science , Technology, Engineering, Mathematics) 

courses and careers (Kohen et al., 2022; Maass et al., 2019 ).Perhaps most critically, there is no systematic evidence regarding AI tools' ability to 

perform core pedagogical tasks such as evaluating cognitive demand. If AI tools cannot 

accurately assess the cognitive level of existing tasks, their utility for more c omplex 

planning activities like modifying tasks or creating new ones is undermined .

## 1.3 Theoretical Framework: The Task Analysis Guide 

Th e present study employs the Task Analysis Guide (TAG) developed by Stein and Smith 

(1998 ) and is widely used as the basis of mathematics education research and 

mathematics teacher education /professional learning (See Appendix B for complete TAG) .

The TAG emerged from the  QUASAR  project, a five -year study of mathematics reform in 

urban middle schools, through extensive analysis of hundreds of classroom tasks and their 

relationship to student learning outcomes (Silver & Stein, 1996; Stein et al, 1996). A key 

finding from this rese arch demonstrat ed that selecting high -level tasks is necessary for 

student engagement in complex thinking, whereas low -level tasks almost never result in 

high -level engagement . Thus, task selection is a critical starting point for instruction (Stein 

& Lane, 1996). 

The TAG categorizes mathematical tasks into four levels of cognitive demand: 

Low Cognitive Demand :

1. Memorization: Tasks that require reproduction of previously learned facts, rules, 

formulas, or definitions without connections to concepts or meaning 

2. Procedures without Connections: Tasks that focus on producing correct answers 

using procedures without developing conceptual understanding 

High Cognitive Demand: 

3. Procedures with Connections: Tasks that focus students' attention on the use of 

procedures for the purpose of developing deeper understanding of mathematical 

concepts and ideas 

4. Doing Mathematics: Tasks that require complex, non -algorithmic thinking and 

demand self -monitoring of one's own cognitive processes 

The TAG provides specific characteristics for each category, offering a rigorous framework 

for task analysis that has been validated through extensive use in professional 

development and research contexts (Stein & Lane, 1996; Stein et al., 1996 ).

## 1.4 Research Questions 

Th e present study addresses the following research questions :

1. Can current AI tools accurately classify the cognitive demand of mathematics tasks 

using an established framework (the Task Analysis Guide)? 2. How does classification accuracy vary across different AI tools (general -purpose vs. 

education -specific)? 

3. Are certain types of tasks systematically more difficult for AI tools to classify 

accurately? 

4. What patterns of misclassification emerge, and what do they reveal about AI tools' 

ability to evaluate cognitive demand? 

# 2. Methods and Results 

## 2.1 Task Selection and Expert Classification 

2.1. 1 Methods 

We selected twelve mathematics tasks representing the four cognitive demand categories, 

with three exemplar tasks per category. All tasks had been previously analyzed and 

categorized by human experts trained in using the Task Analysis Guide. The tasks are 

representative of various mathematical content areas and grade levels and are regularly 

used in teacher training contexts to challenge teachers' thinking about the cognitive 

demand of tasks .

Tasks were labeled A through L and distributed across cognitive demand categories as 

follows: 

Memorization : Tasks A, E, L

Procedures without Connections : Tasks D, H, J

Procedures with Connections : Tasks B, G, I

Doing Mathematics : Tasks C, F, K

## 2.3 Prompting Procedure 

2.3.1 Methods 

For each of the twelve task classifications, we uploaded both the Task Analysis Guide and a 

single mathematics task in either .docx or .pdf format. We used the same standardized 

prompt across all tools and tasks: 

"Based on the 'TAG.docx' I uploaded, determine whether the math task 

'Task[X].docx' uploaded is high -cognitive demand or low -cognitive demand. Give 

detailed reasoning and output the detailed analysis with dimensions in a table." 

This " out of the box"  approach intentionally avoided any  prompt engineering  techniques, 

establishing a baseline performance benchmark for how these tools function without 

specialized optimization. 

2.3.2 Results: Classification Distribution Patterns 

Analysis of classification choices reveal ed a systematic bias toward middle categories: Total Classifications by Category (13 3 total classifications): 

• Memorization: 15 (11%) 

• Procedures without Connections: 53 (40%) 

• Procedures with Connections: 50 (37%) 

• Doing Mathematics: 15 (11%) 

Despite equal representation of categories in the task set (3 tasks from each cognitive 

demand category ), AI tools overwhelmingly classified tasks as Procedures with or without 

Connections . This suggests a central -tendency bias where AI tools default to middle 

categories when uncertain. 

Individual tool patterns varied: 

• ChatGPT never classified any task as Memorization or Doing Mathematics 

• Gemini and School AI also avoided the highest category ( Doing Mathematics )

• Only DeepSeek showed relatively balanced distribution across all four categories 

A comprehensive table of findings can be found in Appendix D.

## 2.4 Error Analysis 

2.4.1 Methods 

For the present study, we conducted two types of error analys es. First, we identified 

instances where AI tools misclassified the broad level of cognitive demand (labeling a high -

demand task as low -demand, or vice versa). These represent the most serious errors, as 

the tools fundamentally misinterpreted the nature of the math task. Second, for all broad -

level misclassifications, we analyzed the reasoning provided by AI tools. Human experts 

examined each detailed output and deconstructed the AI's reasoning line by line to identify 

language that matched (exactly or seman tically) the characteristics outlined in the Task 

Analysis Guide. For each characteristic from the correct classification category, experts 

coded the AI's reasoning as follows :

• 0: Not addressed - The characteristic was not mentioned or considered 

• 1: Addressed but incorrect - The characteristic was discussed but with faulty 

reasoning 

• 2: Addressed but only partially correct - Some aspects were correct but incomplete 

• 3: Addressed and correct - The characteristic was fully and accurately addressed 

For example, Task E, a Memorization task, asks students “True or false? 4/100 < 4/96.” This 

was misclassified by Coteach as a Procedures with Connections task . In its output, it 

stated “[This task] c annot be solved through memorization or routine algorithms. Not a 

commonly memorized comparison. ”

Experts paired this reasoning with the first characteristic of the correct classification for 

Memorization which states , “ [The task] involves reproducing previously learned facts, rules, formulae, or definitions. ” Experts then cod ed the sentence s in the statement 

regarding this characteristic as incorrect (red) or correct (green ) and gave the statement a 

score between 0 and 3. For Coteach’s statement about Task E, a score of 2 was ascribed 

(Addressed but only partially correct) because this task can be solved through routine 

algorithms, but it is not a commonly memorized comparison. 

This allowed us to systematically assess which cognitive demand characteristics AI tools 

misinterpreted or ignored and the reasoning they used to support their choice . Complete 

examples of AI responses can be found in Appendix E. 

2.4. 2 Results: Broad -Level Misclassifications 

Our error analysis focused on broad -level errors where the AI failed to identify the correct 

overall cognitive demand level. For instance, when experts labeled a task as 'high demand ', 

but the AI classified it as ' low demand '. 

Of the 13 3 total classifications, there were only six (5%) instances of a tool misclassifying 

the broad level of cognitive demand. While this suggests AI tools generally distinguish 

between high and low demand, these errors are significant as they represent fundamen tal 

mis interpretation s of some cognitive demand characteristics .

2.4. 3 Results: Patterns Across All Broad -Level Errors 

Several patterns emerge from the detailed coding analysis: 

1.  Surface feature bias : AI tools consistently over -weighted surface textual features (e.g., 

explicit mention of "algorithm" or "procedure") rather than analyzing the cognitive 

processes required. 

2.  Incomplete characteristic analysis : Even when AI tools addressed relevant TAG 

characteristics, they rarely scored a 3 (fully correct). Most scores ranged from 1.0 to 2.0, 

indicating systematic difficulties in applying the framework correctly. 

3.  Task G as a critical failure point : With three misclassifications representing half of all 

broad -level errors, Task G appears to contain features that particularly confound AI 

reasoning about cognitive demand. These detailed findings underscore that even when AI tools achieve reasonable overall 

accuracy, their reasoning about cognitive demand characteristics remains flawed, with 

significant implications for educational applications.

# 3. Discussion 

## 3.1 Interpretation of Findings 

The 62% average accuracy represents a mixed picture for AI tool capabilities in classifying 

cognitive demand. On the one hand, this performance substantially exceeds chance 

performance and demonstrates that AI tools can engage with aspects of complex 

pedagogical frameworks such as the Task Analysis Guide. On the other hand, 62% 

accuracy falls far short of what would be needed for reliable use in educational planning. 

Even the top -performing AI tools made major errors. 

The wide variation in task -level accuracy (9% to 100%) is quite informative . This variation 

suggests that cognitive demand classification is not uniformly difficult for AI; rather, certain 

task characteristics make classification easier or harder. The near -perfect performance on 

Procedures without Connections tasks and the poor performance on Doing Mathematics 

tasks (especially Task K) point to systematic differences in how AI tools process task features , and a need for more objective , boundary -defining language in the rubric used to 

train the tools .

## 3.2 The Middle -Category Bias 

One of t he most interesting findings is the overwhelming tendency of AI tools to classify 

tasks as Procedures with or without Connections. This "middle bias" appears across nearly 

all tools and suggests several possible explanations :

Frequency in training data: AI models may have encountered more examples of procedural 

tasks in their training data, leading to a prior bias toward these categories. 

Feature ambiguity: The extreme categories may require more nuanced understanding. 

Memorization tasks must be distinguished from simple procedures, while Doing 

Mathematics tasks must demonstrate non -algorithmic complexity. Middle categories may 

have more obvious textual markers. 

Central tendency behavior: When uncertain, AI systems may default to middle options as a 

form of computational hedging, like how human s tend to avoid extreme choices (e.g., 0 or 5 

on a Likert scale) under uncertainty (Douven & Schupbach, 2017; Stevens, 1971 ).

Definitional overlap: The Task Analysis Guide distinguishes Procedures with and without 

Connections based on whether procedures develop conceptual understanding , which is a

subtle distinction that may be difficult to infer from task text alone. 

## 3.3 What Makes Tasks Easy or Hard to Classify? 

The perfect accuracy on Tasks D, H, and J versus poor accuracy on Tasks C and K suggests 

that surface features matter significantly. Tasks D, H, and J contain minimal text (M = 17 

words) , and may contain clear language that distinguishes procedural work without 

conceptual depth . For instance, Task H clearly states, “Use cross products to solve the 

problem,” which explicitly tells students the procedure needed to be successful . Whereas 

tasks C and K (both Doing Mathematics tasks) are wordier ( M = 63 words) and may be 

harder to identify because they require recognizing the absence of well -known algorithmic 

paths rather than the presence of specific features. 

Task K had the lowest accuracy rate at 9%, with only one tool (DeepSeek) correctly 

identifying it as Doing Mathematics while all others classified it as Procedures with 

Connections . The tools' reasoning centered on a few recurring themes: they saw the 

solution path as predictable and flagged the absence of open -ended exploration. However, 

experts classify Task K as Doing Mathematics because it situates mathematics in a real -

world context, requires students to construct and explain their own equation ; ultimately 

demand ing a high level of cognitive engagement. The gap between expert and AI judgement 

here likely reflects the tools' tendency to underweight the explanation and modeling demands of the task and instead focu s on the transparent cost structure described in the 

problem. 

## 3.4 Practical Implications 

These findings have several important implications for educational practice: 

Limited readiness for autonomous use: With 62% accuracy, current AI tools are not yet 

reliable enough to independently classify task cognitive demand without human oversight 

or significant  prompt engineering . Even tools designed for supporting teachers in 

mathematics education (e.g., Coteach) need further guidance. 

Potential for decision support: However, AI tools might serve effectively as decision -

support systems, flagging tasks for human review or providing preliminary classifications 

that teachers verify. 

Need for  prompt engineering:  The baseline " out of the box " approach likely 

underestimates potential performance. Optimized rubrics, prompts, exemplars , and 

iterative refinement could substantially improve accuracy (Schulhoff et al., 2024) .

Research -backed refinements are already underway , test ing whether more structured 

prompts and exemplars can meaningfully improve alignment with expert classifications 

(Fox et al., in prep). 

Tool selection matters: The range from 50% to 83% accuracy suggests that selecting the 

right tool for this task is consequential. 

# 3.5 Future Directions 

This baseline study opens several avenues for future research that would address current 

limitations and extend our understanding of AI tools' capacity to assess cognitive demand. 

Expanding task samples and contexts : While our twelve tasks were carefully selected to 

represent each cognitive demand category and are regularly used in teacher training, they 

represent a limited sample of the full range of mathematical tasks teachers encounter. 

Future research should test AI classification accuracy across a larger, more diverse set of 

tasks spanning different mathematical content areas, grade levels, and task formats. 

Additionally, measuring inter -rater reliability among human experts wou ld help distinguish 

between AI errors and genuinely ambiguous tasks that challenge even experienced 

educators. 

Optimizing AI performance through  prompt engineering : Our deliberate choice to use an 

"out of the box " prompt establishes a baseline but does not represent optimal performance 

potential. Systematic investigation of prompting strategies could substantially improve 

accuracy.  Few -shot learning  approaches that provide AI tools with example classifications 

before asking them to classify new tasks may support AI tools’ calibration to the Task Analysis Guide framework. Similarly,  chain -of -thought prompting  that requires step -by -

step reasoning might help AI tools better distinguish subtle category differences, 

particularly between Procedures with and without Connections . Research comparing 

different prompting approaches would provide practical guidance for educators seeking to 

use these tools effectively. Though learning  prompt engineering  techniques is yet another 

time -consuming task for which teachers may not have time. 

Enabling iterative refinement : Our study captured single -pass classifications without 

allowing AI tools to revise their assessments based on feedback. However, in authentic 

planning contexts, teachers might engage in dialogue with AI tools, providing corrective 

feedback that improves subsequent classifications. Future research should investigate 

whether and how AI tools can learn from corrections within a conversation, and whether 

th is iterative approach narrows the performance gap with human experts. 

Multi -tool ensemble approaches : Given the variation in accuracy across tools (50% to 

83%), combining predictions from multiple AI tools might achieve higher reliability than any 

single tool. Ensemble methods could identify consensus classifications or flag tasks where 

tools disagree for human review. Research on optimal ensemble strategies and decision 

rules would be valuable for practical implementation. 

Contextualizing AI performance with human baselines : Our findings show that AI tools 

achieve approximately 60% accuracy, but we lack systematic comparison data on how 

teachers with varying levels of training perform on these same tasks. Assessing 

classification accuracy among pre -service teachers, early -career teachers, and 

experienced teachers who have received professional development on the Task Analysis 

Guide would contextualize AI performance and help determine whether AI tools might 

serve as useful training aids or decision -support systems. 

Tracking temporal changes : AI models update frequently, and our findings represent 

performance at a specific point in time. Longitudinal studies that periodically reassess the 

same tools on the same tasks would reveal whether classification accuracy improves as 

models evolve, and w hether newer models address the systematic biases we observed 

(e.g.,  central tendency bias,  surface feature over -weighting). 

Investigating reasoning quality beyond accuracy : Our detailed error analysis revealed 

that even when AI tools achieved correct classifications, their reasoning often scored 

between 1.0 and 2.0 on our coding scale, indicating incomplete or partially incorrect 

application of Task Analysis Guide characteri stics. Future research should examine 

whether improving reasoning quality , even when final classifications are correct , matters 

for educational applications, particularly if teachers rely on AI -generated expl anations to 

deepen their own understanding of cognitive demand. 4. Conclusion 

This study provides the first systematic evaluation of the ability of general -purpose and 

specialized AI tools to classify the cognitive demand of mathematics tasks using an 

established pedagogical framework. While AI tools demonstrate above -chance 

performance, their 62% average accuracy and strong bias toward middle categories reveal 

important limitations . The dramatic variation in accuracy across tasks (from 9% to 100% )

suggests that task characteristics significantly influence AI performance, with extreme 

categories ( Memorization and Doing Mathematics ) proving particularly challenging. 

These findings suggest that current AI tools are not yet ready for autonomous deployment 

in assessing cognitive demand but show promise as decision -support systems that could 

augment teacher expertise. The variation across tools indicates that thoughtful t ool 

selection matters, and the baseline performance documented here establishes a 

foundation for future work on optimization strategies such as  prompt engineering .

As AI tools become increasingly integrated into educational contexts, understanding their 

capabilities and limitations for core pedagogical tasks is essential. This research 

contributes to that understanding while highlighting the continued irreplaceable v alue of 

teacher expertise in curriculum adaptation and instructional planning.

Central tendency bias : the tendency for raters to avoid extreme classifications and 

instead gravitate toward middle categories, resulting in an underrepresentation of the 

highest and lowest levels of a scale .

Chain -of -thought prompting : a prompting technique in which the AI tool is explicitly 

instructed to reason through a problem step by step before arriving at a final answer, 

encouraging more deliberate and transparent reasoning rather than jumping directly to a 

conclusion .

Few -shot learning : providing an AI model with a handful of labeled examples within the 

prompt to guide its responses on similar tasks .

Out of the box : the default, unmodified approach of querying AI tools without any 

additional prompt engineering, customized rubric language, or task -specific guidance —

simply presenting the task as -is and accepting the tool's initial classification .

Prompt engineering : the practice of deliberately crafting and refining the inputs given to an 

AI tool, including instructions, context, and examples, in order to guide the model toward 

more accurate or consistent outputs .

Lower -Level Demands 

Memorization Tasks 

• Involves either producing previously 

learned facts, rules, formulae, or 

definitions OR committing facts, rules, 

formulae, or definitions to memory. 

• Cannot be solved using procedures 

because a procedure does not exist or 

because the time frame in which the task is 

being completed is too short to use a 

procedure. 

• Are not ambiguous – such tasks involve 

exact reproduction of previously seen 

material and what is to be reproduced is 

clearly and directly stated. 

• Have no connection to the concepts or 

meaning that underlie the facts, rules, 

formulae, or definitions being learned or 

reproduced. 

Higher -Level Demands 

Procedures with Connections Tasks 

• Focus students’ attention on the use of 

procedures for the purpose of developing 

deeper levels of understanding of 

mathematical concepts and ideas. 

• Suggest pathways to follow (explicitly or 

implicitly) that are broad general procedures 

that have close connections to underlying 

conceptual ideas as opposed to narrow 

algorithms that are opaque with respect to 

underlying concepts. 

• Usually are represented in multiple ways 

(e.g., visual diagrams, manipulatives, 

symbols, problem situations). Making 

connections among multiple 

representations helps to develop meaning. 

• Require some degree of cognitive effort. 

Although general procedures may be 

followed, they cannot be followed 

mindlessly. Students need to engage with 

the conceptual ideas that underlie the 

procedures in order to successfully 

complete the task and develo p

understanding. 

Procedures without Connections Tasks 

• Are algorithmic. Use of the procedure is 

either specifically called for, or its use is 

evident based on prior instruction, 

experience, or placement of the task. 

• Require limited cognitive demand for 

successful completion. There is little 

ambiguity about what needs to be done and 

how to do it. 

• Have no connection to the concepts or 

meaning that underlie the procedure being 

used. 

• Are focused on producing correct answers 

rather than developing mathematical 

understanding. 

• Require no explanations, or explanations that 

focus solely on describing the procedure that 

was used. 

Doing Mathematics Tasks 

• Requires complex and non -algorithmic 

thinking (i.e., there is not a predictable, well -

rehearsed approach or pathway explicitly 

suggested by the task, task instructions, or a 

worked -out example). 

• Requires students to explore and to 

understand the nature of mathematical 

concepts, processes, or relationships. 

• Demands self -monitoring or self -regulation 

of one’s own cognitive processes. 

• Requires students to access relevant 

knowledge and experiences and make 

appropriate use of them in working through 

the task. 

• Requires students to analyze the task and 

actively examine task constraints that may 

limit possible solution strategies and 

solutions. 

• Requires considerable cognitive effort and 

may involve some level of anxiety for the 

student due to the unpredictable nature of
