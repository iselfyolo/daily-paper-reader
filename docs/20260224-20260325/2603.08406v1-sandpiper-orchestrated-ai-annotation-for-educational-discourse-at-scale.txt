Abstract 

Digital educational environments are expanding toward complex AI and human discourse, pro-viding researchers with an abundance of data that offers deep insights into learning and instructional processes. However, traditional qualitative analysis remains a labor-intensive bottleneck, severely limiting the scale at which this research can be conducted. We present Sandpiper, a mixed-initiative system designed to serve as a bridge between high-volume conversational data and human qualita-tive expertise. By tightly coupling interactive researcher dashboards with agentic Large Language Model (LLM) engines, the platform enables scalable analysis without sacrificing methodological rigor. Sandpiper addresses critical barriers to AI adoption in education by implementing context-aware, au-tomated de-identification workflows supported by secure, university-housed infrastructure to ensure data privacy. Furthermore, the system employs schema-constrained orchestration to eliminate LLM hallucinations and enforces strict adherence to qualitative codebooks. An integrated evaluations engine allows for the continuous benchmarking of AI performance against human labels, fostering an iterative approach to model refinement and validation. We propose a user study to evaluate the system’s efficacy in improving research efficiency, inter-rater reliability, and researcher trust in AI-assisted qualitative workflows. 

Keywords: Educational Discourse, Mixed-Initiative Systems, Qualitative Analysis, Large Language Models, Human-AI Partnership. 

# 1 Introduction 

The rapid expansion of digital and online educational environments that use human and artificial intelli-gence (AI) discourses is generating an unprecedented volume of semi-structured discourse data, ranging from synchronous tutoring transcripts and classroom recordings to peer-to-peer collaborative discussions. While these corpora hold immense potential for uncovering the nuanced pedagogical dynamics of learn-ing and instruction, leveraging them at scale remains a formidable challenge. Traditionally, qualitative analysis has relied upon human experts to engage in close reading and line-by-line coding—a process that is not only prohibitively expensive and labor-intensive but also prone to cognitive fatigue and inter-rater drift. Consequently, vast amounts of educational data remain under-analyzed, creating a qualitative bottleneck that stifles the scope of educational research. To address these systemic challenges, we introduce Sandpiper, an interactive, mixed-initiative system engineered for the high-throughput, AI-assisted qualitative analysis of educational discourse. Sandpiper is designed to address the limitations of fully automated black-box approaches by framing the AI as a collaborative partner rather than a replacement for human expertise. The platform empowers researchers to maintain methodological agency through granular prompt management and agentic orchestration, which rigorously enforces qualitative codebook schemas during the inference process. # 2 Related Work 

Our work builds upon several distinct threads of research within Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) and computational social science. As qualitative datasets grow in scale and complexity, the HCI community has increasingly explored mixed-initiative systems to augment human analytical capabilities without replacing human agency [2]. Traditional Qualitative Data Analysis (QDA) software, such as NVivo or MAXQDA [4], provides robust tools for manual coding but struggles to scale to thousands of transcripts without significant manual labor. Conversely, fully automated Natural Language Processing (NLP) approaches often lack the nuance required for complex educational discourse, leading to a disconnect between computational outputs and rigorous qualitative methodologies [5]. Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have introduced new paradigms for quali-tative coding. Tools like Label Studio [6] and specialized frameworks have integrated LLMs to suggest codes or pre-annotate datasets [7, 3]. However, a persistent challenge in these systems is maintaining strict adherence to complex, researcher-defined codebooks. Hallucinations and malformed outputs fre-quently introduce noise, requiring researchers to spend extensive time cleaning AI-generated data rather than analyzing it [1]. Sandpiper addresses these limitations by prioritizing schema-enforced orchestration within a mixed-initiative workflow. Unlike systems that treat the LLM as a black box, Sandpiper introduces an Or-chestrator Loop that formally scores and rejects malformed outputs before they reach the researcher. Furthermore, by integrating an Evaluations Dashboard directly into the annotation pipeline, Sandpiper elevates the process of “prompt engineering” to a rigorous scientific activity, enabling researchers to sys-tematically validate AI performance against human baselines. This approach ensures that the scalability of LLMs is matched by the methodological rigor required for educational research.

3.1 Data Normalization & PII Masking (Addressing DG1) 

Upon data ingestion, the system automatically normalizes raw transcripts into a consistent internal JSON “Session” representation. During this phase, the platform’s automated de-identification system strips or masks Personally Identifiable Information (PII) before the data is ever sent to external LLM providers. Crucially, researchers are provided with a verification interface to review the masked transcripts. This human-in-the-loop step ensures compliance with ethical standards and builds researcher trust in the platform’s handling of sensitive educational data. 

3.2 Prompt Management and Schema Enforcement (Addressing DG2) 

A core philosophy of the platform is treating Prompts as rigorous research instruments. In the Prompt editor, researchers explicitly define their coding schemas alongside natural language instructions (see Figure 3). To address the common LLM pitfalls of hallucinated or malformed tags, the Sandpiper employs a schema-constrained annotation engine (detailed in Figure 2). When background workers execute LLM API requests, they utilize an Orchestrator Loop . Rather than accepting raw outputs blindly, the worker scores the output for strict compliance against the user’s defined JSON schema. If the output is malformed, the orchestrator constructs a feedback message detailing the formatting error and re-prompts the LLM. Furthermore, to ensure the utmost privacy of educational data, all LLM inference operations are routed through Cornell’s secure LiteLM AI Gateway cluster. This architecture enables researcher access to state-of-the-art closed models (e.g., GPT, Gemini) without compromising institutional data sovereignty. This iterative technical mechanism combined with local routing guarantees that the returned data perfectly align with the researcher’s qualitative codebook securely. 

3.3 Annotation Workflows and the Chat View 

Researchers steer the AI by defining Runs—selecting a corpus of sessions, a Prompt Version, and a target LLM model. Within the platform’s Session Explorer, researchers utilize the Chat View and Labeling Panel (Fig-ure 5). 3.4 The Evaluations Dashboard (Addressing DG3) 

The most distinguishing feature of the Sandpiper is its integrated Evaluate engine, which turns AI development into a rigorous qualitative research task. Researchers can group multiple Runs into “Run-Sets” to compare annotations across different models, varying prompt versions, or against human labels. The Evaluations Dashboard (Figure 4) automatically calculates key qualitative metrics, including pair-wise agreement matrices, Cohen’s Kappa, precision, and recall. By benchmarking the schema-constrained LLM against expert human coders, researchers can iteratively refine their codebooks and establish the methodological validity of the AI-assisted analysis. 

# 4 Conclusion and Future Work 

The Sandpiper demonstrates a scalable, rigorous, and interactive approach to deploying LLMs for the qualitative analysis of educational discourse. By framing the system as a mixed-initiative platform equipped with schema-enforced inference and integrated evaluation tools, we empower researchers to scale their analyses without compromising methodological integrity. Future work will expand the human-in-the-loop workflows, enabling researchers to seamlessly inte-grate manual corrections and dynamically chunk transcripts via ”Per-segmentation” models prior to coding.
