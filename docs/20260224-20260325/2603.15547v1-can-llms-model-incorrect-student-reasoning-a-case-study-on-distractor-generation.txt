Abstract. Modeling plausible student misconceptions is critical for AI in education. In this work, we examine how large language models (LLMs) reason about misconceptions when generating multiple-choice distrac-tors, a task that requires modeling incorrect yet plausible answers by co-ordinating solution knowledge, simulating student misconceptions, and evaluating plausibility. We introduce a taxonomy for analyzing the strate-gies used by state-of-the-art LLMs, examining their reasoning procedures and comparing them to established best practices in the learning sci-ences. Our structured analysis reveals a surprising alignment between their processes and best practices: the models typically solve the prob-lem correctly first, then articulate and simulate multiple potential mis-conceptions, and finally select a set of distractors. An analysis of failure modes reveals that errors arise primarily from failures in recovering the correct solution and selecting among response candidates, rather than simulating errors or structuring the process. Consistent with these re-sults, we find that providing the correct solution in the prompt improves alignment with human-authored distractors by 8%, highlighting the crit-ical role of anchoring to the correct solution when generating plausible incorrect student reasoning. Overall, our analysis offers a structured and interpretable lens into LLMs’ ability to model incorrect student reason-ing and produce high-quality distractors. 4

## 1 Introduction 

Student modeling is a foundational problem in AI for education [31,22], under-pinning a wide range of applications such as test design [3,11], diagnosis of mis-conceptions, teacher training [21,25] and development of targeted instructional interventions [20,38]. A central component of student modeling is the ability to represent not only what students know, but also the systematic errors and mis-conceptions that characterize incorrect but plausible reasoning. Modern LLMs are trained not just to output the answer to a query, but to generate intermediate reasoning content that reflects a procedure that eventually produces an answer [48,9]. This makes it possible to analyze how LLMs construct their reasoning: which problem-solving strategies they follow, how these strate-gies unfold across a task, and to what extent the resulting behavior aligns with best-practice cognitive accounts from learning sciences. In this work, we investigate these questions in the context of a task that requires performing reasoning that is incorrect yet plausible : generating incor-rect answer alternatives ( distractors ) for multiple-choice questions (MCQs). Distractor generation is a natural application of student models: if a model can faithfully simulate plausible student reasoning—both correct and incorrect— then distractors can be obtained by sampling from simulated student solutions and selecting common incorrect outcomes. To increase our understanding of how modern LLMs perform this task, we introduce a broadly applicable framework for analyzing and comparing reasoning traces. Building on prior work [30], we combine open coding—a method for qual-itative labeling of salient concepts [7]—with guidance from the learning science literature to construct a taxonomy of strategies used in distractor generation. We apply this taxonomy to 240 distractor-generation traces from DeepSeek-V3.2 [9] and GLM-4.7 [16], allowing us to systematically analyze the strategies they use when generating distractors for a dataset of expert-written MCQs [23]. Fig. 1 illustrates the annotation of a single reasoning trace according to the strategies in our taxonomy. Although there remains substantial room for improvement in performance, our analysis reveals a surprising alignment between how modern LLMs generate distractors and best practices from the distractor-generation literature. Specifi-cally, LLMs’ processes closely mirror recommended misconception-based design practices: they typically begin by solving the problem correctly, articulate po-tential student misconceptions, instantiate them through erroneous reasoning, and select plausible incorrect answers from a broader set of candidates. The cor-rect step-by-step solution serves as an anchor from which the model diverges at specific points to generate misconception-aligned distractors. In contrast, modi-fying surface features of the correct answer without modeling underlying student reasoning (i.e., similarity-based heuristics) is only rarely observed. Beyond characterizing LLMs’ processes, our taxonomy also enables a diagno-sis of where and why distractor generation fails. We find that some parts, such as error simulation and the implicit organization of multi-step reasoning, are gen-erally robust. Instead, failures arise more often in recovering the correct solution and downstream selection of distractors. These findings also suggest concrete av-enues for improving distractor generation such as providing additional guidance at test time. Most notably, revealing the correct answer in the prompt signifi-cantly improves performance by 8%. 5 Overall, our results show that LLMs are able to systematically model misconception-based distractor generation when anchored to a correct solution, deepening our understanding of when and how modern LLMs can model plausible incorrect student reasoning. 

## 2 Student Modeling and Distractor Generation 

Student modeling with LLMs. A growing body of work suggests LLMs as promising tools for simulating student behavior—they can generate student re-sponses that expert annotators judge as reflective of common grade-appropriate misconceptions [29] and condition their behavior on a learner’s cognitive level [49] or misconceptions [27]. At the same time, recent literature suggests that LLMs may encode common student error patterns [34,26], but it remains un-clear whether they can effectively leverage this implicit knowledge. While these

findings suggest that LLMs can simulate incorrect student reasoning to some ex-tent, prior evaluations focus primarily on the outputs rather than the reasoning processes that produce them. As a result, it remains unclear which reasoning strategies LLMs use to generate incorrect answers. 

Multiple-choice questions and distractor generation. MCQs are widely used to assess student understanding [43,6], but their effectiveness depends on the quality of the distractors . Crafting high-quality distractors (see § 3.1) is costly and time consuming [40,37]. Prior work has therefore explored automat-ing this process with LLMs [1, inter alia ], particularly for mathematical MCQs [13,19,24,36]. Some approaches decompose distractor generation into producing candidate mistakes and applying them to generate plausible answers, reflecting misconception-based methods [14,39], while others rely on overgeneration fol-lowed by ranking [42]. Despite notable performance gains, existing approaches depend on human involvement or large task-specific datasets, limiting practi-cal deployment. In contrast, leveraging LLMs’ reasoning offers a complementary path to effective distractor generation without these dependencies. 

## 3 A Taxonomy of Strategies for Distractor Generation 

Our goal is to characterize the strategies LLMs use to generate distractors with-out access to historical student data [44]. We develop a taxonomy (Table 1) by combining learning-science theory with empirical analysis to capture both ex-pected strategies and observed LLM behaviour. We first review expert’s strate-gies (§ 3.1) and then describe how we construct our taxonomy (§ 3.2). 

3.1 Crafting Effective Distractors Criteria for effective distractor sets. When designing MCQs, distractors must be plausible, i.e., likely to be selected by students with flawed or incomplete understanding [18,15]. Empirical studies link plausibility to both semantic relat-edness to the correct answer and grounding in identifiable misconceptions [15,28]. Additionally, functional distractors should exhibit so-called negative discrimina-tion by preferentially attracting lower-performing students over high-performers [45]. Furthermore, high-quality distractors must function well as a set . Options should be mutually exclusive, homogeneous in form, and free of unintended cues such as grammatical inconsistencies, overlapping content, etc [17]. 

Strategies for crafting effective distractors. The literature identifies two main strategies for crafting high-quality distractors in the absence of historical data. (1) Similarity-based distractors modify surface features of the correct answer without modeling underlying student reasoning. For example, if the cor-rect answer is 43 , a distractor could invert the sign to − 43 . While easy to imple-ment, such distractors do not reveal specific student misconceptions [5,46,44]. On LLMs’ Student Modeling Strategies 5

(2) Misconception-based distractors derive from common student errors ap-plied to the correct solution process. For example, if students forget to invert the divisor when dividing fractions, a distractor can be generated by applying this error (e.g., 13 ÷ 12!

= 16 ). This approach aligns with repair theory, which ex-plains systematic errors as local “repairs” students apply when their procedural knowledge is incomplete or inapplicable [4]. These repairs follow predictable pat-terns [47]. While simulating step-level buggy procedures is labor-intensive and requires knowledge of common errors [15], such distractors are preferred because they expose specific procedural gaps and are diagnostically informative [2,8]. 

3.2 Taxonomy Construction 

Next, we develop a taxonomy that integrates the above theoretical expectations with patterns observed in LLM distractor-generation traces. First, we summarize the relevant theoretical principles, then describe how we collect and analyze LLM reasoning traces, and finally explain how these theoretical and empirical insights are reconciled into a unified taxonomy. 

Grounding in literature. Based on the distractor generation workflow de-scribed in the literature reviewed in § 3.1, we expect the following strategies. Both similarity-based and misconception-based approaches require access to the correct solution ( → CORR ). Misconception-based distractor generation can be decomposed into a three-step process: (i) identifying a common error ( →

ERR_DESC ), (ii) simulating this error within an otherwise correct step-by-step solution procedure ( → ERR_SIM ), and (iii) stating the incorrect outcome that results from instantiating the error ( → INST ). Finally, item-writing guidelines on distractor functioning—such as minimum selection thresholds and negative discrimination—can be operationalized at the level of individual distractors via explicit plausibility checks ( → PLAUS ), while enforcing constraints over the full distractor set requires explicit curation or selection mechanisms ( → CURATE ). 

Observing LLMs’ reasoning. To ground our taxonomy in LLMs’ actual rea-soning, we also collect and analyze traces of their distractor-generation processes. Specifically, we use the Eedi Math MCQ Dataset [23], which contains grade-school level math questions with four answer choices (i.e., three distractors) per problem. We exclude questions referencing images and only consider problems for which high-level errors are available, ensuring a misconception-based strat-egy is applicable. We additionally remove all problems that rely on the answer choices, taking the form “Which of the following is correct?” (see § A.1 for further details). These filtering steps yield a dataset with N = 429 problems. We use two models, DeepSeek-V3.2 [9] and GLM-4.7 [16], both of which are reason-ing models. We consider two ways of prompting: (i) chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting [48], where models are prompted to think step-by-step before gen-erating distractors but reasoning is disabled; (ii) reasoning prompting , where models solve the task by generating additional reasoning tokens but output only 6 Y. Grounding in observation. We perform manual open coding [7]—a qualita-tive method for breaking text into discrete concepts without predefined categories— on a curated set of 24 traces from both CoT and reasoning sources, covering both LLMs and varying in distractor generation performance. Half of the traces con-tain at most one matching distractor and half contain two or more, based on Eedi annotations. Because reasoning traces are highly verbose, we scale our anal-ysis with model-assisted open coding. Traces are processed in batches of 10 problems: the model first performs sentence-level coding within each trace and then reports recurring strategies across the batch, using the same stratification as for the manual analysis.

4.2 Analyzing LLMs’ Distractor Generation Process 

We analyze reasoning traces in three ways: frequency of various strategies, tem-poral evolution, and sequential organization. Frequencies show which strategies the LLMs use (Table 3), temporal analysis captures how they unfold (Fig. 2), and transition probabilities reveal how the reasoning is composed of subprocesses (Fig. 3). 

Annotating traces using the taxonomy. To perform these analyses, we first need to identify where and how the strategies in our taxonomy appear in LLM reasoning traces. Accordingly, we annotate all occurrences of each strategy in LLMs’ traces—collected in § 3.2—as depicted in Fig. 1. Because the traces are extremely long (average of ≈6,800 characters for DeepSeek reasoning), we adopt a scalable approach that balances efficiency and annotation quality. Fol-lowing prior work [30], we use an LLM as the primary annotator, 10 but validate manually to ensure reasonable quality. Manual validation is performed on a rep-resentative subset of 32 traces, stratified by model, CoT versus reasoning, and performance (proportional match above vs. below 0.5). It is done by comparing the number of insertions and deletions relative to the human annotations. We report an average precision of 0.97 and recall of 0.95. 11 

Our automatic annotation procedure prompts an LLM with (i) a description of the taxonomy, (ii) a small set of hand-picked general examples for each strat-egy tag in the taxonomy, (iii) the full reasoning trace—corresponding to one LLM’s distractor generation on one math problem—to be annotated, and (iv) an instruction to re-generate the trace while segmenting the text into spans and marking each of the spans with a strategy tag. For example, after the span “Yes, this is a plausible distractor” the model is expected to insert the tag <PLAUS> .

Over-all, misconception-based design is a central component of LLMs’ distractor-generation process, alongside plausibility checking, candidate over-generation, and selection. 

Diagnosing failure modes in LLMs’ distractor generation. ## 5 Conclusion 

In this study, we examined how LLMs model incorrect student reasoning when generating math distractors, developing a taxonomy grounded in learning-science literature and observed model behavior. Although distractor-generation perfor-mance remains imperfect, our analysis shows that LLMs predominantly use a solve-first, error-injection strategy: they first solve the problem correctly, identify potential misconceptions, simulate the resulting errors, and generate correspond-ing distractors. This approach closely mirrors misconception-based methods in the educational literature, where the correct answer serves as a critical anchor.

## C Details on Discussion Experiments 

This section provides a detailed description of the experiments performed as part of the discussion (§ 4.3). 

C.1 Solve-First and Error Injection 

To measure how frequently the LLM first solves a problem correctly and then injects an error into the solution, we annotate the LLMs’ step-by-step traces at scale. Following the approach used in our main analysis, we first define the following taxonomy: 

– SOLVE_FIRST : Does the model systematically solve the problem to reach the correct final answer? 

– INJECT_FROM_SOLUTION : Does the model refer to the correct solution or in-termediate steps to generate plausible errors, misconceptions, or distractors? We limit the analysis to problems where the LLM generated at least two steps in a step-by-step solution, ensuring that error injection is possible. Step-by-step solutions are generated using the prompt in Table 15.

Manual analysis for consistency. To evaluate consistency with any reason-able interpretation of the provided error, we first compare the generated answer against all available distractors for the same question that are sourced from the same error. For cases where the answer does not match any available distrac-tor, we inspect the reasoning trace step-by-step to verify whether the error was correctly implemented. Albeit rarely the case, we consider examples consistent if the error is correctly implemented but is combined with a second error. Most instances are straightforward to evaluate; for example, errors such as “rounds too much” are trivial to classify.

## D Additional Experiments 

D.1 Coverage of strategies. 

Beyond the characterization of LLMs’ distractor generation process in § 4.2, it is also informative to examine not only how frequently each strategy occurs on average within a trace, but also how many traces contain at least one instance of that strategy. 

Results. In Table 19, we observe that across both GLM-4.7 and DeepSeek-V3.2 , reasoning traces show substantially higher coverage of taxonomy strategies than CoT traces, especially for evaluative and iterative behaviors such as PLAUS ,

CURATE , and RECON . Core generative steps (e.g., CORR , ERR_DESC , INST ) are nearly universal across models and prompting styles, suggesting they are structural components of distractor generation. Compared to DeepSeek-V3.2 , GLM-4.7 

exhibits less frequent RECON but relatively stronger ERR_SIM in CoT, indicating minor differences between models. 

D.2 Less capable models. 

Our study focuses on highly capable models that enable the collection of reason-ing traces. Nevertheless, it is useful to examine whether smaller models exhibit similar patterns. We therefore evaluate GPT-oss-20b with deterministic sam-pling. The proportional match in reasoning with the simple prompt (0.30 ±

0.03) is significantly lower than with the literature-informed reasoning structure (0.35 ± 0.03), suggesting that smaller models may adhere less strictly to the misconception-based process than larger models.
