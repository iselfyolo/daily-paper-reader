Abstract 

Grading in large undergraduate STEM courses often yields minimal feedback due to heavy instructional workloads. We present a large-scale empirical study of AI grading on real, handwrit-ten single-variable calculus work from UC Irvine. Using OCR-conditioned large language models with structured, rubric-guided prompting, our system produces scores and formative feedback for thousands of free-response quiz submissions from nearly 800 students. In a setting with no single ground-truth label, we evaluate performance against official teaching-assistant grades, student surveys, and independent human review, finding strong alignment with TA scoring and a large majority of AI-generated feedback rated as correct or acceptable across quizzes. Beyond calculus, this setting highlights core challenges in OCR-conditioned mathematical reasoning and partial-credit assessment. We analyze key failure modes, propose practical rubric-and prompt-design principles, and introduce a multi-perspective evaluation protocol for reliable, real-course deployment. Building on the dataset and evaluation framework developed here, we outline a standardized benchmark for AI grading of handwritten mathematics to support reproducible comparison and future research.

# 1 Introduction and Motivation 

Grading at scale remains one of the most persistent challenges in undergraduate education. In high-enrollment STEM courses such as calculus, teaching assistants (TAs) face thousands of open-response submissions under tight deadlines. The resulting workload often leads to two systemic problems: (1) declining grading quality and consistency, and (2) minimal or no personalized feedback—although meaningful and informative feedback is essential for learning. These issues directly undermine fairness, transparency, and student engagement in foundational mathematics instruction. Since calculus is one of the largest and most resource-intensive gateway courses in STEM education nationwide, scalable grading solutions have potential impact across thousands of sections and tens of thousands of students each year. To address this, we developed and deployed a large-scale AI grading system that combines optical character recognition (OCR) with large language models (LLMs) to assess handwritten solutions and generate rubric-aligned feedback. AI-generated scores were provided for reference only; TA scores remained the official course grades. To our knowledge, this is among the first large-scale real-course studies of LLM-based grading in university mathematics. We deployed the system across three academic terms in a large-enrollment single-variable calculus sequence at UC Irvine, covering 20 quizzes and more than 1,000 students. The sequence consists of two consecutive courses Math 2A and Math 2B. 1. Winter 2025. We tested the system on two quizzes in Math 2B, each containing three free-response questions, in a class of over 500 students. 2. Spring 2025. We applied the system to six quizzes in Math 2A (two questions each, one free-response and one multiple-choice or fill-in-the-blank, approximately 400 students) and six quizzes in Math 2B (two or three free-response questions, approximately 360 students). The Spring 2025 datasets served as the primary basis for system refinement; five quizzes from this term were used for detailed comparison against TA scores, student surveys, and independent human reviewers. 3. Fall 2025. We further validated the system on six additional quizzes in Math 2B, each containing two or three free-response questions, with approximately 200 students. All quantitative evaluations in this paper focus exclusively on free-response questions from 2025 Spring, as multiple-choice and fill-in-the-blank items do not reflect the core challenges of AI-based handwriting interpretation and rubric-guided partial-credit assessment and the production of clear, constructive feedback. We evaluate AI-assisted grading via TA-score alignment, student surveys, and an independent human review (over twenty reviewers). AI-assigned scores show strong agreement with human grading, and the generated feedback is generally judged correct or acceptable by independent reviewers, with remaining errors concentrated in OCR failures and a small set of rubric edge cases. Together, these results suggest that the system can reduce TA workload while providing more detailed feedback to students; See Section 6 for full quantitative results. From a technical standpoint, one crucial aspect of grading handwritten mathematics with LLMs is prompt and rubric design. Because there is no unique ground-truth score for many partially correct solutions, the system must translate free-form handwritten student work into consistent, fine-grained judgments that remain aligned with course rubrics. Beyond coping with noisy OCR and varied handwriting, we therefore develop and iteratively refine a structured prompt framework that combines system-level instructions, problem-specific reference solutions, and machine-readable 2rubrics. This framework, described in Section 4, is crucial to stabilizing scores and generating useful feedback across thousands of heterogeneous submissions. Recent work [ 1 , 2, 3, 4 , 5, 6, 7 ],etc on AI-assisted grading of handwritten STEM solutions has grown rapidly with recent LLM advances, but typically emphasizes score reliability rather than course-scale evaluation of feedback quality. We study course-scale deployment on thousands of authentic handwritten calculus submissions and evaluate both scores and feedback using TA grades, student surveys, and independent human review (20+ reviewers). 

Main Contributions 

Our focus is on an underexplored ML problem: how to reliably evaluate and deploy LLM-based reasoning systems without ground truth, under noisy inputs, partial-credit grading, and high-stakes constraints. 

• Large-scale, real-world deployment. We report a large-scale deployment of an end-to-end OCR+LLM grading system (scores and feedback) on free-form handwritten calculus solutions in a high-enrollment, for-credit university course, using thousands of authentic quiz submissions. 

• Prompt-and-rubric grading pipeline. We develop a practical pipeline that couples OCR-conditioned reasoning with carefully designed system prompts and a multi-rubric strategy (typically a flexible and a fixed rubric). The prompts and rubrics are iteratively refined using failure-case analysis to improve partial-credit scoring and formative feedback. 

• Multi-perspective human-in-the-loop evaluation. We introduce a structured evaluation protocol combining TA score alignment, student feedback, and independent reviews from over twenty external reviewers to assess both score reliability and the accuracy/usefulness of AI-generated feedback, where no ground-truth labels exist. 

• Foundation for a future benchmark. We have assembled a large collection of anonymized handwritten solutions together with OCR outputs, tuned rubrics, AI-generated scores and feedback, and independent human review results. As future work, we plan to release benchmark subsets in two tracks. Track A (clean) will pair scanned work with corrected transcriptions and instructor/TA–approved reference grades, enabling controlled evaluation of OCR and rubric-based grading. Track B (noisy) will retain the original OCR outputs and reviewer labels to evaluate robustness and triage—i.e., when the system should defer to human review under noisy inputs, and how reliably it can flag likely OCR-induced errors. Although this work focuses on single-variable calculus, the challenges it addresses—scalability, fairness, and feedback quality—are shared across many large STEM courses, positioning foundational mathematics as a natural testbed for broader AI-assisted assessment. 

# 2 Overview of the AI Grading Pipeline 

Our pipeline consists of three modular stages (Figure 1): region segmentation, OCR-to-L ATEXtranscription, and rubric-based LLM grading with feedback. We use a standardized answer-sheet format that separates each problem into a solution region and a final-answer region, enabling the pipeline to handle variable numbers of problems by processing each region independently. See Appendix Section A.2. We perform OCR and grading at the region level (rather than on whole-page inputs) to reduce cross-problem interference and hallucination; free-form solution regions remain 3Figure 1: Overview of the proposed AI grading pipeline: The core module converts handwritten solutions to LaTeX and evaluates them against reference solutions and structured rubrics, producing detailed scores and feedback. the most challenging. The resulting scores and feedback are delivered to students by email. See Figure 1. 

1. OCR and Transcription. Handwritten student work is digitized via automated OCR and converted to L ATEX to preserve mathematical structure. Among the tools we evaluated, ChatGPT 4.1 mini paired with well-designed prompts achieved a favorable trade-off among accuracy, scalability, and cost; see Section 3 for details. 

2. Rubric-Guided Prompting. We use a two-level prompt design: a system message that provides global grading principles and output constraints, and problem-specific rubrics encoded as structured prompts specifying scoring and feedback criteria, output format, and exemplar solutions. Prompt templates are iteratively refined on sample submissions to better align LLM outputs with rubric intent (Section 4). 

3. LLM Evaluation. The transcribed solutions and rubric prompts are processed by large language models, primarily GPT-4.1-mini, with supplementary evaluations using o3-mini if needed, which produce both numerical scores and explanatory feedback. Because grading lacks an absolute ground truth, we assess reliability via three complementary validations: alignment with TA scores, student feedback, and independent human reviews (Section 6). 

Model choice and evolving capabilities. Unless otherwise noted, we use GPT-4.1-mini for both OCR and grading, balancing accuracy, latency, and cost for course-scale deployment. We additionally use o3-mini for a small subset of algebra-heavy or otherwise challenging cases where stronger reasoning is needed. See section 5 for comparisons. Rather than chasing each new model release, we keep the pipeline stable and allow the backend model to be swapped as capabilities improve; systematic cross-model comparisons and routing policies are left to future work. 

# 3 OCR Component 

For transcribing handwritten mathematical work, we evaluated several OCR options on our course data in Fall 2024/Winter 2025 and selected Mathpix as the most reliable choice at the time. After the release of GPT-4.1/4.1-mini in April 2025, we re-ran our OCR evaluation and found that, with OCR-specific prompting, GPT-4.1-mini produces higher-quality transcriptions on our data. Across thousands of handwritten submissions, around 88% of transcriptions are acceptable , meaning they are either (i) completely correct (character-level L ATEX fidelity) or (ii) sufficient for rubric-based 

4grading . Unlike Mathpix, which primarily targets literal transcription, GPT-4.1-mini can leverage contextual reasoning to resolve ambiguities, improving robustness to messy and heterogeneous student handwriting. In a head-to-head comparison on a fixed challenging subset (the 171-solution subset ), consisting of 59 quizzes, each containing the same three questions (171 solutions in total), GPT-4.1-mini achieved 84% acceptable transcriptions, compared to 55% for Mathpix. See Appendix Section A.3 for representative examples. OCR performance with GPT-4.1-mini is sensitive to prompt design; we use context-aware constraints to improve transcription stability. Below is an example of our basic OCR prompt. OCR prompts       

> This problem asks the student to estimate a definite integral using a left Riemann sum:
> Z51
> (x2−x−1) dx
> with **four subintervals** and **left endpoints**, without evaluating the exact limit. 1. Use OCR to identify any text in this image and convert it into LaTeX code 2. Do not correct any math or logical error or typos.

To better utilize the contextual reasoning ability of GPT-4.1-mini OCR, it is important to state the problem in the prompts. The other two targeted prompt strategies address two common, prompt-addressable sources of transcription instability. 

• Hallucination. When answer boxes are blank or sparsely written, GPT-4.1-mini may generate full solutions. The above Prompt 1 and pre-printing the words “Solution” and “Final Answer” inside boxes stabilize the output and suppress hallucination (see Appendix Section A.2). 

• Autocorrection. GPT-4.1-mini occasionally corrects students’ mathematical mistakes during transcription. An explicit instruction not to correct errors (Prompt 2) helps reduce this behavior, though rare cases remain (less than 2% of our samples). A representative example is provided in Appendix Section A.5. Two major OCR challenges remain: (1) geometric diagrams, where GPT-4.1-mini shows limited ability and, in our experiments, Mathpix did not reconstruct any diagrams from student work; and (2) reliably handling crossed-out or heavily scribbled text. In the latter case, OCR systems may transcribe erased work or drop nearby valid symbols when the crossed-out region visually merges with adjacent ink. We experimented with several prompt refinements to suppress erased work, but this often also removed nearby valid content. Beyond these prompt-addressable issues, we analyzed all 27 problematic OCR cases within the 

171-solution subset . Most GPT-4.1-mini failures are layout-related, with the dominant failure mode involving complex division expressions. In particular, when nested fractions are written with insufficient spacing, the scope of the main fraction bar becomes ambiguous, causing terms to be merged, misparsed, or omitted. Other error types are uncommon. See Appendix Section A.4 for an illustrative example of this fraction-related failure mode. 

# 4 Grading and Structured Prompt Engineering 

With OCR reliability largely established, a central challenge in AI grading lies in prompt de-sign—specifically, constructing structured prompts that ensure consistency, fairness, and rubric 5alignment at scale. Our experiments show that careful system-message engineering and rubric formulation are decisive factors in achieving human-level grading accuracy. 

4.1 System Message Design 

Prompts in our grading system consist of two parts: a system message that defines grading behavior and a rubric section specifying problem-level criteria. We observed that API-based grading can be less thorough than grading performed in the interactive web interface. While the exact cause is not externally documented, this motivated us to make all grading instructions explicit in the system message so that API-based deployment achieves consistent, high-quality outputs. To reduce stochasticity, we set temperature to near-zero values (0.0–0.1) for GPT-4.1-mini to improve response stability and reproducibility. For o3-mini, temperature is not an exposed API parameter and therefore cannot be adjusted. The system message was iteratively refined to emphasize logical consistency, factual correctness, and human-style feedback. Below is an excerpt: System message excerpt 

> You are an expert math grader. When evaluating, please: 1. Distinguish between mathematically correct statements and incorrect reasoning. 2. Avoid contradictions in feedback. 3. Do not penalize false starts if later corrected. 4. Provide concise, constructive explanations for point deductions. 5. Write feedback in clear, natural language suitable for students.

These structured instructions help reduce logical inconsistency and excessive penalization of harmless early mistakes—common failure modes under naive prompting. Representative examples are provided in Appendix Section A.6. 

4.2 Rubric and Prompt Framework 

Our rubric framework, developed from thousands of Math 2A and B submissions, is designed to balance interpretability and grading precision. Each problem may be associated with multiple rubrics to accommodate different valid solution strategies. For most questions, we adopt a dual-rubric design consisting of: 1. Concise reference solutions , summarizing one or more canonical correct approaches. 2. Two complementary rubrics :

• Flexible rubric — human-like and tolerant of diverse reasoning styles; supports free-form evaluation to credit partially correct work and uncommon but valid approaches. 

• Fixed rubric — fine-grained and point-based, with problem-specific checks targeting required steps and common errors in standard solutions. See Appendix A.7 for a representative example comparing the two rubrics and their JSON outputs, illustrating how the flexible rubric complements a checklist-style rubric on non-canonical but correct solutions. Importantly, flexibility does not systematically inflate scores: 6in our experiments, when locally correct steps are globally inconsistent or numerically incorrect, the flexible rubric may assign lower scores than the fixed rubric. 3. Selection rule. To reduce model confusion, each submission is evaluated using both rubrics via independent API calls, and we adopt a max-rule : the higher of the two rubric scores is selected as the final grade, together with the corresponding feedback. We evaluate this design on Math 2A and B quizzes 5–6, which contain more challenging problems and admit a wider range of valid solution paths and partial attempts. To assess alignment with human grading behavior, we compare grades produced by the fixed rubric alone, the flexible rubric alone, and the max-rule against TA scores (used as a reference rather than an absolute ground truth). Using mean absolute error (MAE) as the primary metric, the max-rule consistently achieves the lowest MAE across both courses and quizzes (Fig. 2). These results suggest that the max-rule yields more stable and human-aligned scores by crediting valid reasoning that may be under-recognized by a single rubric. 

Figure 2: Bars correspond to the flexible rubric, fixed rubric and max-rule, respectively (left to right within each group): A-Math 2A, B-Math 2B 

Exemplar-guided rubric generation. We use examples to help the model write new grading rubrics in a consistent format. Using primarily Spring 2025 Math 2A and B quizzes, we built a small library of representative problems by problem type (e.g., definite integrals) and refined a high-quality rubric for each. For a new problem in the same or a closely related category in a later term (e.g., Fall 2025), we provide the problem statement, a reference solution, and the closest existing rubric to a large language model, and ask it to produce a rubric at the same level of detail. The resulting rubric typically needs only light human editing (e.g., adding a newly observed misconception or a course-specific convention) before deployment. Future work will organize these rubrics into a lightweight database and automate more of this rubric-writing step. 

Two sources of potentially unjustified point deductions. We observed two recurring cases in which an LLM grader may deduct points even when the underlying mathematics is correct. First, the model may penalize disorganized or nonstandard presentation, despite correctly recognizing a valid solution. Second, our answer sheet separates the work area from the final-answer box: contextual reasoning improves OCR accuracy in the solution area, but the final-answer box provides 7little surrounding context and is therefore more prone to OCR errors. Consequently, the final answer may be misread (making a correct response appear incorrect) even when the solution transcription is accurate and the mathematics is valid. To mitigate these issues, we plan to incorporate a human-review fallback in future deployments when correct answers are clearly supported but full credit is not assigned. In the reported experiments, we already include rubric guidance that discourages style-based penalties and tolerates minor OCR-induced discrepancies in the final-answer box when the correct value is unambiguously supported by the solution steps. Representative rubric guidance used in our experiments is provided in Appendix Section A.8. 

# 5 o3-mini vs. GPT-4.1-mini Grading Variance and Targeted Ac-curacy Evaluation 

We compare o3-mini and GPT-4.1-mini on grading accuracy and score variance using the algebra-heavy 171-solution set . To isolate grader effects, we fix OCR to 4.1-mini and vary only the grading model (GPT-4.1-mini vs. o3-mini). As o3-mini emphasizes multi-step reasoning, we expect it to perform better on algebraically involved items; two independent reviewers found feedback similarly accurate overall but preferred o3-mini when differences arose. However, o3-mini has higher variance and cost, so we reserve it for the most challenging items and apply simple multi-run stabilization for reliability. See Appendix A.10 for details. 

Variance reduction. For each item, we run o3-mini three times, take the mean score, and output the run closest to this mean (with its feedback). Inspired by self-consistency in LLM reasoning as a stability signal [ 8], we avoid majority voting—which typically requires many independent runs—and instead use this closest-to-the-mean rule for continuous partial-credit grading. Two independent human reviewers preferred the selected run in majority cases for feedback accuracy. See Appendix Section A.10.3 for a comparison with majority vote in fluctuating cases. 

Human review. If exactly one of the three runs assigns full credit while the other two do not, we flag the item for human review. 

# 6 Evaluation Against Human Grading and Feedback 

We evaluate AI-assisted grading for single-variable calculus in two courses (A and B), covering nearly 800 students, and assess both score alignment and feedback quality. A central challenge is the absence of a ground-truth benchmark: there is no publicly available expert-graded dataset for calculus solutions, and human graders themselves show nontrivial disagreement, especially on borderline or unconventional answers (Appendix A.10.4). This variability makes it difficult to define a universally accepted “correct” score, and it makes feedback evaluation inherently subjective. Given this inherent ambiguity, we adopt a pragmatic, multi-perspective evaluation framework consisting of three complementary components: 

• Evaluating alignment with TA scores. We compare AI-assigned scores with those assigned by teaching assistants (TAs). While TA grading can be inconsistent and often lacks detailed personalized feedback, it is the practical reference in university assessments, so alignment with TA practices is a necessary condition for deployment. Because the TA Gradescope rubric is designed for efficient human scoring, our AI rubric is adapted for automated interpretation and feedback; thus, we interpret score comparisons as measuring outcome alignment rather than strict rubric equivalence. 8• Direct student feedback. We return AI-generated scores and feedback to students (see Appendix A.11 for an example message) and collect survey responses from math 2A students on perceived correctness and usefulness. This helps identify potential misgrades and characterize how students interpret AI feedback, while recognizing that students may perceive the AI as overly harsh when its scores are lower than the TA-assigned scores. 

• Independent review by external evaluators. A team of trained graduate students, undergraduate students, and advanced high school students independently evaluates the AI outputs using the same rubrics applied by the AI, assessing both correctness and fairness and assigning their own scores. This provides an additional, partially decoupled, third-party perspective beyond TA alignment and student feedback. Due to practical, technical, and administrative constraints (e.g., segmentation failures, scan quality issues, unmatched test codes, and reviewer availability), not all quizzes could be included in every comparison; these exclusions were determined prior to evaluation and are independent of grading outcomes (i.e., non-selective). See Appendix SectionA.1 for a categorical breakdown. Also, we evaluate agreement across all free-response submissions without stratifying by solution correctness, since OCR reliability and rubric-based reasoning must function even when the underlying mathematics is correct; grading alignment is therefore nontrivial in both correct and incorrect cases.

# 7 Limitations 

Our study has several limitations that point to clear next steps. 

Intervention policy. A key bottleneck for large-scale deployment is deciding when to trust automated grading versus defer to additional checks or human review. While OCR errors and rubric–prompt interactions remain major sources of failure, we do not yet have a systematic method for deciding when to trigger such checks or defer to human review. In our experiments, we flag only a narrow set of issues (e.g., a correct final answer receiving less than full credit, or high variance across repeated runs), and these triggers are simple, manually designed heuristics rather than a unified decision rule. Related ML work on learned deferral, uncertainty-aware risk-averse deployment, and fair abstention offers principled perspectives for triggering human review (e.g., [ 9 , 10 , 11 ]), but adapting these frameworks to OCR-conditioned, partial-credit grading without ground truth remains open. 

High-stakes deployment. Our experiments focus on low-stakes quizzes ( ≈10% of the course grade); extending AI-assisted grading to midterms and finals would raise the cost of errors and require substantially more conservative human-in-the-loop controls. 

Choosing the most accurate grade/feedback. When multiple OCR engines or graders are available (e.g., GPT-based OCR vs. Mathpix, or different LLM graders), we currently rely on human reviewers to resolve disagreements and decide which output is more accurate. This does not scale and also slows our ability to evaluate or swap in new models. Developing a reliable automatic mechanism to detect disagreements and select the most reliable score+feedback is a future project. 12 Rubric aggregation (max-rule). We use the max-rule to aggregate rubric-level evidence, and it aligns best with TA grades in our experiments. However, we have not exhaustively compared it against other reasonable choices (e.g., averaging or other monotone score transforms), so conclusions about the optimal aggregation rule are specific to our setting. 

Scope. Our evaluation focuses on single-variable calculus at one institution; additional deployments across courses and institutions are needed to establish generalizability and to understand operational impact under different grading cultures and student populations. 

# 8 Conclusion 

We presented a large-scale evaluation of AI-assisted grading for single-variable calculus at UC Irvine, covering thousands of quiz solutions from nearly 800 students. Our system integrates OCR, prompt-guided reasoning, and rubric-based scoring in an automated workflow, and was evaluated through comparisons with TA grades, structured human review, and student feedback. Our results suggest that current AI systems can already serve as reliable grading assistants: they handle most routine problems accurately, provide usable feedback, and substantially reduce manual grading effort. Key challenges remain, including OCR failures on diagram- or geometry-heavy work and cancellations, consistent partial-credit assignment across diverse solution variants, and principled policies for triggering human review in low-confidence cases. Looking ahead, we plan to release well-documented benchmark subsets from our collected database to support reproducible evaluation of OCR and rubric-based grading. Specifically, we will provide two tracks: Track A (clean) with corrected transcriptions and instructor/TA–approved reference grades, and Track B (noisy) retaining original OCR outputs and reviewer labels to assess robustness and inform triage (i.e., when to defer to human review). We hope these resources will enable more transparent, scalable comparisons and accelerate progress toward reliable automated assessment in large-enrollment STEM courses.

A.1 Dataset Coverage Across Validation Protocol 

Due to practical technical and administrative constraints, not all submitted quizzes could be included in every validation protocol. We distinguish (i) universal exclusions that apply to any downstream analysis, and (ii) additional constraints specific to particular protocols (e.g., AI–TA score comparison or independent human review). Exclusions were driven by processing or logistical feasibility rather than by grading outcomes, and are intended to be non-selective with respect to score disagreement. 

Universal exclusions (apply to all protocols). First, some quizzes were not successfully processed by our pipeline (most commonly due to segmentation failures when students wrote outside designated boxes, scan-quality issues, or occasional OCR/input-extraction failures). In addition, as part of model iteration and coordination with course staff, the first quiz in both Math 2A and Math 2B was excluded from our evaluation sets. Finally, one Math 2B TA section exhibited systematic scanning artifacts (e.g., extraneous lines on scanned pages) that materially degraded segmentation and input extraction; we therefore excluded that section from all analyses. 

Additional constraints for AI–TA score comparison (record linkage). The AI–TA com-parison further requires linking each processed submission to a Gradescope record via the assigned de-identification test code. This linkage sometimes fails because a subset of students omitted or mistyped the code, and in some cases TA score exports from Gradescope were incomplete or missing for reasons outside our control, preventing a reliable per-question score comparison. 

Additional constraints for independent human review (audit logistics). The independent review was organized at the TA-section level: each reviewer reviewed quizzes from a single TA section, and not all TA sections could be covered; for example, when a TA taught multiple discussion sections, we sometimes reviewed only a subset of those sections due to limited reviewer capacity. A small fraction of assigned audits were not completed because some reviewers did not finish them.

Relation to majority-vote self-consistency [ 8 ]. Our multi-run stabilization selects the run whose score is closest to the mean across three runs, rather than the most frequent score as in majority-vote self-consistency. To examine the practical difference, we analyzed the 45 cases in which the three o3-mini runs produced non-identical scores. In the majority of these cases, two runs shared the same score, making majority voting and closest-to-mean selection equivalent. Only five cases exhibited three distinct scores; among these, outcomes were mixed across reviewers, with no consistent preference for either rule. This suggests that, at the course scale and with three runs, closest-to-mean selection behaves similarly to majority voting in most cases, while differing only in a small number of genuinely ambiguous instances. We restrict to three runs to balance stability and cost in course-scale deployment, where majority voting over many samples would be impractical. In the rare cases where all three runs disagree, the discrepancy reflects intrinsic grading ambiguity and such cases are treated conservatively (e.g., flagged for downstream review).As a next step, we will quantify how often three-way disagreements occur at scale; if they remain rare, a conservative 28 policy is to defer such cases to human review, whereas more frequent occurrences would motivate additional stabilization strategies that jointly consider both scores and feedback, rather than score aggregation alone.
