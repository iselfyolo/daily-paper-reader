# Not All Invariants Are Equal: Curating Training Data to Accelerate Program Verification with SLMs 

Ido Pinto 1 Yizhak Yisrael Elboher 1 Haoze Wu 2 Nina Narodytska 3 Guy Katz 1

## Abstract 

The synthesis of inductive loop invariants is a critical bottleneck in automated program verifi-cation. While Large Language Models (LLMs) show promise in mitigating this issue, they often fail on hard instances, generating invariants that are invalid or computationally ineffective. While fine-tuning is a natural route to mitigate this limi-tation, obtaining high-quality training data for in-variant generation remains an open challenge. We present a rigorous data curation pipeline designed to extract high-quality training signals from raw verifier-generated invariants. First, we formalize the properties required for a high-quality train-ing invariant. Second, we propose W ONDA , a pipeline that refines noisy data via AST-based normalization, followed by LLM-driven semantic rewriting and augmentation with provable qual-ity guarantees. We demonstrate that fine-tuning Small Language Models (SLMs) on this curated dataset result in consistent and significant perfor-mance gain. In particular, a fine-tuned 4B pa-rameter model matches the utility of a GPT-OSS-120B baseline and approaches the state-of-the-art GPT-5.2, without incurring reasoning-time over-head. On challenging instances from the recent InvBench evaluation suite, our approach doubles the invariant correctness and speedup rates of base models; and improves their Virtual Best Perfor-mance (VBP) rates on the verification task by up to 14.2%.

## 1. Introduction 

Automated program verification is a powerful technique for ensuring the reliability of critical software infrastructure. At the core of deductive verification lies the challenge of loop invariant synthesis, the identification of a logical property that holds before the loop begins and after each of its itera-tions, sufficient to prove a program’s correctness. Despite decades of research into symbolic methods like Craig inter-polation (McMillan, 2003) and constraint solving (Col ´on et al., 2003; Gupta & Rybalchenko, 2009), finding inductive invariants remains a challenging problem, and a practical bottleneck for modern verifiers. The advent of Large Language Models (LLMs) has intro-duced a new paradigm: generate-and-verify . Models such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) and Claude (Anthropic, 2024) have demonstrated a latent ability to hypothesize loop invariants (Pei et al., 2023). Recent tools such as Loopy (Kamath et al., 2024), Lemur (Wu et al., 2024b), and ACInv (Liu et al., 2025) leverage this capability, wrapping LLMs in iterative refinement strategies that filter hallucinations using SMT solvers. However, despite this progress, a significant gap re-mains. As observed in the recent InvBench evaluation (Wei et al., 2025), while LLM-based verifiers represent a promis-ing direction, they do not yet offer a significant advantage over state-of-the-art symbolic tools, such as UAutomizer, which do not leverage LLMs. Given these limitations, the natural question arises: can we 

train an LLM that specializes in the task of invariant gener-ation? Such specialization is standard practice in many do-mains and has proven highly successful. Indeed, within the program verification literature, this specific problem has re-ceived increasing attention (Pei et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2025). However, somewhat surprisingly, the success of these train-ing efforts has only been partial. Recent work on fine-tuning invariant generation models reports improvements on easy verification problems, yet achieving significant speedups on hard benchmarks remains elusive. We posit that the primary limitation of current neural invari-ant synthesizers is data quality rather than model scale. In particular, a 4B-parameter small language model (SLM) fine-tuned on W ONDA -curated data is able to achieve verification performance gains comparable to strong models such as GPT-OSS-120B and GPT-5.2.

## 2. Related Work 

Traditional Invariant Synthesis. Invariant synthesis has been extensively studied through various formalisms. Ab-stract Interpretation methods (Cousot & Cousot, 1977) gave rise to various for techniques for discovering specific classes of invariants, such as affine relationships (Karr, 1976) and linear restraints (Cousot & Halbwachs, 1978) among vari-ables. Model Checking techniques (Clarke et al., 2018) and Predicate Abstraction (Flanagan & Qadeer, 2002; Lahiri & Bryant, 2007) were also used to infer invariants, by refining abstract states based on a set of predicates. Craig Interpola-tion (McMillan, 2003) is also widely used to generate loop invariants from proofs of unsatisfiability in bounded model checking traces. Tools like UAutomizer (Heizmann et al., 2013) and Eldarica (Hojjat & R ¨ummer, 2018) build on these approaches. Another approach is to cast invariant generation as a constraint-satisfaction problem, and apply off-the-shelf solvers (Col ´on et al., 2003; Gupta & Rybalchenko, 2009; Fedyukovich & Bod ´ık, 2018). Finally, dynamic analysis tools like Daikon (Ernst et al., 2007) infer likely invariants by observing program execution traces. While efficient, dynamic methods are unsound, as they can only guarantee correctness for the observed executions. 

Learning-based Invariant Synthesis. Early techniques for data-driven invariant synthesis relied on decision trees (Garg et al., 2016), algebraic inference (Sharma et al., 2013), and Horn-ICE learning (Ezudheen et al., 2018). With the rise of LLMs, more recent frameworks, such as Loopy (Kamath et al., 2024) and LaM4Inv (Wu et al., 2024a), began employ-ing iterative “generate-and-verify” loops, using symbolic solvers to filter or refine LLM proposals. LEMUR (Wu et al., 2024b) introduced backtracking algorithms to actively repair invalid invariants, while iRank (Chakraborty et al., 2023) leverages contrastive ranking to prioritize high-quality candidates and reduce verifier overhead. Other work extends these capabilities to specific domains, such as C++ class invariants in ClassInvGen (Sun et al., 2025) and complex loop structures in ACInv (Liu et al., 2025). (Wei et al., 2025) demonstrated the potential of LLMs for invariant gen-eration, by fine-tuning LLMs on invariants generated by the symbolic solver UAutomizer. Here, we take a related approach, but seek to improve and diversify the data used for fine-tuning, to allow the LLM to significantly outdo the symbolic engine used for generating it originally. 

## 3. Preliminaries 

We ground our approach in Hoare logic (Hoare, 1969), a seminal framework for reasoning about program correctness. A Hoare triple {p} S {q} asserts that if precondition p holds before executing statement S, then postcondition q holds afterward. While sequential statements are straightforward to compose, loops pose a fundamental challenge: they may execute for an unbounded number of iterations, requiring an auxiliary predicate, a loop invariant , to enable finite reasoning. For a loop while B do S with precondition p and post-condition q, a valid invariant I must satisfy: (i) Initia-tion: p ⇒ I, meaning the invariant holds upon loop en-try; (ii) Consecution: {I ∧ B} S {I}, meaning the invari-ant is preserved by each iteration; and (iii) Sufficiency: 

I ∧ ¬ B ⇒ q, meaning that upon termination, the invari-ant implies the postcondition. A predicate satisfying both initiation and consecution is termed an inductive invari-ant . Throughout this paper, we refer to this property as 

correctness and use the terms interchangeably; if the predi-cate additionally satisfies sufficiency, it constitutes a formal proof of the postcondition q.3Not All Invariants Are Equal

3.1. Program Verification 

Let P denote a program, let Loc( P ) denote its set of pro-gram locations, and let L(P ) ⊆ Loc( P ) denote its loop entry locations. 

Definition 3.1 (Property) . A property is a pair (l, φ ), where 

l ∈ Loc( P ) and φ is a predicate over the variables of pro-gram P . For a given execution of P , a property holds , or is 

satisfied , if φ holds , when P reaches line l.

Definition 3.2 (Verification Query) . A verification query 

is a triple ⟨A, P, q ⟩ where P is a program, q is a property (the postcondition), and A is a set of properties representing preconditions. 

Definition 3.3 (Verification Query Validity) . A verification query ⟨A, P, q ⟩ is valid if every execution of P that sat-isfies the (precondition) properties in A also satisfies the postcondition q.

Definition 3.4 (Verification Oracle) . A verification oracle 

V takes a verification query ⟨A, P, r ⟩ and returns: 

V(A, P, r ) ∈ { TRUE , FALSE , UNKNOWN }

where T RUE indicates the query is valid, F ALSE indicates a counterexample exists, and U NKNOWN indicates the or-acle could not determine the result (e.g., due to timeout or inherent incompleteness). Here, we consider only sound 

oracles: if V(A, P, r ) = TRUE , then the query is valid; if 

V(A, P, r ) = FALSE , then a counterexample exists. Verification queries use assume( φ) and assert( φ)

statements. assume( φ) at line l restricts traces to those satisfying φ (equivalent to if (¬φ) halt ), while 

assert( φ) jumps to ERROR if violated (equivalent to if (¬φ) goto ERROR ). For query ⟨A, P, q ⟩, we annotate 

P with assume( φ) for each (l, φ ) ∈ A and assert( φ)

where q = ( l, φ ). P is safe with respect to the verification query if the and only if ERROR is unreachable (see the gray block in Figure 1). 

3.2. Program Verification using Invariants 

Given a verification query ⟨A, P, q ⟩, the task can be addressed through a direct invocation of the verifier: 

V(A, P, q ). Alternatively, we can break the problem into sub-problems, by introducing a candidate invariant prop-erty I = ( l, φ ), where l ∈ L (P ). This approach splits the verification task into two distinct queries: 1. Correctness Check (V1): Verify that the property I is correct (inductive) within the program P given A:

V1 := V(A, P, I )

2. Sufficiency Check (V2): Verify that the target postcondi-tion q holds, assuming the correctness of the candidate invariant I:

V2 := V(A ∪ { I}, P, q )

The final outcome is determined by Table 1. This proce-dure is sound; conclusive outcomes (T RUE or F ALSE ) are guaranteed to be correct (Wei et al., 2025). Compared to direct verification, this approach aims to augment the prob-lem with new knowledge that can be useful to prove the desired property. The correctness and validity checks are independent and can therefore be performed in parallel, and in the case where both queries are simpler than the original query, the wall-clock verification time is reduced.

Generating Invariants. The usefulness of the aforemen-tioned techniques is conditional upon our ability to generate useful invariant candidates — i.e., such that will allow us to reach a T RUE /F ALSE outcome, as per Table 1. We define the invariant generation task, which is the primary focus of this work, as follows: given a verification query ⟨A, P, q ⟩,a verification oracle V, and a designated loop entry loca-tion l ∈ L (P ), the objective is to synthesize a predicate

## 4. Methodology 

The main component of the “generate-and-verify” loop paradigm is the LLM that performs invariant synthesis. Ide-ally, we want it to produce correct and sufficient invariants that speed up the verification process. In practice, off-the-shelf LLMs are not very effective at this task and need to be fine-tuned for the invariant generation domain. Some-what counterintuitively, the state-of-the-art approach to fine-tuning LLMs for the invariant generation task using data produced by modern verification tools does not show a per-formance boost in challenging verification problems (Wei et al., 2025). In this work, we claim that fine-tuning can in fact be quite useful, provided we have “good”, high-quality 

data. To this end, we investigate two research questions: (a) how to define “good” training in this context; and (b) how to design a procedure for producing such training data. Given a verification query ⟨A, P, q ⟩, a baseline verifier V

and loop locations L(P ), our goal is to produce training samples that pair programs with high-quality loop invariants. We claim that a good invariant I = ( l, φ ) should satisfy the following characteristics: 1. Non-Degeneracy: we exclude trivial invariants φ ∈{FALSE , TRUE }.2. Correctness: invariant φ holds at location l on all exe-cutions, i.e., V(A, P, I ) = TRUE .3. Usefulness: using I expedites verification times. This entails sufficiency, that is, V(A ∪ { I}, P, q ) = TRUE ;and also that tv < t b, where tb is the time it takes the verifier to dispatch the original query V(A, P, q ),and tv is the time it takes to dispatch the correctness and sufficiency queries, in parallel; i.e., the maximum between the solving times of V(A, P, I ) and V(A ∪

I, P, q ).4. Compactness: invariant I has a succinct syntactic form, which we hypothesize facilitates learning and improves generalization. 

Grounding to Verifier-Generated Invariants. Following Wei et al. (2025), we initialize our curation process using invariants extracted from UAutomizer. When UAutomizer proves a verification query ⟨{ p}, P, q ⟩, it emits discovered invariants {(l, φ raw )} as part of its proof. These invariants satisfy correctness by construction. However, they are not guaranteed to be useful (they may not provide speedup) or 

compact (they often contain tool-specific artifacts). Figure 4 (Appendix A) depicts an example: while correct, the raw invariant is cluttered with type casts and verbose structure, making it a poor training target. We address these issues with a two-stage pipeline: 1. Invariant Normalization (§4.1): AST-based rewriting that removes tautologies, contradictions, minimizes parentheses and strips redundant type casts. 2. LLM-Based Simplification (§4.2): A data augmentation step where an LLM generalizes verbose invariants into compact candidates, each verified for correctness and usefulness. 4.2. LLM-Based Invariant Simplification 

AST normalization removes syntactic noise, but many invari-ants remain verbose due to semantic complexity: enumer-ated cases, redundant bounds, or overly strong constraints. These patterns require reasoning beyond local rewrites. To bridge this gap, we employ an LLM as a simplification function fθ : Φ → ΦN , where Φ is the set of possible predi-cates and N is the number of generated candidates. Unlike the rules in Table 2, fθ is not guaranteed to be semantics-preserving ( fθ (φ)̸ ≡ φ). Instead, we leverage the LLM’s ability to perform abstraction , aiming for candidates that are more compact yet still sufficient to prove property q. The resulting predicate can be equivalent, stronger, weaker or incomparable with the original predicate; this does not jeop-erdize soundness, as we later invoke the verifier to ensure the correctness and sufficiency of the rewritten predicates. 

Simplification Procedure. To guide the LLM toward high-quality invariants, we provide a prompt context C =

⟨P, φ, G⟩ containing the program P , the reference invariant 

φ, and a set of transformation goals G. The following trans-formation goals capture our main insights for achieving the desired characteristics of compactness and usefulness .

Range generalization. We observed that a large number of raw invariants have a case-enumeration structure; for example, Wki=1 (x = i) might come from a loop that runs for k steps. We prompt an LLM to simplify such invariants into a more compact range invariant, 1 ≤ x ≤ k, which is easier to learn than the enumeration representation. 

Constraint factoring. Some invariants form Boolean expres-sions that have unnecessarily complex logical structure, e.g., 

(a ∧ b1) ∨ . . . ∨ (a ∧ bk). We encourage an LLM to perform logical simplification, e.g., rewrite this as a ∧ (b1 ∨ . . . ∨ bk).

Closed-form discovery. Often, we would like to generalize the normalized invariant to replace case enumerations with arithmetic relations, where linear expressions are preferred for solver efficiency, though non-linear closed forms are also possible. This gives us compact, reusable invariants that are easier to learn; for example, Pni=1 i → n(n+1) 2 .

Redundancy removal. Some expressions are implied by program semantics and hold throughout the program, e.g., preconditions or variables with fixed values. To simplify them, we can remove these redundant clauses to obtain more compact and easier-to-learn invariants. Overall, our strategy encourages semantic abstraction over syntactic rewriting; for the full prompt, see Appendix B.2.

## 6. Results and Analysis 

Benefits of W ONDA . Table 3 depicts our main results. For each model, we show indicator rates ( Rvalid/correct/speedup ), the mean speedup among accelerated instances ( ¯S>1) and performance of virtual best (VBP). Across all scales, V2 curation significantly boosts performance. For example, 

Qwen3-4B-V2 nearly doubles its base model’s correctness (22 .8% → 44 .4% ) and speedup rate ( 11 .1% → 24 .7% ). 

Qwen3-8B-V2 also shows a significant improvement, with correctness rate going from 23 .9% on the base model to 

42 .8% on the fine-tuned model; and the corresponding speedup rate goes from 8.5% to 10 .7% . In all cases, we observe a VBP time reduction of 12–20 seconds. 

Comparison to Large Models. Qwen3-4B-V2 achieves the best VBP among our trained models, 165.5s, which matches the VBP of GPT-OSS-120B (165.8s). More in-terestingly, when we add the inference latency to the run-time calculation ( VBP E2E ), Qwen3-4B-V2 , GPT-OSS-120B, and GPT-5.2 achieve comparable performance gains (165.7, 167.6, and 163.4). This suggests that extended reasoning does not always translate to better wall-clock performance. We note that the effect of model invocation overhead will be-come even more pronounced in verification frameworks that involves repeated prompting of LLMs (Wu et al., 2024b). 

Quality over Quantity. The Qwen3-0.6B results reveal a nuanced trade-off. The base model achieves approximately the same correctness ( 28 .5% ± 2.8 vs. 27 .9% ± 0.5) as its V2 counterpart. However, these base model invariants do not translate to better practical performance: Qwen3-0.6B-V2 achieves a higher speedup rate ( 14 .1% vs. 12 .2% ), a larger mean speedup factor ( 8.5× vs. 5.3×), and lower VBP (174.0s vs. 182.9s). This suggests that the base model pro-duces invariants that do not accelerate verification, whereas V2 curation teaches the model to generate invariants that are practically useful for reducing solver runtime. 

Timeouts remain a Challenge. The Solved column re-veals that resolving baseline timeouts remains a hard open problem. GPT-5.2 on average solves 2.67 instances across runs, and our best SLM ( Qwen3-4B-V2 ), despite being much smaller, achieves comparable results (2.33). 

Ablation Analysis. Table 4 highlights the impact of each pipeline stage. Fine-tuning in V0 initially decreases valida-tion (e.g., 99 .2% → 81 .3% for Qwen3-4B), suggesting that training on inconsistently formatted data introduces syntac-tic noise. V1 normalization restores syntax, with validation jumping to 97 .0% (8B) and 97 .6% (4B), while stagnant VBP scores suggest no semantic gain. The transition from V1 to V2 yields the most significant improvements. For both Qwen3-4B and 8B, the pipeline achieves perfect 100 .0% validation, while correctness rises substantially (reaching 44 .4% and 42 .8% respectively). Most notably, speedup nearly doubles for both models, rising from 13% → 21 .7% for the 8B model and from 

12 .7% → 24 .7% for the 4B model. Across these scales, VBP drops significantly, confirming that verified, simplified invariants provide superior supervision compared to raw or merely normalized data. 

## 7. Conclusion 

We introduced W ONDA , a novel data curation pipeline for neural invariant generation. We demonstrated that training on data collected through W ONDA is more effective than training directly on invariants generated by symbolic tools. 8Not All Invariants Are Equal 

Moreover, the improvement in verification time brought by our best fine-tuned SLM is comparable to that of large language models such as GPT-5.2 and GPT-OSS-120B. Moving forward, we plan to evaluate W ONDA with addi-tional backend verifiers, besides UAutomizer. In addition, WONDA currently treats invariant generation as a supervised learning problem over input-output pairs, without exposing intermediate reasoning steps or leveraging verifier feedback during training. Both incorporating chain-of-thought su-pervision and reinforcement learning driven by verification outcomes are interesting next challenges to tackle. 

## Impact Statement 

This work contributes to our understanding of how to cu-ration training data for improving model performance on logical reasoning tasks. In addition, this work help make formal verification more practical in high-stake domains where reliability of the software system is important. We do not anticipate significant negative societal impacts arising from this work.

## D. Training Data Statistics 

The V 2 dataset consists of 7,283 total samples, characterized by a stable token length distribution across both training (5,826 samples) and validation (1,457 samples) splits. As shown in Figure 6, the dataset is categorized into two primary quality tiers: Correct and Sufficient (Grade 2, i.e. all φ s.t. Q(φ) = 2 ), which comprises 4,516 samples, and Provides Speedup (Grade 3, i.e. all φ s.t. Q(φ) = 3 ), which includes 2,767 samples. The mean total sequence length is approximately 518 tokens, reflecting the complexity of the verification queries, while the generated invariants themselves are highly concise, with a mean length of only 15.8 tokens. While the baseline verification across the entire dataset shows a mean speedup of 1.21x, the Grade 3 subset demonstrates a more substantial performance gain—visible in the speedup metrics of Figure 6—with a mean speedup of 2.13x and peaks reaching up to 41.39x. This indicates that the curated invariants in the V2 dataset not only preserve correctness but also offer significant computational advantages for the formal solver.
