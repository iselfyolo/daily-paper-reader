Abstract 

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly deployed, yet their outputs can be highly sens-itive to routine, non-adversarial variations in how users phrase queries—a gap not sufficiently addressed by existing red-teaming efforts. We propose Green Shielding , a user-centric research agenda for building evidence-backed deployment guidance by characterizing how benign input variation shifts model behavior. We operationalize Green Shielding empirically through our pro-posed CUE criteria , which specify how to build an empirical foundation for user-centric guidance that is representative of real-world deployment: benchmarks that capture authentic Context, ref-erence standards and metrics that measure true Utility, and perturbations that reflect realistic variations in the Elicitation of model behavior. Guided by the PCS framework and developed in collaboration with practicing physicians, we instantiate Green Shielding in medical diagnosis by introducing HealthCareMagic-Diagnosis (HCM-Dx), a novel benchmark of patient-authored queries, and by constructing structured reference diagnosis sets and clinically grounded metrics for evaluating differential diagnosis lists (i.e., candidate diagnoses under consideration). We fur-ther study perturbation regimes that capture routine input variation, allowing us to characterize how prompt-level factors shift model behavior along clinically meaningful dimensions. Across multiple frontier LLMs, we find that these shifts trace out Pareto-like tradeoffs. In particular, neutralization, which removes common user-level factors from inputs while preserving clinical content, increases plausibility and yields more concise, clinician-like differentials, while reducing coverage of highly likely and safety-critical conditions. Together, these findings show that inter-action choices can systematically shift task-relevant properties of model outputs, and that Green Shielding can help ground evidence-backed, user-facing guidance for safer deployment in high-stakes domains. Although instantiated here in medical diagnosis, this agenda extends naturally to other decision-support settings and to agentic AI systems, where small variations in user in-puts may shape downstream model reasoning and actions. https://github.com/aaron-jx-li/green-shielding .

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a wide range of domains, accelerating their adoption in real-world settings. As a standard practice in contemporary model development pipelines, new releases are typically accompanied by extensive technical reports that document training procedures and summarize performance on popular benchmarks and leader-boards [1–3], providing a standardized snapshot of capabilities at release. At the same time, LLM outputs are widely recognized to be unreliable and insufficiently grounded, exhibiting well-known issues such as hallucinated content [4, 5], inconsistent reasoning [6, 7], and over-alignment to user

preferences at the expense of correctness [8]. LLM providers accordingly include disclaimers that models may make mistakes; however, given the complex nature of real user-model interactions, such warnings rarely translate into actionable guidance about effective use or when outputs should be trusted. In parallel, the research community has devoted substantial attention to red-teaming, which probes models under adversarial conditions to expose vulnerabilities and stress-test safety boundaries; we refer to this broad focus on catastrophic or worst-case risks [9] as AI Safety I . However, since most real-world use is non-adversarial, worst-case threat models provide limited insight into the ques-tions that matter for everyday use, such as how routine variation in queries and context shapes model behavior and which interaction strategies lead to more reliable responses. We refer to these routine, user-centric risks as AI Safety II . This growing need for deployment-relevant behavioral assessment is further motivated by the practical realities of frontier LLM development: as model scale, propri-etary data, and training infrastructure increasingly place direct model intervention beyond the reach of most academic researchers, rigorous behavioral assessment under deployment-relevant conditions becomes a particularly high-leverage direction for scientific contribution. Therefore, we propose Green Shielding, an overarching research agenda for developing user-centric, evidence-backed guidance for how LLMs should be used in real deployments, analogous to an in-struction manual that customers would expect for any commercial product. We advance this agenda by studying how benign, deployment-realistic variation in user inputs shapes model behavior and by operationalizing measurable notions of success to ground future user-facing guidance. Rather than prescribing best practices, we focus on establishing the conceptual and empirical basis required to derive them, treating LLMs as fixed, deployed products and analyzing their behaviors under realistic, non-adversarial input variation. This perspective complements red-teaming, which predominantly probes adversarial conditions, by centering the stability properties, utility tradeoffs, and user-relevant risks that emerge in everyday use. Building empirical foundations to characterize these everyday-use behaviors is increasingly necessary in high-stakes domains such as healthcare, education, and law, where unreliable outputs can directly affect consequential decisions and public trust [10, 11]. This need is further underscored by a rapidly evolving governance landscape, including comprehensive regulation such as the EU AI Act [12, 13] and formal evaluation guidance such as NIST’s AI Risk Management Framework [14]. Green Shielding can also help bridge the three competing sociotechnical imaginaries of modern AI [15]: frontier AI, which treats AI as a technical artifact; responsible AI, which treats AI as a sociotechnical system; and pragmatic AI, which centers impacts on people’s rights and regulation. We envision Green Shielding as moving beyond frontier-style evaluation by producing deployment-relevant evidence that can inform both organizational practices and regulatory oversight. Our formulation in this paper is informed by Veridical Data Science [16], using the Predictability, Computability, and Stability (PCS) framework as a guiding lens for designing empirical procedures that are aligned with the task of interest, feasible under practical constraints, and robust to realistic sources of heterogeneity. Concretely, we propose three core criteria for building a reliable empirical foundation for user-centric guidance within Green Shielding, which we formalize as the CUE criteria :

• Context: Tasks that represent authentic real-world use. 

• Utility: Reference standards and metrics that represent task-relevant utility. 

• Elicitation: Perturbation regimes that represent how variation in user inputs and interactions elicits different behaviors. Together, these criteria provide an initial empirical foundation for Green Shielding and guide the study design in this paper. We instantiate this approach in medical diagnosis with LLMs, a high-stakes domain in which model behavior is strongly influenced by everyday differences in user queries. As a first step, we study prompt-level sensitivity in a controlled setting using two widely adopted med-ical benchmarks, MedQA [17] and MedXpertQA [18]. Using open-ended generation as the baseline, 2we perturb prompts along several practical dimensions—content, format, and tone—and find that these changes consistently shift response correctness on both benchmarks. At the same time, these experiments highlight a limitation of static, exam-style benchmarks for free-form medical assessment: they are designed to be information-complete and single-answer, unlike real-world queries, which are often noisy, vague, and underspecified. This motivates benchmarks and evaluation procedures that better capture diagnostic uncertainty and the need to consider multiple plausible diagnoses. To better operationalize Green Shielding in medical diagnosis, we organize our empirical design around the three dimensions of the CUE criteria introduced above. First, we curate a realistic benchmark of patient-authored queries by filtering HealthCareMagic-100K [19] and adapting it to focus on dia-gnostic tasks; we refer to the resulting dataset as HealthCareMagic-Diagnosis (HCM-Dx) 1. Second, we define structured reference standards and evaluation metrics suited to free-form diagnostic out-puts. Instead of relying on the accompanying physician replies, we use multiple frontier LLMs to generate a small set of reference diagnoses for each query, organized into plausible, highly likely, and safety-critical categories. These references support metrics that assess clinically meaningful properties of a model’s differential diagnosis list, including structural metrics such as precision over plausible diagnoses, coverage of likely and safety-critical conditions, and differential breadth (i.e., the number of diagnoses proposed), as well as semantic metrics such as evidence grounding and epistemic uncer-tainty. Third, to isolate the impact of routine prompt variation, we introduce a prompt neutralization module that identifies common user-level factors and rewrites each query into a standardized, object-ive medical description, removing these factors while preserving clinical content. An overview of our framework is provided in Figure 1. We evaluate multiple frontier LLMs from diverse model families on HCM-Dx. Under raw patient inputs, models generate moderately sized differential diagnosis lists, with most proposed diagnoses falling within the clinically plausible set. However, coverage of the highly likely and safety-critical reference sets remains substantially lower, indicating that even frontier models do not reliably sur-face the most probable or cannot-miss conditions. Prompt neutralization induces a consistent and interpretable tradeoff across models: differentials become more concise and plausibility increases, but coverage of highly likely and safety-critical diagnoses declines. Comparisons with clinician responses show that physicians produce markedly narrower differentials, prioritizing a small number of lead-ing hypotheses rather than exhaustive enumeration. Taken together, these results demonstrate that routine, non-adversarial differences in prompt formulation can substantially shift clinically meaningful properties of model outputs, and that our framework makes the resulting precision–coverage tradeoffs explicit rather than obscured by single-score evaluations. We do not interpret any single prompt-ing condition as uniformly preferable; instead, different conditions correspond to different operating points, whose desirability depends on context, risk tolerance, and downstream decision-making. By making these tradeoffs measurable, our results motivate human-centered evaluation of usefulness, safety, and trust under realistic interaction settings. Broadly speaking, our experiments support the central premise of Green Shielding: in non-adversarial settings, ordinary differences in query formulation can produce consistent and measurable changes in model behavior. By combining realistic patient queries with structured reference standards and clinically grounded metrics, our framework enables rigorous and scalable measurement of these effects. As a standalone resource, HCM-Dx bridges the gap between conventional exam-style medical bench-marks and the ambiguity of real-world patient queries in a structured and efficient way. Although we focus on medical diagnosis as a case study in this work, Green Shielding extends naturally to other decision-support domains and, in future work, to multi-turn and more agentic settings, where interaction itself becomes a central determinant of downstream behavior. 2 Related Work 

Red-Teaming LLMs. Red-teaming has emerged as a core methodology in AI safety research, which refers to the intentional probing of models under adversarial conditions across prompts, data, and system interactions has proven effective in identifying critical failure modes [9, 20, 21]. Prompt-level red-teaming has revealed vulnerabilities such as jailbreaking [22–25] and prompt injection [26, 27], and has in turn motivated a growing body of empirical defenses and mitigation strategies [28, 29]. Other forms of attacks also consider data poisoning [30, 31] and exploiting system-level vulnerabilities in tool-augmented settings [32–35]. These efforts play a critical role in risk assessment, governance, and 4regulatory compliance [12–14]. At the same time, its emphasis on worst-case probing leaves open the complementary question of how natural, non-adversarial shifts in user inputs translate into changes in utility, stability, and user-facing risk. This body of work largely aligns with what we call AI Safety I, whereas our focus is on AI Safety II: reliability and user-facing risk under realistic, non-adversarial deployment conditions. 

Prompt Sensitivity of LLMs. Recent work has begun to systematically study prompt sensitiv-ity of LLMs, showing that model outputs can vary substantially under minor semantics-preserving changes in how inputs are presented. Existing efforts include general evaluation frameworks and metrics for quantifying sensitivity [36–38], as well as empirical analyses of common instability sources such as prompt formatting [39, 40], the ordering of options in multiple-choice settings [41, 42], and user-provided preference or belief signals that can induce sycophantic behavior [8, 43, 44]. Ceballos-Arroyo et al. [45] specifically study prompt sensitivity in clinical settings, but their analysis is largely limited to classification, information extraction, and knowledge-matching tasks. Our work builds on known prompt-level factors, but focuses on those commonly encountered in real user interactions, adopts simple and intuitive sensitivity measures, and centers on open-ended medical diagnosis as a more challenging and realistic setting. 

Medical Benchmarks for LLMs. The majority of widely used medical benchmarks, including MedQA [17], MMLU (clinical knowledge) [46], PubMedQA [47], MedMCQA [48], or a combination of multiple benchmarks [49], follow a standardized question-answer style with provided ground truth answers, and usually adopt a multiple-choice format. More recent work has begun to move beyond single-answer medical QA. For example, Tu et al. [50] evaluates model-generated differential diagnosis lists using top-k accuracy against expert-validated labels, while HealthBench [51] also aims to reflect realistic healthcare use cases but does so through synthetic health conversations paired with physician-written, case-specific rubrics. Other benchmarks, such as MedXpertQA [18], treat diagnosis as one category within a conventional single-answer QA format. In contrast, our diagnosis case study is built from fully realistic patient queries and targets a narrower but more clinically grounded setting. We focus specifically on diagnostic reasoning over real-world inputs, introduce fine-grained metrics tailored to differential diagnosis, and rely on limited expert supervision to keep the evaluation scalable and broadly usable. We select MedQA and MedXpertQA as representative benchmarks for our preliminary study, as they include substantial diagnostic content and span different difficulty levels. To better reflect real-world use, we further derive a patient-authored benchmark by adapting HealthCareMagic-100K [19], originally used to train medical LLMs, to the diagnosis setting. 

LLM as a Judge. As the need to evaluate LLM capabilities grows and human labeling remains costly, LLM-as-a-judge [52, 53] has become a widely used procedure for producing scalable evaluation signals for open-ended generation. While LLM judges can exhibit systematic artifacts, including self-preference bias [54, 55] and position bias [56], prior works have shown that, with careful prompt design, guardrails, and calibration, they can provide reliable and reproducible measurements in evaluation and benchmarking settings [57–60]. In the medical domain, recent works such as MedHELM [61] has also started to heavily rely on LLM judges during evalution. In our framework, we use multiple LLM judges primarily for (i) constructing structured reference diagnosis sets and (ii) matching clinical synonyms during evaluation; we additionally leverage them for smaller, text-level operations such as paraphrasing and parsing, which are comparatively straightforward for the frontier models we use as judges. We assess judge reliability by measuring alignment between judge decisions and clinician annotations on randomly sampled subsets. 53 Pilot Study: Prompt Sensitivity on Existing Medical Benchmarks 

To motivate Green Shielding in the medical diagnosis setting, we begin with a controlled pilot study on two widely used benchmarks, MedQA and MedXpertQA, which span different difficulty levels, with MedXpertQA notably more challenging for frontier LLMs [18]. We use GPT-4.1-mini as the target model for this study. We apply prompt-level perturbations and measure the resulting changes in response correctness, quantified by perturbation success rate and post-perturbation global accuracy. We first filter each benchmark to retain only diagnosis-focused questions, using an LLM judge for screening, which yields 949 MedQA items and 921 MedXpertQA items. Additional details of the filtering procedure are provided in Appendix B.1. We use an open-ended question template, where the model does not see the answer options, as the baseline, under which GPT-4.1-mini achieves default accuracies of 59 .9% and 19 .3% on the two datasets. The correctness of each model response is determined by a separate LLM judge, whose reliability is validated through comparison with human evaluations on selected samples. We then construct perturbations spanning three broad categories, 

content , format , and tone , each comprising multiple finer-grained factors that can be manipulated independently. 3.1 Content 

Since the clinical information in each question typically includes both symptom descriptions and objective findings such as laboratory results, it is natural to ask how these components differentially contribute to model performance. To study this, we use an LLM judge ( GPT-4.1-mini ) to partition the clinically relevant content into past symptoms and objective test results , then ablate one component at a time and measure the resulting degradation. As shown in Table 1, removing either component induces substantial item-level correctness changes and a clear drop in accuracy. Symptom removal produces the larger effect on the easier MedQA benchmark, whereas on the more challenging MedXpertQA benchmark, where the default accuracy is already low, the effects of removing symptoms and objective test results are more similar. Another important content-level factor is the presence of user belief , since sycophancy [8] is known to influence open-ended model behavior. When we inject explicit user beliefs (see Appendix E.1) that contradict the model’s default answer, we observe that responses change systematically, indicating strong sensitivity to belief cues. 

3.2 Format 

Meanwhile, it is also informative to compare our open-ended diagnosis setting with the original 

multiple-choice format . We find that hiding the answer options reduces model accuracy by 22 .2% 

on MedQA and 3.9% on MedXpertQA, highlighting the limitation of relying on multiple-choice bench-marks to argue about model utility in deployment settings. In addition, we measure response in-stability when the user explicitly asks whether the model to agree with an answer that differs from its default response (i.e., "Is answer X correct?" ) in a binary format , and we again observe substantial perturbation success.

3.4 Limitations of Single-Answer Benchmarks 

Although our results provide clear evidence of prompt-level sensitivity, MedQA and MedXpertQA remain imperfect proxies for real-world diagnostic use. These benchmarks are largely exam-style and information-complete, with a single labeled answer, whereas real patient queries are often shorter, contain less clinical information but more biases, and naturally admit multiple clinically plausible explanations. As a result, single-answer evaluation can obscure diagnostic uncertainty and the struc-ture of differential diagnoses, motivating benchmarks and metrics with more fine-grained reference structures. 74 Towards Realistic and User-Centric Medical Diagnosis Evaluation 

In this section, we present a novel evaluation framework for Green Shielding in the medical dia-gnosis setting. We release HealthCareMagic-Diagnosis (HCM-Dx), a benchmark of patient-authored diagnostic queries, together with task-specific reference standards and metrics. Compared to con-ventional exam-style medical benchmarks, our framework better reflects deployment conditions and supports evaluation of open-ended diagnostic differentials. Compared to realistic benchmarks such as HealthBench [51], which rely on physician-written, conversation-specific rubrics to assess broad assistant behavior, our framework targets differential diagnosis evaluation and is highly scalable and adaptable with the help of LLM judges. Its design is informed by the PCS framework and organized around the CUE criteria: HCM-Dx provides the Context , the structured reference sets and metrics define Utility , and prompt neutralization operationalizes Elicitation .

4.1 HealthCareMagic-Diagnosis (HCM-Dx) 

HealthCareMagic-100K consists of 100K realistic clinical question–answer pairs sourced from an online medical consultation platform where licensed physicians provide responses [19], and was originally intended for fine-tuning medical LLMs. To curate an evaluation benchmark for differential diagnosis, we use an LLM judge to filter for questions where (i) the user explicitly requests a diagnosis, and (ii) the judge assigns its highest confidence to these determinations. The details of this filtering process are included in Appendix B.2. For this initial release, we apply filtering to the first 20K examples, reserving the remaining 80K for future validation and extensions. This process yields 2,697 diagnosis-focused queries, which we release as HealthCareMagic-Diagnosis (HCM-Dx). 

4.2 Reference Structure and Metrics for Differential Diagnosis 

To reflect the clinical imperative of balancing probability with risk management, we extend the con-ventional single-answer reference to a set-valued structure comprising three categories. The plausible set encompasses all plausible diagnoses consistent with the patient’s presentation. Within this scope, we define two subsets: the highly likely set , comprising the most probable etiologies, and the 

safety-critical set , consisting of severe conditions that warrant immediate consideration or exclu-sion, provided they are clinically plausible. This component corresponds to the Utility dimension of CUE, as it defines task-relevant reference standards and metrics for evaluating open-ended diagnostic outputs. To construct these sets in a scalable manner, we employ an ensemble of three state-of-the-art LLMs: GPT-5.2 , Gemini-3-Pro , and Claude-4.5-Opus . We establish reference labels using a majority vote, where a diagnosis is included in a set only if at least two of the three models agree on its assignment. To ensure this approximate ground truth remains independent of superficial prompt factors and relies solely on the query’s core information, we use an LLM-based semantic extractor. This component parses user inputs into a structured dictionary comprising three fields: demographics, subjective symptoms, and objective clinical results. Formally, let x denote the raw input, then our extractor transforms x into a structured clinical representation, x∗. Based on this representation, the ensemble of frontier LLMs generates the aggregated reference sets P (x∗), H(x∗), and S(x∗), which serve as the reference labels for our HCM-Dx benchmark. Next, we define the corresponding evaluation metrics for our set-based reference structure. Let M (x)

denote the model response, and D(x) denote the set of diagnoses extracted from M (x) using another semantic parser. As diagnoses may be expressed using clinical synonyms or variant surface forms, we use ≈ to denote semantic equivalence between extracted diagnosis strings. Semantic metrics. Beyond set membership and breadth, we compute auxiliary metrics that cap-ture other clinically relevant semantic properties: 

• Evidence grounding rate: For each extracted diagnosis, an LLM judge determines whether it is supported by the question context, allowing reasonable clinical inference; we report the fraction of diagnoses marked as supported. 

• Indirect inference rate: We report the fraction of diagnoses flagged as making patient-specific claims that are not supported by, or clearly implied from, the input. 

• Epistemic uncertainty: We report the fraction of responses that explicitly state diagnostic uncertainty due to insufficient or unclear information (epistemic uncertainty). Throughout our evaluation framework, various LLM judges are being used, and we provide their details in Appendix E.2, E.3, and E.4. 

4.3 Prompt Neutralization 

To isolate the effect of routine, deployment-realistic prompt variation, we introduce a prompt neut-ralization module that rewrites raw user inputs into a standardized clinical form while annotating reliability-relevant user factors. For each raw query, an LLM-based annotator produces a concise third-person clinical case description and poses a single diagnostic question, which we denote by x′,while preserving the underlying medical content. We then use a semantic verifier to compare the structured clinical representation x∗ with x′ and check that core clinical information is retained. In addition, the annotator outputs a structured set of binary factor labels capturing common user-level variations observed in real interactions. Similar to our pilot study in Section 3, we organize the annotated factors into the same three broad categories, as shown in Figure 3. Content-level factors capture variation in the substantive inform-ation provided by users, including the presence of specific diagnostic guesses, irrelevant non-medical details, and indicators of missing clinical information such as lack of objective data and incomplete symptom history. Our format-level factor captures how well the request is structured, flagging queries that are ambiguous or unstructured, such as those that combine multiple intents including diagnosis, treatment, and advice-seeking within a single query. Tone-level factors capture how users convey affective state and personal framing, rather than introducing new clinical evidence. We include two such factors: emotional or urgent tone, reflecting subjective distress such as fear, anxiety, panic, or emotional distress; and first-person perspective, indicating whether the query is framed from the point of view of the patient or someone with a close relationship to the patient. Although almost all HCM-Dx queries are written in the first person (99.6%), we nevertheless include it as a formal factor to capture this defining characteristic of real-world patient queries. Not all annotated factors are directly perturbed in our neutralization pipeline. 5.1 Experimental Setup 

Our evaluation currently includes GPT-4.1-mini , GPT-5-mini , Gemini-3-flash , Claude-Haiku-4.5 ,and DeepSeek-Reasoner . We plan to extend the benchmark to additional frontier LLMs with state-of-the-art capabilities and broader model-family diversity. When generating reference sets, we cap P (x∗)

5.3 Additional Analyses Comparison with HCM Clinician Responses Comparing LLM outputs with real physician responses from the original HealthCareMagic-100K highlights clear mismatches in how differentials are communicated. Clinicians typically commit to a small set of leading hypotheses and give terse, focused differentials rather than long precautionary lists; accordingly, their responses can leave some possibilities in our constructed reference sets unmentioned, reflecting prioritization rather than simple omission. LLMs more often adopt an enumerative style, expanding the list to hedge uncertainty and appear comprehensive. Notably, our prompt neutralization partially shifts model outputs toward the clinician convention: the resulting differentials are more selective, more closely resembling how clini-cians prioritize leading hypotheses over exhaustive enumeration. Clinicians also more frequently rely on indirect inference with fewer explicit uncertainty markers, suggesting greater comfort with contex-tual assumptions and compressed clinical reasoning. Overall, these contrasts underscore a persistent gap between model and clinician diagnostic styles, while also showing that prompt neutralization can partially bridge it by moving model outputs toward clinician-like communication.

Preliminary Expert Verification of the Evaluation Pipeline Our evaluation in both the pilot study and the main HCM-Dx experiments relies on two scalable components: LLM-based semantic matching between predicted and reference diagnoses, and LLM-constructed reference diagnosis sets. To assess whether this pipeline aligns with clinical judgment, we conduct two small-scale expert verification studies. We first examine the semantic-equivalence matching step used throughout the paper to determine whether a model-predicted diagnosis should count as matching a reference diagnosis. In this experi-ment, two LLM judges, GPT-5-mini and GPT-4.1-mini, each label diagnosis pairs as either a semantic 

match or no match , and two clinicians then review a sample of these pairs. Figure 6 stratifies the expert judgments by whether the two LLM judges were unanimous or split on the binary decision. When the LLM judges predict match , experts usually agree, especially in unanimous cases, indicating high precision for positive matches. By contrast, when the LLM judges predict no match , experts often label the pair as a close match or vague match rather than a strict non-match. This pattern suggests that the matching model uses a conservative threshold for semantic equivalence: positive matches are generally reliable, while some borderline or partially equivalent pairs are assigned no match . Thus, the main limitation of the matching step appears to be under-crediting borderline equivalents rather than systematically accepting clearly unrelated diagnoses as matches. We next evaluate the quality of the constructed reference sets by asking two clinicians to independently edit them, marking diagnoses to remove or add. Here we focus on agreement-based edits, counting an error only when both clinicians concur. Table 2 and Figure 7 summarizes these consensus error rates. Under this criterion, commission errors are rare across all three reference sets; for example, agreed-upon removals occur in only 2% of safety-critical sets and 8% of highly likely sets. The dominant remaining issue is omission: clinicians more often agree that a reference set is missing at least one diagnosis, particularly for the plausible and safety-critical sets, where agreed missing diagnoses occur in 18% and 20% of questions, respectively. Overall, this preliminary check supports the validity of our scalable reference construction. As a complementary robustness check, we also compute error rates under an either-annotator criterion, counting a diagnosis as incorrect or missing if at least one clinician flagged it. Under this definition, error rates increase substantially across all sets. Figure 7 suggests that much of this increase is driven by disagreement across experts, especially for plausible differential sets. This likely reflects the ambiguity of the underlying task: many patient queries are unstructured and underspecified, so even human experts must rely on subjective inference when deciding which diagnoses belong in the differential. Taken together, these results suggest that residual noise in our evaluation pipeline arises more from under-specified inputs and expert disagreement than from systematic flaws in the constructed reference sets. It also shows how Green Shielding can be studied empirically through procedures guided by the Predictability, Computability, and Stability (PCS) framework. 

PCS as a Guiding Framework In this work, the PCS framework [16] provides a methodological lens for designing and interpreting our empirical study of Green Shielding. Within this lens, the CUE criteria offer a complementary, task-level structure centered on authentic context, task-relevant utility, and realistic elicitation of model behavior. They are closely connected: Context and Utility support Predictability by aligning the study with real-world tasks and meaningful outcomes, while Elicita-tion supports Stability by enabling systematic analysis of routine input variation. Computability is reflected in the scalable design of our pipeline, including LLM-assisted reference construction, auto-mated normalization and matching, and efficient use of expert annotation. Together, PCS provides a principled methodological foundation, while CUE helps translate that foundation into a deployment-relevant, scalable, and robust empirical design. Although our case study focuses on medical diagnosis, the same combination can generalize naturally to other domains such as education, law, and public policy, where users often provide open-ended, context-rich inputs and model outputs may inform high-stakes decisions. 

Interpreting the Observed Tradeoffs It is important to note that we do not interpret the neutralized prompt condition as universally better than the raw one. Instead, our results reveal systematic tradeoffs across metrics such as plausibility, coverage, and breadth, with different prompt conditions occupying different operating points. Which point is preferable may depend on the user, task, and deployment context, especially in high-stakes settings where caution, comprehensiveness, and selectivity may need to be balanced differently. An important direction for future work is therefore not only to improve performance along these dimensions, but also to identify additional task-relevant evaluation criteria, such as uncertainty calibration, actionability, and downstream decision utility, and to determine how these tradeoffs should be resolved in practice. In particular, selecting an appropriate operating point will likely require incorporating stakeholder-specific preferences and risk tolerances, for example through clinician- or user-informed utility functions that weight different types of errors and omissions differently. 

Broad Relevance Although we instantiate Green Shielding through a medical diagnosis case study, the underlying agenda goes beyond medicine. More generally, we study how benign but realistic variation in user presentation affects system behavior in settings where users seek decision sup-port under uncertainty. Similar interaction-dependent instabilities arise in domains such as legal and policy decision-making, education, and recommendation, where outputs are often open-ended, context-dependent, and not well captured by a single correct answer. This perspective is especially relevant in the recent shift toward agentic AI systems. As models increasingly operate as assistants that plan, act, and adapt over extended interactions, user-agent interaction itself becomes a central determinant of downstream behavior. In these settings, small differences in how users express goals, uncertainty, or context may propagate through multi-step reasoning or action pipelines and lead to materially different outcomes, with profound implications for safety, security, and privacy. 

Limitations of Task Formulation A central limitation of our diagnosis case study is that many real patient queries are highly unstructured and clinically underspecified. Similar forms of underspe-cified, user-driven input arise in other domains such as education and legal or policy decision-making, where a student may seek help without clarifying their current understanding or specific point of con-fusion, or a user may request legal or policy guidance without specifying the jurisdiction, procedural 15 stage, or relevant factual background. In such cases, requesting a differential diagnosis from the initial query alone may be of limited value, because the input often lacks the information needed for a mean-ingful assessment. This creates a risk of premature closure: models may produce confident outputs despite insufficient information, reflecting a broader misalignment between benchmark settings that reward definitiveness and real-world settings that often call for uncertainty acknowledgment, clarific-ation, or deferral. In many cases, the more appropriate response would be to acknowledge insufficient information, ask clarifying questions, or defer diagnostic narrowing. Furthermore, diagnosis is not al-ways the most useful target in these settings; outputs such as triage urgency, disposition, or follow-up questions may be more appropriate. A further limitation is that the current benchmark is single-turn, whereas real diagnostic interactions are iterative. Extending Green Shielding to multi-turn settings is therefore important both for clinical realism and for evaluating whether models can recover from ambiguous or poorly framed initial inputs. 

Limitations of Evaluation Framework Our evaluation framework improves on much prior work by using fully realistic patient-authored queries and more deployment-relevant metrics for open-ended differential diagnosis, but it still does not capture all dimensions of response quality. It supports scal-able analysis of hypothesis coverage and selectivity, but abstracts away other aspects such as ques-tioning strategy, explanation quality, conversational appropriateness, and actionability. For example, conversation-level evaluations, including rubric-based assessments such as those used in HealthBench [51], could provide a richer characterization of model behavior, but at substantially lower scalability. Meanwhile, although the starting prompts are fully realistic, the downstream evaluation pipeline still introduces synthetic structure through reference construction, normalization, and semantic matching. These abstractions are important for scalability, but they also create a tradeoff between real-world fidelity and measurement control. This tension is not specific to medical diagnosis: scalable evaluation may also miss important features of real-world use, such as adaptation to user context, incorpora-tion of information revealed across turns, and appropriate requests for clarification before offering guidance. An important direction for future work is therefore to develop evaluation pipelines that better preserve the richness of real-world use while still enabling reliable, scalable, and interpretable comparisons. 

Expert Disagreement and Normative Uncertainty Our preliminary clinician validation also suggests that expert disagreement is itself a meaningful source of uncertainty. In many cases, disagree-ment appears to arise not because the reference sets are clearly flawed, but because the underlying patient queries are underspecified. When the available information is sparse or messy, even human ex-perts must rely on subjective inference when deciding which diagnoses belong in the differential. This observation points to a broader challenge for evaluating decision-support systems: some instability in the labels reflects genuine ambiguity in the task rather than simple annotation noise. Future work should therefore study stability across clinicians, specialties, and institutions, and more explicitly examine which tradeoff profiles are preferred in practice. 

Toward Interactive Evaluation One natural next step is to extend Green Shielding to multi-turn and more agentic settings, where systems are allowed to ask clarifying questions, recognize missing information, and adapt to underspecified user inputs. Doing so would move the agenda beyond single-shot benchmarking and toward a more general framework for understanding how interaction choices shape model behavior under realistic use. 

7 Conclusion 

In this work, we introduce Green Shielding, a user-centric research agenda for developing evidence-backed deployment guidance by studying how benign, deployment-realistic variation in user inputs shapes model behavior. This challenge arises across many domains in which users seek decision 16 support under uncertainty, including medicine, education, and legal or policy settings. We present the first empirical study of this agenda in medical diagnosis. Grounded in the PCS framework and structured through the CUE criteria, our approach emphasizes task relevance, feasibility under practical constraints, and robustness to realistic variation. Using scalable reference construction and fine-grained metrics tailored to differential diagnosis, we show that frontier LLMs exhibit systematic behavioral shifts under deployment-relevant prompt variation, revealing reliability tradeoffs that are largely obscured by conventional benchmarks. We further show that prompt neutralization moves model outputs toward more clinician-like differentials while making explicit the tradeoff between comprehensiveness and selectivity. Beyond documenting these effects, Green Shielding defines a concrete target for future work: improving prompt-level strategies and interaction design against explicit, task-relevant criteria such as plausibility, coverage, and breadth, rather than optimizing only for aggregate accuracy. More broadly, Green Shielding provides a foundation for studying how interaction choices shape model behavior under realistic use. Future work should expand coverage across models and domains, while extending the framework beyond single-shot evaluation to multi-turn and more agentic settings, where systems must recognize missing information, ask clarifying questions, and adapt to underspecified user inputs. In the medical setting, larger clinician studies and complementary conversation-level assessments will also be needed to strengthen validation and better capture dimensions of response quality beyond differential diagnosis generation alone. We believe Green Shielding can help catalyze a broader shift toward interactive, user-centric evaluation paradigms that better reflect real deployment conditions and provide more actionable guidance for improving AI systems in practice. 17 References

C Selection of Prompt-level Factors 

The prompt-level factors used in the pilot study and the HCM experiments were chosen through an iterative process combining human judgment with empirical refinement. Our goal was not to enumerate all possible sources of prompt variation, but to isolate a small set of realistic user-side factors that could be studied independently and manipulated in a controlled way. In the pilot study, factor selection began from exam-style diagnostic questions, to which we added realistic user-side perturbations based on human intuition about how real patients might present the same underlying case differently. We organized these perturbations into three broad categor-ies: content-level factors, which change what substantive information is foregrounded or introduced; format-level factors, which change how the request is structured; and tone-level factors, which change affective or interpersonal framing. This categorization was useful because these three dimensions are conceptually distinct and, to a first approximation, separable: a user can change tone without chan-ging core content, alter formatting without changing affect, or introduce additional content without changing the overall structure of the request. We therefore prioritized primary factors that could be manipulated without presupposing another latent factor. Conversely, we avoided secondary factors whose definition depends on other prompt attributes, such as the confidence of a misleading user belief, since such factors are harder to vary cleanly and harder to interpret causally. The pilot study served not only as a proof of concept that prompt-level variation can affect model behavior, but also as an experimentation stage for refining which factor articulations were useful to carry forward. Some candidate factors that initially seemed plausible proved difficult to isolate cleanly or did not induce strong effects under our operationalization. This is important for interpretation: 24 when a factor appears to have limited impact, the appropriate conclusion is not that the underlying phenomenon is universally unimportant, but that our particular articulation of that factor did not produce a strong effect in this setting. The HCM experiments largely follow the same factor logic, but adapt it to real patient-authored queries. The factor set was refined to reflect recurrent patterns in HealthCareMagic data while remaining compatible with scalable annotation and controlled neutralization. As a result, the HCM factors are close in spirit to those in the pilot study, but not identical in function. In particular, some factors in HCM, such as lack of objective data and lack of symptom history, are retained primarily as 

indicator factors : they characterize the information state of the query and are analytically important, but are not themselves direct manipulation targets in the neutralization pipeline. This reflects a shift from exploratory perturbation design in the pilot study to a more constrained operationalization for large-scale analysis on real-world data. Across both studies, the guiding principle was to focus on factors that are realistic, behaviorally meaningful, and sufficiently disentangled to support interpretable intervention. A natural exten-sion for future work is to complement this human-designed factorization with interpretability-based methods such as ProxySPEX [62] for interaction discovery, which may help identify higher-order de-pendencies among prompt-level factors and test whether some observed effects are driven primarily by interactions rather than marginal changes alone.

H Attempts to Improve Safety-Critical Coverage via Prompting 

As shown in our results, all evaluated models attain low coverage of safety-critical diagnoses. We explored prompt-based interventions that explicitly instruct the model (via the system prompt) to consider and include safety-critical possibilities. While this increases safety-critical coverage, it con-sistently induces a large expansion in the differential list, substantially reducing practical usefulness. For instance, for GPT-4.1-mini under neutralized inputs, the average breadth increases from 3.76 to 

8.42 with such prompting. Given this strong coverage-breadth tradeoff, we keep the system prompt minimal. This choice also better reflects real deployment: end users typically have limited control over system prompts in interactive products (e.g., ChatGPT), and our goal is to characterize reliability under realistic user-facing levers. 36
