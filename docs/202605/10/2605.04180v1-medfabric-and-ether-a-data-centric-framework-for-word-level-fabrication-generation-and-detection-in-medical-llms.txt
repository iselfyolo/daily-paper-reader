Abstract. Large Language Models exhibit strong reasoning and seman-tic understanding capabilities but often hallucinate in domains that re-quire expert knowledge, among which fabrications, the generation of fac-tually incorrect yet fluent statements, pose the greatest risk in medi-cal contexts. Existing medical hallucination datasets inadequately cap-ture fabrication phenomena due to limited fabrication coverage, stylis-tic disparities between human and LLM-authored texts, and distribu-tional drift during hallucinated sample synthesis. To address this, we propose a data-centric pipeline to generate realistic and word-level fab-rications that preserve syntactic and stylistic fidelity while introducing subtle factual deviations, resulting in MedFabric . Building upon this dataset, we introduce EtHER , a modular word-level fabrication de-tector integrating Text2Table Decomposition, Word Masking and Fill-ing and Hybrid Sentence Pair Evaluation to enhance factual alignment. Empirical results demonstrate that EtHER outperforms state-of-the-art detectors by over 15% on word-level fabrication benchmarks while maintaining consistent performance across structural similarities, offer-ing a comprehensive framework for reliable and domain-specific factual-ity detection. Our results can be reproduced via the repository: https: //anonymous.4open.science/r/medfabric-74D2/README.md .

1 Introduction 

Large Language Models (‘LLM’s) have shaken up the world with its strong semantic understanding, in-context learning, and logical reasoning capabilities [12]. However, without domain-focused training, LLM commonly hallucinates in domains that require expertise knowledge [30]. LLM hallucinations consist of multiple forms, such as misinterpreting question, providing incomplete informa-tion and misusing concepts [6], the most problematic of which being fabrication,  

> arXiv:2605.04180v1 [cs.CL] 5 May 2026 2Tung Sum Thomas et al.

which generates factually contradictory information [8], especially in medical and legal fields which emphasize on factuality [14], hence highlighting the need of fabrication detection. We argue that training medical-specific hallucination detector (classifier) re-quires specialized datasets due to domain shift. Existing medical hallucination datasets are curated by gathering correct and wrong answers from different sources, e.g. Med-HALT and MedHallu [26,28] gather ground truths from human sources and generates hallucinations with LLMs. Other medical datasets gather both correct and wrong answers from human-written sources, e.g. MedMCQA and Medqa-USMILE gathers question, correct and wrong answers from multiple-choice questions of medical licensing exams [25,11]. Nevertheless, existing hallucination training + detection pipelines exhibit two critical limitations, one on training data and the other on model. Training data does not mimic realistic LLM fabrication scenarios so that models trained on it overfit to confounding variables. Hallucinated samples often display distinct stylistic and structural patterns compared to their truthful counterparts [28,33]. This introduces confounding cues that detectors exploit, leading to spurious classification based on surface style rather than factual content. On the other hand, existing detector models are fragile when stylistic arti-facts are controlled. Existing detectors trained under data from general domain show an asymmetric prediction performance towards ground truth rather than fabricated samples in out-of-distribution (‘OOD’) medical samples. Specifically, embedding-based detectors tend to equate stylistic similarity with semantic ac-curacy 5, while LLM-based evaluators depend heavily on prompt phrasing and thus exhibit inconsistent judgments across runs [7]. These issues collectively produce asymmetric predictive behavior between factual and fabricated samples, motivating the development of a new halluci-nation training + detection pipeline containing (1) a data generation pipeline capable of producing stylistically consistent yet factually incorrect fabrications, and (2) a robust detection model framework that captures word-level fabrica-tions with precision and reproducibility. To address limitation in training data, we develop a data generation pipeline to create realistic and controllable word-level fabrications that mimics re-alistic LLM fabricating behaviors. Our method operates directly at the lexical level, preserving both syntax and writing style of authentic LLM outputs. This pipeline consists of three stages: i) rewriting human-authored ground truths into LLM-style counterparts to align stylistic distributions, ii) generating evidence-conditioned fabrications that introduce fine-grained factual deviations while main-taining contextual coherence, and iii) enforcing semantic and structural similar-ity constraints to ensure fabricated statements remain lexically close to their truthful counterparts. The resulting dataset, MedFabric , offers a challenging and realistic benchmark with high structural similarity between ground truth and fabricated samples, and subtle word difference that causes text semantic shifts, which then illustrates failure modes of existing detectors, both LLM- and embedding-based. Regarding limitations on detecting model, we further introduce a modular word-level fabrication detector designed to address the shortcomings of existing systems, named ‘EvaluaTe Hallucination with TablE decomposition and woRd masking’ EtHER . The proposed framework simplifies medical texts into in-distribution samples and leverage individual strength of embedding-based and LLM-based detectors collectively by integrating three complementary compo-nents: i) a Text2Table Decomposition module that transforms unstructured medical statements into structured entity known as description tables, hence improving semantic interpretability and reducing contextual ambiguity; ii) a Word Masking and Filling module that performs fine-grained factual align-ment by reconstructing masked text segments with phrases retrieved from ver-ified biomedical knowledge; and iii) a Hybrid Sentence Pair Evaluation 

module that combines embedding-based similarity metrics with LLM-based rea-soning for stable, low-variance factuality judgments. Our empirical results show that EtHER not only consistently outperform existing detectors on prediction performance in word-level fabrication data like 

MedFabric by about 15% overall, but also show robust and consistent pre-diction performance on existing dataset with lower structural similarity and its rewritten variations. 

Contributions This work establishes a unified paradigm for studying, detect-ing, and ultimately mitigating fabrication-type medical hallucinations in LLMs, paving the way toward trustworthy LLM systems for high-stakes medical ap-plications and reducing workload of medical expertise in addressing patients consultations. To conclude, the contribution of this work includes: 

– Uncovering overlooked significance of stylistic pattern between ground truth and fabricated medical samples on detector training; 

– Suggesting a data generation pipeline to simulate realistic medical fabrica-tion scenarios; and 

– Proposing a fabrication detector to align with word-level fabrications; 

2 Related Work 

LLM hallucination [8] refers to generating factually incorrect or nonsensical con-tent, a long-standing issue in language modeling. Early work by Azaria and Mitchell [1] marked the complete data-model pipeline by introducing a hidden-state-based classifier and a simple true–false dataset, despite being limited to short synthetic statements. Subsequent datasets such as HaluEval [17] extended coverage to conversational hallucinations and inspired multi-type categoriza-tion frameworks [33,8]. Building on this taxonomy, MedHallu [28] provides a medical-domain benchmark that classifies hallucinations into four types: ques-tion misinterpretation, incomplete information, mechanistic misattribution, and methodological fabrication. However, fabrication cases remain scarce, despite their critical importance in high-stakes domains such as healthcare, law, and journalism [18]. This motivates our construction of a fabrication-focused dataset to capture these subtle yet consequential errors. In the model spectrum of detecting hallucinations [9], existing approaches generally fall into three categories: 1) Uncertainty-based methods quan-tify confidence through token probabilities [15], verbalized self-assessments [20], 4 Tung Sum Thomas et al. 

or response consistency across multiple generations [22], yet they rely on la-tent spaces that do not inherently separate truthful and hallucinated responses, 2) Supervised approaches train classifiers on labeled hallucination data un-der the assumption that truthfulness is implicitly encoded within LLM internal states [1], but their scalability is constrained by costly human annotation, and 3) LLM-agentic frameworks employ one or more LLMs as evaluators to ver-ify factuality [10], though they remain prone to self-hallucination and exhibit inconsistent performance [3]. 

3 Pipeline: MedFabric dataset and EtHER detector 

Existing medical hallucination datasets provide limited and low-quality cover-age of fabrication cases, with only 0.5% of the MedHallu corpus involving such instances [28]. Fabrications represent a distinct data distribution, reflected by poor performance from detectors trained mainly on non-fabrication hallucina-tions when evaluating fabrication-specific samples (Table 3). This shows the need for a fabrication-focused benchmark, which we introduce as MedFabric (Sec. 3.1). However, with this new fabrication-focused benchmark, we observe that most existing fabrication detectors suffer from significant performance degrada-tion, which we hypothesize as embedding models’ inability to accurately dis-tinguish between subtle word-level fabrications (Sec. 3.2), hence suggesting the need of a word-level detection pipeline EtHER in response to MedFabric .

These findings suggest that existing hallucination detectors largely overfit to stylistic and structural patterns instead of learning factual consistency. Such over-reliance results in three key limitations, especially in the medical domain where factual precision is emphasized: 1. Low fabrication retrieval recall. Existing models consistently exhibit poor recall for fabricated samples relative to their performance on ground truths. This asymmetric performance poses unignorable risks for medical use cases, as undetected fabrications may propagate misinformation in diagnostic or clinical contexts [29]; 2. Embedding-based detectors fail on word-level fabrications. For highly structurally similar datasets such as MedFabric , embedding-based approaches cannot effectively distinguish between fabrications and ground truths (Fig. 5) as both texts exhibit high embedding similarity. In the contrary, word-level detectors such as zero-shot LLM-based evaluators achieve more balanced and accurate results (Fig. 2) [2,23], and; 3. Instability of zero-shot LLM evaluations. LLM-as-a-judge systems [7] are typically sensitive to sampling randomness [31] on OOD samples, due to information loss in discrete rating systems and misalignment of data dis-tribution between training and testing data. This indicates the need for a repeatable and generalisable evaluation framework. To address these challenges, we propose EtHER , a modular word-level fab-rication detector designed for stable and interpretable factuality assessment. 

EtHER consists of three core modules: (1) a Text2Table Decomposition 

module that converts long medical statements into structured entity–description tables to enhance semantic sensitivity 3.2; (2) a Word Masking and Fill-ing module that enables word-level factual alignment with retrieved evidence (Fig. 3.2; and (3) a Hybrid Sentence Pair Evaluation module that combines embedding-based and LLM-based reasoning to enhance judgment consistency and low-variance (Sec. 3.2). 

Text2Table Decomposition To improve detection sensitivity, this module converts unstructured medical statements into structured tables of biomedical entities with their contextual relations. The system extracts key entities from LLM responses and links each to its description (e.g., “TGFBI expression” ⇒

“unfavorable lymphatic recurrence” in Fig. 3). Complex sentences are decom-posed into multiple entity–context pairs, while pronouns are replaced with ex-plicit references to ensure self-contained rows. Unlike knowledge-graph triples, this decomposition preserves nuanced semantic dependencies without distort-ing meaning, yielding a structured, interpretable representation that supports precise downstream fabrication detection (Sec. 3.2). 

Word Masking and Filling To assess textual and semantic alignment be-tween LLM responses and retrieved evidence, we introduce a word-masking and knowledge-filling framework. From the entity–description table (Sec. 3.2), two complementary masked tables are generated by alternately masking distinct word sets so every word is hidden once. Hybrid Sentence Pair Evaluation To detect subtle word-level fabrications while minimizing LLM randomness in OOD samples, we introduce a hybrid eval-uation module that combines embedding-based similarity with LLM-based rea-soning. Sentence pairs from reconstructed tables (Sec. 3.2) are first encoded using open-source embedding models to measure semantic distance, with pairs exceed-ing a threshold flagged as inconsistent. These candidates are then re-evaluated by an LLM agent to distinguish factual conflicts from benign lexical variations. Final decisions are aggregated via a rule-based layer to determine response-level fabrication. This hybrid design merges the stability of embeddings with the in-terpretive precision of LLM reasoning, while preceding modules (Sec. 3.2, 3.2) ensure concise, self-contained inputs that reduce hallucination and multi-turn drift [21,16] even if initial inputs contain OOD behavior.

4.1 Outperformance in identifying subtle fabrications 

EtHER shows outperformance in detecting subtle fabrications compared to state-of-the-art baselines, especially in datasets with high structural similarity between fabricated and ground-truth samples. It retrieves nearly twice as many fabrications as the model average (68.6% vs. 34.8%) while maintaining compa-rable F1 performance on truthful answers (57.5% vs. 58.3%), yielding a higher overall F1 score (60.6% vs. 44.3%). All three zero-shot models, i.e. EtHER ,LLM-as-a-judge, and GCA, achieve balanced and robust results, outperforming fine-tuned detectors that overfit to stylistic artifacts rather than factual cues. In 10 Tung Sum Thomas et al. 

contrast, EtHER leverages LLMs’ general knowledge with a structured, word-level workflow to simplify domain-specific semantics into general reasoning space, enabling accurate and consistent detection of fine-grained fabrications. 

4.2 Robust generalization across different structural similarity 

EtHER maintains stable performance across datasets with varying structural similarity. As shown in Fig. 4, it achieves comparable performance with fine-tuned baselines, having about 70% accuracy across datasets with low ROUGE scores, i.e. the original MedHallu dataset [28] and a ground-truth-rewritten vari-ation. Fine-tuned models, however, perform well only at low similarity and de-grade sharply as similarity increases with the average F1 score dropping from 69.54% to 59.84% in MedHallu ground-truth-rewritten variation, and further to 42.10% in MedFabric . This pattern indicates overreliance on stylistic cues rather than factual grounding. In contrast, zero-shot models remain stable with 51.10 – 54.05% F1, while EtHER enhances generalizability through a structured workflow for consistently outperformance at 60.60 - 70.10%.

5 Summary and Future Work 

In this work, we address the challenge of detecting subtle, word-level fabrications in medical domain by introducing a unified data-model framework designed to advance fabrication detection beyond stylistic pattern. This includes a novel generation pipeline that results in MedFabric and a word-level fabrication de-tector EtHER . MedFabric is generated by a data-centric pipeline that rewrites human-authored answers with LLMs, constructs realistic fabrications condition-ing on evidence and enforces structural and semantic similarity through itera-tive quality control. Building on this foundation, EtHER serves as a word-level fabrication detector that integrates structured reasoning and factual alignment into LLM evaluation, containing a Text2Table Decomposition module to convert long-form medical statements into semantically rich and interpretable tables, a Word Masking and Filling module to perform fine-grained factual consistency checks with retrieved knowledge, and a Hybrid Sentence Pair Evaluation module to integrate embedding-based evaluation with LLM-based reasoning for stable Title Suppressed Due to Excessive Length 11 

and generalisable judgments. Together, these innovations motivate robust detec-tion of subtle factual inconsistencies, and outperform existing detectors across datasets of different structural similarity. This work not only bridges the gap between linguistic realism and factual rigor in hallucination benchmarks, but also establishes a scalable foundation for trustworthy hallucination detection for realistic, domain-specific LLM use cases.

This section provides formal definitions for rewriting (App. A) and fabrication (App. A) from Sec. 3.1. We denote by Q, K and X the space of medical questions, retrieved knowledge sources, and natural-language answers respectively.

human ∈ X , the LLM rewriting operator fLLM : Q × K × X → X 

generates a rewritten ground truth ˆxgts LLM = fLLM (q, k, x gts 

human ), whose semantic content satisfies Content (ˆ xgts LLM ) ≈ Content (xgts 

human ) and remains entailed by the retrieved evidence ( k |= ˆ xgts LLM ).

(q, k ), the fabrication operator gLLM : Q × K × X → X produces a fabricated answer ˆxfab LLM = gLLM (q, k, ˆxgts LLM ), which diverges from factual correctness while preserving stylistic and structural similarity.
