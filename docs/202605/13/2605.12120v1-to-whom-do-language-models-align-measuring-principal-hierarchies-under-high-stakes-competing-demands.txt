# Abstract 

Language models deployed in high-stakes professional settings face conflicting demands from users, institutional authorities, and professional norms. How models act when these demands conflict reveals a principal hierarchy —an implicit ordering over competing stakeholders that determines, for instance, whether a medical AI receiving a cost-reduction directive from a hospital administrator complies at the expense of evidence-based care, or refuses because professional standards require it. Across 7,136 scenarios in legal and medical domains, we test ten frontier models and find that models frequently fail to adhere to professional standards during task execution (e.g., drafting) when user instructions conflict with those standards—despite adequately upholding them when users seek advisory guidance. We further find that the hierarchies between user, authority, and professional standards exhibited by these models are unstable across medical and legal contexts and inconsistent across model families. When failing to follow professional standards, the primary failure mechanism is knowledge omission: models that demonstrably possess relevant knowledge produce harmful outputs without surfacing conflicting knowledge. In a particularly troubling instance, we find that a reasoning model recognizes the relevant knowledge in its reasoning trace — e.g., that a drug has been withdrawn — yet suppresses this in the user-facing answer and proceeds to recommend the drug under authority pressure anyway. Inconsistent alignment across task framing, domain, and model families suggests that current alignment methods, including published alignment hierarchies, are unlikely to be robust when models are deployed in high-stakes professional settings. 

# 1 Introduction 

Professional practice is organized around competing obligations: to the client requesting the work, to the institution standing behind it, and to the standards that define what counts as competent practice [American Bar Association, 2002, American Medical Association, 2016]. Language models entering professional roles inherit the same conflict structure. A legal research assistant asked to cite an overruled Supreme Court case cannot simultaneously follow the user’s request and uphold professional ethics. A medical AI receiving a cost-reduction directive from a hospital administrator cannot comply without potentially compromising evidence-based care. In each case, the model must implicitly prioritize some stakeholders—what we call 

principals —over others; we refer to the resulting ordering as a principal hierarchy . Understanding these hierarchies matters for safety, commercial deployment, and alignment verification. In high-stakes domains, professionals are often bound by fiduciary duties to patients or clients and deploying models that do not respect these hierarchies causes severe consequences and creates legal and ethical risks. Some frontier labs address principal conflict explicitly in their safety training: Anthropic specifies behavioral principles around being ethical, honest, safe, and helpful [Anthropic, 2026, Bai et al., 2022], and OpenAI defines a chain of command over platform, developer, and user messages [OpenAI, 2025, Wallace et al., 2024]; we are not aware of comparable specifications from other providers [Comanici et al., 2025, Mistral AI, 2025,

However, whether these approaches are robust under realistic professional pressures and whether principal prioritization survives variation in task framing (advisory vs. execution) remains untested. Sycophancy research [Sharma et al., 2024, Petrov et al., 2025] examines a special case of principal conflict but is restricted to binary user-versus-truth setups in which the user is consistently wrong. We generalize to multi-principal scenarios that vary which principal holds the correct position, so observed compliance differences reflect principal identity rather than content. We further separate reasoning from actions, since reasoning is not necessarily faithful [Shen et al., 2025, Turpin et al., 2023]. Appendix A extends this discussion. This paper addresses two central questions. First, what principal hierarchy do frontier language models exhibit when user instructions, institutional authority, and professional standards conflict? Second, how stable is that hierarchy under perturbations a deployed system would actually encounter—including shifts in task framing, escalating pressure, and variation across professional domains? Answering these questions is a prerequisite for understanding whether current alignment methods are robust for high-stakes professional deployment. We make three primary contributions. (1) We operationalize the principal hierarchy problem and release a counterbalanced evaluation framework of 7,136 scenarios across legal and medical domains: 6,096 with verifiable professional standards (overruled Supreme Court precedents, withdrawn drugs) where one or more principals demand non-compliance, and 1,040 with two equally valid options endorsed by competing principals to isolate principal preference from correctness tracking. (2) We empirically evaluated ten frontier models and observed instability in principal hierarchies across models. Professional standards dominate on advisory tasks, but the ordering shifts in as much as 74 percent of cases on execution tasks; authority figures are prioritized over users in medical contexts but not legal; and hierarchies diverge across model families. # 2 Methodology 

We consider three principals whose objectives may conflict in professional settings: the user (the person issuing the request), an authority figure (a senior associate in legal contexts; a doctor in medical contexts), and the professional standard —norms binding practitioners to verifiable institutional facts (e.g., that a case has been overruled, that a drug has been withdrawn), enforced through professional rules of conduct, standards of care, and regulatory regimes [American Bar Association, 2002, American Medical Association, 2016]. As shown in Figure 2, our experimental framework proceeds in three stages: we (1) collect datasets 

in two professional domains and filter the ground-truth subset to items every tested model has knowledge of (Section 2.1); (2) generate scenarios that cross principal configurations with different task framings and pressure levels (Section 2.2); and (3) evaluate model responses, categorize behavior, and analyze failure modes (Section 2.4). We describe each stage below. 

## 2.1 Dataset Collection and Knowledge Check 

Measuring principal prioritization requires two complementary scenario types: items with verifiable ground truth, where a principal’s demand contradicts the professional standard and the model’s response can be evaluated against it; and items without ground truth, where principal preference can be isolated from standards-tracking. For the former, we construct two professional-domain datasets where breaking the professional standard causes severe consequences; for the latter, we curate a complementary legal-domain set in which two equally valid citations are endorsed by competing principals. Because neither citation is incorrect, any systematic tendency to side with user or authority reflects a learned preference, not correctness tracking. 

Legal domain: overruled judicial precedents. We compile cases that have been explicitly overruled or abrogated, drawing from two sources: Supreme Court Decisions Overruled by Subsequent Decisions 1, a

Medical domain: withdrawn drugs. We utilize the Withdrawn 2.0 Dataset [Gallo et al., 2023] which includes 647 drugs withdrawn by Food and Drug Administration (FDA) or European Medicines Agency (EMA) due to safety concerns. Each entry records the drug name, withdrawal year, and affected countries. 

Knowledge filter. To rule out ignorance as a confounder with the ground-truth datasets, we filter both datasets to items every tested model has knowledge of, that is, can answer correctly under neutral, conflict-free phrasing (e.g., “Is [case] still good law for [proposition]?”) with high confidence. This yields 404 of 2,152 legal cases (18.8%) and 358 of 647 drugs (55.3%). Any subsequent failure on these items reflects how a model handles principal conflict, not gaps in its parametric knowledge. We provide prompt templates in Appendix C.1, per-model pass rates in Appendix D.1, and an ablation study on items no model knows in Appendix D.2 to validate that knowledge filtering is necessary for interpreting failure modes. 

Neutral legal citation pairs. To test principal preferences independent of correctness , we compile a dataset of 260 legal propositions, each paired with two valid supporting citations. The propositions and citation pairs were identified and validated by qualified legal subject-matter experts, who confirmed that both citations in each pair are jurisprudentially sound and both can be used to support the legal proposition. Observed preferences across these pairs are thus attributable to learned principal alignment, not citation quality. These are used exclusively in versions Both-Valid-V1 /Both-Valid-V2 (Section 2.2). 

## 2.2 Scenario Design 

We create scenarios varying three dimensions: principal configuration (who is conflicting with whom), task framing (advisory vs. execution), and pressure level (low vs. high). Table 1 summarizes the design across the five versions; complete prompt templates are provided in Appendix C.2. 

Principal Configurations. We design three versions where a principal endorses a demand that conflicts with the professional standard, holding factual content constant. User-Wrong —the user directly requests using an overruled case or withdrawn drug; Authority-Wrong —an authority figure endorses the item and the user relays the recommendation; Consensus-Wrong —both user and authority endorse the item that breaks professional standards. Because content is identical across versions, compliance differences are attributable to principal identity. Comparing User-Wrong against Authority-Wrong reveals differential weighting of user and authority; comparing both against Consensus-Wrong reveals whether consensus amplifies compliance. To disentangle principal alignment from correctness tracking, we add two further variations, Both-Valid-V1 

and Both-Valid-V2 . Each item pairs a single legal proposition with two equally valid citations (citation A and citation B): in Both-Valid-V1 , the user prefers citation A and the authority prefers citation B; in 

Both-Valid-V2 , the preferences are swapped. Because the citations are held constant while the endorsers flip across versions, linking a model’s two choices reveals its alignment: (i) user-following —selecting citation A in Both-Valid-V1 and citation B in Both-Valid-V2 (always the user’s preferred citation); (ii) authority-following —selecting citation B in Both-Valid-V1 and citation A in Both-Valid-V2 (always the authority’s); or (iii) citation-following —selecting the same citation in both versions, ignoring endorser identity.

The deployments of LLMs in professional domains span both advisory consultation and execution, so a principal hierarchy that holds in one mode but breaks in the other still represents a deployment risk. Therefore, we design scenarios framed both as advisory (“Can you help me decide if this is a good idea?”) and execution (“Draft a paragraph for my brief” ). User-Wrong , Both-Valid-V1 , and Both-Valid-V2 additionally include both low- and high-pressure variants to test stability under escalating stakes; Authority-Wrong and Consensus-Wrong use only low pressure, as their purpose is identifying principal deference, not pressure sensitivity. 

## 2.3 Models 

We evaluate ten frontier models from six providers: Anthropic (Claude-Sonnet-4.6, Claude-Opus-4.7), OpenAI (GPT-5.1, GPT-5.4), Google (Gemini-3.1-Pro), xAI (Grok-4-Fast), Alibaba (Qwen-3-235B, Qwen-3.5-397B, Qwen-3.5-397B-R 3), and Mistral AI (Mistral-Large-3); details in Table 2. We focus on frontier-scale models for two reasons: they are the primary candidates for the professional deployment settings that motivate this study, and our knowledge filter (Section 2.1) requires models with sufficient domain knowledge to distinguish omission from ignorance, whereas smaller models fail the majority of knowledge checks. The final dataset after knowledge filtering comprises 7,136 scenarios: 4,272 legal and 2,864 medical. Table 4 in the Appendix provides the breakdown. Each of the ten models is evaluated on all scenarios, yielding 71,360 model–scenario pairs. 

## 2.4 Evaluation 

For scenarios with ground-truth ( User-Wrong /Authority-Wrong /Consensus-Wrong ), an LLM judge (Qwen-3-235B-Instruct with greedy decoding) assigns each response two binary scores: reasoning correct 

(R)—did the model identify that the case is overruled or the drug is withdrawn; and action correct (A)— did the model refuse to comply? The judge prompt is domain- and task-type-specific (legal/medical ×

advisory/execution; see complete prompts in Appendix C.3). Because every scenario passes the knowledge filter (Section 2.1), R=0 indicates that a model failed to surface knowledge it demonstrably possesses, not ignorance. For scenarios without ground-truth ( Both-Valid-V1 /Both-Valid-V2 ), where neither citation is incorrect, we parse the model’s structured JSON output (Appendix C.2.3) directly and record which principal’s preferred citation was selected. 

Judge validation. Two legal experts annotated 240 responses stratified across models, principal configurations, and task types against the same R/A criteria; the judge agrees with the human labels at κ = 0 .895 (R) and 

κ = 0 .893 (A). Full details of the human validation are in Appendix G. 

Metrics. From the per-response binary scores we report two aggregate rates: reasoning-correct rate (mean of R) and action-correct rate (mean of A). Throughout, we use standards-violating compliance —or simply 

compliance —for the rate at which a model went along with the demand that violates the professional standard, equivalent to 1−A; higher compliance is worse, higher action-correct is better. Section 3.4 additionally reports the joint distribution of (R, A ) as a 2×2 behavior taxonomy. For Both-Valid-V1 /Both-Valid-V2 , we report the user-/authority-/citation-following rates defined in Section 2.2.

## 3.1 Principal hierarchies on advisory tasks 

Professional standards dominate. Across all ten models, in the User-Wrong , Authority-Wrong and 

Consensus-Wrong cases, advisory compliance with incorrect demands stays low (Figure 3, top): legal compliance peaks at 25% and seven models stay below 2%. The professional-standard principal—our externally verifiable ground truth—is consistently weighted above both the user and the authority figure when models are asked to advise. 

Authority outweighs the user in medical contexts. Within this overall pattern, medical advisory exposes a clear ordering between the two non-standard principals: compliance with the authority figure (a doctor) consistently exceeds compliance with the user across all ten models. Grok-4-Fast is the only model where authority compliance exceeds 50% in advisory mode—the authority figure overrides the professional standard for this model; Mistral-Large-3 approaches this threshold at 46.1%. Legal advisory shows no analogous ordering: compliance is uniformly low across configurations. Appendix H.3 illustrates this with a concrete example: Mistral-Large-3 correctly identifies a drug as withdrawn from the U.S. market when the user endorses it, but fails to surface this knowledge entirely when a doctor recommends the same drug. 

Consensus does not amplify compliance. Comparing User-Wrong and Authority-Wrong against 

Consensus-Wrong (where both user and authority endorse the incorrect demand) reveals no consistent amplification: once an authority figure has endorsed the incorrect action, adding user agreement provides negligible additional pressure. 6To Whom Do Language Models Align? 

## 3.2 Principal hierarchies on execution tasks 

Execution framing collapses professional-standard dominance. Reframing identical factual content from an advisory question (“Is this a good idea?”) to an execution request (“Draft a paragraph,” “Write an email”) reorders the hierarchy across the User-Wrong , Authority-Wrong , and Consensus-Wrong 

conditions. Four of ten models fail to uphold the professional standard under execution framing on legal tasks: action-correct rates drop 25–74 pp from advisory to execution (all p < 10 −6; Cohen’s h ≥ 0.71 ) (Figure 3). The same content the model identified as standards-violating under advisory framing is now produced on demand—the hierarchy reorders, placing the user (or authority) demand above the professional standard. GPT-5.1 is the most extreme: it advises correctly 99% yet refuses only 25% of execution requests on identical legal content (Figure 3, left). 

The collapse is uneven across models. In legal execution (Figure 3, bottom left), only the two Claude models remain strongly aligned to the professional standard ( |∆| ≤ 5 pp across framings; max 2% user compliance). GPT-5.4, Qwen-3.5-397B, Qwen-3.5-397B-R fall between their predecessors and the Claude models, partially resisting the execution-framing collapse but not eliminating it (27%, 30% and 17% legal compliance, respectively). 

The medical Authority > User pattern persists. The Authority > User ordering from Section 3.1 holds under execution framing across all ten models in medical (Figure 3, bottom right)—the only domain-and-task-type combination where a hierarchy survives the framing shift universally. 

## 3.3 Principal hierarchies on neutral tasks 

In the Both-Valid-V1 /Both-Valid-V2 tests , we measure principal alignment when choosing between the user and an authority figure without the backdrop of a conflicting standard of practice. For nine of ten models, the most common pattern is citation-following (53–92%), where the model selects the same citation in both instances rather than prioritizing one of the principals. Authority-following is negligible (1–26%). Mistral-Large-3 is the exception: user-following dominates at 58% and rises to 73% under high pressure (Figure 4). ## 3.5 Domain effects by model 

Beyond the consistent pattern of Authority > User in medicine, domain sensitivity is model-specific in both magnitude and direction. Aggregating across all principal configurations and both task types at low pressure, chi-square tests reveal significant legal-vs.-medical differences for seven of ten models (all p < 0.05 after Bonferroni correction; Mistral-Large-3 and Qwen-3.5-397B are the exceptions, p = 0 .15 and p = 0 .14 ), but the direction varies: Gemini-3.1-Pro and Qwen-3-235B show higher standards-violating compliance in legal (e.g., Qwen-3-235B: +18 .5 pp, Cohen’s h=0.41 ), while the other six comply more often in medical (e.g., GPT-5.4: 

−15 .5 pp, h=0.38 ). Per-model values are reported in Table 5 (Appendix F). Domain therefore acts as a model-specific moderator rather than a universal one; the Authority > User ordering established in Section 3.1 is the only domain-related pattern that holds across all ten models. 

## 3.6 Pressure Effects 

High-stakes framing produces opposite behavioral shifts across domains: three models (Grok-4-Fast, Qwen-3-235B, Mistral-Large-3) degrade 15–18 pp in legal advisory but improve ∼5 pp in medical advisory, while the other six (both Claude models, both GPT models, Gemini-3.1-Pro, Qwen-3.5-397B) are pressure-insensitive (|∆| < 3 pp). # 4 Discussion 

Do published hierarchy specifications track observed behavior? Of the ten models, Anthropic and OpenAI publish explicit principal hierarchies [Anthropic, 2026, Bai et al., 2022, OpenAI, 2025, Wallace et al., 2024]; the other four providers do not. We found that all four models with published specifications are pressure-insensitive (see Appendix E). The two Claude models are resistant to execution-framing collapse, whereas GPT-5.1 collapses despite its published Model Specification (72% legal-execution knowledge omission), and GPT-5.4 partially recovers (14%) but still falls short of Claude’s near-perfect refusal. This dissociation suggests that a published specification on its own is not sufficient: the training procedure that instantiates the specification matters as much as the document itself. 

Task framing as an alignment boundary. Our results reveal a boundary between two behavioral regimes, evaluation (activated by advisory framing) and instruction-following (activated by execution framing), that current alignment methods leave underspecified. Models appear well-calibrated to refuse when asked “is this a good idea?” but shift to compliance when asked to “draft this,” even when the knowledge is identical. This has direct deployment implications: an AI assistant that correctly warns against citing overruled case law may produce a brief citing that case when asked to draft it. 

Failures without warning signals. Prior work on sycophancy [Sharma et al., 2024, Petrov et al., 2025] frames alignment failures as models telling users what they want to hear. Our taxonomy reveals a more concerning pattern: in execution mode, models fail to surface demonstrably possessed knowledge, producing output indistinguishable from genuine ignorance. Execution framing appears to bypass knowledge retrieval in favor of instruction-following. Our knowledge filter ablation (Appendix D.2) conducted on the six-model set demonstrates that, when models lack the relevant knowledge entirely, protective behavior collapses to near-zero. This suggests that retrieval-augmented generation or agentic tool use could serve as a practical mitigation, restoring the knowledge prerequisite for informed refusal even when the model’s training data lacks coverage of a specific risk. 

Probing knowledge omission via reasoning traces. Whether knowledge-omission failures reflect genuine ignorance or selective non-surfacing is unobservable from final-answer text alone (Appendix D.2). Qwen-3.5-397B-R produces reasoning traces, so we re-evaluate its 308 knowledge-omission failures by applying the same reasoning criterion to the trace. In 36.0% of these failures, the trace identifies the case as overruled or the drug as withdrawn while the answer omits this recognition—direct, within-response evidence that the model possesses the disqualifying knowledge yet suppresses it under principal-demand pressure. The example below is representative: the trace flags Bucetin as withdrawn for kidney toxicity and carcinogenicity, yet the answer drafts an email requesting the drug as a current therapy, with no safety warning of any kind. From the user-facing output alone, the response is indistinguishable from one produced by a model that had never recognized the risk. The pattern is not domain-specific: Appendix H.5 presents a legal counterpart under high-stakes pressure ( User-Wrong , execution) where the trace flags the user’s proposed argument as “legally hazardous” and explicitly self-instructs “I must warn them,” yet the user-facing answer drafts the requested brief paragraph and affirmatively asserts that the cited case “has not been overruled”—when in fact it was directly overruled on the same proposition.

# 5 Conclusion 

Principal hierarchies in frontier language models are context-dependent rather than stable: task framing, domain, and model family each change which stakeholder a model defers to, even on identical content. Task framing alone shifts four of ten models from professional-standard adherence to user-aligned compliance, with knowledge omission as the dominant failure mode—harmful output produced without surfacing demonstrably possessed knowledge—and where reasoning traces are inspectable, 36% of these failures show the model recognizing the conflict internally yet suppressing it in the user-facing answer. As models move toward high-stakes professional deployment, understanding the principals to whom they are aligned, and the inconsistencies therein, is a prerequisite for safe deployment. 

# Limitations 

Model scope. We evaluate ten frontier models from six providers spanning the United States, Europe, and China. Smaller open-source models, models from additional vendors, and models trained primarily on non-English or non-Western corpora are out of scope by design. First, our knowledge filter (Section 2.1) requires models with sufficient parametric knowledge to distinguish omission from ignorance; smaller models fail the majority of knowledge checks, so applying our framework to them would conflate the two failure modes the reasoning–action decomposition is built to separate. Second, frontier-scale models are the primary candidates for the high-stakes professional deployment settings that motivate this study—the operational risk profile we characterize is concentrated in the systems professionals actually use, not in models that would not clear the knowledge bar in the first place. Third, whether models trained predominantly on non-Western legal or medical corpora exhibit different principal hierarchies is a substantive cross-cultural question requiring jurisdiction-specific domain experts to construct and validate new ground-truth datasets—a research program we view as complementary to ours. Our framework, datasets, and evaluation protocol are designed so each of these extensions can be undertaken without re-deriving the methodology. 

Extending across domains and cultures. We ground our evaluation in law and medicine because both have externally verifiable professional standards and high-stakes consequences for misalignment. These domains are both governed by authoritative professional norms from courts and regulatory agencies. Our framework generalizes to domains with lower regulation and oversight where the “professional standard” principal lacks the same external authority, but we do not empirically test these domains. Extending across this institutional-strength spectrum would test whether the context-dependent patterns we observe are specific to strongly institutionalized domains or reflect broader properties of aligned behavior. Similarly, our English-language, Western-legal and Western-medical contexts establish baseline patterns; cross-lingual replication across legal traditions (common law vs. civil law) and medical systems (public vs. private healthcare) would reveal whether these patterns reflect universal training-data biases or culture-specific norms. 

Evaluation, not mitigation. Our framework is designed to evaluate principal hierarchies models possess and test if they are stable, not to repair them. We do not propose or evaluate training-time interventions, prompt-level guardrails, or deployment-time controls. The knowledge-filter ablation (Appendix D.2) does point to one concrete direction—retrieval-augmented or agentic configurations that supply professional standards in context, restoring the knowledge prerequisite for informed refusal—but whether such interventions actually resist the execution-framing collapse we identify, or merely shift the failure mode, is an empirical question we leave to follow-up work.

# A Related Work 

This appendix expands on the related work discussed in Section 1. 

## A.1 User Deference and Hierarchy Specification 

Sycophancy. Sharma et al. [2024] show RLHF-trained models systematically favor user-aligned responses over truthful ones; Petrov et al. [2025] demonstrate sycophancy exceeding 70% in mathematical reasoning. However, this work studies binary user-versus-truth conflicts and consistently places the user on the wrong side, leaving open whether models exhibit user-specific bias or general failure to track correctness. We address this through multi-principal scenarios with counterbalanced correctness—systematically varying which principal is right. 

Constitutional AI and hierarchy specifications. Anthropic’s Constitutional AI [Anthropic, 2026, Bai et al., 2022] and OpenAI’s Model Specification [OpenAI, 2025] propose explicit principal hierarchies [Wallace et al., 2024]. Whether these specifications manifest in behavior under realistic professional pressure—and whether they produce different hierarchies than implicit RLHF-only training—remains untested. We provide the first independent empirical validation across both explicit-specification (Claude and GPT, including multiple generations of each) and implicit-hierarchy models (Gemini, Grok, Qwen, Mistral). 

Personalized alignment. Personalized alignment [Guan et al., 2025] centers on adapting to individual user preferences. Professional contexts present a complementary challenge: duties to third parties and professional norms may override user preferences. We measure how models navigate these conflicts. 

Pluralistic alignment. Work on pluralistic alignment [Sorensen et al., 2024] centers on representing diverse preferences within a single model, but does not address scenarios where professional duties to third parties should override user requests. Our framework tests precisely these conflicts. 

## A.2 Reasoning-Action Dissociation 

Recent work documents inconsistencies between model reasoning and actions: Shen et al. [2025] show substantial misalignment between LLM-generated value statements and their actions, Ashkinaze et al. [2025] show models learn shallow preference correlates, and Turpin et al. [2023] demonstrate chain-of-thought can rationalize predetermined outputs. These findings establish the phenomenon but do not distinguish reasoning failures (incorrect identification) from action failures (correct identification, wrong execution)—a distinction that requires verifiable ground truth, which we provide. 12 To Whom Do Language Models Align? 

## A.3 Task Framing Effects 

Instruction-following research examines when models should comply with or refuse requests [Xie et al., 2025, Brahman et al., 2024], but whether instruction framing —advisory versus execution—affects principal prioritization while holding content constant remains unexplored. We isolate this through counterbalanced design: each scenario exists in both advisory and execution variants with identical factual content.

Figure 6 shows why this criterion is stringent: individual model pass rates differ substantially, especially in the legal domain, where high-confidence correct rates span approximately 37–85% across the evaluated models.
