# PHAGE: Patent Heterogeneous Attention-Guided Graph Encoder for Representation Learning 

Yongmin Yoo, Qiongkai Xu, Zhangkai Wu, Longbing Cao 

Frontier AI Research Centre, Macquarie University School of Computing, FSE, Macquarie University

Abstract 

Patent claims form a directed dependency struc-ture in which dependent claims inherit and re-fine the scope of earlier claims; however, ex-isting patent encoders linearize claims as text and discard this hierarchy. Directly encod-ing this structure into self-attention poses two challenges: claim dependencies mix relation types that differ in semantics and extraction reliability, and the dependency graph is de-fined over claims while Transformers attend over tokens. PHAGE addresses the first chal-lenge through a deterministic graph construc-tion pipeline that separates near-deterministic legal citations from noisier rule-based technical relations, preserving type distinctions as hetero-geneous edges. It addresses the second through a connectivity mask and learnable relation-aware biases that lift claim-level topology into token-level attention, allowing the encoder to differentially weight each relation type. A dual-granularity contrastive objective then aligns representations with both inter-patent taxon-omy and intra-patent topology. PHAGE outper-forms all baselines on classification, retrieval, and clustering, showing that intra-document claim topology is a stronger inductive bias than inter-document structure and that this bias per-sists in the encoder weights after training. 

1 Introduction 

In the legal and technical landscape of intellec-tual property, patent claims are not a flat sequence; rather, they form a complex hierarchy where de-pendent claims inherit and refine earlier claims to define the precise scope of an invention. Through explicit claim references, dependent claims inherit limitations from antecedent claims and add nar-rower technical detail, yielding a directed depen-dency structure (Krestel et al., 2021). This means that claims that are adjacent in text are not neces-sarily the most strongly related, while claims that are distant in the document may be directly con-nected through dependency relations. As the volume of patent filings continues to grow (WIPO, 2024), accurate patent retrieval and classification become increasingly impor-tant. Existing patent encoders, including BERT-for-Patents (Srebrovic and Yonamine, 2020) and PatentBERT (Lee and Hsiang, 2020), have im-proved downstream performance by capturing the distributional semantics of technical vocabu-lary. However, these models still primarily encode claims as sequential representations, overlooking the hierarchical dependency structure that defines an invention’s legal boundaries. Prior work has enriched patent representations with external signals such as IPC codes and cita-tion networks (Li et al., 2022; Ghosh et al., 2024), and explored graph-based architectures and section-level augmentation (Björkqvist and Kallio, 2023; Zuo et al., 2025). However, these approaches in-corporate structure at the document level or as auxiliary input, rather than injecting claim-level dependencies directly into the encoder’s compu-tational flow. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • Relation-aware patent encoding. We pro-pose PHAGE, a Transformer encoder that in-corporates a heterogeneous claim dependency graph directly into the self-attention mecha-nism. This allows claim representations to be guided by and conditioned on distinct explicit and implicit relation types. • Deterministic graph construction for claim structure. We introduce a deterministic pipeline for building a heterogeneous claim graph from claims, combining explicit claim references with rule-based technical links to reduce reliance on learned edge prediction. • Dual-granularity structural supervi-sion. We propose a contrastive objective that jointly uses patent-level supervision and claim-level structure to improve representation learning. Beyond these methodological contributions, our experiments reveal that intra-document claim topol-ogy provides a stronger inductive bias than cross-patent citation structure, that heterogeneous edge typing induces depth-wise specialization in the en-coder without layer-level supervision, and that the resulting structural bias is internalized into the model weights and persists at inference without graph construction. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Transformer-based Patent Representation Architectures 

To address the complex syntax and vocabulary of technical literature, various Pre-trained Language Models (PLMs) tailored for the patent domain have been introduced. A foundational approach involves pre-training from scratch; for instance, BERT-for-Patents (Srebrovic and Yonamine, 2020) leveraged over 100 million patent documents to capture the distributional semantics of technical terms, estab-lishing a strong baseline. Other strategies focus on domain adaptation via fine-tuning. PatentBERT (Lee and Hsiang, 2020) optimized BERT-base specifically on claims for classification, while SciBERT (Beltagy et al., 2019) demonstrated effective transferability from scien-tific text, often enhanced by linguistically informed masking (Althammer et al., 2021). More recently, efficiency-oriented models have emerged: PatentS-BERTa (Bekamiri et al., 2024) improved sentence representations for similarity search using silver labels, and ModernBERT has been adapted to pro-cess long-context technical disclosures more effi-ciently (Yousefiramandi and Cooney, 2025). Despite these advancements, a critical limitation persists: conventional PLMs predominantly treat patent documents as linear text sequences. These models rely on token-level objectives that overlook the intrinsic topological structure of patents, failing to explicitly model the directed acyclic graph of claim citations and neglecting the non-sequential logical dependencies that define legal boundaries. 

2.2 Graph Representation Learning in Patent Analysis 

To incorporate structural information beyond tex-tual statistics, recent research has pivoted towards graph-based methodologies in patent analysis. A dominant stream focuses on modeling the macro-level citation network to refine document embed-dings. For instance, PaECTER (Ghosh et al., 2024) and SearchFormer (Vowinckel and Hähnke, 2023) utilize citation links as supervision signals, train-ing models to pull cited patents closer in the vec-tor space. More recently, PAI-NET (Lee and Bai, 2025) extended this by incorporating prior art re-lationships and technical field hierarchies into a retrieval-augmented generation framework, further enhancing semantic search capabilities. Beyond citation networks, graph-based indices linking claims to descriptions have been proposed for more structured retrieval (Björkqvist and Kallio, 2023). However, these methodologies primarily treat the patent document as a single node within a larger network or focus on external connectivity. They largely overlook the intra-document topology, specifically the hierarchical dependency among claims, which is critical for delineating the precise legal scope of an invention. 

2.3 Structure-Aware Encoding and Data Augmentation 

Recent innovations seek to bridge the semantic gap by exploiting granular intra-document features. A prominent direction involves Data Augmenta-tion (DA) strategies to inject structural priors. No-tably, section-level augmentation has been intro-duced to leverage the distinct discourse roles of abstracts, claims, and descriptions, mitigating the embedding over-dispersion problem inherent in contrastive learning (Zuo et al., 2025). Similarly, PatentMind (Yoo et al., 2025) characterizes patents as multi-aspect reasoning graphs to enhance simi-larity evaluation. Some works have attempted to model claim de-pendencies using specialized graph networks. For example, the FLAN Graph (Gao et al., 2024) cap-tures fine-grained claim dependencies to improve patent approval prediction, while HGM-Net (Song et al., 2025) integrates hierarchical contrastive learning with multimodal graph attention networks for feature fusion. Although general graph-guided architectures like GraphBERT (Zhang et al., 2020) exist in the broader NLP domain, they typically rely on static syntactic graphs and lack the capacity to resolve the latent technical couplings unique to patent claims. Consequently, prior patent-specific works largely operate through input-level augmen-tation, separate graph modules, or post-hoc mod-eling. Crucially, they neglect to inject the fine-grained claim topology directly into the encoder’s computational flow as an inductive bias, a gap that our proposed PHAGE framework aims to bridge by internalizing the legal hierarchy within the at-tention mechanism. 

3 Methodology 

We propose the Patent Heterogeneous Attention-Guided Graph Encoder (PHAGE), a representa-tion learning framework that transforms patent claims from linear text sequences into topologically grounded representations. A complete summary of key notations is provided in Appendix ?? . Our pipeline operates in three stages. First, we con-struct a heterogeneous Claim Dependency Graph (CDG) via a deterministic multi-signal extraction pipeline that captures explicit legal citations, tech-nical term flows, and functional couplings as dis-tinct edge types. Second, we inject this CDG into the self-attention mechanism of a pre-trained en-coder. By applying independently learnable biases conditioned on each edge type, this relation-aware attention mechanism modulates information flow according to dependency semantics. Finally, we refine the representations using a dual-granularity contrastive objective that bridges inter-document technological similarity with intra-document claim topology, where the claim-level alignment signals are weighted by learnable, relation-specific param-eters that allow the model to calibrate the contribu-tion of each relation type during training. 

3.1 Construction of Heterogeneous Claim Dependency Graph

The dependencies within this structure, however, are fundamentally heterogeneous: explicit legal ci-tations, term-level anaphoric references, and func-tional couplings each carry distinct semantics and varying extraction reliability. Explicit citation edges ( rcite ) are near-deterministic, as they are de-rived from legally mandated reference patterns, whereas implicit signals are inherently suscepti-ble to extraction noise. 5 Discussion 

The results establish three findings that, to our knowledge, have not been previously demonstrated in patent representation learning. 

Finding 1: Intra-document claim topology provides a stronger inductive bias than inter-document citation structure. PHAGE surpasses citation-aware baselines across all three tasks de-spite using only within-patent claim dependencies, whereas those baselines leverage cross-patent cita-tion graphs spanning millions of examiner-verified links. The ablation controls for the confound of contrastive training: No Graph , which applies the same setup without structural injection, still under-performs citation-aware models (0.582 vs. 0.630 PatEmbed-Base Ma-F1). The gains therefore origi-nate from claim-level topology itself, which legally partitions inventive scope through explicit inher-itance and narrowing at a resolution that whole-patent citation proximity cannot provide. 

Finding 2: Heterogeneous edge typing induces depth-wise specialization without layer-level supervision. The learned attention biases b(l,r )

(Section C) exhibit consistent stratification: layers 0–9 assign higher biases to implicit relations ( rterm ,

rfunc ) than to citations, while layers 18–23 suppress all cross-claim biases and amplify the intra-claim bias. This follows from gradient flow through typed adjacency: lower layers receive stronger gradients from abundant implicit edges, while upper layers, closer to pooling, optimize for within-claim co-herence. The ablation corroborates this: remov-ing B causes larger degradation than uniform wr,confirming that specialization is realized through attention rather than the loss. 

Finding 3: Topology-guided training reshapes encoder weights beyond the training regime. 

PHAGE trains with CDG-guided attention but performs inference without it, yet retains nearly all gains over w/o both (0.635 vs. 0.586 Ma-F1). The connectivity mask, blocking roughly 80% of cross-claim paths, forces the encoder to develop dependency-aligned routing patterns that persist af-ter removal. Any new patent can thus be encoded in a single forward pass without graph construction. 

6 Conclusion 

We presented PHAGE, a patent encoder that in-jects claim-level dependency structure into Trans-former self-attention via a heterogeneous claim graph, relation-aware biases, and a dual-granularity contrastive objective. Under a frozen-encoder pro-tocol, PHAGE consistently outperforms domain-adapted and citation-aware baselines across clas-sification, retrieval, and clustering. Our results demonstrate that the structural bottleneck in patent representation learning lies within patents rather than between them, and that this inductive bias is permanently internalized into encoder weights without requiring graph construction at inference. This principle extends to any domain where docu-ments encode latent hierarchical dependencies that sequential encoding discards. Limitations 

While the term-flow extraction relies on English article conventions (e.g., transitions from "a" to "the" or "said"), its overall impact is quantitatively minor and architecturally isolated. Adapting to lan-guages without articles, such as Chinese, Japanese, and Korean, requires alternative lexical heuristics for identifying definition loci. However, this spe-cific component ( rterm ) constitutes only 7.9% of the CDG edges. The remaining 92.1% depend on structural patterns and verb lexicons that are highly transferable across languages. Crucially, the core PHAGE architecture remains completely language-agnostic. Since the connectivity mask and relation-aware biases operate purely on abstract claim-level adjacency matrices, extending the model to non-English corpora requires updating only the surface-level extraction rules, leaving the entire encoding and training framework intact. PHAGE uses the CDG during training but per-forms inference with a standard forward pass, cre-ating a train-inference asymmetry in the attention mechanism. We deliberately adopt this design for two reasons. First, it preserves compatibility with the frozen-encoder evaluation protocol used throughout our experiments: embeddings can be extracted from any new patent without construct-ing a claim graph, which is critical for scalable deployment. Second, the strong performance un-der this protocol empirically demonstrates that the structural inductive bias from CDG-guided train-ing is successfully internalized into the model pa-rameters; the ablation gap between w/o both and PHAGE full (0.586 vs. 0.635 Ma-F1) confirms that the encoder retains the benefit of structural training even when the graph is absent at inference. Investi-gating whether CDG injection at inference yields additional gains remains an interesting direction for future work. References
