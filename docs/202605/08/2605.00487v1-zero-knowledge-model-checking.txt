## Abstract 

We introduce a technology to formally verify that a software system satisfies a temporal specification of functional correctness, with-out revealing the system itself. Our method combines a deductive approach to model checking to obtain a formal certificate of cor-rectness for the system, with zero-knowledge proofs to convince an external verifier that the system—kept secret—complies with its specification of correctness—made public. We consider proof certificates represented as ranking functions, and introduce both an explicit-state and a symbolic scheme for model checking in zero knowledge. Our explicit-state scheme assumes systems represented as transition graphs. We use polynomial commitments to convince the verifier that the public proof certificates correspond to the secret transition relation. Our symbolic scheme assumes systems speci-fied as linear guarded commands and uses piecewise-linear ranking functions. We apply Farkas’ lemma to obtain a witness for the va-lidity of the ranking function with public and secret components, and employ sigma protocols for matrix multiplication and range proofs to convince the verifier of the witness’s existence. We built a prototype to demonstrate the practical efficacy of our two schemes on linear temporal logic verification examples. Our technology en-ables formal verification in domains where both the safety and the confidentiality of the system under analysis are critical.

## 1 Introduction 

Model checking is the problem of determining whether a software, hardware, or cyber-physical system satisfies a formal specifica-tion of correctness. Unlike traditional testing, which is inherently non-exhaustive, model checking provides formal proof that the sys-tem behaves as intended [ 6, 10 , 18 ]. It is an established standard in electronic design automation, protocol verification, and control soft-ware development in domains where safety and security are highly critical. Moreover, it is increasingly viewed as a promising avenue for the safety assurance of AI systems. Formal proofs of correctness guarantee the absolute (or probabilistic) absence of violations with respect to a given specification, thereby enabling verifiable compli-ance with safety standards and fostering trust between technology providers, regulators, and users of such systems. Scalability has been a central focus of the formal methods com-munity for decades, yet the industrial adoption of model checking faces another pressing challenge: confidentiality. The traditional formal verification algorithms require disclosure of the system description in order to convince an external verifier of the sys-tem’s compliance with its specification. In a setting where formal verification is a requirement potentially involving multiple juris-dictions, standardisation bodies, or collaborating companies, the obligation to disclose a system description is likely to create tension between regulators’ or customers’ need to ensure compliance with safety requirements and providers’ desire to protect their intellec-tual property. To date, no technology exists that can provide an external verifier with a formal proof of safety and correctness in zero knowledge, without disclosing the system itself. Zero-knowledge proofs provide a cryptographic mechanism by which a prover can convince a verifier that a given statement is true without revealing any information beyond the validity of the statement itself. They are widely used in areas such as crypto-graphic protocol design, blockchain and cryptocurrency systems, privacy-preserving authentication and identity management, se-cure electronic voting, and confidential data access control. In the context of system verification and certification, zero-knowledge proofs offer a the opportunity to demonstrate compliance with for-mal specifications while preserving confidentiality, as correctness claims can be validated without disclosing system internals, propri-etary designs, or sensitive customer data, eliminating reliance on trust between parties. Despite their maturity and broad adoption in multiple domains, the application of zero-knowledge proofs to formal verification remains rare [53, 55, 56]. We introduce the first approach to model checking in zero knowl-edge: Zero-Knowledge Model Checking (ZKMC). Our method com-bines proof-theoretic model checking with zero-knowledge proofs to construct a proof certificate that the system satisfies the spec-ification and then convince an external verifier that our public 

artefacts—the specification and the proof certificate—are valid with respect to our secret artefact—the system under analysis—without revealing any information about the system itself. Our setting considers the model checking of reactive systems with discrete state spaces against 𝜔 -regular specifications, sub-suming linear temporal logic (LTL) [ 61 ]. We formulate the model checking problem as establishing that the Büchi automaton for the complementary specification never accepts an execution of the system, that is, that the language of the corresponding synchronous composition is empty. To prove this, we require a ranking function

showing that the automaton cannot accept any execution [ 72 , 73 ]. Accordingly, our proof certificate consists precisely of this rank-ing function. Building on this premise, we realise ZKMC through two complementary algorithms: an explicit-state and a symbolic algorithm. Our explicit-state algorithm assumes systems represented as finite-state transition graphs whose states are labelled with atomic propositions, i.e., as Kripke structures (Section 4). We associate the system with a ranking function over the state space of the sys-tem alongside an appropriate automaton. The task is to convince an external verifier of the validity of the proof certificate without revealing the system’s initial states or its transition relation. Our scheme employs KZG polynomial commitments with batch evalua-tion to ensure that any hypothetical state or transition that would violate the publicly disclosed proof certificate cannot correspond to an actual initial state or transition of the secret system. Upon successful verification, which is guaranteed by the standard cryp-tographic properties of polynomial commitments [ 47 ], the proof of correctness follows by contraposition. Our proof is two group elements regardless of state-space size; prover work is quadratic in the number of state of the system. Our symbolic algorithm assumes reactive systems represented by integer variables and guarded commands defined through lin-ear constraints with integer coefficients, potentially yielding very large or infinite state spaces. We require state propositions to be represented as Boolean combinations of linear inequalities, and ranking functions to be expressed as piecewise-defined affine func-tions; we derive equivalent proof obligations in a uniform form, each having a secret component corresponding to the coefficients of the original reactive system. The task is to convince an exter-nal verifier that each obligation holds without revealing the secret component. Our scheme computes a Farkas witness for each obliga-tion and then, to establish the validity, employs specialised sigma protocols for zero-knowledge matrix multiplication and equality sub-protocols over two-tier Pedersen commitments [ 21 ], and we give a new zero-knowledge range proof construction ( zkrp ). Upon successful verification, we obtain a zero-knowledge proof of correct-ness for the reactive system under analysis. Prover and verifier cost are independent of the state space and polynomial in the number of guarded commands and automaton transitions. We developed a prototype implementation and evaluated our two algorithms on a range of liveness verification tasks. Our bench-marks include a 3-way handshake protocol with exponential back-off, a model of DHCP, and a round-robin scheduling algorithm (Section 6). Our explicit-state algorithm can verify systems with up to approximately 29 states within a 2 h time limit. Under the same time constraint, our symbolic algorithm handles significantly larger tasks, scaling to approximately 221 states. We emphasise that the explicit-state algorithm makes no assumptions about the struc-ture of the system, whereas the symbolic algorithm requires the system to be expressed as linear guarded commands. These results therefore highlight a trade-off between expressivity and perfor-mance, with the explicit-state and symbolic approaches addressing different use cases. In summary, our contribution is threefold. First, we introduce the first framework for model checking in zero knowledge, com-bining cryptographic principles with proof techniques for formal verification. Second, we present two schemes that realise this frame-work: an explicit-state algorithm for finite-state transition graphs and a symbolic algorithm for guarded command languages. Third, we implement both schemes in a prototype and demonstrate the practical efficacy of our framework on LTL model checking tasks applied to standard communication and coordination protocols.

Remark 2.2 (Specification- and Certification-Induced Leak-age). Our framework ensures that neither the commitment 𝑐 M nor the proof 𝜋 reveals any information about M. However, selecting a secure specification 𝜙 and certificate 𝑣 𝜙 remains the user’s respon-sibility. In the extreme case where the public specification 𝜙 or the certificate 𝑣 𝜙 uniquely determine the secret system M, any party capable of verifying the proof could, in principle, reconstruct the sys-tem regardless of the protocol’s cryptographic guarantees. Developing systematic specification and certification schemes that mitigate this risk is beyond the scope of this work and constitutes an open problem. 

We further emphasise that side channels arising from the prover’s computation, denial-of-service attacks, or attacks that combine our protocol with auxiliary channels (such as the timing of certificate generation, which may depend on M) are addressed by standard countermeasures in the literature. Summarising, we require the design of three components: a cer-tification scheme for model checking, a commitment scheme for the system, and a zero-knowledge proof scheme that jointly cap-tures certification and commitment. We instantiate our framework for LTL model checking in Section 3, and present two alternative commitment and zero-knowledge schemes in Sections 4 and 5.

Remark 5.1 (Soundness and Completeness of Farkas Wit-nesses). Farkas’ lemma characterises the unsatisfiability of systems of linear inequalities over the rationals, whereas our witnesses (𝜆 s, 𝜇 s)

are integer and restricted to the range [− 𝑀, 𝑀 ]. This approach is sound for the obligations in Eqs. (22) to (24) , since every integer solu-tion is also a rational one. However, the converse fails on two accounts. First, the integer restriction of the dual system may be unsatisfiable even when its rational counterpart is satisfiable, in which case no witness can be found. Second, a rational witness may exist but require coefficients outside [− 𝑀, 𝑀 ], making it unrepresentable in our encod-ing. Thus, our witnesses are sufficient but not necessary, and a prover may fail to produce one even when the obligation holds. 

Each obligation of the form of Eq. (28) comprises three classes of operations: (i) matrix–vector multiplications and additions, (ii) range membership proofs, and (iii) equality proofs. This decompo-sition suggests a modular approach: we design a specialized zero-knowledge sub-protocol for each class, prove each independently and then compose them into a single zero-knowledge proof. 

Cryptographic Requirements. Our encoding imposes two require-ments on the cryptographic setting. First, since we operate over matrices and vectors, we need a commitment scheme that exposes their row and column structure to the sub-protocols. Second, since we operate over integers while many cryptographic protocols op-erate over a prime-order field Z𝑝 , we need to reconcile integer arithmetic with field arithmetic without unwanted wraparound.

## 6 Experimental Evaluation 

We implemented an end-to-end prototype 2 of both the explicit-state and symbolic algorithms and use it to investigate three re-search questions. (RQ1) Can either scheme verify realistic protocols within practical time and memory budgets? (RQ2) How do the two schemes compare in scalability on these protocols (explicit-state in terms of state-space size, symbolic in terms of the number of proof obligations)? (RQ3) In which settings is each scheme preferable? Our main finding is that the two approaches are complementary. The explicit-state scheme performs well on small systems with negligible setup cost but degrades quadratically in |𝑆 |. By contrast, the symbolic scheme scales to systems with up to 220 .9 states and

## 7 Related Work 

Our work lies at the intersection of zero-knowledge proofs and automated reasoning, a new research direction for the cybersecu-rity and formal methods communities. Prior work has proposed zero-knowledge protocols for theorem proving with different logi-cal backends, including zkUNSAT for propositional unsatisfiabil-ity [ 56 ], zkSMT for first-order logic unsatisfiability in decidable theories [ 55 ], and zkPi for interactive theorem proving [ 25 , 53 ]. Although model checking can be broadly viewed as a special case of theorem proving, our setting differs fundamentally from prior work from both formal methods and security and privacy perspec-tives. We target the automated verification of software systems and operate at a different level of abstraction than the existing approaches, which focus on pure validity questions over logical statements. Crucially, prior work assumes a public theorem and a secret proof witness; this assumption does not directly carry over to our setting, where the theorems—in this paper, the obligations of Theorem 3.2—contain both public and secret components. Even more importantly, our approach mandates commitments to the system as a core component, a requirement considered tangentially in prior work. Commitments are essential to prevent attacks in which a malicious prover replaces the system under analysis with a different one, whether during, before, or after the proof. Privacy-preserving verification is a broader, emerging area at the intersection of computer-aided verification and applied cryp-tography. Prior work has explored this space through the lens of multi-party computation (MPC), with solutions for CTL model checking and runtime verification [ 42 , 46 ]. To the best of our knowl-edge, our work is the first to implement a privacy-preserving formal verification algorithm using zero-knowledge proofs. Proof certificates are rooted in program logics and the axiomatic semantics of software systems [ 33 ]. The proof rule of Theorem 3.2 builds on results on fair termination in nondeterministic Büchi automata [ 40 , 72 ]. Our scheme extend to alternative proof rules for generalised Büchi automata [ 50 ] and to other temporal specifi-cations [ 22 , 23 ], assuming systems and proof obligations are rep-resented either explicitly or as linear guarded commands, as in Sections 4 and 5. In principle, our approach is also amenable to proof rules for probabilistic model checking [ 3 – 5, 28 , 43 , 51 ] and to the verification of cyber-physical systems [ 64 , 65 ]. However, ex-tending ZKMC to reasoning over continuous domains, which is needed for probabilistic reasoning and for systems with continuous state spaces, requires a generalisation of our cryptographic routines and remains future work. An important application domain for ZKMC is AI systems, where we identify two main directions: verification of robustness proper-ties of neural networks, both in isolation and under feedback con-trol [ 9 , 45 , 66 , 68 – 70 , 74 ], and neural certificates for software [ 36 –38 ]and for cyber-physical systems [ 1, 2, 16 , 54 , 58 , 75 , 76 ]. Although this setting is, in principle, a special case of our approach, in prac-tice it is likely to yield an intractable number of proof obligations and will therefore require the development of specialised symbolic techniques for neural networks, which we leave to future work. Our symbolic algorithm combines Farkas witnesses with zero-knowledge proofs for linear algebra in a novel way. The duality of linear programming, of which Farkas’ lemma is a special case, is a standard technique in the synthesis and verification of ranking functions [ 19 , 62 ], while zero-knowledge proofs for linear-algebraic relations are now well studied [ 14 , 21 ]. To the best of our knowledge, our approach is the first to use linear programming duality as the structural backbone of a zero-knowledge proof. A natural alternative is to encode a model-checking proof as an arithmetic circuit and verify its execution using a general-purpose zkSNARK or zkVM [ 34 , 39 , 53 , 67 ]. We deliberately avoid this ap-proach for two reasons. First, the arithmetisation cost of explicit-state search scales with the size of the state space and quickly be-comes prohibitive. Even for our medium-sized DHCP benchmarks (approximately 10 5 states), the resulting circuit would contain hun-dreds of millions of constraints. Second, and more fundamentally, a proof-theoretic perspective decouples the certificate of correctness from the procedure that produced it. The prover may use any model-checking algorithm, whether explicit-state, symbolic, abstraction-based, or even manual, provided that the resulting ranking function satisfies the public obligations of Theorem 3.2. Our schemes verify these obligations directly, with costs determined by the size of the certificate rather than by the effort required to construct it. 

## 8 Conclusion 

We have presented the first approach to model checking in zero knowledge, enabling formal verification in scenarios where the confidentiality of the system is crucial. Our implementation has demonstrated the practical viability of our new technology. Our work raises many scientific questions, from the security implica-tions related to commitment before and after a model checking proof, as well as the information a public specification may reveal about a secret system. ## A Open Science 

We provide three tools and prototypes, as well as a series of bench-mark data files, that enable the program committee to evaluate the paper’s core contributions. We provide: 

• encoder - A lightweight preprocessing front-end tool writ-ten in Python which implements a toy certifier input lan-guage for guarded-command programs, Büchi automata, and piecewise-linear ranking functions, and computes batches and obligations, using Z3 [ 26 ] to derive the Farkas wit-nesses. This tool has built-in benchmarking capabilities to time explicit and symbolic output generation. 

• zkmc-explicit - A prototype implementation of our ex-plicit approach, written in Rust, which takes output from 

zkterm-tool as input and provides library methods to prove and verify as per our specification. This prototype comes with a benchmark tool that times setup, proving, and verifying of a given input. 

• zkmc-symbolic - A prototype implementation of our sym-bolic approach, written in Rust, which takes output from 

zkterm-tool as input and provides library methods to prove and verify as per our specification. This prototype comes with a benchmark tool that times setup, proving, and verifying of a given input. 

• Example guarded command files - Various models that cor-respond to our benchmarked instances in guarded com-mand form, which can be readily used with zkterm-tool 

to build files which can then be used as input into our proto-types. These files consist of: a minimal example.gc ; a sim-ple counter from 0 to maxVal ; our round-robin.gc exam-ple used in benchmarking; three of our models of exponen-tial backoff, namely exp_backoff_state_opt_small.gc ,
