Abstract 

Multi-agent deliberation among LLMs can improve reasoning, but deployment requires deciding when the current answer is reliable enough to act on and when it should be escalated to human review. We formulate this as budgeted act-or-defer decision making. At each round, the system maps the debate prefix to a low-dimensional state, computes a k-nearest-neighbor lower confidence bound on state-conditional correctness using calibration data, and acts only when the bound exceeds a user-specified reliability threshold. The certificate controls wrong actions through the decomposition β = δ + α + εact , separating calibration failure, residual action risk, and representation gap. The guarantee is conditional, not distribution-free: it relies on a valid local bias envelope and an action-region representation-gap bound, and each assumption is paired with falsification-style diagnostics. Because the same absolute wrong-action budget has different meanings across tasks of different difficulty, we set budgets relative to each task’s final-round error using training data only, and evaluate safety by normalized budget usage 

WA /β . On six benchmarks against nine baselines, the method uses 9–12% of the pre-declared budget on activated datasets, reaching up to 84% automation and 96% acted-on accuracy; on stress-test datasets, it defers rather than forcing unreliable automation. Rather than relying on per-task post-hoc threshold search, the method prospectively converts a user-declared wrong-action budget into an auditable act-or-defer operating point before deployment, under explicitly stated assumptions. 

1 Introduction 

Multi-agent debate, where multiple LLM instances exchange responses over several rounds, has been shown to improve reasoning [Du et al., 2024, Liang et al., 2024]. To deploy such systems in practice, one needs a stopping criterion with principled control over the probability of acting on a wrong answer. In human-in-the-loop deployments this stopping decision is also an escalation decision: the system should act autonomously only when the local reliability bound supports action, and otherwise defer the instance to human review. Existing approaches either operate at a fixed final round with only marginal coverage (e.g., split conformal prediction [Vovk et al., 2005, Angelopoulos and Bates, 2023]) or rely on heuristic consensus thresholds without explicit wrong-action budgets. Neither provides per-instance, per-round reliability bounds that enable adaptive stopping. We address this gap by constructing local lower confidence bounds on the correctness probability using k-nearest-neighbor ( k-NN) neighborhoods in a debate-state metric space. The debate prefix at round t is mapped to a state Ut in a metric space (U, d ). The local correctness function qt(u) = Pr( Y = ˆYt | Ut = u) is bounded from below via a local bias envelope bt,k (u) that controls the

Difficulty-normalized deployment budgets. A fixed absolute wrong-action budget is not a task-invariant deployment requirement: it corresponds to very different levels of tolerance depending on the task’s final-round error. The paper does not provide distribution-free conditional correctness; the contribution is to expose assumptions, budget their cost (Proposition 4), and stress-test them with diagnostic proxies (Table 11). The representation gap (Assumption 4) is the unavoidable price of a deployable low-dimensional stopping state: any practical system commits to a finite summary of Ft, and εact quantifies the one-sided cost of that commitment in the action region; our diagnostics do not prove this gap is small but are designed to falsify obviously insufficient representations and to guide conservative inflation of εact (Appendix I). Accordingly εact enters the wrong-action bound additively, and the certificate degrades gracefully if Assumption 4 fails (Proposition 4). The 2D default state (p(1)

Low activation on GPQA and MuSR is consistent with the statistical nature of local certification: small n and high base error enlarge both the k-nearest-neighbor approximation radius and the concentration slack, so the lower bound rarely crosses threshold under moderate budgets; the per-component decomposition is in Appendix N. We view this deferral as intended behavior rather than a failure. 

8 Related Work 

Multi-agent debate and LLM confidence. Multi-agent debate [Du et al., 2024, Liang et al., 2024] improves reasoning but lacks explicit wrong-action budgets; related directions include stability detection [Hu et al., 2025] and self-consistency [Wang et al., 2023]. LLM confidence estimation [Kadavath et al., 2022, Xiong et al., 2024, Geng et al., 2024] and calibration [Guo et al., 2017] focus on aggregate metrics, not local certified bounds. 

Conformal, selective prediction, and anytime-valid inference. Split conformal [Vovk et al., 2005, Angelopoulos and Bates, 2023] provides marginal coverage; extensions include risk control [Bates et al., 2021, Angelopoulos et al., 2024] and LLM applications [Kumar et al., 2023, Quach et al., 2024]. Localized methods [Guan, 2023, Hore and Barber, 2023] share our localization but target coverage, not correctness. Key distinctions: (a) selective prediction [El-Yaniv and Wiener, 2010, Geifman and El-Yaniv, 2017] uses marginal score thresholds without local state-conditional act-or-defer certificates—under the same difficulty-normalized deployment budget, these baselines consume 18–82% of the pre-declared wrong-action allowance (Table 1), while our state-conditional bound extracts high-precision automation subsets at ≤12% on activated datasets; (b) CRC [Angelopoulos et al., 2024] provides aggregate risk control but not adaptive stopping or state-conditional bounds; (c) localized conformal targets coverage, not correctness. We are not aware of prior work that combines all four elements—adaptive multi-round stopping, local state-conditional certification, wrong-action budgeting with a task-relative-risk deployment policy, and multi-agent deliberation—in a single framework (Appendix E). 

9 Conclusion 

We reframe multi-agent deliberation as budgeted act-or-defer for human-in-the-loop deployment: local k-NN lower bounds on state-conditional correctness trigger autonomous action when reliable and defer otherwise, with β = δ + α + εact . The guarantee is conditional on two auditable structural assumptions (bias envelope and representation gap), each paired with a diagnostic proxy rather than a formal verifier; assumption failures enter the wrong-action bound additively (Proposition 4), making the cost of each violation explicit rather than hidden behind a global correctness claim. Under training-selected difficulty-normalized budgets, the framework uses 9–12% of the allowed budget on activated datasets (BBH 84%/96%, MMLU-Pro 71%/94%, ARC 75%/97%, LogiQA 28%/89%), while baselines at risk-calibrated defaults consume 18–82%; on stress tests (GPQA, MuSR) the method defers rather than forcing unreliable automation, the intended behavior under insufficient calibration evidence. The contribution is not better subset quality at a matched automation level—tuned scalar thresholds can recover the same operating point after per-task search—but the prospective conversion of a declared wrong-action budget into an auditable act-or-defer policy before deployment, under explicitly exposed assumptions; we view this as a template for other inference-time compute pipelines where repeated autonomous action is cheap but occasional wrong action is costly. The central open challenge is the geometry–sufficiency trade-off governing εact , motivating future work on learned representations and extensions to open-ended generation. 9References

B.6 Computational complexity 

Let n be the calibration size, T the number of debate rounds, |K| the number of candidate k values, and D the state dimension. 

Precomputation (one-time per split): Running all T -round debates for n calibration instances costs

Per-test-instance: For each test instance, the cost is O(T · |K| · n · D) for brute-force k-NN search across all rounds and k values. In practice, two factors provide a safety margin: (i) penalized splines under -approximate variation, making ω˜q (r) ≤ ωt(r) unlikely but ˜qt smoother than qt;(ii) when ˜qt under-smooths (tracks noise), ω˜q (r) > ω t(r), inflating the envelope conservatively. Impact on validity. The theoretical guarantee (Corollary 8) holds for any smoother, provided 

ˆωt(r) ≥ ωt(r) with high probability. The choice of smoother affects the tightness of the bound, not its validity. An undersmoother (too flexible) will track noise in Z, inflating ˆωt and making the bias envelope conservative. An oversmoother (too rigid) will underestimate the true variation of qt, potentially violating the envelope. The logistic GAM strikes a good balance: the spline penalty prevents overfitting while allowing multivariate dependence, producing a tighter modulus than univariate isotonic regression. 

C.5 Envelope stress test 

A central concern is whether ˆωt ≥ ωt holds in practice—i.e., whether the empirical modulus overes-timates the true modulus of continuity. We test robustness by inflating ˆωt by multiplicative factors 

{1.0, 1.25 , 1.5, 2.0} and measuring the impact on Act and WA (Table 4). If the baseline modulus were a tight upper bound on ωt, inflating it would shrink the bias correction bt,k (u) = ˆ ωt(ht,k (u)) ,lowering Lt and reducing automation. The results show that even at 2× inflation, Act degrades only modestly (MMLU-Pro: 29.8% →27.8%; BBH: 74.9% →72.3%) while WA remains unchanged. This insensitivity confirms that the empirical modulus is already conservative (substantially above ωt), providing a safety margin against modulus misspecification. ARC remains at 0% Act at all factors because it does not certify at β = 0 .15 .

Multi-agent LLM debate and inference-time compute. Multi-agent debate [Du et al., 2024, Liang et al., 2024] and scalable multi-agent reasoning [Qian et al., 2025] improve accuracy by having LLMs critique each other’s answers; ChatEval [Chan et al., 2024] and debate-for-judgment pipelines [Khan et al., 2024] extend this to evaluation. Theoretical motivations include AI safety via debate [Irving et al., 2018]. Recent work questions whether more debate rounds always help [Choi et al., 2025, Xie et al., 2025, Wang and Huang, 2024, Li et al., 2024, Zhang et al., 2024] and studies stability of collective answers [Hu et al., 2025]. A separate line improves single-agent reasoning via self-consistency [Wang et al., 2023] and chain-of-thought [Wei et al., 2022]. None of these works provide instance-level wrong-action certificates or a budgeted stopping rule; they treat debate as an accuracy-improving heuristic rather than a certifiable procedure. 

LLM confidence, calibration, and selective generation. LLMs can self-assess correctness via probe-based methods [Kadavath et al., 2022], verbalized confidence [Tian et al., 2023, Xiong et al., 2024, Lin et al., 2022], or consistency checks [Manakul et al., 2023, Kuhn et al., 2023]. Broader surveys cover estimation strategies [Geng et al., 2024] and calibration [Guo et al., 2017, Naeini et al., 2015, Platt, 1999, Sharma et al., 2024, Marx et al., 2023, Vaicenavicius et al., 2019, Blasiok et al., 2023]; see also verified-uncertainty calibration [Kumar et al., 2019] and context-dependent effects [Zhao et al., 2021]. Selective generation [Ren et al., 2023, Varshney et al., 2023, Feng et al., 2024, Yang et al., 2024] refuses to answer below a confidence threshold. All these methods operate on marginal (instance-averaged) scores; our state-conditional lower bound instead certifies the correctness probability at a specific debate state, yielding an explicit wrong-action budget rather than a tuned score threshold. 

Conformal prediction and risk control. Split and full conformal prediction [Vovk et al., 2005, Papadopoulos, 2008, Lei et al., 2013, 2018, Angelopoulos and Bates, 2023] provide marginal coverage for prediction sets; extensions to regression and classification include [Romano et al., 2019, 2020, Angelopoulos et al., 2021]. Conformal risk control generalizes coverage to arbitrary risk functions [Bates et al., 2021, Angelopoulos et al., 2024, 2022, Fisch et al., 2022], and language-model applications [Kumar et al., 2023, Quach et al., 2024] adapt these tools to generation. Adaptive and online conformal methods track distribution shift over time [Gibbs and Candès, 2021, Gibbs et al., 2023, Barber et al., 2023], while game-theoretic and e-value formulations unify anytime-valid inference [Shafer et al., 2011, Ramdas et al., 2022, 2023, Vovk and Wang, 2021, Grünwald et al., 2024, Howard et al., 2021, Waudby-Smith and Ramdas, 2024, Johari et al., 2022, Ville, 1939, Wald, 1945]. Our framework differs in target and geometry: instead of constructing marginally valid prediction sets, we build a pointwise lower bound on state-conditional correctness and use it as a stopping rule; conformal risk control is a natural aggregate baseline (Table 1). 

Local and conditional conformal methods. Classical coverage guarantees are marginal; strength-ening them to condition on features is known to be fundamentally hard [Vovk, 2013, Barber et al., 2021]. Localized conformal methods [Guan, 2023, Hore and Barber, 2023, Gupta et al., 2020] reweight calibration data near a query point, and Mondrian / group-conditional conformal [Vovk et al., 2005, Barber et al., 2023] partitions the feature space. Our k-NN bound is similar in spirit—local reweighting via nearest neighbors—but targets a different functional (wrong-action probability in the action region, not coverage of a prediction set), and pairs the local estimator with an explicit bias envelope bt,k rather than assuming smoothness implicitly. 25 Distribution-free inference and concentration. The concentration inequalities that underpin our simultaneous validity proof are classical: Hoeffding’s inequality [Hoeffding, 1963], empirical Bernstein bounds [Maurer and Pontil, 2009], and general concentration machinery [Boucheron et al., 2013, Wasserman, 2006]. Stone’s theorem [Stone, 1977] and its k-NN specializations [Cover and Hart, 1967, Fix and Hodges, 1951, Devroye et al., 1996, Györfi et al., 2002, Biau and Devroye, 2015, Chaudhuri and Dasgupta, 2010] give the nonparametric rate underlying Corollary 11; Tsybakov [Tsybakov, 2009] provides the minimax lower bounds. 

Selective classification and opinion dynamics. Selective classification [El-Yaniv and Wiener, 2010, Geifman and El-Yaniv, 2017] provides accuracy–coverage trade-offs under a reject option; our act-or-defer rule is a multi-round, state-conditional, budget-aligned refinement. The “social probability” P (t)social connects to classical opinion-dynamics models—DeGroot consensus [DeGroot, 1974], bounded-confidence dynamics [Hegselmann and Krause, 2002, Deffuant et al., 2000], and their steady-state analyses [Baccelli et al., 2021]—and to foundational aggregation theory [Arrow, 1951]. We treat agent debate as a signal-generating process rather than a truth-seeking dynamical system: the state Ut summarizes the resulting signal, and calibration data determines whether the signal warrants action. 

Benchmarks and auxiliary. We use standard LLM reasoning benchmarks [Hendrycks et al., 2021, Wang et al., 2024, Clark et al., 2018, Suzgun et al., 2023, Liu et al., 2020, Sprague et al., 2024, Rein et al., 2023] throughout. Compared to all of the above, the distinguishing combination in this paper is: (a) a state-conditional 

local lower bound, (b) a budget-aligned stopping rule with an explicit three-way decomposition 

β = δ + α + εact , (c) adaptive k with an oracle-style slack inequality (Theorem 6), and (d) application to multi-round multi-agent debate with diagnostic proxies for each structural assumption. We are not aware of prior work combining these four elements in a single framework.

K Matched-automation protocol 

The matched-automation comparison in §6.2 is measured by the following protocol. 

Protocol. Fix a target automation rate A⋆ equal to our method’s Act on the test split under the training-selected λ⋆d. For each baseline, we grid-search its threshold θ on the training split only such that the induced training-time Act lies within ±2% of A⋆; ties are broken by selecting the smallest test-time dWA . We then evaluate Acc |Act, WA, and WA /β on the held-out test split. No test-set quantity enters the threshold choice. Baselines whose monotonicity does not admit an Act close to
